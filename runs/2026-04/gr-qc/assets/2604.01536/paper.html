Geometric origin of the cosmological constant from Einstein-Chern-Simons gravity compactified to four dimensions

Abstract

We present a model in which the cosmological constant emerges as a purely geometric effect from the four-dimensional compactification of five-dimensional Einstein–Chern–Simons gravity. Within this framework, the compactification of the extra dimension gives rise to an effective cosmological constant Λ\Lambda that depends on the compactification radius rcr_{c}, the dimensional coupling parameter ll, and the trace h~\tilde{h} of the compactified field hah^{a}, rather than being introduced as an ad hoc free parameter. We demonstrate that the resulting field equations are structurally equivalent to those of General Relativity with a cosmological constant. Consequently, all known vacuum solutions—including the Schwarzschild–de Sitter, Kerr–de Sitter, and FLRW spacetimes—remain valid in this setting. As a concrete application, we explicitly derive the Kottler (Schwarzschild–de Sitter) black hole solution.

We identify two distinct dynamical regimes. In the weak-field regime, Λ∝l2​h~/rc3\Lambda\propto l^{2}\tilde{h}/r_{c}^{3} and its sign is controlled by l2​h~l^{2}\tilde{h}, requiring fine-tuning of the coupling constants to reproduce the observed value Λobs≈10−52​m−2\Lambda_{\text{obs}}\approx 10^{-52}\,\text{m}^{-2}. In the strong-field regime, the dependence on ll and h~\tilde{h} drops out through algebraic cancellation and one obtains Λ≈3/(4​rc2)\Lambda\approx 3/(4r_{c}^{2}), independently of the Chern-Simons coupling. This regime naturally reproduces Λobs\Lambda_{\rm obs} for a compactification radius rc≈0.78​H0−1≈8.2×1025​mr_{c}\approx 0.78\,H_{0}^{-1}\approx 8.2\times 10^{25}\,\text{m}, without any fine-tuning, with the cosmological constant determined purely by the compactification radius, and the Bekenstein-Hawking entropy of the cosmological horizon yields Scosm=4​π​kB​rc2/lPl2∼10122​kBS_{\rm cosm}=4\pi k_{B}r_{c}^{2}/l_{\rm Pl}^{2}\sim 10^{122}\,k_{B}, in agreement with the Gibbons-Hawking result and admitting a direct geometric interpretation in terms of rcr_{c}.

This framework reframes the cosmological constant problem geometrically: rather than asking why Λ\Lambda is so small, one asks why the compactification radius is so large—a reformulation consistent with the possibility of coexisting with a large extra dimension without violating established gravitational tests.

I Introduction

The formulation of gravity as a gauge theory requires, as a fundamental principle, that the action be constructed independently of any fixed spacetime background. In this sense, the dynamical geometry itself must emerge from the gauge structure, rather than being imposed a priori. A natural realization of this idea is provided by Chern–Simons (ChS) theories, whose actions are invariant under a given gauge algebra and do not rely on a background metric.

In particular, a gravitational Lagrangian satisfying these requirements can be formulated as a Chern–Simons form for the (Anti-)de Sitter algebra. Such a construction leads to a geometrically motivated action principle in which the vielbein eae^{a} and the spin connection ωa​b\omega^{ab} arise as components of a single gauge connection valued in the (A)dS algebra [1, 2, 3].

In five dimensions, the corresponding Chern–Simons action can be written explicitly as

Here, κ5\kappa_{5} is the five-dimensional gravitational coupling constant, εa​b​c​d​e\varepsilon_{abcde} is the five-dimensional Levi-Civita symbol, eae^{a} is the 1-form vielbein, and Ra​b=d​ωa​b+ωa​ωc​bcR^{ab}=\mathrm{d}\omega^{ab}+\omega^{a}{}_{c}\omega^{cb} is the curvature 2-form in the first-order formalism, obtained from the 1-form spin connection ωa​b\omega^{ab}, which in turn is related to the Riemann curvature tensor. If Chern-Simons (ChS) theories are the appropriate gauge theories to provide a framework for gravitational interaction, then they must satisfy the correspondence principle; namely, they must be reducible to General Relativity (GR).

In a previous work, it was shown that standard five-dimensional (5D) GR can be obtained from a ChS gravity theory based on a certain Lie algebra 𝔅5\mathfrak{B}_{5}, whose generators satisfy the commutation relations shown in Eq. It is straightforward to prove that GR in 5D can also be obtained from a ChS gravity theory based on a Lie algebra 𝔅~5\mathfrak{\tilde{B}}_{5}, which is derived from the de Sitter (dS) algebra using the aforementioned S-expansion method. The only difference between 𝔅5\mathfrak{B}_{5} and 𝔅~5\mathfrak{\tilde{B}}_{5} lies in the sign of the commutator between generalized translations [𝑷a,𝑷b]\left[\boldsymbol{P}_{a},\boldsymbol{P}_{b}\right] from the algebra shown in Eq. By applying Theorem VII.2 of Ref. [5] and the extended Cartan’s homotopy formula as in Ref. [8], it was found that the five-dimensional ChS Lagrangian for the 𝔅5\mathfrak{B}_{5} and 𝔅~5\mathfrak{\tilde{B}}_{5} algebras is given by

Furthermore, in Refs. [13, 12], it was found that five-dimensional Einstein-Chern-Simons gravity is consistent with a four-dimensional spacetime. In fact, these references established that the Randall-Sundrum [14, 15] compactification of action (2) leads to the following four-dimensional action:

where κ\kappa is the four-dimensional gravitational coupling constant. It is important to emphasize that the field hah^{a} induces a cosmological constant arising from the compactification of the five-dimensional Einstein–Chern–Simons theory to four dimensions. Its sign, positive (de Sitter) or negative (Anti-de Sitter), is fixed by the sign of the product l2​h~l^{2}\tilde{h}, as demonstrated explicitly in Sec. II.

The origin of the cosmological constant remains one of the most profound open problems in theoretical physics. Quantum field theory estimates for the vacuum energy density exceed the observed value by approximately 120 orders of magnitude–a discrepancy often regarded as the worst prediction in the history of physics [16, 17, 18]. In standard General Relativity, Λ\Lambda must be introduced as a free parameter without any fundamental explanation for its small but nonzero value. The framework presented here offers a conceptually different perspective: the cosmological constant emerges naturally from the compactification of Einstein-Chern-Simons gravity, with its magnitude determined by the compactification radius rcr_{c} rather than being put in by hand. As we shall demonstrate, the observed value Λobs≈10−52​m−2\Lambda_{\text{obs}}\approx 10^{-52}\,\text{m}^{-2} corresponds to rc∼1026​mr_{c}\sim 10^{26}\,\text{m}, remarkably close to the current Hubble radius. While this does not solve the cosmological constant problem in the fundamental sense–since one must still explain why rcr_{c} takes this particular value–it provides a geometric mechanism that relates Λ\Lambda to the large-scale structure of spacetime, potentially offering new avenues for understanding dark energy.

This article is organized as follows. In Sec. II, we show that the field equations obtained from the four-dimensional compactification of five-dimensional Einstein-Chern-Simons gravity coincide with the Einstein field equations with a cosmological constant that depends on the trace of the compactified field and the compactification radius rcr_{c}. In Sec. III, we derive the explicit field equations for a static, spherically symmetric metric and show that the most general solution is a Kottler-type (Schwarzschild-de Sitter) black hole. In Sec. IV, we analyze the implications of this framework for Λ\LambdaCDM cosmology: we identify two distinct dynamical regimes of the cosmological constant, derive an estimate for the compactification radius, discuss the embedding of Λ\LambdaCDM within Einstein-Chern-Simons gravity, and examine the geometric reformulation of the cosmological constant problem. Finally, Sec. V summarizes our main results and outlines directions for future work.

II Einstein Field Equations from Einstein-Chern-Simons Gravity

In this section, we show that the field equations (11) and (12) coincide with the Einstein field equations with a specific cosmological constant, which depends on the trace h~\tilde{h} of the four-dimensional component of the 5​D5D field hah^{a} and the compactification radius rcr_{c}. Indeed, in tensorial language, the action (3) takes the form [13, 12]

where it can be seen that as l⟶0l\longrightarrow 0 then K⟶0K\longrightarrow 0, and actions (3) and (5) become the four-dimensional Einstein-Hilbert action. The field h~μ​ν\tilde{h}_{\mu\nu}, inherited from the five-dimensional theory, is in principle an independent symmetric tensor. However, since we seek solutions that reduce to standard General Relativity with a cosmological constant—thus preserving all classical tests of gravity—the natural requirement is that h~μ​ν\tilde{h}_{\mu\nu} contributes to the field equations only through a term proportional to the metric tensor, mimicking the structure of a cosmological term Λ​gμ​ν\Lambda g_{\mu\nu}. This is achieved by the Ansatz

h~μ​ν=14​F~​(ϕ~)​g~μ​ν,\tilde{h}_{\mu\nu}=\frac{1}{4}\tilde{F}(\tilde{\phi})\tilde{g}_{\mu\nu}, (6)

where F~\tilde{F} is a function of a scalar field ϕ~=ϕ~​(x~μ)\tilde{\phi}=\tilde{\phi}(\tilde{x}^{\mu}), with x~μ=(x0,xi)\tilde{x}^{\mu}=(x^{0},x^{i}) for i=1,2,3i=1,2,3. This choice guarantees that the resulting field equations retain the form of Einstein’s equations with an effective cosmological constant, ensuring that all known solutions of General Relativity with Λ\Lambda–such as Schwarzschild-de Sitter, Kerr-de Sitter, and FLRW spacetimes–remain valid within this framework. The physical content of the compactification is then encoded entirely in the geometric origin of Λ\Lambda, rather than in modifications to the structure of the solutions themselves. With this Ansatz, we can write

R~νμ​h~μν=14​F~​(ϕ~)​R~,h~=h~μ​ν​g~μ​ν=F~​(ϕ~),\tilde{R}^{\mu}_{\ \nu}\tilde{h}^{\nu}_{\ \mu}=\frac{1}{4}\tilde{F}(\tilde{\phi})\tilde{R},\qquad\tilde{h}=\tilde{h}_{\mu\nu}\tilde{g}^{\mu\nu}=\tilde{F}(\tilde{\phi}), (7)

which corresponds to an action for four-dimensional gravity non-minimally coupled to a scalar field. This implies that the cosmological constant in question may be interpreted as a consequence of 5​D5D to 4​D4D compactification. It is important to note that Eq. (11) can be rewritten in the form

The distinction is conceptual: in the present framework, Λ\Lambda emerges from the compactification of the fifth dimension, providing a geometric origin that transcends both interpretations.

In this expression, the ±\pm signs reflect the fact that the product l2​h~l^{2}\tilde{h} may be negative or positive, leading to either a negative cosmological constant (Anti-de Sitter, Λ<0\Lambda<0) or a positive one (de Sitter, Λ>0\Lambda>0). Consistency with the observed accelerated expansion of the universe requires the positive branch.

Several important features of this result deserve emphasis:

(i) Geometric origin of Λ\Lambda: The cosmological constant emerges naturally from the compactification procedure, without being introduced by hand into the action. This provides a geometric explanation for dark energy within the framework of Einstein-Chern-Simons gravity.

(ii) Dependence on the coupling constant: In the limit l→0l\rightarrow 0, we have Λ→0\Lambda\rightarrow 0, recovering standard General Relativity without a cosmological constant. This shows that Λ\Lambda is intrinsically linked to the Chern-Simons corrections and would vanish in pure Einstein gravity.

It is worth emphasizing that the field equations (11) are structurally identical to the Einstein field equations with a cosmological constant. Consequently, all known vacuum solutions of General Relativity with Λ\Lambda–including Schwarzschild-de Sitter, Kerr-de Sitter, and FLRW spacetimes–remain valid within this framework. The key difference lies not in the geometric structure of the solutions, but in the origin and interpretation of Λ\Lambda: rather than being a free parameter, it is now determined by the compactification radius rcr_{c} and the coupling constants of the five-dimensional Einstein-Chern-Simons theory, as expressed in Eq. (16).

IV Λ\LambdaCDM Cosmology from Einstein-Chern-Simons Compactification

The Λ\LambdaCDM model constitutes the standard framework of modern cosmology, successfully describing the large-scale structure and evolution of the universe in terms of a cosmological constant Λ\Lambda, cold dark matter, baryonic matter, and radiation. Observational evidence from Type Ia supernovae, the cosmic microwave background (CMB), and baryon acoustic oscillations (BAO) strongly supports this model, indicating that approximately 68% of the energy content of the universe consists of dark energy, well characterized by Λ≈10−52​m−2\Lambda\approx 10^{-52}\,\text{m}^{-2}. Despite its remarkable observational success, the model faces growing tensions between early- and late-universe probes, including a statistically significant discrepancy in the determination of the Hubble constant H0H_{0}, hints of dynamical dark energy with equation of state w≠−1w\neq-1, and a mild but persistent discrepancy in the amplitude of matter fluctuations. These anomalies suggest that Λ\LambdaCDM, while an excellent approximation to reality, may be incomplete, and motivate the search for theoretical frameworks that provide a deeper origin for the cosmological constant. However, within standard General Relativity, the cosmological constant must be introduced as a free parameter with no fundamental explanation for its value. This is particularly troubling given that quantum field theory estimates of the vacuum energy density exceed the observed value by some 121 orders of magnitude—a discrepancy known as the cosmological constant problem. The Λ\LambdaCDM model, while phenomenologically successful, simply adopts Λ\Lambda as an empirical input and offers no resolution to this puzzle.

The framework developed in this work has a natural and significant implication for Λ\LambdaCDM cosmology. As demonstrated in Sec. II, the field equations obtained from the compactification of five-dimensional Einstein-Chern-Simons gravity are structurally identical to the Einstein field equations with a cosmological constant. This structural identity has an important consequence: the observational successes of Λ\LambdaCDM cosmology are preserved within this framework, even as its growing tensions motivate the search for a deeper origin of Λ\Lambda. The Friedmann equations governing the expansion of the universe, the growth of perturbations, and the formation of large-scale structure remain unchanged. What changes is solely the interpretation and origin of Λ\Lambda.

In standard Λ\LambdaCDM, the cosmological constant is an unexplained parameter. In the present framework, it emerges geometrically from the compactification of Einstein-Chern-Simons gravity, with its magnitude determined by the compactification radius rcr_{c} and the coupling constants ll and h~\tilde{h} through the relation (16). Thus, the Λ\LambdaCDM model can be embedded within a higher-dimensional theory that provides a geometric origin for dark energy, without modifying any of its observational predictions.

IV.1 Two Dynamical Regimes of the Cosmological Constant

Starting from Eq. (16), we identify two physically distinct regimes depending on the relative magnitude of the dimensionless product ε​V\varepsilon V.

Regime I: weak-field limit (ε​V≪1\varepsilon V\ll 1). In this regime, the denominator of Eq. (16) is dominated by the unity term, and the cosmological constant reduces to

Λ≈κ​V=±3​π​l2​h~8​κ2​rc3.\Lambda\approx\kappa V=\pm\frac{3\pi l^{2}\tilde{h}}{8\kappa^{2}r_{c}^{3}}. (31)

In this limit, Λ\Lambda is controlled by the ratio l2​h~/rc3l^{2}\tilde{h}/r_{c}^{3}, and the sign of the cosmological constant is determined by the sign of l2​h~l^{2}\tilde{h}. In terms of the vacuum energy density, this reads

κ​ρvac=Λ=±3​π​l2​h~8​κ2​rc3.\kappa\rho_{\text{vac}}=\Lambda=\pm\frac{3\pi l^{2}\tilde{h}}{8\kappa^{2}r_{c}^{3}}. (32)

The condition ε​V≪1\varepsilon V\ll 1 translates, using Eqs. (4) and (9), into

π​l2​h~2​rc≪κ,\frac{\pi l^{2}\tilde{h}}{2r_{c}}\ll\kappa, (33)

which is satisfied when the Chern-Simons corrections are parametrically small compared to the gravitational coupling. In this regime, Λ\Lambda is directly proportional to the product l2​h~l^{2}\tilde{h}, so that the sign and magnitude of the cosmological constant are controlled jointly by the Chern-Simons coupling constant and the trace of the compactified field hah^{a}.

Regime II: strong-field limit (ε​V≫1\varepsilon V\gg 1). When the Chern-Simons corrections dominate, the cosmological constant saturates to

Λ≈κε=34​rc2,\Lambda\approx\frac{\kappa}{\varepsilon}=\frac{3}{4r_{c}^{2}}, (34)

a remarkable result in which Λ\Lambda depends only on the compactification radius rcr_{c}, becoming entirely independent of the coupling constants ll and h~\tilde{h}. This saturation behavior follows from an algebraic cancellation: when ε​V≫1\varepsilon V\gg 1, the Chern-Simons sector appears in both the numerator and denominator of Eq. (16), so that Λ\Lambda approaches the limiting value 3/(4​rc2)3/(4r_{c}^{2}) independently of ll and h~\tilde{h}, with the value of Λ\Lambda determined entirely by the compactification radius rcr_{c} in this regime.

It is instructive to verify the consistency of the Kottler solution with the sign structure of Table 1. The Kottler metric (30) is obtained by setting c1=2​M>0c_{1}=2M>0 and c2=−Λ<0c_{2}=-\Lambda<0 in Eq. (28). Substituting c2=−Λc_{2}=-\Lambda into Eq. (29), with ε>0\varepsilon>0 and Λ>0\Lambda>0, one obtains

Thus, a negative cosmological constant is a purely weak-field phenomenon within this framework.

Such a scenario would require a dynamical V=V​(t)V=V(t), possibly arising from a time-dependent scalar field ϕ~​(t)\tilde{\phi}(t), and would naturally connect Branch 2 with the present universe described by Branch 1. We leave the exploration of this dynamical extension, and its implications for a time-varying cosmological constant, as an interesting direction for future work.

The numerical proximity of rcr_{c} and H0−1H_{0}^{-1} suggests that the size of the fifth dimension and the Hubble horizon may be governed by the same underlying physics.

In contrast, within Regime I, the observational constraint Λobs≈10−52​m−2\Lambda_{\text{obs}}\approx 10^{-52}\,\text{m}^{-2} imposes a relation among three free parameters,

In this framework, the entropy of the cosmological horizon acquires a geometric interpretation: it is determined by the ratio of the compactification radius to the Planck length.

IV.3 Geometric Reformulation of the Cosmological Constant Problem

The cosmological constant problem, in its most acute form, asks why the quantum vacuum energy—which by dimensional analysis should be of order EPl4/(c​ℏ)3∼10112​J/m3E_{\rm Pl}^{4}/(c\hbar)^{3}\sim 10^{112}\,\text{J/m}^{3}, corresponding to ΛQFT∼1069​m−2\Lambda_{\rm QFT}\sim 10^{69}\,\text{m}^{-2}—differs from the observed value Λobs≈10−52​m−2\Lambda_{\rm obs}\approx 10^{-52}\,\text{m}^{-2} by 121 orders of magnitude. Within the present framework, this question is recast in geometric terms. From Eq. (34), the observed value Λobs\Lambda_{\rm obs} determines the compactification radius through

rc=34​Λobs≈8×1025​m.r_{c}=\sqrt{\frac{3}{4\Lambda_{\rm obs}}}\approx 8\times 10^{25}\,\text{m}. (44)

This geometric restatement does not resolve the fine-tuning, but reformulates it in terms of a length scale: rather than asking why Λobs/ΛQFT∼10−121\Lambda_{\rm obs}/\Lambda_{\rm QFT}\sim 10^{-121}, one asks why rc∼1026​mr_{c}\sim 10^{26}\,\text{m}. Whether this reformulation offers a more tractable path toward a fundamental explanation remains an open question.

It is instructive to compare this result with the original Kaluza-Klein scenario, where the compactification radius is typically set equal to the Planck length, rc∼lPl≈10−35​mr_{c}\sim l_{\rm Pl}\approx 10^{-35}\,\text{m}, so that Kaluza-Klein excitation modes acquire masses beyond the Planck scale and remain experimentally inaccessible [26]. The present framework inverts this logic: here, rc∼H0−1r_{c}\sim H_{0}^{-1} is cosmological, suggesting that the extra dimension operates at cosmological rather than microscopic scales. Whether this is observationally viable lies beyond the scope of the present work, though the second Randall-Sundrum model [15] provides a proof of concept that an infinite fifth dimension need not violate known tests of gravity.

V Concluding Remarks

In this work, we have studied the four-dimensional compactification of five-dimensional Einstein-Chern-Simons gravity. Our main results can be summarized as follows:

First, we have shown that the field equations obtained from the compactification coincide with the Einstein field equations with a cosmological constant Λ\Lambda that depends on the compactification radius rcr_{c}, the coupling constant ll, and the trace h~\tilde{h} of the compactified field. Crucially, the structure of the field equations is identical to that of standard General Relativity with Λ\Lambda, which means that all known vacuum solutions—including Schwarzschild-de Sitter, Kerr-de Sitter, and FLRW spacetimes—remain valid within this framework. The novelty lies not in the form of the solutions, but in the geometric origin of the cosmological constant.

Second, we have found that the most general static, spherically symmetric solution to these field equations is a Kottler-type (Schwarzschild-de Sitter) black hole, characterized by the line element (30) with the cosmological constant given by Eq. (16).

Third, and most significantly, this framework offers a new perspective on the cosmological constant problem—one of the most pressing challenges in modern physics. Observational evidence from Type Ia supernovae, the cosmic microwave background, and baryon acoustic oscillations indicates that approximately 68% of the energy content of the universe consists of dark energy, well described by Λ≈10−52​m−2\Lambda\approx 10^{-52}\,\text{m}^{-2}. Yet quantum field theory, using the Planck scale as a natural cutoff, predicts a vacuum energy density corresponding to Λ∼1069​m−2\Lambda\sim 10^{69}\,\text{m}^{-2}—some 121 orders of magnitude larger than the observed value. Within the standard Λ\LambdaCDM model, the cosmological constant must be introduced as a fine-tuned free parameter without any fundamental explanation for its magnitude. Moreover, the model faces growing observational tensions—including a statistically significant discrepancy in the determination of the Hubble constant H0H_{0}, hints of dynamical dark energy with equation of state w≠−1w\neq-1, and a mild but persistent discrepancy in the amplitude of matter fluctuations—that suggest Λ\LambdaCDM, while an excellent approximation to reality, may be incomplete.

The mechanism presented here provides a conceptually different approach: Λ\Lambda is not a free parameter but emerges naturally from the compactification of Einstein-Chern-Simons gravity, with its value determined by the relationship Λ∝l2​h~/rc3\Lambda\propto l^{2}\tilde{h}/r_{c}^{3}, as shown in Eq. (16). This scaling implies that, for fixed values of the coupling constants ll and h~\tilde{h}, a large compactification radius naturally produces a small cosmological constant. Conversely, the observed value of Λ\Lambda constrains the combination l2​h~/rc3l^{2}\tilde{h}/r_{c}^{3}. If the coupling constants are of order unity in natural units, this constraint implies a compactification radius rcr_{c} of the order of the Hubble radius, rc∼H0−1∼1026​mr_{c}\sim H_{0}^{-1}\sim 10^{26}\,\text{m}. While the individual values of ll, h~\tilde{h}, and rcr_{c} remain undetermined at this level of analysis, this scaling behavior suggests a potential connection between the size of the extra dimension and the large-scale structure of the observable universe. It is worth noting that the present framework, with VV and rcr_{c} constant, preserves the full predictive structure of Λ\LambdaCDM without resolving its observational tensions directly; addressing these would require a dynamical extension in which rc=rc​(t)r_{c}=r_{c}(t), a natural direction for future work.

Fourth, within the cosmological analysis we identified two distinct dynamical regimes. In the weak-field regime (ε​V≪1\varepsilon V\ll 1), the cosmological constant takes the form Λ∝l2​h~/rc3\Lambda\propto l^{2}\tilde{h}/r_{c}^{3}, whose sign depends on l2​h~l^{2}\tilde{h} and requires fine-tuning to reproduce Λobs\Lambda_{\rm obs}. In the strong-field regime (ε​V≫1\varepsilon V\gg 1), the dependence on ll and h~\tilde{h} drops out through algebraic cancellation and one obtains Λ≈3/(4​rc2)\Lambda\approx 3/(4r_{c}^{2}), independently of the Chern-Simons coupling. This second regime is particularly significant: it naturally yields Λobs\Lambda_{\rm obs} for rc≈8.2×1025​m≈0.78​H0−1r_{c}\approx 8.2\times 10^{25}\,\text{m}\approx 0.78\,H_{0}^{-1}, a value consistent with the observed Hubble radius, without any fine-tuning of ll or h~\tilde{h}.

Fifth, a further consequence of the strong-field regime concerns the thermodynamics of the cosmological horizon of the Kottler solution. Using Λobs=3/(4​rc2)\Lambda_{\rm obs}=3/(4r_{c}^{2}), the Bekenstein-Hawking entropy of the cosmological horizon takes the form Scosm=3​π​kB/(Λobs​lPl2)=4​π​kB​rc2/lPl2S_{\rm cosm}=3\pi k_{B}/(\Lambda_{\rm obs}\,l_{\rm Pl}^{2})=4\pi k_{B}r_{c}^{2}/l_{\rm Pl}^{2}, which yields Scosm∼10122​kBS_{\rm cosm}\sim 10^{122}\,k_{B} for rc∼H0−1r_{c}\sim H_{0}^{-1}, in agreement with the Gibbons-Hawking result [24, 25]. This provides a geometric interpretation of the horizon entropy in terms of the compactification radius.

It is important to emphasize that this mechanism does not solve the cosmological constant problem in the fundamental sense, since one must still explain why rcr_{c} takes this particular value. However, it reframes the problem geometrically: rather than asking why Λ\Lambda is so small, one asks why the compactification radius is so large. This geometric reformulation, consistent with the findings of Randall and Sundrum [15], leads to the intriguing possibility that we could consistently coexist with a large (or even infinite) fifth dimension without violating established gravitational tests.
