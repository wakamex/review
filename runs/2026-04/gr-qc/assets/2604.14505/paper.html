Gravitational Lensing Signatures of Hayward-like Black Holes

Chen-Hung Hsiao\corrauthchsiao@fudan.edu.cn    Limei Yuan    Yidun Wan\corrauthydwan@fudan.edu.cn
Abstract

Context. The Hayward-like black hole Calza:2025mrt, a nonsingular metric that resolves the Cauchy horizon issue, presents a potentially viable alternative to the classical Schwarzschild solution.

Aims. We aim to examine whether the Hayward-like nonsingular black hole produces distinct gravitational lensing signatures in both weak and strong-field regimes that could distinguish it from a Schwarzschild black hole using current or future astronomical facilities.

Methods. We utilize the Gauss-Bonnet theorem method Gibbons:2008rj to analyze weak-field deflection and the Strong Deflection Limit (SDL) method Bozza:2001xd; Bozza:2002zj to compute SDL observables (θ∞,s,rmag\theta_{\infty},s,r_{\mathrm{mag}}) and time delays (Δ​T2,1\Delta T_{2,1}).

Results. In the weak-field limit, the deflection angle includes a small positive correction proportional to m​ℓ2/b3m\ell^{2}/b^{3}, indicating slightly stronger light bending than in Schwarzschild, though the effect remains observationally negligible at large impact parameters. Current galaxy-scale Einstein-ring data, such as from ESO 325-G004, cannot yet constrain the regular-core scale ℓ\ell. In the strong-deflection regime, for Sgr A* and M87*, the asymptotic position θ∞\theta_{\infty} is identical to Schwarzschild’s. Nevertheless, ℓ\ell modifies strong-lensing coefficients a¯,b¯\bar{a},\bar{b}, influencing angular separations ss, relative flux ratio rmagr_{\mathrm{mag}}, and time delays Δ​T2,1\Delta T_{2,1}. Our predicted values for these observables remain consistent with current data, suggesting that future high-precision measurements of strong-field lensing may distinguish Hayward-like from Schwarzschild black holes.

1 Introduction

Gravitational lensing serves as a powerful probe of compact objects, sensitive to the spacetime geometry in both the weak- and strong-field regimes. The Hayward-like black hole, a nonsingular metric that resolves the Cauchy horizon issue, presents a potentially viable alternative to the classical Schwarzschild solution. A key question is whether its distinct geometry yields observable lensing signatures that could distinguish it from a Schwarzschild black hole with current or future astronomical facilities. In this paper, we systematically investigate the gravitational lensing by a Hayward-like black hole across both weak- and strong-field regimes to address this question. We obtain the following key results:

  1. 1.

    In the weak-field region, the deflection angle increases with the regular-core scale ℓ\ell. This represents a positive deviation from the standard Schwarzschild lensing scenario, where ℓ=0\ell=0. The Analysis of the SDL reveals parameters for computing the analytical deflection angles in the strong-field regime.

  2. 2.

    The SDL observables (θ∞,s,rmag\theta_{\infty},s,r_{\mathrm{mag}}) are computed using data corresponding to M87* and Sgr A*. For both black hole candidates, the angular separation ss and the relative flux ratio rmagr_{\mathrm{mag}} are ℓ\ell-dependent. Notably, the asymptotic position θ∞\theta_{\infty} was found to be independent of the ℓ\ell.

  3. 3.

    The calculated shadow size of Hayward-like black hole remains consistent with the EHT measurements for M87* and Sgr A*. The time delay between the first and second relativistic images, Δ​T2,1\Delta T_{2,1}, increases monotonically with ℓ\ell.

Background and motivation Gravitational lensing (GL) is an important tool for studying astrophysical phenomena and testing gravitational theories Wambsganss:1998gg; ORBi-f3f7ce61-7628-4d1a-b937-387c26218fd7; Prat:2025ucy; Bartelmann:2016dvf. When light travels far away from the massive object (lens), the lensing takes place at the weak field region; however, when light propagates close to the gravitational object—where the gravitational field is so prominent that the angular deflection becomes divergent at a particular limit—it is known as the strong deflection limit Bozza_2002; Bozza:2001xd; Bozza:2002zj; Bozza:2003cp; Bozza:2008ev.

Figure 1 depicts gravitational lensing geometry, where the reference (optical) axis is one joining the observer OO and the lens LL. The spacetime under consideration, with the lens causing strong curvature, is asymptotically flat; the observer and the source are situated in the flat spacetime region. A light ray emitted from the source SS at the angular position β\beta is deflected by the lens LL and observed by OO at the angular position θ\theta. The amount of deflection is measured by the deflection angle α\alpha.

Black holes are among the most important gravitational lenses. The deflection angle due to a black hole can be obtained directly from the metric of the black hole. For simplicity, we consider a lens described by a static spherically symmetric metric:

d​s2=−B​(ρ)​d​t2+A​(ρ)​d​ρ2+D​(ρ)​ρ2​(d​θ2+sin2⁡θ​d​φ2).ds^{2}=-B(\rho)dt^{2}+A(\rho)d\rho^{2}+D(\rho)\rho^{2}\left(d\theta^{2}+\sin^{2}\theta d\varphi^{2}\right)\;. (1)

Since the metric of the black hole is static and spherically symmetric, one can consider the null trajectories to be confined to the equatorial plane. Given the metric, the deflection angle can be obtained from the null geodesic equation as Virbhadra:1998dy; Weinberg:1972kfs

α=2​Δ​φ​(ρ0)−π,\alpha=2\Delta\varphi(\rho_{0})-\pi\;, (2)

where the factor of 2 is a consequence of the symmetry of the setup for the observer and the source (the trajectory from the source to the lens is symmetric to that from the lens to the observer), and Δ​φ​(ρ0)\Delta\varphi(\rho_{0}) is the change in azimuthal angle from the closest coordinate to infinity expressed as Virbhadra:1998dy; Weinberg:1972kfs

Δ​φ​(ρ0)=∫ρ0∞(A​(ρ)D​(ρ))1/2(D​(ρ)​B​(ρ0)D​(ρ0)​B​(ρ)​ρ2ρ02−1)1/2​d​ρρ.\Delta\varphi(\rho_{0})=\int^{\infty}_{\rho_{0}}\frac{\left(\frac{A(\rho)}{D(\rho)}\right)^{1/2}}{\left(\frac{D(\rho)B(\rho_{0})}{D(\rho_{0})B(\rho)}\frac{\rho^{2}}{\rho_{0}^{2}}-1\right)^{1/2}}\frac{d\rho}{\rho}\;. (3)

The black hole’s gravitational field deflects a light ray and causes a change in the cross-section of a bundle of rays. The magnification of an image is defined as the ratio of the flux of the image to that of the unlensed source. According to Liouville’s theorem, the surface brightness is preserved as the light is lensed by a black hole. Thus, the magnification of an image is the ratio of the solid angles of the image and the unlensed source:

μ:=d​Ωθd​Ωβ=(sin⁡βsin⁡θ​d​βd​θ)−1.\mu:=\frac{d\Omega_{\theta}}{d\Omega_{\beta}}=\left(\frac{\sin\beta}{\sin\theta}\frac{d\beta}{d\theta}\right)^{-1}\;. (4)

The Ohanian lens equation, which relates the distances between the observer, lens, and source planes, and its variations are the most reliable exact geometric relations used in strong lensing, as discussed by Bozza Bozza:2008ev. This equation reads

DS​tan⁡β=DL​sin⁡θ−DL​S​sin⁡(α−θ)cos⁡(α−θ),D_{S}\tan\beta=\frac{D_{L}\sin\theta-D_{LS}\sin(\alpha-\theta)}{\cos(\alpha-\theta)}\;, (5)

where DLD_{L} is the angular-diameter distance from the observer to the lens, and DL​SD_{LS} is the distance from the lens to the source plane, with DS=DL+DL​SD_{S}=D_{L}+D_{LS}.

Einstein’s general relativity (GR) breaks down at the singularity of a black hole. Historically, non-singular black holes, such as the Bardeen and Hayward black holes Hayward:2005gi; 1968qtr..conf...87B, have been studied. The gravitational lensing effect of non-singular black holes has been studied Eiroa:2010wm; Zhao:2017cwk; Liu:2026yyj; 10.1088/1674-1056/ae29fa. A recent work Calza:2025mrt constructs Hayward-like non-singular black holes that resolve the Cauchy horizon issue in the original Hayward black hole Poisson:1990eh. A Hayward-like black hole has the metric

d​s2=−(1−2​M​(ρ)ρ)​d​t2+(1−2​M​(ρ)ρ)−1​d​ρ2+r2​(ρ)​d​Ω2,ds^{2}=-\left(1-\frac{2M(\rho)}{\rho}\right)dt^{2}+\left(1-\frac{2M(\rho)}{\rho}\right)^{-1}d\rho^{2}+r^{2}(\rho)\,d\Omega^{2}\,, (6)

where

M​(ρ)=m​ρ3ρ3+2​m​ℓ2,r​(ρ)=ρ+2​m​ℓ2ρ2.M(\rho)=\frac{m\,\rho^{3}}{\rho^{3}+2m\ell^{2}},\qquad r(\rho)=\rho+\frac{2m\ell^{2}}{\rho^{2}}\,. (7)

and satisfies the condition that ρ≥ρ0\rho\geq\rho_{0}, with d​r​(ρ)d​ρ|ρ0=0\frac{\mathrm{d}r(\rho)}{\mathrm{d}\rho}|_{\rho_{0}}=0. Here, ℓ\ell is the regular-core (length) scale and has an upper bound ℓ<4​m3​3\ell<\frac{4m}{3\sqrt{3}}, and mm is the ADM mass parameter (in geometric units). Setting ℓ=0\ell=0 reduces (6) to the Schwarzschild metric. More details of a Hayward-like black hole are given in Appendix B.

In what follows, we shall take the Hayward-like black holes as lenses to study their gravitational lensing effects, in order to discern them from the Schwartzschild black holes using the near-future observational data.

2 Weak-field approximation by GBT and Einstein Ring

We first consider the weak-field scenario. Far from the lens – black hole – is regarded as the weak field region, where the lens’ gravitational field is weak. When light is deflected in this region, the deflection angle α\alpha can be expanded as a Taylor series in 1/r1/r or, equivalently, in 1/b1/b, where bb is the impact parameter. A powerful approach to compute the weak-field deflection is the Gauss-Bonnet theorem (GBT) method Gibbons:2008rj. Appendix A briefly reviews the GBT method. Figure 2 depicts the lensing geometry for the GBT method.

The deflection angle can be obtained by integrating the Gaussian curvature KK of region DD in Fig. 2:

where φ\varphi is the azimuthal angle in the metric and ρmin\rho_{\min} is the minima radial coordinate correspond to given impact parameter bb. To include higher-order corrections, one must expand uminu_{\min} in powers of 1/b1/b Ono:2017pie. From the null geodesic equations we find that

umin​(φ)=sin⁡φb+mb2​(1+cos2⁡φ)+4​m​ℓ2b4​(1+cos2⁡φ).u_{\min}(\varphi)=\frac{\sin\varphi}{b}+\frac{m}{b^{2}}\bigl(1+\cos^{2}\varphi\bigr)+\frac{4m\ell^{2}}{b^{4}}\bigl(1+\cos^{2}\varphi\bigr)\,. (11)

Changing the integration variable from ρ\rho to u=1/ρu=1/\rho, Eq. (33) becomes

α=−∫0π𝑑φ​∫0umin​(φ)K​(u)​h​u−2​𝑑u,\alpha=-\int_{0}^{\pi}d\varphi\int_{0}^{u_{\min}(\varphi)}K(u)\sqrt{h}\,u^{-2}\,du\,, (12)

α≃4​mb+15​π4​m2b2+163​m​ℓ2b3+𝒪​(m3,m2​ℓ2).\alpha\simeq\frac{4m}{b}+\frac{15\pi}{4}\frac{m^{2}}{b^{2}}+\frac{16}{3}\frac{m\ell^{2}}{b^{3}}+\mathcal{O}\!\left(m^{3},m^{2}\ell^{2}\right). (13)

The leading term αSchw≃4​m/b\alpha_{\rm Schw}\simeq 4m/b is the standard weak-field Schwarzschild result and depends only on the ADM mass parameter. The next term, 15​π4​m2b2\frac{15\pi}{4}\frac{m^{2}}{b^{2}}, is the usual post-Newtonian correction for a Schwarzschild lens. In contrast, the first imprint of the regular-core scale ℓ\ell appears at order b−3b^{-3}, Δ​αℓ∼m​ℓ2/b3\Delta\alpha_{\ell}\sim m\ell^{2}/b^{3}, and has therefore a small but positive contribution in the weak-field regime. Figures 3 and 4 show this behavior by comparing α​(b)\alpha(b) for the Hayward-like metric with the Schwarzschild prediction. Because the deviation is positive, which is different from that for the standard Hayward black hole, the regular-core scale of the standard Hayward black hole often leads to a decrease (negative deviation) in the deflection angle compared with a Schwarzschild black hole of the same mass. We conclude that the ℓ\ell has slightly amplified the deflection. The positive deviation increases with the regular-core scale ℓ\ell. In addition, the deflection angle increases as the regular-core scale ℓ\ell increases, as illustrated in Fig. 5.

In the weak-field regime and under the small-angle approximation, the lens equation (5) simplifies to

At the leading order, this simplifies to the familiar point-mass expression

while the higher-order terms generate smaller positive corrections to θE\theta_{E}. In particular, since ℓ\ell enters the deflection only at order 1/b31/b^{3}, its contribution to the Einstein ring is even weaker for astrophysical configurations where b=DL​θE≫mb=D_{L}\theta_{E}\gg m. In the Appendix C, we show that the calculated Einstein ring using observational data in ESO 325-G004 is consistent with the observed value of the Einstein ring in ESO 325-G004 Smith:2005pq; Smith:2013ena. The theoretical results of deflection angles and Einstein rings are consistently slightly larger than those in the Schwarzschild case. This implies that while the current model is consistent with available data, future observations with higher angular resolution might be able to constrain ℓ\ell.

3 Strong Field Limit and Observables

While the corrections introduced by the regular-core scale ℓ\ell are minimal in the weak-field regime, its signature is expected to be far more pronounced in the strong-gravity region near the black hole. The lensing in this regime is called strong field lensing. To investigate this, we employ the Strong Deflection Limit (SDL) method, which provides an analytic description of the relativistic images formed by photons orbiting near the photon sphere of the black hole Bozza:2001xd; Bozza:2002zj.

Identifying the observational fingerprints of the Hayward-like black hole requires a detailed analysis of its strong lensing properties. It is a well-established result that the deflection angle diverges logarithmically as the impact parameter approaches the critical value bp​sb_{ps} corresponding to the photon sphere radius ρp​s\rho_{ps} Bozza:2001xd; Bozza:2002zj; Virbhadra:1999nm; Tsukamoto:2016jzh. The radius of the photon sphere of the Hayward-like metric (6) is defined as the largest root of the equation:

(r​(ρ))′r​(ρ)=(1−2​M​(ρ)ρ)′(1−2​M​(ρ)ρ),\frac{\left(r(\rho)\right)^{\prime}}{r(\rho)}=\frac{\left(1-\frac{2M(\rho)}{\rho}\right)^{\prime}}{\left(1-\frac{2M(\rho)}{\rho}\right)}\;, (17)

where the prime denotes differentiation with respect to ρ\rho. For the Hayward-like metric under consideration, equation (17) simplifies to:

ρp​s3+2​m​ℓ2=3​m​ρp​s2.\rho_{ps}^{3}+2m\ell^{2}=3m\rho_{ps}^{2}\;. (18)

Around the photon sphere b→bp​sb\to b_{ps}, the deflection angle can be expanded as:

α​(b)=−a¯​ln⁡(bbp​s−1)+b¯+𝒪​(b−bp​s),\alpha(b)=-\bar{a}\ln\left(\frac{b}{b_{ps}}-1\right)+\bar{b}+\mathcal{O}(b-b_{ps})\;, (19)

where a¯\bar{a} and b¯\bar{b} are the SDL coefficients characterizing the deflection angle.

We first determine the critical impact parameter. By rewriting the metric (6) components in terms of the generalized areal radius r​(ρ)r(\rho), the line element becomes:

d​s2=−(1−2​mr​(ρ))​d​t2+(1−2​mr​(ρ))−1​d​ρ2+r2​(ρ)​d​Ω2.ds^{2}=-\left(1-\frac{2m}{r(\rho)}\right)dt^{2}+\left(1-\frac{2m}{r(\rho)}\right)^{-1}d\rho^{2}+r^{2}(\rho)\,d\Omega^{2}\,. (20)

A remarkable feature of this specific Hayward-like construction is that the condition for the photon sphere, when expressed in terms of the areal radius r​(ρ)r(\rho), mirrors that of the Schwarzschild metric: r​(ρp​s)=3​mr(\rho_{ps})=3m. Consequently, the critical impact parameter bp​sb_{ps}, which determines the angular size of the black hole shadow, reads:

bp​s=r2​(ρp​s)1−2​mr​(ρp​s)=3​3​m.b_{ps}=\sqrt{\frac{r^{2}(\rho_{ps})}{1-\frac{2m}{r(\rho_{ps})}}}=3\sqrt{3}m\;. (21)

It is independent of ℓ\ell and retains its exact Schwarzschild value. The coefficient a¯\bar{a} is then analytically expressed as:

a¯=2​Bp​s​Ap​s(D​ρ2)p​s′′​Bp​s−(Dp​s​ρp​s2)​Bp​s′′=ρp​s3​ρp​s−6​m.\bar{a}=\sqrt{\frac{2B_{ps}A_{ps}}{(D\rho^{2})^{\prime\prime}_{ps}B_{ps}-(D_{ps}\rho^{2}_{ps})B^{\prime\prime}_{ps}}}=\frac{\rho_{ps}}{3\rho_{ps}-6m}\;. (22)

In the limit ℓ→0\ell\to 0 (where ρp​s→3​m\rho_{ps}\to 3m), we immediately recover the Schwarzschild result a¯=1\bar{a}=1. Due to the analytical complexity of the coefficient b¯\bar{b}, we evaluate the coefficient b¯\bar{b} numerically.

The behavior of a¯\bar{a} and b¯\bar{b} is shown in Fig. 7, Fig. 8, and Table 1. We observe that a¯\bar{a} monotonically increases with ℓ\ell, whereas b¯\bar{b} decreases as ℓ\ell increases. Our numerical results for ℓ=0\ell=0 recover the standard Schwarzschild values (a¯=1,b¯≈−0.40023\bar{a}=1,\bar{b}\approx-0.40023).

The resulting strong-field deflection angle is plotted in Fig. 6. As the impact parameter approaches bp​sb_{ps}, α\alpha exceeds 2​π2\pi, signifying that photons complete at least one loop around the black hole before escaping to the observer.

3.1 Lens Equation and Relativistic Images

The strong deflection regime, where the angles β\beta and θ\theta are small, allows for the small-angle approximation in the lens equation Bozza:2001xd; Bozza:2008ev; Virbhadra:1999nm:

β=θ−DL​SDS​Δ​αn,\beta=\theta-\frac{D_{LS}}{D_{S}}\Delta\alpha_{n}\;, (23)

where Δ​αn=α​(θ)−2​n​π\Delta\alpha_{n}=\alpha(\theta)-2n\pi is the effective deflection after subtracting nn full loops. Substituting the expansion in Eq. (19) into the lens equation (23), the position of the nn-th relativistic image is given by Bozza:2002zj:

θn≃θn0+bp​s​en​(β−θn0)​DSa¯​DL​S​DL,\theta_{n}\simeq\theta_{n}^{0}+\frac{b_{ps}e_{n}(\beta-\theta_{n}^{0})D_{S}}{\bar{a}D_{LS}D_{L}}\;, (24)

where en=exp⁡(b¯−2​n​πa¯)e_{n}=\exp\left(\frac{\bar{b}-2n\pi}{\bar{a}}\right), and θn0\theta_{n}^{0} represents the image position for a perfect 2​n​π2n\pi deflection:

θn0=bp​s​(1+en)DL.\theta_{n}^{0}=\frac{b_{ps}(1+e_{n})}{D_{L}}\;. (25)

The magnification of these images, μn\mu_{n}, is determined by the conservation of surface brightness. Using Eq. (24) in the small-angle limit, the magnification for the nn-th image is Bozza:2002zj:

μn=(βθ​d​βd​θ)−1|θn0=bp​s2​en​(1+en)​DSa¯​β​DL​S​DL2.\mu_{n}=\left.\left(\frac{\beta}{\theta}\frac{d\beta}{d\theta}\right)^{-1}\right|_{\theta^{0}_{n}}=\frac{b^{2}_{ps}e_{n}(1+e_{n})D_{S}}{\bar{a}\beta D_{LS}D^{2}_{L}}\;. (26)

The first relativistic image (θ1\theta_{1}, n=1n=1) is the brightest, as magnification decays exponentially with nn (since en≪1e_{n}\ll 1). For perfect alignment (β=0\beta=0), these expressions diverge, indicating the formation of an Einstein ring.

To connect the theoretical formalism with potential astronomical observations, we consider three primary observables defined by Bozza Bozza:2001xd. The first is the asymptotic position of the set of images, θ∞=bp​s/DL\theta_{\infty}=b_{ps}/D_{L}. The second observable is the angular separation, s=θ1−θ∞s=\theta_{1}-\theta_{\infty}, between the first relativistic image and the rest of the packed images. Physically, ss represents the visible ”thickness” between the first relativistic image and the rest of the packed images; if ss is too small, the ring cannot be resolved from the rest of the images by current telescopes. The third observable is the relative flux ratios, rmagr_{\mathrm{mag}}, defined as the ratio of the flux of the first image to the sum of the fluxes of all subsequent images (converted to a logarithmic magnitude scale). This metric quantifies the dominance of the outermost ring in the total luminosity. These are explicitly calculated as Bozza:2002zj:

s≈θ∞​exp⁡(b¯−2​πa¯),rmag=2.5​log10⁡(μ1∑n=2∞μn)≈5​πa¯​ln⁡10.\begin{split}s&\approx\theta_{\infty}\exp{\left(\frac{\bar{b}-2\pi}{\bar{a}}\right)}\;,\\ r_{\mathrm{mag}}&=2.5\log_{10}\left(\frac{\mu_{1}}{\sum^{\infty}_{n=2}\mu_{n}}\right)\approx\frac{5\pi}{\bar{a}\ln{10}}\;.\end{split} (27)

Since relativistic images arise from photons that encircle the black hole different number of times, another crucial and natural observable in the SDL is the time delays among these images. Following the standard treatment of strong-deflection lensing, we restrict our attention to the nearly aligned configuration, where the source angular offset is small (β≈0\beta\approx 0). The time delay between the nn-th and mm-th images located on the same side of the lens is given by Bozza:2003cp; Lu:2016gsf:

Δ​Tn,m=2​π​(n−m)​bp​s+2​Ap​sBp​s​bp​sc^​eb¯2​a¯​(e−m​πa¯−e−n​πa¯),\Delta T_{n,m}=2\pi(n-m)b_{ps}+2\sqrt{\frac{A_{ps}}{B_{ps}}}\sqrt{\frac{b_{ps}}{\hat{c}}}e^{\frac{\bar{b}}{2\bar{a}}}\left(e^{-\frac{m\pi}{\bar{a}}}-e^{-\frac{n\pi}{\bar{a}}}\right), (28)

where Ap​sA_{ps} and Bp​sB_{ps} are the matrix components in (1) evaluated at ρp​s\rho_{ps} and c^\hat{c} is a metric-dependent coefficient defined in Bozza_2002 as:

c^=β^p​s​Bp​s(Dp​s​ρp​s2)3​((D​ρ2)p​s′)22​(1−Bp​s)2,\hat{c}=\hat{\beta}_{ps}\sqrt{\frac{B_{ps}}{(D_{ps}\rho^{2}_{ps})^{3}}}\frac{\left((D\rho^{2})^{\prime}_{ps}\right)^{2}}{2(1-B_{ps})^{2}}, (29)

where ′ represents the derivative with respect to ρ\rho.

3.2 Observables for Sgr A* and M87*

We now estimate these lensing observables for the supermassive black hole candidates Sgr A* and M87*, which currently serve as the premier astrophysical testbeds for strong-field gravity due to the unprecedented horizon-scale resolution achieved by the Event Horizon Telescope (EHT). Assuming these black hole candidates are Hayward-like, we adopt the following standard observational parameters: for M87*, a mass of M=(6.5±0.7)×109​M⊙M=(6.5\pm 0.7)\times 10^{9}M_{\odot} and 16.816.8 Mpc EventHorizonTelescope:2019pgp; EventHorizonTelescope:2019ggyfrom us; and for Sgr A*, a mass of M=4.28−0.1+0.21×106​M⊙M=4.28^{+0.21}_{-0.1}\times 10^{6}M_{\odot} and 8.32−0.14+0.078.32^{+0.07}_{-0.14} kpc from us, derived from stellar dynamics gillessen2017update.

The results of our calculations are presented in Tables 3 and 3. Notably, the asymptotic position of the relativistic images, θ∞\theta_{\infty}, remains constant (19.80​μ​as19.80\,\mu\text{as} for M87* and 26.53​μ​as26.53\,\mu\text{as} for Sgr A*) regardless of ℓ\ell. This implies a strong observational degeneracy: A measurement of θ∞\theta_{\infty} alone cannot distinguish the Hayward-like black hole from a standard Schwarzschild black hole and impose no further constraint on the ℓ\ell. Consequently, breaking this degeneracy heavily relies on the measurement of other observables, which could manifest the effect of ℓ\ell. 4 Conclusion and discussion

In this work, we began by examining gravitational lensing in the weak-field regime for the Hayward-like spacetime. We observed that the deflection angle exhibits a positive deviation, increasing with the parameter ℓ\ell. This behavior provides a distinct contrast to the standard Hayward black hole, which typically yields a negative deviation. To justify our prediction fit within current astrophysical bounds, we applied it to the observed Einstein ring (θE\theta_{E}) of the ESO 325-G004. Assuming the total lens mass consists of a central Hayward-like black hole embedded within the galaxy’s visible and dark matter, our theoretically derived θE\theta_{E} remains strictly consistent with the 1​σ1\sigma observational uncertainty window of 2.85±0.402.85\pm 0.40 arcsec.

Transitioning to the strong-field regime, we utilized the SDL framework. By analytically extracting the coefficient a¯\bar{a} and numerically evaluating b¯\bar{b}, we determined the strong-field deflection angle along with lensing observables: the asymptotic position θ∞\theta_{\infty}, the angular separation ss, and the relative flux ratio rmagr_{\mathrm{mag}}. Our analysis shows that for a fixed impact parameter, the strong-field deflection angle exceeds that of the Schwarzschild metric and grows monotonically as ℓ\ell increases.

To evaluate the prospective observability of these strong-field phenomena, we modeled Sgr A* and M87* as Hayward-like black holes to estimate their theoretical SDL parameters and observables. While current EHT limits cannot yet probe these precise geometric scales, future interferometric facilities aiming for angular resolutions near 100​nas100\,\text{nas} (nanoarcseconds) could potentially differentiate the Hayward-like black hole from the standard Hayward black hole based solely on accurate measurements of the asymptotic position θ∞\theta_{\infty}.

If observational resolutions eventually approach ∼10​nas\sim 10\,\text{nas} or better, visually separating the first relativistic image from the photon ring may be feasible. It is possible to measure observables ss and rmagr_{\mathrm{mag}}, potentially constraining the ℓ\ell in the Hayward-like black hole and distinguishing it from the classical Schwarzschild black hole. Because regular black holes can potentially be related to exact vacuum solutions of generally covariant gravity theories Zhang:2025ccx, ultra-high-resolution lensing presents a compelling, albeit distant, frontier for testing fundamental gravity. We, however, emphasize that resolving such scales remains well beyond the capacity of contemporary technology. Consequently, alternative phenomenological avenues – such as the enhanced magnifications predicted in the microlensing of regular black holes Boos:2025nzc – may serve as more accessible observational targets in the near future. Investigating the specific microlensing signatures of Hayward-like spacetimes in this context remains a highly relevant direction for upcoming research.

Beyond the standard SDL parameters, we also provided an exhaustive tabulation of the signed magnifications and angular positions for the first and second relativistic images, alongside the differential time delay Δ​T2,1\Delta T_{2,1} between them. Consequently, these features can be broadly categorized into “imaging” observables (which are severely constrained by current angular resolution and dynamic range limitations) and “timing” observables like Δ​T2,1\Delta T_{2,1}. In the geometric-optics approximation, if the background source happens to be a compact, time-variable radio emitter, Δ​T2,1\Delta T_{2,1} might theoretically be detected as a phase lag between two highly demagnified signal echoes.

While this framework lays a phenomenological foundation for future strong-gravity radio-timing studies – perhaps eventually aligning with next-generation arrays like the SKA – we must maintain a grounded perspective. In the Hayward-like black hole presented here, the predicted deviation in Δ​T2,1\Delta T_{2,1} from the Schwarzschild baseline is exceedingly small. Therefore, its role at the current stage is primarily conceptual rather than a readily accessible diagnostic tool. Nonetheless, as both weak- and strong-field observational techniques advance, gravitational lensing remains a potential future opportunity for probing Hayward-like and other geometries and the fundamental nature of spacetime.
