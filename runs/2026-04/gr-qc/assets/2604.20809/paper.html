Geodesic Completeness in General Cosmological Scenarios

William H. Kinney Department of Physics, University at Buffalo, SUNY
Buffalo, NY 14260 USA
Abstract

The well-known Borde-Guth-Vilenkin Theorem shows that inflationary spacetimes are generically geodesically past-incomplete, necessitating the existence of a pre-inflationary boundary of some sort, possibly singular. I discuss the generalization of the BGV theorem to spacetimes beyond inflation, including inhomogeneous and cyclic models. As an example, I show that the cyclic model proposed by Ijjas and Steinhardt is geodesically incomplete.

1 Geodesic Completeness in FRW spacetime

Under what circumstances is a spacetime finite to the past? It is clear that power-law evolution of the scale factor,

a​(t)∝tα,α>0,a\left(t\right)\propto t^{\alpha},\quad\alpha>0, (1)

results in a universe with finite age, since a→0a\to 0 as t→0t\to 0. Such evolution follows from a constant equation of state p=w​ρp=w\rho with w=const.,w>−1w=\text{const.},\ w>-1. The existence of an initial singularity is not so clear in the case of de Sitter evolution, with

a​(t)∝eH​t,H=const.a\left(t\right)\propto e^{Ht},\quad H=\text{const.} (2)

since a→0a\to 0 only when t→−∞t\to-\infty. At first glance, this suggests that inflation originating from a de Sitter phase might be extended infinitely into the past, neatly solving the problem of an initial singularity in cosmology. However, the coordinate time tt corresponds to the time as measured by a comoving observer, and is not invariant under coordinate transformations. We need a way to categorize spacetime as bounded or unbounded in a way that does not depend on the choice of coordinate system. This is the idea of geodesic completeness: a spacetime is geodesically complete if all possible geodesic world lines extend infinitely into both the past and the future as measured by the proper time along the world line,

∫−∞t𝑑s→∞,∫t+∞𝑑s→∞.\int_{-\infty}^{t}{ds}\to\infty,\qquad\int_{t}^{+\infty}{ds}\to\infty. (3)

For exponential expansion, comoving geodesics extend infinitely into both the past and the future, but that is not sufficient to demonstrate geodesic completeness, which requires this to be true for all geodesics.

We consider the case of a general Friedmann-Robertson-Walker (FRW) spacetime. A theorem introduced by Borde, Guth, and Vilenkin (the BGV theorem) [2] states that any spacetime for which the average expansion rate HavH_{\text{av}} is positive is geodesically incomplete, where

Hav≡1Δ​s​∫H​𝑑s,H_{\text{av}}\equiv\frac{1}{\Delta s}\int{Hds}, (4)

∫titfH​𝑑t\displaystyle\int_{t_{i}}^{t_{f}}{Hdt} =∫titf1a​d​ad​t​𝑑t=∫aiafd​ln⁡a=ln⁡(af)−ln⁡(ai).\displaystyle=\int_{t_{i}}^{t_{f}}{\frac{1}{a}\frac{da}{dt}dt}=\int_{a_{i}}^{a_{f}}{d\ln{a}}=\ln{\left(a_{f}\right)}-\ln{\left(a_{i}\right)}. (5)

Care is needed when formulating a precise statement of the theorem. This can be seen from considering the example of a “loitering” universe,

a​(t)=ai​(1+eH​t).a\left(t\right)=a_{i}\left(1+e^{Ht}\right). (15)

This appears to be a counterexample to the BGV theorem, since the Hubble parameter is everywhere finite and positive, yet the space is geodesically complete. Such loitering models can evade the BGV bound precisely because their early-time behavior asymptotically mimics Minkowski space. To see why it is not a counterexample, consider the integral

∫−∞t0H​𝑑t=ln⁡(af)−ln⁡(ai)=ln⁡(1+eH​t0).\int_{-\infty}^{t_{0}}{Hdt}=\ln{\left(a_{f}\right)}-\ln{\left(a_{i}\right)}=\ln{\left(1+e^{Ht_{0}}\right)}. (16)

Thus, geodesic incompleteness of the bounding de Sitter space implies geodesic incompleteness of the target space with expansion history H​(t)H\left(t\right). No such bounding space exists for the loitering cosmology (15), so the theorem does not apply. In a flat background, the scale factor (15) also has H˙>0{\dot{H}}>0, and therefore violates the Null Energy Condition, since H˙>0{\dot{H}}>0 requires p<−ρp<-\rho. This suggests that a rigorous statement of geodesic incompleteness will implicitly include the assumption that the Null Energy Condition is satisfied, p≥−ρp\geq-\rho. We next consider an example of the construction of such a bounding space.

2 The Ijjas-Steinhardt Cyclic Model is Geodesically Incomplete

It has been known since work by Tolman in the 1930s that cyclic cosmology suffers from the problem of entropy growth [13]. The second law of thermodynamics operates identically whether the universe is expanding or contracting, and the growth of entropy with time prevents cyclic behavior. Black hole formation is a particularly difficult problem: black holes formed during one period of expansion and contraction inevitably lead to a strongly inhomogeneous bounce, regardless of the background dynamics. A model proposed by Ijjas and Steinhardt (IS) [5, 6] suggests a solution to the entropy problem in cyclic cosmology. In this model, the Hubble parameter for the expansion rate is periodic in time, but the cosmic scale factor grows exponentially from one oscillation to the next, diluting entropy between cycles.

We consider a cosmology with alternating expanding and contracting phases with period TT, such that the Hubble parameter is exactly periodic,

H​(t+T)=H​(t).H\left(t+T\right)=H\left(t\right). (22)

The scale factor, however, grows exponentially from one cycle to the next, as a mechanism for shedding the entropy produced in each cycle,

a​(t+T)=eN​a​(t),a\left(t+T\right)=e^{N}a\left(t\right), (23)

where NN is a constant chosen to be large enough to ensure a sufficiently smooth boundary condition at the bounce. Calling H+​(t)>0H_{+}\left(t\right)>0 the Hubble parameter during expansion, and H−​(t)<0H_{-}\left(t\right)<0 the corresponding rate of contraction, the condition for entropy dissipation is

∫0τH+​(t)​𝑑t≫−∫τTH−​(t)​𝑑t,\int_{0}^{\tau}{H_{+}\left(t\right)dt}\gg-\int_{\tau}^{T}{H_{-}\left(t\right)dt}, (24)

where τ\tau is the duration of the expanding phase. Despite periodic H​(t)H\left(t\right), a difference in average equation of state between expanding and contracting phases results in growth of the scale factor. A simplified example is an expanding phase dominated by a cosmological constant w+=−1w_{+}=-1, so that H+=const.H_{+}=\mathrm{const.}, and a+​(t)=eH​ta_{+}\left(t\right)=e^{Ht}. The contracting phase is dominated by a scalar field ϕ\phi with negative potential, so that

ρ−=12​ϕ˙2+V​(ϕ)≃0.\rho_{-}=\frac{1}{2}\dot{\phi}^{2}+V\left(\phi\right)\simeq 0. (25)

Approximately vanishing density means that H−≃0H_{-}\simeq 0, with the result that a−​(t)≃const.a_{-}\left(t\right)\simeq\mathrm{const.} This is the well-studied case of “ekpyrotic” contraction (for a review, see Ref. [10].) In this limit,

N+=∫0τH+​(t)​𝑑t≃H+​τ,N_{+}=\int_{0}^{\tau}{H_{+}\left(t\right)dt}\simeq H_{+}\tau, (26)

and

N−=∫τTH−​(t)​𝑑t≃0,N_{-}=\int_{\tau}^{T}{H_{-}\left(t\right)dt}\simeq 0, (27)

which trivially satisfies the general condition (24). In this limit, entropy is diluted during a period of late exponential expansion (inflation), with a subsequent slow contraction phase during which the scale factor remains approximately constant.

We consider a universe with H​(t)H(t) periodic with period TT, which satisfies the condition (24) for entropy dissipation, N++N−>0N_{+}+N_{-}>0. We assume that the bounce is rapid enough that we can take H​(t)H\left(t\right) to be discontinuous across the bounce at the boundary of the interval, with scale factor a​(t)a\left(t\right) continuous. The condition for entropy dissipation (24) means there must exist a mean expansion rate ℋ\mathcal{H} on the interval 0<t<T0<t<T such that

ℋ≡1T​∫0TH​(t′)​𝑑t′>0.\mathcal{H}\equiv\frac{1}{T}\int_{0}^{T}{H\left(t^{\prime}\right)dt^{\prime}}>0. (28)

Requiring that the Null Energy Condition holds everywhere except the bounce is equivalent to the condition that H​(t)H\left(t\right) be constant or decreasing,

d​H​(t)d​t≤0,\frac{dH\left(t\right)}{dt}\leq 0, (29)

for tt in the interval 0<t<T0<t<T. Then

The proper time of a geodesic path on any past interval integrating from t=−tit=-t_{i} to t=0t=0 is given by

Any spacetime periodic in H​(t)H\left(t\right) and satisfying the condition (24)(\ref{eq:EntropyCondition}) must therefore be geodesically incomplete.

Theorem: Consider a geodesic 𝒞\mathcal{C} parameterized by affine parameter α\alpha. If there exists a constant Δ>0\Delta>0 such that

Θav≡1αf−α0​∫α0αfΘ​𝑑α≥Δ∀α0∈(αi,αf),\Theta_{\text{av}}\equiv\frac{1}{\alpha_{f}-\alpha_{0}}\int_{\alpha_{0}}^{\alpha_{f}}{\Theta d\alpha}\geq\Delta\quad\forall\alpha_{0}\in\left(\alpha_{i},\alpha_{f}\right), (62)

then 𝒞\mathcal{C} is past-incomplete.

Proof We use α\alpha as a general affine parameter, which reduces to proper time ss for timelike geodesics and is affine for null geodesics. From Eqs. (54) and (61),

Θav≡1αf−α0​∫α0αfΘ​𝑑α=F​(γf)−F​(γ0)αf−α0≥Δ,\Theta_{\text{av}}\equiv\frac{1}{\alpha_{f}-\alpha_{0}}\int_{\alpha_{0}}^{\alpha_{f}}{\Theta d\alpha}=\frac{F\left(\gamma_{f}\right)-F\left(\gamma_{0}\right)}{\alpha_{f}-\alpha_{0}}\geq\Delta, (63)

where F​(γ)F\left(\gamma\right) is given by

F​(γ)=32​ln⁡(γ+1γ−1),F\left(\gamma\right)=\frac{3}{2}\ln\left(\frac{\gamma+1}{\gamma-1}\right), (64)

if 𝒞\mathcal{C} is timelike, and

F​(γ)=3γ,F\left(\gamma\right)=\frac{3}{\gamma}, (65)

if 𝒞\mathcal{C} is null. Then

Δ≤Θav<F​(γf)αf−α0∀α0∈(αi,αf).\Delta\leq\Theta_{\text{av}}<\frac{F\left(\gamma_{f}\right)}{\alpha_{f}-\alpha_{0}}\quad\forall\alpha_{0}\in\left(\alpha_{i},\alpha_{f}\right). (66)

Then

α0>αf−F​(γf)Δ∀α0∈(αi,αf).\alpha_{0}>\alpha_{f}-\frac{F\left(\gamma_{f}\right)}{\Delta}\quad\forall\alpha_{0}\in\left(\alpha_{i},\alpha_{f}\right). (67)

Therefore αi\alpha_{i} is bounded from below,

αi≥αf−F​(γf)Δ,\alpha_{i}\geq\alpha_{f}-\frac{F\left(\gamma_{f}\right)}{\Delta}, (68)

and the affine length of the geodesic 𝒞\mathcal{C} is finite. Therefore, the spacetime is geodesically incomplete.
