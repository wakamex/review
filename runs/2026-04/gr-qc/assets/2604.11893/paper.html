Relativistic signatures of scalar dark matter in extreme-mass-ratio inspirals

Robrecht Keijzer robrecht.keijzer@kuleuven.be Institute for Theoretical Physics, KU Leuven, Celestijnenlaan 200D, 3001 Leuven, Belgium Leuven Gravity Institute, KU Leuven, Celestijnenlaan 200D, 3001 Leuven, Belgium    Simon Maenaut Center of Gravity, Niels Bohr Institute, Blegdamsvej 17, 2100 Copenhagen, Denmark    Henri Inchauspé Institute for Theoretical Physics, KU Leuven, Celestijnenlaan 200D, 3001 Leuven, Belgium Leuven Gravity Institute, KU Leuven, Celestijnenlaan 200D, 3001 Leuven, Belgium    Thomas Hertog Institute for Theoretical Physics, KU Leuven, Celestijnenlaan 200D, 3001 Leuven, Belgium Leuven Gravity Institute, KU Leuven, Celestijnenlaan 200D, 3001 Leuven, Belgium
Abstract

We study gravitational wave emission by circular extreme-mass-ratio systems in a spherically symmetric scalar environment. Previous studies have focused on the impact of scalar radiation channels, revealing a rich structure of resonances, sharp features and floating orbits. Through the backreaction of the cloud on the metric, corrections to the gravitational sector come in at the same order. We develop the computational methods, and provide a characterization of this new, fully relativistic cloud signature. Remarkably, corrections to the polar sector can dominate all other dissipative corrections. We identify scalar field masses M​μ≲0.12M\mu\lesssim 0.12 as the regime where polar can overtake axial and scalar channels at small separation. For small M​μM\mu, vacuum dephasing is dominated mostly by conservative and polar cloud corrections, with scalar radiation acting as only a minor correction. At large M​μM\mu, both terms terms are shown to be highly non-negligible. Our results therefore motivate including these relativistic signatures in beyond-vacuum EMRI templates.

Introduction. Over the last decade, gravitational wave (GW) astronomy has opened a precision window onto compact object dynamics in the strong gravity regime [1, 2, 9]. In vacuum, General Relativity predicts that astrophysical, stationary black holes are uniquely characterised by their mass and spin [63, 29, 84]. Next-generation GW detectors bring the exciting possibility to challenge this simplified picture with unprecedented accuracy [9, 64, 32]. Intermediate and extreme-mass-ratio inspirals (IMRIs/EMRIs) are especially promising probes [85, 6, 4, 32, 16], uniquely accessible in the milliHertz frequency range of space-based detectors, such as the recently adopted LISA mission [32], or Tianqin [76, 71] and Taiji [54]. Their large mass asymmetry allows the secondary to probe the strong gravity regime in a narrow frequency band for years, making subtle cumulative effects measurable [16, 65, 30]. Environmental imprints from accretion disks [90, 11, 3, 100, 68, 10, 40, 39, 31, 53, 44, 58, 46], a third perturber [97, 19, 96], dark-matter structures [10, 31, 77, 83, 74, 49, 99, 56, 66, 33, 35, 26, 28, 86, 15, 41, 18, 80], and ultralight scalar fields [59, 60, 51, 94, 88, 95, 25, 89, 47, 102, 14, 12, 13, 20, 92, 91, 24, 45, 7, 48, 67, 69, 21, 93] have been studied. Ultralight scalar fields are an especially exciting prospect: they can assemble dense ‘scalar clouds’ through superradiance around spinning black holes [42, 23, 22, 43], are predicted by extensions to the standard model and cosmology [5, 61, 62, 50], and allow a direct measurement of the dark matter field properties through only gravitational interactions [14, 24, 67].

Recent relativistic analyses have mapped scalar radiation for EMRIs embedded in a scalar cloud [24, 45, 48, 70], showing that it may rival GW losses in parts of parameter space. Sharp resonances and floating orbits could be a smoking gun signature of beyond vacuum GR deviations [82, 102, 24]. However, as noted in Ref. [24] but not included as a correction in Ref. [45, 48, 70], corrections through the backreaction of the cloud on the metric come in as a fully relativistic effect at the same order. For spherically symmetric clouds, leading corrections to axial GW fluxes mostly decouple, and reduce to a gravitational redshift effect [24, 27]. This metric coupling is found to give a subdominant, but possibly non-negligible correction to scalar radiation [24]. In this work, we perform the first study on the fully coupled polar sector in a scalar cloud environment. Remarkably, corrections to the polar sector of the GW flux can dominate all other dissipative cloud signatures in the waveform. As a function of the scalar field mass, we identify M​μ≲0.12M\mu\lesssim 0.12 as the regime where the polar term overtakes axial and scalar channels at small separation, while remaining non-negligible at higher M​μM\mu. The new signature is well described by a redshift for M​μ≲0.08M\mu\lesssim 0.08 at small separations. Comparing the dephasing caused by each correction identifies a significantly more involved picture than purely dissipative scalar radiation. Conservative corrections can further dominate flux corrections, both in the high and low M​μM\mu regime. Our results therefore motivate including polar and conservative corrections in beyond-vacuum EMRI templates, with the possibility to significantly alter existing estimates of cloud parameter estimations.

Numerical Procedure. We solve for the scalar background in terms of Confluent Heun functions [52], adapted to include a scalar mass μ\mu. With these solutions, the roots of the quasi bound frequency can be solved in a similar way to Ref. [52]. In the metric Eq. (4), corrections to the geodesic parameters are given in Ref. [24, 27]. Axial perturbations can be solved through a single second order master equation. We use the variation of parameters approach (see Ref. [24]). For the homogeneous solution at infinity, we derive the solution in two parts. One part solves for a constant mass scaling M→γ​MM\to\gamma M, with γ=1+Mc/M\gamma=1+M_{c}/M, through a simple rescaling of the vacuum solution (r→r/γ,σ→γ​σ)(r\to r/\gamma,\sigma\to\gamma\sigma). Discussion. In this work, we have shown that the fully coupled polar sector can give the dominant dissipative cloud correction, a signature that was previously missing in the literature. We provide a characterisation of the flux profile from its two limits: an approximate redshift at close, and a mass shift at large separation. In the spherical cloud parameter space, we identify M​μ≲0.12M\mu\lesssim 0.12 as the regime where polar overtake axial and scalar flux channels, while remaining non-negligible in the higher M​μM\mu regime. On the level of the waveform, we showed that conservative corrections can further dominate the total dephasing. Interestingly, for more compact clouds studied in [24, 45, 48, 70, 67], conservative effects can nearly cancel the scalar flux. A comprehensive parameter estimation analysis will be needed to fully quantify how this could affect the inference. While these methods are applied to a simplified setup (spherical cloud, non-spinning primary, circular orbits), we expect the general conclusions to still hold. Monopole corrections from a dipole cloud have been shown to give a similar metric redshift function [38]. With Newtonian expressions for the dipole profile (Ref. [98, 42]), this redshift still scales quadratically with the field mass, indicating that it could again dominate scalar radiation at small M​μM\mu. If dipole and quadrupole contributions break the simple redshift degeneracy, this could excitingly increase the dipole detectability towards lower scalar masses. In the high M​μM\mu regime, the polar correction should still be understood as a mass shift at large radii. For a spinning primary, Refs. [48, 70] show that spin effects mostly come in at more relativistic, higher M​μM\mu. This would suggest that polar corrections can still dominate in the lower M​μM\mu regime. Finally, there are still other important issues that remain to be answered. An evolution of the environmental system through only radiative fluxes has recently been questioned in Refs. [46, 72], where non-radiative torques will come in as an additional dephasing. Corrections to the cloud eigenfrequency additionally contribute to the non-radiative modes, scalar horizon fluxes get corrections from terms ϕ+​ϕ0​ψpol\phi^{+}\phi_{0}\psi_{\text{pol}} (see Ref.
