Highly eccentric non-spinning binary black hole mergers:
quadrupolar post-merger waveforms

Nishkal Rao nishkal.rao@students.iiserpune.ac.in Department of Physics, Indian Institute of Science Education and Research, Pashan, Pune - 411 008, India    Gregorio Carullo gregorio.carullo@ligo.org School of Physics and Astronomy and Institute for Gravitational Wave Astronomy, University of Birmingham, Edgbaston, Birmingham, B15 2TT, United Kingdom
Abstract

We present numerically-informed closed-form expressions for the dominant (ℓ,m)=(2,2)(\ell,m)=(2,2) waveform harmonic of the post-merger emission from mergers of non-spinning binary black holes with comparable masses on highly eccentric orbits. Using 233 non-spinning eccentric simulations from the RIT catalog, we construct time-dependent complex quasinormal mode amplitudes via a Bayesian procedure. We build multivariate polynomial models, represented as functions of the symmetric mass ratio ν\nu and two dynamics parameters evaluated at merger: the mass-rescaled effective energy E^effmrg\hat{E}_{\mathrm{eff}}^{\mathrm{mrg}} and angular momentum jmrgj_{\mathrm{mrg}}. We further validate the post-merger non-circular waveform model by comparing it against simulations from the SXS catalog. Our models achieve mismatches around ∼10−3\sim 10^{-3}, including for near-extreme eccentricities. The model can be directly combined with effective-one-body and phenomenological inspiral waveforms to produce accurate inspiral-merger-ringdown waveforms, essential for parameter estimation of both astrophysical and fundamental physics properties of the signals’ sources.

I Introduction

Black-hole (BH) coalescences are nowadays routinely detected through laser interferometric techniques, with more than 200 events reported in the fourth Gravitational-wave Transient Catalog (GWTC-4) [7], constructed through the LIGO-Virgo-Kagra (LVK) [1, 15, 20] detector network. With the upgraded sensitivity of gravitational-wave (GW) detectors during the fourth observational run (O4) [7], the rate of detection of binary BH mergers has nearly doubled, enabling new probes on the astrophysics of binary compact objects [5], cosmology [4] and the strong-field dynamics of General Relativity (GR) [8, 9, 10].

BH coalescences are characterized by a long inspiral, during which the BHs orbit gradually shrinks, losing energy through GW emission. This is followed by a rapid plunge, resulting in the merger phase forming a common horizon. Subsequently, during the ringdown [205, 172, 202, 171, 69, 68, 70] phase, the newly formed remnant settles down to a perturbed BH which dynamically approaches equilibrium by mainly emitting a spectrum of damped sinusoidal GW signals. As predicted by linear BH perturbation theory [181, 207, 208, 201, 202, 171, 200], the ringdown is mostly characterized by QNMs [158, 100, 32, 37] with (complex) frequencies completely described by the BH mass MM and angular momentum JJ, due to the no-hair conjecture (for electromagnetically neutral BHs, consistent with LVK data [62, 111]). This simplicity has sparked interest in utilizing BHs not only as astrophysical tracers, but also as testing grounds for GR and the BH paradigm, a program known as Black Hole Spectroscopy [37, 95, 94, 33, 109, 36, 38, 27, 28, 63, 65].

Current analyses carried out by the LVK collaboration mostly use waveform models that describe binary BH coalescences on quasi-circular orbits, the exception being Ref. [3]. This is motivated by the fact that GW radiation is very efficient in circularizing binaries during a long inspiral [163, 164]. This is especially the case in the isolated formation channel [143, 144], where the binary has little interaction with the surrounding environment. Consequently, in this scenario, one expects to have negligible eccentricities shortly before the merger of stellar mass compact binaries, soon after entering the frequency window of ground-based detectors [198]. However, dynamical formation and interactions in dense stellar environments can imprint residual eccentricity even close to merger [186, 209, 72, 25, 187, 183, 210, 82]. Although a priori one expects that such large residual eccentricities close to merger are unlikely, to obtain an unbiased inference of astrophysical and fundamental scenarios, it is necessary to confirm this expectation using observational data. Importantly, several massive events (e.g., GW190521 [13, 14] and GW231123 [2]) present peculiar features, such as unexpectedly misaligned or even near-extremal spin magnitudes, which are challenging to obtain in standard formation scenarios and gave rise to a variety of interpretations [134, 123, 129, 199, 80, 81, 194, 66, 90, 196, 117, 110, 67, 6, 92, 178, 161, 71, 130, 30, 170]. This motivates the exploration of alternative formation models, including those with non-negligible eccentricity at merger. Indeed, several studies pointed to the need to include non-circular corrections to accurately analyze GW190521 [55, 105, 102]. More broadly, precise modeling of eccentric GW waveforms is key to disentangle formation channels [185, 209, 99, 184, 58].

Even before starting to infer population-level binary properties, ignoring eccentricity could lead to a significant reduction in the detection rates of eccentric binaries by current GW detectors [118, 12, 165] and introduce systematic biases in parameter estimation [159, 176, 93, 195], which could manifest as spurious deviations from GR [40, 156, 41, 112, 191], or give false evidence for spin precession in sufficiently short signals [55, 93, 184]. For this reason, full Inspiral-Merger-Ringdown (IMR) waveform models that are routinely used for data analysis by the LVK collaboration have been extended to include signatures of orbital eccentricity in the inspiral [12, 121, 184, 136, 104, 168], and applied to measure orbital eccentricity in detected events [119, 44, 77, 174, 113, 148, 167, 203, 3, 124].

There is a rich history in the development of waveform models for binary mergers, evolving from systems with no intrinsic angular momentum (“non-spinning”), to the inclusion of spin components aligned with the orbital angular momentum, and finally misaligned components giving rise to spin-induced precession [73]. One of the semi-analytical approaches that has been proven to be accurate, flexible, and efficient is the Effective-One-Body (EOB) framework [52, 54, 83, 89, 87, 84, 152, 155, 21]. EOB is a perturbative framework in the symmetric mass ratio ν=m1​m2/(m1+m2)2\nu=m_{1}m_{2}/(m_{1}+m_{2})^{2} of the binary components, describing the inspiral of the coalescing system by translating the problem of two comparable-mass merging objects into the evolution of a test mass orbiting a deformed Kerr metric. This description was only carried out until the merger phase, leaving the post-merger waveform portion to be described through phenomenological ansätze [54], later informed to numerical simulations. Because of the smooth transition from inspiral to plunge [54, 42], EOB has proven to be highly effective in allowing for the integration of Numerical Relativity (NR) calibration [53, 86] onto waveform models. NR-informed strategies proved to be faithful, accurate, and sufficiently fast for analyses of GW waveforms. Thereby, contemporary GW analyses often rely on waveform models derived from a combination of multiple analytical approaches to the coalescing two-body problem in GR, which are later calibrated against NR simulations for the late stages of binary mergers.

Specifically to the ringdown, Ref. [87] provided a phenomenological, NR-informed, accurate representation of the ringdown for non-precessing, equal-mass, and equal-spin quasi-circular binaries. The effectiveness of this type of post-merger ansätze has been extensively verified in Ref. [91], and full waveforms generated by stitching it together with EOB inspiral waveforms including higher harmonics [78, 152], aligned-spin effects [88, 43, 151, 155], orbital precession [97, 96, 101, 169], tidal effects [18, 153, 19]. Due to efficient circularisation, the same family of quasi-circular merger-ringdown templates has also been faithfully used to complement inspiral waveforms that include sufficiently small eccentricity [76, 23, 149, 154, 182, 150].

In view of extending these closed-form post-merger models to arbitrary eccentricities in bound orbits, Ref. [64] constructed a model for the late-time QNM-driven regime. In the context of the EOB formalism, recent efforts in Refs. [98, 22] have also been undertaken in the test-mass regime to characterize and phenomenologically model the GW merger-ringdown modes emitted by a small mass plunging and merging into a Kerr BH on eccentric equatorial orbits, extending the Schwarzschild analysis presented in Ref. [21]. Alternative approaches to model non-circular waveforms of comparable-mass binaries include phenomenological or NR-informed surrogates for the merger-ringdown phase, albeit currently these models cover configurations in which most of the eccentricity has been radiated before the merger, and non-circular corrections are small [56, 135, 137, 175, 121, 108, 166, 157, 122]. In this paper, within the context of modeling comparable-mass astrophysical binaries, we extend the late-time model presented in Ref. [64] for eccentric, non-spinning ringdown waveforms to describe the early post-merger emission.

Eccentricity has been classically defined [163, 164, 147] by generalizing the Newtonian definitions of deviations from circularity, such as the eccentricity and mean anomaly, measuring fractions of the orbital period through an angle. Recent progress [121, 176, 177, 184, 127, 77, 44, 192, 45, 193] is based on redefining eccentricity through quantities constructed directly from the waveforms observed by a far-away observer (e.g. by interpolating the waveform frequency evolution). While these parameterizations are (nearly, see below) gauge-invariant, they are constructed from the waveform frequencies at apastron and periastron. In practice, estimating these frequencies for highly eccentric configurations, often with only a few waveform cycles available from finite-length simulations, is challenging. Further, eccentricity is intrinsically ill-defined at merger [58], preventing the application of this approach to dynamical captures that do not complete several orbits before merging. Thereby, to develop non-circular ringdown models applicable to short-duration or dynamical capture waveforms, we use the dynamics-based parameterization introduced in Ref. [58], valid for arbitrary orbits (including highly eccentric, or dynamically bound). In particular, we provide parametric fits of the waveform using the (dimensionless) effective energy E^effmrg\hat{E}_{\mathrm{eff}}^{\mathrm{mrg}} and angular momentum jmrgj_{\mathrm{mrg}}, which we at times also combine to arrive at the effective impact parameter at merger b^mrg\hat{b}_{\mathrm{mrg}} (see Ref. [58] for details). This approach, coupled to the non-circular ansätz presented in Ref. [21] for extreme mass-ratio inspirals, and here extended to the comparable-mass case, provides all the necessary ingredients to build a non-circular model for arbitrary eccentricities.

The paper is structured as follows. We define the conventions used in the manuscript in Sec. II, and the employed model is detailed in Sec. III. Extraction of the relevant quantities, including a description of the numerical dataset, is presented in Sec. IV. Global fits are constructed in Sec. V. Finally, Sec. VI contains discussion, future work, and conclusions.

II Conventions and numerical dataset

We use geometric units c=G=1c=G=1 throughout the paper. A spin-weighted spherical harmonics Yℓ​m−2​(θ,φ){}_{-2}Y_{\ell m}(\theta,\varphi) decomposition [53, 42, 140, 139] of the outgoing GW radiation is considered

h+−i​h×=∑ℓ=2∞∑m=−ℓℓhℓ​m​(t)​Yℓ​m−2​(θ,φ).h_{+}-{\rm i}h_{\times}=\sum_{\ell=2}^{\infty}\sum_{m=-\ell}^{\ell}h_{\ell m}(t){}_{-2}Y_{\ell m}(\theta,\varphi)\,. (1)

where h+h_{+} (h×h_{\times}) is the plus (cross) polarization of the GW waveform, and we refer to hℓ​mh_{\ell m} as “modes”.

Individual initial Christodoulou masses of the two BHs are denoted as m1,2m_{1,2}, the total initial rest mass M=m1+m2M=m_{1}+m_{2} (set to unity) [47], and the mass ratio q≡m1/m2≥1q\equiv m_{1}/m_{2}\geq 1.

II.1 Quasi-circular simulations

As the algorithm validation dataset, we use GW waveforms simulated using the Spectral Einstein Code (SpEC) developed by the Simulating eXtreme Spacetime (SXS) collaboration [48] interfaced through the sxs package [49]. We consider the dominant mode (ℓ,m)=(2,2)(\ell,m)=(2,2) from the highest resolution waveforms extrapolated with a third-order polynomial to future null infinity.

Non-spinning:

As a baseline for our non-circular model extension of non-spinning progenitors, we use the quasi-circular dataset present in Table II, Ref. [152], pertaining to symmetric mass ratio, 0.08≲ν≲0.250.08\lesssim\nu\lesssim 0.25 (corresponding to 1≤q≤101\leq q\leq 10).

Equal-mass, equal spins:

To validate the RatExp template’s effectiveness against previous quasi-circular results, we use the equal-mass (m1=m2m_{1}=m_{2}) and equal-spin waveforms with the individual spins either both aligned or anti-aligned with the orbital angular momentum, for the set of simulations considered in Ref. [87].

II.2 Eccentric simulations

We consider 233 non-spinning, non-circular bounded simulations in highly eccentric orbits, available in the fourth public release of the RIT catalog of strain waveforms extrapolated to future null infinity [115]. The RIT catalog is used to probe higher orbital eccentricities close to merger compared to the SXS catalog. The convergence of the waveforms has been extensively studied and evaluated for different mass ratios and spins of the binaries [141]. Future work will include the newer release of the SXS catalog [190].

We employ the non-spinning dataset in Table I, Ref. [58], which spans the range 0.18≲ν≲0.250.18\lesssim\nu\lesssim 0.25 (corresponding to 1≤q≤31\leq q\leq 3). The dataset is constructed keeping into account data quality considerations, by checking for balance laws through computations of fluxes [58], limiting the available dataset to binaries with e0≤0.9e_{0}\leq 0.9. When studying the dependence of the template coefficients on the parameter space, for qualitative purposes we will make use of the gauge-dependent initial eccentricity e0e_{0}, but for quantitative purposes we always use the notions of eccentricity given by the dynamical quantities {b^mrg,E^effmrg,jmrg}\{\hat{b}_{\mathrm{mrg}},\,\hat{E}_{\mathrm{eff}}^{\mathrm{mrg}},\,j_{\mathrm{mrg}}\} accessed through the simulation metadata and appropriately evolved to merger via the GW fluxes [58]. These quantities are computed at infinity, and are thus gauge-invariant [85, 116], modulo Bondi-van der Burg-Metzner-Sachs transformations which act non-trivially at infinity [146]. In this work, we won’t consider the latter effect, which is expected to be negligible for quadrupolar waveforms. In terms of this dynamics-based parameterization, we correspondingly analyze the range 1.91≲b^mrg≲3.411.91\lesssim\hat{b}_{\mathrm{mrg}}\lesssim 3.41, 0.87≲E^effmrg≲0.960.87\lesssim\hat{E}_{\mathrm{eff}}^{\mathrm{mrg}}\lesssim 0.96, and 1.85≲jmrg≲3.221.85\lesssim j_{\mathrm{mrg}}\lesssim 3.22. This range sets the region of validity of the constrained global fits developed below.

Due to simulation inaccuracies, the strain does not always converge towards zero at late times [74, 64]. Hence, we pre-process the simulations by subtracting a constant complex value extracted at late times.

III Waveform model

Our model is constructed as a modification of the non-precessing TEOBPM model [106], falling into the class of KerrPostmerger templates, as defined in Chapter 6, Ref. [37]. The template is implemented within the pyRing package [60, 61]. Via phenomenological fits, this template aims to phenomenologically effectively capture transients [34, 128, 21], non-linearities [107, 140, 189, 75, 146, 128, 29, 75, 51, 162, 179, 142, 50, 46, 212] and mass-spin variations [189, 180, 211, 145, 57] in the comparable mass regime, where no first-principles model exists yet.

We start by factoring out the contribution of the stationary fundamental mode from the hℓ​mh_{\ell m} modes, and introduce the QNM-rescaled waveform [87, 152, 155, 21]

h¯ℓ​m​(τ)=ei​(ωℓ​m+​τ+ϕℓ​mref)​hℓ​m​(τ).\bar{h}_{\ell m}(\tau)={\rm e}^{{\rm i}(\omega_{\ell m}^{+}\tau+\phi_{\ell m}^{\rm ref})}h_{\ell m}(\tau)\,. (2)

where ϕℓ​mref\phi_{\ell m}^{\rm ref} is the value of the phase ϕℓ​m+\phi_{\ell m}^{+} at the reference time treft_{\rm ref} (see below), ωℓ​m+\omega^{+}_{\ell m} indicates the prograde fundamental QNM (complex) frequency, fixed by the final Kerr BH mass and spin and read through the qnm package [197], and τ=t−tref\tau=t-t_{\rm ref} a relative time coordinate. Negative-mm modes are constructed through hℓ,−m=(−1)ℓ​hℓ​m∗h_{\ell,-m}=(-1)^{\ell}h^{*}_{\ell m}, as a consequence of the symmetries inherent in the Kerr metric and equatorial symmetry of the initial data [42, 139].

The model only considers co-rotating contributions (see Sec. 5.1.6, Ref. [37]), since counter-rotating contributions are excited only for high anti-aligned intrinsic binary spins, precessing systems and extreme mass ratios [33, 26, 133, 131, 213]. We restrict our model to the dominant quadropolar mode and ignore higher modes for the current study. Their inclusion can be achieved with analogous methods and will be considered in future work. The model also neglects spherical-spheroidal mixing [31, 53, 126, 35, 138] (although this is not a fundamental limitation [169]) and beyond-linear QNM couplings [75, 146, 128, 29, 75, 51, 162, 179, 142, 50, 46, 212].

We use different ansätze for the quasi-circular and eccentric cases, introduced below.

HypTan:

The HypTan template for the amplitude and phase is based on Ref. [87], and given by

In highly eccentric scenarios, the peak of the waveform does not generally provide an accurate estimate of the merger time [58, 64] due to a large burst of GW radiation on the closest approach before merger [58, 153, 103, 24, 125], resulting in non-monotonicity of the amplitude peak against eccentricity. Thereby, in the RatExp template, we define the merger time (tref=tmrgt_{\rm ref}=t_{\rm mrg}) as the inflection point of the gradient of the waveform phase (i.e., waveform frequency) right before the QNM-dominated phase (see Fig. 2). We found that this latter choice provided slightly smoother relationships than setting this time as the last amplitude peak before merger, as in Ref. [64]. Due to the simulation’s finite resolution, before determining the inflection point, we apply a filter to eliminate high-frequency noise. We use the merger-remnant quantities as previously described to constrain the coefficients for the amplitude and phase, and proceed with constructing parametric fits for the free coefficients against notions of eccentricity. The merger time (inflection point) then determines the NR merger frequency (f22mrgf_{22}^{\rm mrg}) amplitude A22mrgA_{22}^{\rm mrg} and its derivative at merger A¨22mrg\ddot{A}_{22}^{\rm mrg} as defined above. These quantities are then used to fix the remaining template coefficients as detailed in Ref. [21].

The improvements observed in the non-circular case, showcased below, can be attributed not only to the new functional form of the RatExp template, but also the inclusion of eccentricity into the merger data to constrain the template coefficients, and to the incorporation of the non-circular parameters into the parameter-space fit.

IV Fitting algorithm workflow and validation

IV.1 Coefficients extraction procedure from single simulations

We perform a Bayesian fit to determine the amplitude and phase coefficients defined in Sec. III and reported in Table 1, treated as the free parameters to be inferred from the numerical data. We employ the Bayesian algorithm implemented in the bayRing package [59, 179], relying on the cpnest nested sampler [204]. We determined the appropriate sampler settings as a balance between computational speed and accuracy of extraction after testing for convergence (by increasing sampler settings). We employ 256256 live points and 512512 maximum Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) steps, an evidence tolerance of 0.10.1, with three independent chains set up at different random initial seeds. For reporting, we selected the run with the largest evidence and quoted the posterior median as the point estimate with the 90%90\% credible interval as the uncertainty. We verified that the three chains produced consistent posterior medians and evidence estimates, ensuring the selected run is representative. The priors were wide enough to capture the variation of the amplitude and phase in their domains of existence, and to ensure accurate recovery, and are also outlined in Table 1. In all cases, the phase ϕ22ref\phi_{22}^{\rm ref} is varied uniformly in 𝒰​[0, 2​π]\mathcal{U}[0,\,2\pi].

Since RIT waveforms have a single resolution and extraction radius, a Gaussian error estimate on the GW complex strain is assumed, thereby setting the likelihood function. The estimate of the noise floor is obtained by extracting the late-time constant amplitude from the simulations, which sets the precision threshold required for the fit. We use a consistent estimate for the errors in the strain obtained from the SXS catalog. Data beyond tend=tpeak+80​Mt_{\rm end}=t_{\rm peak}+80\,M are excised from the fit. Although the choice of the end-time only slightly reduces signal power for lower eccentricities, it eliminates contamination from simulation artifacts at late times across all scenarios, including those with lower data quality [58, 64].

IV.2 Global fit construction

After fitting the amplitude and phase coefficients {c2A,c3A,c2ϕ,c3ϕ,c4ϕ}\{c_{2}^{A},\,c_{3}^{A},\,c_{2}^{\phi},\,c_{3}^{\phi},\,c_{4}^{\phi}\} for each NR simulation (“local fits”), through the Bayesian procedure just described, we proceed to construct fits of the coefficients variation across the binary parameter space. We parameterize the coefficients using the symmetric mass ratio ν\nu and effective spin χ\chi, and combinations of non-circular parameters {b^mrg,E^effmrg,jmrg}\{\hat{b}_{\mathrm{mrg}},\,\hat{E}_{\mathrm{eff}}^{\mathrm{mrg}},\,j_{\mathrm{mrg}}\} [58, 64].

The procedure is similar to what is described in Refs. [58, 64], but now using a simpler polynomial fitting template for the parameters of interest

From the obtained posterior distributions in the local fits, we compute the medians YY and their respective errors σ\sigma defined as the 90%90\% confidence intervals. We infer the posterior distribution of the polynomial coefficients p𝜶p_{\boldsymbol{\alpha}}, describing the amplitude and phase coefficients Y~\tilde{Y} across the parameter space. We use the (log)-likelihood function defined by the residuals, assuming that errors are Gaussianly distributed

It is essential to account for errors by incorporating them into the inferred parameters, so that we not only obtain a point estimate of the quantities of interest but also present the complete information available at a given resolution, including associated uncertainties.

To prevent fits instabilities and overfitting, we (linearly) normalize the parameter space of 𝑸={ν,b^mrg,E^effmrg,jmrg}\boldsymbol{Q}=\{\nu,\,\hat{b}_{\mathrm{mrg}},\,\hat{E}_{\mathrm{eff}}^{\mathrm{mrg}},\,j_{\mathrm{mrg}}\} to [−1, 1][-1,\,1] and use broad priors for the polynomial coefficients, p𝜶∈(−500, 500)p_{\boldsymbol{\alpha}}\in(-500,\,500). The physical bounds (constraints) on the parameters 𝑸\boldsymbol{Q} are imposed by setting a NaN likelihood to the cases excluding the values in Table 1. We use 256×2𝖽−1256\times 2^{\mathsf{d}-1} live points where 𝖽={1, 2, 3}\mathsf{d}=\{1,\,2,\,3\} is the order of the fit, and use 44 parallel processes to check for convergence and combine them in a cumulative posterior distribution by weighting them through their Bayesian evidence, with 50005000 maximum MCMC steps and an evidence tolerance of 0.10.1. We further checked for convergence by increasing the live points and reducing the evidence tolerance, and by assessing the stability of the posteriors and the inferred values.

The obtained coefficients and a python implementation of the fits will publicly available, at the time of journal submission, on the Github repository nc_ringdown  .

In Appendix A, we validate our fitting algorithm by showing that in the quasi-circular case, we improve by an order of magnitude in mismatch upon previous results in the literature that relied on simpler least-squares fits.

V Eccentric Global fit

In this section, we first present the results of fitting the single RIT catalog simulations, and then present a closed-form global fit in terms of the binary parameters for the coefficients extracted above, using the dynamics-based parameterization of Ref. [58].

V.1 Single-simulations results

We start by extracting the coefficients values for each eccentric RIT simulation using the RatExp template Eq. (5) with coefficients {c2A,c3A,c2ϕ,c3ϕ,c4ϕ}\{c_{2}^{A},\,c_{3}^{A},\,c_{2}^{\phi},\,c_{3}^{\phi},\,c_{4}^{\phi}\}, via the Bayesian algorithm outlined in Sec. IV.1.

A more challenging case with higher eccentricity, in which the quasi-circular template yield a poor description, is instead shown in Fig. 3. The figure shows how the biggest improvement comes from the usage of non-circular merger-remnant data.

We repeat this procedure for all RIT simulations passing our data quality selection criteria. The median parameters of the coefficients describing the amplitude and phase, extracted from the Bayesian fits for all simulations, are summarised in Fig. 1. To highlight their variation within the parameter space, we plot them as a function of two non-circular parameters b^mrg\hat{b}_{\mathrm{mrg}}, E^effmrg\hat{E}_{\mathrm{eff}}^{\mathrm{mrg}}, and mass ratio ν\nu. A non-monotonic (as expected for eccentric data), but overall smooth structure across the parameter space is observed. A few outliers (discussed in more detail below) are also observed, suggesting undetected inaccuracies in the simulations that our data-quality filter, based on balance laws, does not capture. The mismatch between the reconstructed waveforms and the RIT counterparts is also presented, ranging between 10−6−10−310^{-6}-10^{-3}, showcasing the accuracy of the chosen template.

Finally, a note on the c2Ac_{2}^{A} results. We noticed strong bi-modalities of the c2Ac_{2}^{A} nested samples symmetrically around zero. Depending on the simulation, the posterior samples mostly fell in one of these peaks, a behavior consistently observed across multiple randomly-seeded chains or when increasing sampler settings. This resulted in a set of c2Ac_{2}^{A} posteriors from different simulations spanning symmetrically both positive and negative values. These symmetric peaks denote a strong degeneracy in the template used between the c2Ac_{2}^{A} and c3Ac_{3}^{A} coefficients, suggesting that the template could be improved by removing one of the two coefficients, and possibly extending the template time-dependence to recover the more complex cases. Since the majority of the posteriors ended up falling in the c2A>0c_{2}^{A}>0 branch, when constructing the global fit, we decided to disregard the simulations falling within the c2A<0c_{2}^{A}<0 branch, ensuring a monotonic fitting behaviour. This still yields an accurate global fit (see below) since the template waveform is only very weakly affected by jumping between the two branches, namely by a sign inversion of c2Ac_{2}^{A} accompanied by a corresponding shift in c3Ac_{3}^{A}.

A correlation was sometimes observed also for c2ϕc_{2}^{\phi} and c3ϕc_{3}^{\phi}, albeit to a lesser extent. This correlation did not instead give rise to bi-modal islands when aggregating posteriors across different simulations, which is why it did not have an impact on our global fit procedure. The bounds on the coefficients in Table 1 have been chosen to prevent further degeneracy issues while also avoiding overly restrictive priors, thereby ensuring both an accurate global fit, and an agnostic exploration of the template parameter space.

V.2 Non-circular parameterization

Once the ringdown template coefficients are obtained from each simulation, we aim to connect them throughout the parameter space, for which we assume the parameterisation of [58, 64] in terms of the binary parameters, constructed as follows. Starting from the ADM energy and angular momentum calculated at the beginning of the simulation {E0ADM,J0ADM}\{E^{\rm ADM}_{0},\,J_{0}^{\rm ADM}\} and taking into account the losses due ot GW radiation, it is possible to compute the values of the mass-rescaled (adimensional) effective energy and angular momentum at merger {E^effmrg,jmrg}\{\hat{E}_{\mathrm{eff}}^{\mathrm{mrg}},\,j_{\mathrm{mrg}}\}. These two parameters, coupled with the binary mass ratio ν\nu, are sufficient to describe the coefficient variations in the nonspinning binary parameter space for generic orbits.

Alternatively, a convenient combination of these variables in the form of an effective impact parameter at merger, b^mrg\hat{b}_{\mathrm{mrg}}, is constructed. The latter parameter was previously considered for bound orbits, both in the test-mass [21] and comparable-mass limit [58, 64]. In the latter case, accurate expressions of the merger properties and of the remnant BH parameters {Mf,af}\{M_{f},\,a_{f}\} were obtained as a function of b^mrg\hat{b}_{\mathrm{mrg}} only. Since in principle these quantities should be described by two parameters (e.g. {E^effmrg,jmrg}\{\hat{E}_{\mathrm{eff}}^{\mathrm{mrg}},\,j_{\mathrm{mrg}}\}), accurate fits in terms of the single variable b^mrg\hat{b}_{\mathrm{mrg}} was taken as indicating a “quasi-universal” behaviour [58, 64]. However, ringdown late-time complex amplitudes were found to require more than a single parameter [64]. Hence, in what follows, we consider various combinations of {ν,b^mrg,E^effmrg,jmrg}\{\nu,\,\hat{b}_{\mathrm{mrg}},\,\hat{E}_{\mathrm{eff}}^{\mathrm{mrg}},\,j_{\mathrm{mrg}}\} to construct the global fit, aiming to find the best combination of parameters allowing for an accurate global fit.

To showcase how these parameters impact the waveform template, in Fig. 4, we display the waveform (both oscillatory components, and amplitude and phase) variation as a function the dynamical impact parameter b^mrg\hat{b}_{\mathrm{mrg}}, when assuming a polynomial global fit (constructed below) of the amplitude and phase coefficients given by Eq. 8, with 𝖽=3\mathsf{d}=3. The time-dependent amplitude (frequency) displays a smooth monotonic increase (decrease) with b^mrg\hat{b}_{\mathrm{mrg}}.

V.3 Global fits results

We generate parametric fits for the amplitude and phase coefficients {c2A,c3A,c2ϕ,c3ϕ,c4ϕ}\{c_{2}^{A},\,c_{3}^{A},\,c_{2}^{\phi},\,c_{3}^{\phi},\,c_{4}^{\phi}\} extracted from single simulations, via the Bayesian algorithm outlined in Sec. IV.1. We parameterize their dependence via symmetric mass ratio and a combination of non-circular parameters, specifically combinations of one, two, or three of {ν,e0,b^mrg,E^effmrg,jmrg}\{\nu,\,e_{0},\,\hat{b}_{\mathrm{mrg}},\,\hat{E}_{\mathrm{eff}}^{\mathrm{mrg}},\,j_{\mathrm{mrg}}\}, namely m={1,2,3}m=\{1,2,3\} in Eq. 8. For each parameter combination, we consider linear, quadratic and cubic fits, namely 𝖽={1,2,3}\mathsf{d}=\{1,2,3\} in Eq. 8. Given the result of each of these global fits, we generate the corresponding ringdown waveform templates, now only as a function of {ν,e0,b^mrg,E^effmrg,jmrg}\{\nu,\,e_{0},\,\hat{b}_{\mathrm{mrg}},\,\hat{E}_{\mathrm{eff}}^{\mathrm{mrg}},\,j_{\mathrm{mrg}}\}. The effectiveness of the template is verified by computing mismatches between the global-fit waveform template and the NR data, fixing all parameters except for a free phase. The results of this procedure are displayed in Fig. 5, which constitutes the main result of this work.

In all cases, the vast majority of our template mismatches lies within [10−4,10−2][10^{-4},10^{-2}], an order of magnitude improvement with respect to existing quasi-circular fits. As expected, the mismatches monotonically improve when increasing the number of parameters through which the fits are constructed, and generally decrease when increasing the polynomial order. The third-order global fit for the amplitude and phase coefficients as functions of the symmetric mass ratio and two eccentric parameters, {ν,E^effmrg,jmrg}\{\nu,\hat{E}_{\mathrm{eff}}^{\mathrm{mrg}},\,j_{\mathrm{mrg}}\} with 𝖽=3\mathsf{d}=3, is found to yield the best representation of the numerical data (closely followed by the replacement of jmrgj_{\mathrm{mrg}} with b^mrg\hat{b}_{\mathrm{mrg}}, in agreement with expectations from previous work [64]). For this best-performing three parameter combination, no mismatches above 10−110^{-1} are present, with only a few simulations above 10−210^{-2}. Overall, mismatches increase by ∼3\sim 3 orders of magnitudes from single-simulations fits when imposing a smooth dependence on the binary parameter space.

Fig. 6 displays the cumulative distribution function of the mismatch results, which shows that, when considering only single-parameters fits, the best (worst) mismatches are obtained when fitting against E^effmrg\hat{E}_{\mathrm{eff}}^{\mathrm{mrg}} (jmrgj_{\mathrm{mrg}} or ν\nu). The ν\nu-only fits represent the case when the non-circular dependence on the binary parameter space is ignored in the coefficients fits (albeit not in the merger data), which unsurprisingly scores among the lowest-performing parameterisations. Linear fits serve as a computationally inexpensive and sufficiently accurate method to capture the waveform variations, but accuracy tends to improve with increasing polynomial order. In some cases (e.g. for E^effmrg\hat{E}_{\mathrm{eff}}^{\mathrm{mrg}}), the mismatches do not decrease monotonically as a function of the maximum polynomial order 𝖽\mathsf{d}. This is due to the imperfect representation of NR data via our templates, not by the coefficients’ fit convergence, which has been checked with higher sampler settings. We verified this statement by checking that the χ~2\tilde{\chi}^{2} statistic (maximum likelihood agreement) of the coefficients fits themselves decreases monotonically for increasing 𝖽\mathsf{d}; the same check is also repeated for all parameters combinations discussed below. There exists a narrow parameter space for moderate E^effmrg\hat{E}_{\mathrm{eff}}^{\mathrm{mrg}}, higher symmetric mass ratios, and higher b^mrg\hat{b}_{\mathrm{mrg}} that is not well-captured in the global fits, resulting in mismatches greater than 10−210^{-2}. This could be due to the incompleteness of the single-parameter model against the interplay of the mass ratio and the eccentric parameters. In Fig. 7, we show the cubic fit of the dominant amplitude parameter (c2Ac_{2}^{A}) using E^effmrg\hat{E}_{\mathrm{eff}}^{\mathrm{mrg}}, to understand the variation of one of the amplitude coefficients and the resultant fit employed, with the corresponding mismatches. Despite correctly capturing the overall global trend, the datapoints’ bifurcation and structure in the residual indicate the (expected) need to extend the dimensionality of the fit, including the mass ratio and full non-circular dependence. In the same figure we also report the performance of this fit against a complete representation of the parameter space as a function of {ν,E^effmrg,b^mrg}\{\nu,\hat{E}_{\mathrm{eff}}^{\mathrm{mrg}},\,\hat{b}_{\mathrm{mrg}}\}, showcasing that the worst mismatches (∼10−2\sim 10^{-2}) are obtained in the equal-mass and largest impact parameter case, without a discernible E^effmrg\hat{E}_{\mathrm{eff}}^{\mathrm{mrg}} dependence.

In double-parameter fits, we always include the mass-ratio dependence, augmenting it with a single non-circular parameter. These combinations correspond to the quasi-universal parameterization explored in Ref. [58, 64]. Mismatches markedly improve compared to the single-parameter case, and again the best (worst) results are obtained when fitting against E^effmrg\hat{E}_{\mathrm{eff}}^{\mathrm{mrg}} (jmrgj_{\mathrm{mrg}}), see Fig. 6. The two-dimensional case for c2Ac_{2}^{A}, now in terms of both symmetric mass ratio ν\nu and non-circular parameter E^effmrg\hat{E}_{\mathrm{eff}}^{\mathrm{mrg}}, is displayed in Fig. 8. The fits show improved mismatches, particularly capturing high eccentricity cases. The equal-mass and large impact parameter case still shows large mismatches. Further structure in the residuals indicates the extent of the quasi-universality violation and the necessity of adding another variable to the fit.

Finally, accounting for the complete dimensionality of the parameter space, we use the symmetric mass ratio ν\nu, along with two non-circular parameters combinations, in a constrained three-dimensional global fit, as demonstrated in Figs. 9. As expected, mismatches further decreased compared to the two-dimensional fits, including for the challenging large impact parameter cases. As shown in Fig. 6, the best representation is provided by the combination {ν,E^effmrg,jmrg}\{\nu,\hat{E}_{\mathrm{eff}}^{\mathrm{mrg}},\,j_{\mathrm{mrg}}\} (closely followed by {ν,E^effmrg,b^mrg}\{\nu,\hat{E}_{\mathrm{eff}}^{\mathrm{mrg}},\,\hat{b}_{\mathrm{mrg}}\}) using cubic order, yielding only three cases with mismatches larger than 10−210^{-2}.

VI Conclusions

We presented NR-informed parametric fits for the quadrupolar post-merger waveform emitted by non-spinning BH binaries in highly eccentric orbits, based on the non-circular dynamics parameterisation introduced in [58]. We found that the key ingredient to extend quasi-circular models in this regime is the inclusion of non-circular merger data obtained in Ref. [58]. The rational exponential ansätz, introduced in Ref. [21] for the time-dependent ringdown amplitude, also contributes to significantly improves the agreement with highly-eccentric numerical data with respect to previous hyperbolic tangent forms. We calibrate the template against NR simulations obtained from the non-spinning, non-circular bounded RIT catalog in the range corresponding to symmetric mass ratio across 0.18≲ν≲0.250.18\lesssim\nu\lesssim 0.25, and two dynamics-based non-circular parameters: effective energy 0.87≲E^effmrg≲0.960.87\lesssim\hat{E}_{\mathrm{eff}}^{\mathrm{mrg}}\lesssim 0.96, and impact parameter 1.91≲b^mrg≲3.411.91\lesssim\hat{b}_{\mathrm{mrg}}\lesssim 3.41.

A parameterisation in terms of the {ν,b^mrg,E^effmrg}\{\nu,\,\hat{b}_{\mathrm{mrg}},\,\hat{E}_{\mathrm{eff}}^{\mathrm{mrg}}\} allows for an accurate and smooth coverage of the binary parameter space via third-order polynomial fits. We find that the effective energy at merger is the most informative parameter, allowing us to capture the bulk of the parameter space dependence. Overall, waveform mismatches improve by more than an order of magnitude compared to state-of-the-art quasi-circular template belonging to the same waveform family, with the vast majority within the range [10−4,10−2][10^{-4},10^{-2}], and only three cases with worse values. While reproducing past results, we also found an order of magnitude improvement in the mismatch of quasi-circular non-spinning binaries due to the improved accuracy of our fitting algorithm.

Future directions include extending the fits to higher modes, the spinning case, incorporating test-mass data to ensure a smooth transition to the extreme-mass-ratio limit [98, 22], and modeling the kick dependence identified in Ref. [173].

Appendix B Constrained Least Squares Global Fit

Given the high dimensionality of the parameter space, we validate the Bayesian fits by comparing them with a multivariate least-squares global-constrained fit. The latter targets minimization of the residual functional using the scipy.optimize.minimize module with the Sequential Least Squares Programming (SLSQP) method. An overview of the algorithm is illustrated in the Alg. 1 snippet, which includes pseudocode for the major functions and modules utilized.

We apply a constrained optimization, bounding the amplitude and phase coefficients as prescribed by the eccentric priors in Table 1. The polynomial coefficient bounds are set to p𝜶∈(−500, 500)p_{\boldsymbol{\alpha}}\in(-500,\,500) to ensure fit stability.

Wherever possible, we attempt an (unconstrained) linear pre-fit (via scipy.optimize.lsq_linear) and use that as one of the seeds for the minimization. Algorithm convergence was enforced by repeating the fit using 100100 distinct iterations, verifying that it delivers compatible residuals, and selecting the maximum likelihood value. If optimization fails at the requested order, we retry with decreasing polynomial order and (if successful) pad coefficients to the original size.

To prevent overfitting in our constrained polynomial fit, we apply a small amount of regularization [206] to the residual objective function that we are optimizing. We found this addition particularly important due to the high dimensionality, especially when the coefficients are sparsely distributed.

We compare the χ~2\tilde{\chi}^{2} statistic (proportional to the total residuals) using both the Bayesian results and the least square global fits. We find comparable results between the two methods, with least squares underperforming at higher orders. We further compare the two methods by constructing a probability-probability plot of the waveform template mismatches against NR data, when evaluated using the coefficients obtained using each of the two fitting methods. The result, shown in Fig. 16, indicates that the Bayesian method outperforms the bounded least squares for the three-parameter fits at the third polynomial order.
