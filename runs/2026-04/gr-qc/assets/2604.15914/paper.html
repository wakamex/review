Viable Cosmological Solutions from Hybrid Potentials

Koralia Tzanni tzanni@aegean.gr Department of Environment, University of the Aegean, University Hill, Mytilene 81100, Greece    John Miritzis imyr@aegean.gr Department of Marine Sciences, University of the Aegean, University Hill, Mytilene 81100, Greece
Abstract

We study flat Friedmann-Lemaître-Robertson-Walker (FLRW) models with a perfect fluid matter source and a scalar field minimally coupled to matter with power-law-exponential “hybrid”potential. Using expansion-normalised variables, we formulate the field equations as a constrained three-dimensional dynamical system and determine its equilibrium structure. We show that viable cosmological histories, consisting of a transient matter or radiation era followed by late-time accelerated expansion, arise in restricted regions of parameter space. A central result is that the physically relevant trajectories are confined to an invariant plane, which contains both the transient matter point ℬ\mathcal{B} and the accelerated point 𝒞\mathcal{C}. We further show, by centre-manifold analysis, that the accelerated point 𝒞\mathcal{C} is not a global attractor: it attracts trajectories with ϕ>0\phi>0 and repels those with ϕ<0\phi<0. For dust, a standard matter era requires vanishing coupling of the scalar field to matter, while for radiation the interaction term vanishes identically. Finally, we discuss the issue that the qualitative cosmological dynamics may be independent of the precise functional form of the scalar-field potential.

1 Introduction

The expansion history of the Universe is marked by two distinct phases of accelerated expansion: an early inflationary epoch [7, 14] and the present late-time acceleration [17, 39]. Scalar fields provide a unified framework for modeling both eras [24, 22]. While the observed acceleration typically requires scalar fields with non-negative potentials to effectively act as a cosmological term, potentials that attain negative values cannot be excluded from realistic cosmological scenarios–see, e.g., [16, 35, 20, 19] for discussion and motivation.

In this work, we focus on a class of models containing a scalar-field coupled to ordinary matter with a constant coupling parameter and a potential of the form

V​(ϕ)=V0​ϕn​e−k​ϕ,V(\phi)=V_{0}\,\phi^{n}e^{-k\phi}, (1.1)

where V0>0V_{0}>0 and k>0k>0 and n∈ℕn\in\mathbb{N}. The parity of nn qualitatively affects the behaviour of the potential. For even nn the potential remains non-negative, whereas for odd values of nn it can cross zero and become negative, as illustrated in Fig. 1 for n≥2n\geq 2. The case n=1n=1 is simpler, since the potential does not exhibit the inflection–type behaviour at ϕ=0\phi=0 that occurs for odd values n>2n>2.

Power–law, and in particular inverse power–law, scalar-field potentials of the form V​(ϕ)∝ϕnV(\phi)\propto\phi^{n} have been widely studied in quintessence cosmology; see, for example, [29, 25, 32, 38, 6, 36]. Exponential potentials, on the other hand, arise naturally in cosmological model building and have been extensively discussed in the contexts of both early-universe inflation and late-time acceleration; see for example the review papers [11, 34]. Their qualitative phase-space structure was systematically analysed within the dynamical-systems framework in [10].

Hybrid exponential–power–law potentials of the form (1.1) arise in effective scalar-field cosmology and can also emerge in scalar–tensor or modified-gravity theories after suitable conformal transformations; see, for example, [28, 11, 34, 10, 18, 15]. For recent dynamical-systems studies of the cosmological background evolution characterized by different scalar field models see for example, [33, 31, 2, 5]. For a study of quintessence models with potentials given by the product of polynomial and exponential terms that exhibit realistic accelerating solutions, see [1]. The same potential was used to describe the dilaton field in holographic models [23]. Such behaviour is also consistent with the interpretation of cosmic acceleration as a bifurcation phenomenon in the underlying dynamical system [13]. More broadly, these ideas fit naturally within the mathematical-cosmology framework surveyed in [12], where attractors, singularities, and stability methods are discussed in a unified perspective.

The main contribution of the present work is threefold. First, we analyse a coupled scalar-field cosmology with hybrid power-law-exponential potential in a dynamical-systems framework that accommodates both non-negative and sign-changing behaviour of the potential. Second, we show that the viable cosmological trajectories live on an invariant plane, which contains the physically relevant matter and accelerated critical points. We note here that, throughout this work, by a viable cosmological history we mean a trajectory that passes near a transient matter- or radiation-dominated saddle with the appropriate behaviour of the scale factor and subsequently approaches a late-time accelerated state. Third, we demonstrate, using centre-manifold reduction, that the accelerated point 𝒞\mathcal{C} is only conditionally stable: it attracts trajectories with ϕ>0\phi>0, but repels those with ϕ<0\phi<0. This provides a geometrically transparent description of when the model yields a realistic cosmological history.

The plan of the paper is as follows. In Section 2 we formulate the field equations for flat FLRW models and rewrite them as a constrained three-dimensional dynamical system using expansion-normalised variables. In Section 3 we classify the equilibrium points and determine the conditions under which a transient matter-dominated or radiation-dominated epoch is followed by accelerated expansion. We summarise the main results and raise the issue that the precise functional form of the potential has little effect on the qualitative cosmological dynamics. Appendix A contains the centre-manifold analysis near the accelerated critical point.

2 Scalar field coupled to matter

We assume that ordinary matter is described by a perfect fluid with equation of state

Here a​(t)a\left(t\right) is the scale factor, H=a˙/aH=\dot{a}/a is the Hubble function, and an overdot denotes differentiation with respect to cosmic time tt. We adopt the metric and curvature conventions of [37]. Interaction terms of the form −ρ​ϕ˙-\rho\dot{\phi} as in (2.4) were studied in [8] for exponential potentials, see also [9]. Although there is an energy exchange between the fluid and the scalar field, it is easy to see that the set, ρ>0,\rho>0, is invariant under the flow of Eqs. (2.2)–(2.4). This trivial physical requirement is not satisfied if one assumes arbitrary interaction terms, cf. [27].

For even values of nn, the potential satisfies V​(ϕ)≥0V(\phi)\geq 0. Assuming that initially H​(t0)>0H(t_{0})>0, the Raychaudhuri equation (2.2) implies that HH is a strictly decreasing function of tt. Since V​(ϕ)≥0V(\phi)\geq 0, the Friedmann equation (2.1) prevents HH from changing sign, and the Universe remains ever expanding. Standard arguments (see Proposition 4 in [26]) show that the matter density and the scalar kinetic energy both decay asymptotically and the scalar field rolls monotonically toward the asymptotic regime ϕ​(t)→+∞\phi(t)\to+\infty as t→+∞t\to+\infty. For odd values of nn, the potential possesses a global maximum at some ϕm>0\phi_{m}>0 and is unbounded from below as ϕ→−∞\phi\to-\infty. In this case, for initially expanding models with ϕ​(t0)<ϕm\phi(t_{0})<\phi_{m} and insufficient initial velocity, the scalar field fails to cross the maximum and the evolution is driven toward the negative region of the potential. Under these conditions, the Universe collapses in a finite-time singularity. We refer the reader to [20, 19] for a complete analysis.

Finite equilibria of the system (2.1)–(2.4) correspond to stationary points of the potential, satisfying V′​(ϕ)=0V^{\prime}(\phi)=0. For the hybrid potential V​(ϕ)=V0​ϕn​e−k​ϕV(\phi)=V_{0}\phi^{n}e^{-k\phi}, the condition V′​(ϕ)=0V^{\prime}(\phi)=0 occurs at ϕm=n/k\phi_{m}=n/k, which corresponds to a local maximum of the potential. As expected for an equilibrium at a maximum of the potential, this de Sitter point is unstable. In addition, for n≥2n\geq 2, the potential satisfies V​(0)=0V(0)=0 and V′​(0)=0V^{\prime}(0)=0, giving rise to an equilibrium at ϕ=0,ϕ˙=0,ρ=0,H=0,\phi=0,\dot{\phi}=0,\rho=0,H=0, corresponding to Minkowski spacetime. The definition of the variable zz becomes singular whenever the potential satisfies V​(ϕ)=0V(\phi)=0. For the potential (1.1), this occurs at ϕ=0\phi=0 for all n>0n>0. For this reason, the present analysis is restricted to the region ϕ≠0\phi\neq 0. This should be understood as a limitation of the chosen phase-space variables rather than as a singularity of the underlying cosmological system.

A distinguished feature of the present system is the set of invariant planes z=kz=k. Indeed, from the evolution equation for zz, one sees that z=kz=k is preserved by the flow. This plane corresponds to the asymptotic regime in which the logarithmic slope of the potential approaches the constant value kk, and it will play a central role in what follows. In particular, the physically relevant critical points associated with the matter era and the late-time accelerated phase lie on this plane, so that viable cosmological histories are determined by the dynamics on and near z=kz=k.

3 Viable cosmology

The following analysis is based on the qualitative description of the evolution via a dynamical system approach. To assess the cosmological relevance of the equilibrium points, we impose the following qualitative requirements. A viable trajectory should pass near a transient matter-dominated or radiation-dominated solution, represented by a saddle point with the appropriate scale-factor behaviour, and then approach a late-time accelerated state. The analysis below shows that, among the admissible equilibria, this scenario is realised only for trajectories connecting the matter point ℬ\mathcal{B} to the accelerated point 𝒞\mathcal{C}. In order for a critical point to adequately describe the matter-dominated epoch, it must satisfy three essential conditions:

  • (i)

    Positive matter density, Ω>0\Omega>0;

  • (ii)

    Correct scale factor evolution, a∝t2/3a\propto t^{2/3}, that is equivalent to weff≈0w_{\mathrm{eff}}\approx 0;

  • (iii)

    Transient nature, i.e. the point must be a saddle in the dynamical systems sense.

Similarly, a viable late-time attractor must fulfill:

  • (iv)

    Accelerated expansion, weff<−1/3w_{\mathrm{eff}}<-1/3;

  • (v)

    Stability, i.e. the point must be stable (sink).

We systematically explore the parameter space (k,γ,Q)(k,\gamma,Q) to identify regions where both conditions are simultaneously satisfied, that is, a transient matter era coexists with an accelerated future attractor.

Remarks on individual points

𝒜±,ℬ\mathcal{A}_{\pm},\mathcal{B}—Zero-potential family y=0y=0.

These points correspond to regimes where the potential energy is negligible compared to kinetic and matter components. They are determined by the exponential tail of the potential, z=kz=k and describe kinetic-dominated or scaling solutions. Points 𝒜±=(±1, 0,k)\mathcal{A}_{\pm}=(\pm 1,\,0,\,k) correspond to purely kinetic cosmological states, lying on the boundary x2+y=1x^{2}+y=1 and requiring z=kz=k so that d​z/d​τ=0dz/d\tau=0. In both cases, Ω=0\Omega=0 and the effective equation of state is weff=1w_{\mathrm{eff}}=1, indicating a stiff fluid dominated by the scalar kinetic term. Hence, these points cannot represent a matter-dominated phase nor an accelerated attractor.

Point ℬ\mathcal{B} corresponds to a scaling solution in which both the scalar field and the matter sector contribute to the total energy density. It enters the physically admissible phase space, Ω≥0\Omega\geq 0, when

Q≤6​2−γ| 4−3​γ|,Q\leq\sqrt{6}\,\frac{2-\gamma}{|\,4-3\gamma\,|}, (3.1)

for γ≠4/3\gamma\neq 4/3 and is always allowed for γ=4/3\gamma=4/3. For typical fluids with 1≤γ<4/31\leq\gamma<4/3, this condition is satisfied for all Q≲1Q\lesssim 1. The condition for acceleration, weff<−1/3w_{\text{eff}}<-1/3, is satisfied provided (4−3​γ)​Q<2​(2−γ)​(2−3​γ)(4-3\gamma)Q<\sqrt{2(2-\gamma)(2-3\gamma)} and γ<2/3\gamma<2/3; thus, point ℬ\mathcal{B} cannot correspond to an accelerated attractor for any physically interesting values of γ\gamma. Therefore point ℬ\mathcal{B} remains as a candidate only for the matter epoch. Matter conditions (i)-(iii) are satisfied for

Point 𝒞=(k/6,1−k2/6,k)\mathcal{C}=\left(k/\sqrt{6},1-k^{2}/6,k\right) exists always for potentials taking negative values and only for k≤6k\leq\sqrt{6} when the potential is non negative. It can only be considered as a late attractor since it does not satisfy the conditions for a matter point. Point 𝒞\mathcal{C} is accelerated and has non-positive eigenvalues whenever

𝒟\mathcal{D}—de Sitter

Pure-potential point 𝒟=(0,1,0)\mathcal{D}=(0,1,0) although represents exponential acceleration with a​(t)∝eta(t)\propto e^{t} is unstable (saddle point) and cannot serve as a late-time attractor. Also, it does not satisfy the matter condition Ω>0\Omega>0 to be considered as a matter point.

Point ℰ\mathcal{E} represents a scaling configuration where the scalar field and matter coexist with fixed relative energy densities. It enters the phase space when

(4−3​γ)​Q≤2​(k2−3​γ)k,\left(4-3\gamma\right)Q\leq\frac{2\left(k^{2}-3\gamma\right)}{k}, (3.4)

Therefore an acceptable trajectory cannot pass near point ℰ\mathcal{E} to point 𝒞\mathcal{C} to represent the matter and accelerated epochs respectively. Point ℰ\mathcal{E} cannot be used for the accelerated epoch either, since it has to satisfy conditions (iv) and (v) in Section 3. These conditions imply

If point ℰ\mathcal{E} is considered as an accelerated late attractor satisfying the above conditions, then the only remaining candidate for the matter epoch is point ℬ\mathcal{B}. However, conditions (3.2) and (3.5) are incompatible for all the physically relevant values of γ≥2/3\gamma\geq 2/3.

In summary, point ℰ\mathcal{E} cannot participate in a viable cosmological history, because the parameter conditions under which it can represent a transient matter-like epoch or a late-time accelerated phase are incompatible with those required for an other relevant point to be involved.

From the above analysis, we conclude that the only viable and physically relevant trajectory is the one passing near the matter point ℬ\mathcal{B} and being attracted to the late accelerated point 𝒞\mathcal{C}, satisfying the corresponding conditions (3.2) and (3.3).

Below we summarise the results for the physically relevant values γ=1\gamma=1 (dust), γ=4/3\gamma=4/3 (radiation) and γ=2/3\gamma=2/3 (ordinary matter marginally satisfying the strong energy condition).

Dust (γ=1\gamma=1).

For dust, γ=1\gamma=1, the relevant critical points are collected in Table 4.

In this case, the requirement of a standard matter era forces the coupling to vanish, Q=0Q=0. This is one of the main physical conclusions of the analysis: in the present model, a nonzero coupling is incompatible with the conventional dust-dominated phase a​(t)∝t2/3a(t)\propto t^{2/3}. On the invariant plane z=kz=k, the matter point ℬ\mathcal{B} is a saddle, while the accelerated point 𝒞\mathcal{C} is conditionally attracting: trajectories with ϕ>0\phi>0 approach 𝒞\mathcal{C}, whereas trajectories with ϕ<0\phi<0 are repelled from it. A viable cosmological history is therefore represented by a trajectory that passes near ℬ\mathcal{B} and then approaches 𝒞\mathcal{C}. By contrast, trajectories on the ϕ<0\phi<0 branch do not settle at the accelerated point and instead evolve toward collapse in finite time when nn is odd.

The phase portrait illustrated in Fig. 2, highlights the distinction between non-negative and sign-changing potentials. For even values of nn, where V​(ϕ)≥0V(\phi)\geq 0, the phase space is bounded and trajectories on the expanding branch are driven toward the accelerated solution 𝒞\mathcal{C}. In contrast, for odd values of nn, where the potential becomes negative for ϕ<0\phi<0, the accelerated point is only conditionally stable: trajectories with ϕ>0\phi>0 approach 𝒞\mathcal{C}, while those with ϕ<0\phi<0 are driven away, leading the Universe to collapse in a finite time.

Radiation era (γ=4/3\gamma=4/3).

For γ=4/3\gamma=4/3, the interaction terms in (2.2) and (2.3) vanish identically, and the system admits a transient radiation-dominated phase with the correct behaviour a​(t)∝t1/2a(t)\propto t^{1/2}. When k<2k<\sqrt{2}, point 𝒞\mathcal{C} yields accelerated expansion. However, this acceleration is again only conditional: on the invariant plane z=kz=k, point 𝒞\mathcal{C} is attracting for trajectories with ϕ>0\phi>0, whereas off the plane it behaves as a saddle and repels the ϕ<0\phi<0 branch. This branch dependence is precisely the one identified by the centre-manifold reduction discussed above: the late-time accelerated state is attracting only on the side corresponding to z<kz<k (equivalently ϕ>0\phi>0). Thus the model admits a viable radiation-to-acceleration history, but not a global late-time attractor.

Case γ=2/3\gamma=2/3.

For γ=2/3\gamma=2/3, corresponding to matter marginally satisfying the strong energy condition, the correct matter-era scaling a​(t)∝t2/3a(t)\propto t^{2/3} fixes the coupling to Q=2/3Q=\sqrt{2/3}. In this case the point ℬ=(1/2,0,k)\mathcal{B}=(1/2,0,k) is a saddle with Ω=3/4\Omega=3/4, and therefore represents a transient matter-dominated phase. For k<2k<\sqrt{2}, point 𝒞\mathcal{C} again gives accelerated expansion and, as in the previous cases, acts as an attractor only for the branch ϕ>0\phi>0. The resulting cosmological history is therefore viable, but only conditionally so.

4 Conclusion

The present analysis shows that the hybrid potential V​(ϕ)=V0​ϕn​e−k​ϕV(\phi)=V_{0}\,\phi^{n}e^{-k\phi} supports viable cosmological histories only under restricted conditions. The central geometric feature are the invariant planes z=kz=k, which contain both the transient matter point ℬ\mathcal{B} and the accelerated point 𝒞\mathcal{C}. In all viable cases, cosmological evolution proceeds along or near these planes from a saddle matter or radiation phase toward late-time acceleration.

A physically significant restriction concerns the coupling QQ. For dust, γ=1\gamma=1, a standard matter era requires Q=0Q=0, so the conventional dust epoch is incompatible with nonzero coupling in this model. For γ=2/3\gamma=2/3, the correct matter-era scaling fixes Q=2/3Q=\sqrt{2/3}, while for radiation, γ=4/3\gamma=4/3, the interaction term vanishes identically. In all three cases, accelerated expansion requires k<2k<\sqrt{2}.

Significance of the qualitative properties of the potential.

At this point we note a striking similarity of the qualitative behaviour of the solutions of our dynamical system (2.7) and the system corresponding to a scalar field with a double exponential potential, V​(ϕ)=V1​e−α​ϕ+V2​e−β​ϕ,V\left(\phi\right)=V_{1}e^{-\alpha\phi}+V_{2}e^{-\beta\phi}, [35]. Firstly, equilibria denoted by ℬ\mathcal{B} in both systems, with analogous coordinates, represent a transient matter dominated epoch with the same scale factor dependence, a​(t)∝t2/3a\left(t\right)\propto t^{2/3} for QQ close to zero. Likewise, equilibria denoted by 𝒞\mathcal{C} in both systems, with analogous coordinates represent a future accelerated epoch with the same scale factor dependence for QQ close to zero. Similarly, equilibria 𝒜±\mathcal{A}_{\pm} and 𝒟\mathcal{D} share the same properties in both systems. Secondly in both systems, trajectories passing near the matter point ℬ,\mathcal{B}, asymptotically approach the future accelerating attractor 𝒞\mathcal{C}.

This comparison supported by the conclusions on general negative potentials in [20, 19], allow us to conjecture that the qualitative cosmological dynamics is largely insensitive to the precise functional form of the scalar-field potential. Instead, it is the global qualitative properties of the potential, such as the existence of a local maximum, its asymptotic behaviour at large field values, and whether it attains negative values, that determine the structure of the phase space and the nature of viable cosmological trajectories. In particular, these features control the presence of unstable de-Sitter solutions, transient matter phases, accelerated attractors at late times, or eventual recollapse. Once the essential qualitative features of the potential are fixed, the resulting cosmic evolution is probably largely constrained, and the conclusions drawn in this work may be extend beyond the specific potential considered here.

Appendix A Centre manifold reduction at the accelerated point

In this appendix we determine the local dynamics near the non–hyperbolic accelerated critical point 𝒞\mathcal{C} by means of centre manifold theory [21]. This analysis is required because the linearisation at 𝒞\mathcal{C} possesses a zero eigenvalue, rendering linear stability arguments inconclusive.
