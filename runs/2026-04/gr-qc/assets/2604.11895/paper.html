Novel ringdown tests of general relativity with black hole greybody factors

Abstract

We present GreyRing, a new model for the post-merger signal in black-hole binary coalescences based on the greybody factor of the remnant. The model accurately reproduces the full frequency-domain ringdown signal of a large set of comparable-mass, aligned-spin numerical relativity waveforms, achieving mismatches of order 𝒪​(10−6){\cal O}(10^{-6}) for the dominant (ℓ,m)=(2,2)(\ell,m)=(2,2) mode, and typically outperforming state-of-the-art time-domain models. Building on this model, we introduce a novel consistency test of strong gravity based on the greybody factor: the remnant mass and spin inferred from GreyRing can be compared with those obtained through standard black hole spectroscopy. This agnostic test relies exclusively on the post-merger signal and does not require the inclusion of overtones or the choice of very early ringdown starting times, combining the advantages of inspiral-merger-ringdown consistency tests and traditional black hole spectroscopy. We apply the test to GW250114 and find that the remnant mass and spin inferred from GreyRing are consistent with those measured from the full signal. Remarkably, the inferred parameters can be measured with a precision comparable to, or slightly better than, that achieved with standard black-hole spectroscopy. Our greybody-factor waveform model allows for new precision tests of strong gravity using the ringdown signal.

Introduction. The final stage of a binary black hole (BH) merger, the so-called ringdown, provides one of the cleanest probes of strong-field gravity. During this phase the newly formed BH relaxes toward equilibrium through damped oscillations, and the gravitational waveform is commonly described as a superposition of quasinormal modes (QNMs) of the remnant spacetime [23]. In general relativity (GR), the remnant is a rotating (Kerr) BH and the entire QNM spectrum is uniquely determined by its mass MM and dimensionless spin χ=J/M2\chi=J/M^{2} [124, 105, 121, 46, 78, 19] (we use geometrical units, G=c=1G=c=1). This property underlies the program of BH spectroscopy, in which multiple QNMs extracted from the data are checked for consistency with the Kerr prediction [58, 20, 68]. Such measurements enable stringent tests of GR [22, 25, 63, 4, 5], of the nature of compact remnants [41, 87, 88, 89], and of near-horizon environments [15, 40].

In practice, however, accurate ringdown measurements remain challenging. While BH perturbation theory predicts an infinite tower of QNMs, only a few are expected to be observable in realistic mergers. Even in the loudest event observed to date, GW250114, only two QNMs have been confidently measured, allowing for a ringdown test of GR at the level of ≈30%\approx 30\% [3, 1]. The starting time of the ringdown is inherently uncertain and the number of modes that should be included in the analysis must be carefully guided by theoretical models. Only certain QNM sets are excited and detectable at any given time post-merger [52], and ringdown tests that include too many free parameters can overfit the data. This has fueled an ongoing debate on the robustness of ringdown measurements (see e.g. [73, 51, 72, 43], and [23] for a review).

Because only a small subset of QNMs can be measured, an independent determination of MM and χ\chi is highly desirable. Current consistency tests estimate MM and χ\chi either from the inspiral-merger signal or from the full waveform [7, 8, 5], and compare these values with those inferred from the ringdown. This approach inherits systematic uncertainties from the modeling of the earlier stages of the signal, which may bias the inferred remnant properties [69, 57] (see [37, 45, 66, 76] for proposed mitigation strategies based on numerical relativity surrogates and post-peak analyses). For instance, unmodeled eccentricity or precession effects could bias the inferred values of MM and χ\chi, potentially mimicking a deviation from GR [94, 69]. These considerations motivate the search for complementary descriptions of the post-merger signal that do not rely exclusively on a QNM decomposition.

In this context, recent attention has focused on the BH greybody factor, which characterizes the scattering of perturbations by the spacetime curvature surrounding the BH. The greybody factor depends only on the underlying geometry, so it is a natural “no-hair” quantity that can be used to infer MM and χ\chi from the observed ringdown waveform. Moreover, greybody factors are stable under small perturbations of the effective potential [112, 97, 109], in contrast with the well-known spectral instability of QNMs [95, 15, 74, 48, 17]. Recent work suggested that the greybody factor modulates the ringdown amplitude in the frequency domain [98, 99] (see also [10] for some earlier work). Although originally investigated within BH perturbation theory for a plunging test particle [98, 99, 112, 110], this description was later shown to capture remarkably well the spectral amplitude of comparable-mass mergers [96, 113].

In this work we present GreyRing, a new model for the post-merger ringdown signal based on the greybody factor of the remnant. Building on recent theoretical insights [111], the model accurately reproduces both the amplitude and the phase of the frequency-domain post-merger signal, extending previous models that were limited to the amplitude [98, 99, 112, 110, 113]. We show that GreyRing reproduces a large representative set of comparable-mass, aligned-spin numerical relativity waveforms with typical mismatches of order 𝒪​(10−6){\cal O}(10^{-6}) for the dominant quadrupole mode, thus enabling new ringdown tests of gravity with current and future detectors.

GreyRing. The gravitational-wave signal in the time domain can be written as

h+−i​h×=1dL​∑ℓ​mhℓ​m​(t)​Yℓ​m−2​(ι,φ),h_{+}-ih_{\times}={1\over d_{L}}\sum_{\ell m}h_{\ell m}(t)\,{}_{-2}Y_{\ell m}(\iota,\varphi)\,, (1)

where h+h_{+} and h×h_{\times} denote the plus and cross polarizations of the gravitational wave, dLd_{L} is the luminosity distance to the source, and Yℓ​m−2{}_{-2}Y_{\ell m} are spin-weighted spherical harmonics with angular multipole numbers (ℓ,m)(\ell,m), evaluated at the polar angle ι\iota (inclination) and azimuthal angle φ\varphi specifying the direction of the observer in the source frame. The frequency-domain counterpart of each multipole is h~ℓ​m​(ω)=∫−∞+∞𝑑t​ei​ω​t​hℓ​m​(t)\tilde{h}_{\ell m}(\omega)=\int_{-\infty}^{+\infty}dt\,e^{i\omega t}h_{\ell m}(t). Recently, Ref. [111] showed that the greybody factor is directly encoded in the component of the Green’s function governing the linear ringdown response of a BH in the frequency domain (see also [10, 98] for earlier work based on a model valid for sources at asymptotic infinity), providing an analytical foundation for previous phenomenological proposals describing the spectral amplitude of the signal [98, 99, 112, 110, 113]. Based on this theoretical insight, we propose to model each multipole as

h~ℓ​m​(ω)=M​Aℓ​m(M​ω)pℓ​m​Rℓ​m​(M​ω,χ)​ei​cℓ​mM​ω​ei​(ϕc+ω​tc).\tilde{h}_{\ell m}(\omega)={MA_{\ell m}\over(M\omega)^{p_{\ell m}}}\,R_{\ell m}(M\omega,\chi)\,e^{i{c_{\ell m}\over M\omega}}e^{i(\phi_{c}+\omega t_{c})}\,. (2)

Here Rℓ​mR_{\ell m} is the complex reflection amplitude, defined in terms of the greybody factor as the ratio between the outgoing and ingoing wave amplitudes in the remnant geometry (see Supplemental Material); ϕc\phi_{c} and tct_{c} are the phase and time of coalescence, respectively; and Aℓ​mA_{\ell m}, pℓ​mp_{\ell m}, and cℓ​mc_{\ell m} are source-dependent fitting parameters. In particular, the model for the amplitude,

|h~ℓ​m​(ω)|=M​Aℓ​m(M​ω)pℓ​m​|Rℓ​m​(M​ω,χ)|,|\tilde{h}_{\ell m}(\omega)|=M\,{A_{\ell m}\over(M\omega)^{p_{\ell m}}}\,|R_{\ell m}(M\omega,\chi)|\,, (3)

was previously validated against numerical relativity simulations of binary BH mergers, and (Aℓ​m,pℓ​m)(A_{\ell m},p_{\ell m}) were fitted as functions of the progenitor parameters [113]. Here, for the first time, we complete the waveform template by including a a model for the phase:

arg⁡(h~ℓ​m​(ω))=arg⁡(Rℓ​m​(M​ω,χ))+ω​tc+ϕc+cℓ​mM​ω.\arg\!\left(\tilde{h}_{\ell m}(\omega)\right)=\arg\!\left(R_{\ell m}(M\omega,\chi)\right)+\omega t_{c}+\phi_{c}+{c_{\ell m}\over M\omega}\,. (4)

The term ∼cℓ​m/(M​ω)\sim c_{\ell m}/(M\omega) is the simplest among a family of parametrizations discussed in the Supplemental Material.

Overall, each (ℓ,m)(\ell,\,m) multipole is modeled by 7 real parameters: MM, χ\chi, the time and phase of coalescence (ϕc,tc)(\phi_{c},\,t_{c}), and 3 free parameters (Aℓ​mA_{\ell m}, pℓ​mp_{\ell m}, cℓ​mc_{\ell m}).

In the lower panel we present a similar comparison for the phase. We quantify the model accuracy through the mismatch

ℳ=1−⟨hℓ​mNR|hℓ​mmodel⟩⟨hℓ​mNR|hℓ​mNR⟩​⟨hℓ​mmodel|hℓ​mmodel⟩.\mathcal{M}=1-\frac{\braket{h^{\rm NR}_{\ell m}|h^{\rm model}_{\ell m}}}{\sqrt{\braket{h^{\rm NR}_{\ell m}|h^{\rm NR}_{\ell m}}\braket{h^{\rm model}_{\ell m}|h^{\rm model}_{\ell m}}}}\,. (5)

Here hℓ​mNRh^{\rm NR}_{\ell m} is the numerical relativity waveform, hℓ​mmodelh^{\rm model}_{\ell m} is given by Eq. (2), and the inner product is defined without detector-noise weighting, i.e.,

⟨h1|h2⟩=∫ωiωf𝑑ω​h~1​(ω)​h~2∗​(ω),\braket{h_{1}|h_{2}}=\int_{\omega_{i}}^{\omega_{f}}d\omega\,\tilde{h}_{1}(\omega)\,\tilde{h}_{2}^{*}(\omega)\,, (6)

as our goal is to assess the model’s intrinsic accuracy. We do not extremize over tct_{c} and ϕc\phi_{c}, as these degrees of freedom are already included within the phase model and determined by the fitting procedure. For the case shown in Fig. 1, we find ℳ≃2.0×10−6\mathcal{M}\simeq 2.0\times 10^{-6}.

We test the model against 311311 SXS simulations of comparable-mass, quasicircular, nonprecessing binary BHs with mass ratios in the range [1,4][1,4] [31, 114]. In Fig. 2 we assess its performance for the dominant multipoles (ℓ=m=2, 3, 4\ell=m=2,\,3,\,4) in terms of the mismatch ℳ\mathcal{M}. For (ℓ,m)=(2, 2)(\ell,m)=(2,\,2), GreyRing achieves mismatches in the range ℳ∈[3×10−8,5×10−5]{\cal M}\in[3\times 10^{-8},5\times 10^{-5}], and typically ℳ=𝒪​(10−6){\cal M}={\cal O}(10^{-6}). The mismatch for (ℓ,m)=(3, 3)(\ell,m)=(3,\,3) and (ℓ,m)=(4, 4)(\ell,m)=(4,\,4) is at the level of 𝒪​(10−5){\cal O}(10^{-5}) and 𝒪​(10−3){\cal O}(10^{-3}), respectively. We expect that including spherical-spheroidal mode mixing [21] and nonlinearities [49, 91, 42] will improve the mismatch for higher ℓ\ell’s. The mismatches for the present model are at least one order of magnitude lower than for the amplitude-only model [113]. Remarkably, typical GreyRing model mismatches are comparable to (or better than) state-of-the-art time-domain models calibrated using numerical relativity simulations [52], such as the TEOBPM template [53, 56, 92, 93].

Tests of gravity with GreyRing. The high accuracy of our model enables a novel agnostic test of GR: we can use GreyRing to infer MM and χ\chi from the frequency-domain signal, and compare the resulting posteriors with those obtained with a standard QNM-based ringdown template. This is conceptually similar to inspiral–merger–ringdown consistency tests, which infer the remnant properties from the progenitor binary using fits calibrated to numerical relativity simulations in GR [4]. Unlike those tests, our approach relies exclusively on the post-merger signal, and it does not require connecting the inspiral to the ringdown via numerical relativity modeling. Moreover, a consistency test can be performed by extracting (M,χ)(M,\,\chi) using only the ℓ=m=2\ell=m=2 multipole in GreyRing and only the fundamental ℓ=m=2\ell=m=2 QNM in standard BH spectroscopy, without the need for higher modes [68, 34] or overtones [73, 67, 100, 26, 37, 51, 71, 60, 43, 85, 84, 14]. This is a significant advantage, because (i) for highly symmetric binaries – including GW250114 [2, 1], which has enabled the most stringent ringdown tests to date [5] – higher multipoles are strongly suppressed; and (ii) the inclusion of overtones introduces ambiguities related to the choice of the starting time, the number of modes to include in the template, and the risk of overfitting [75, 51, 14].

As a first step, we have applied this test by performing Bayesian parameter estimation on synthetic data (see the Supplemental Material). We assume nonprecessing binaries, so that ±m\pm m multipoles are related by h~ℓ​m​(ω)=(−1)ℓ​h~ℓ−m∗​(−ω)\tilde{h}_{\ell m}(\omega)=(-1)^{\ell}\tilde{h}^{*}_{\ell\,-m}(-\omega).

We consider the GW250114-like numerical simulation SXS:BBH:3617, and perform injection–recovery tests with two independent models. We first generate injections using the QNM model calibrated to the simulation and perform a standard QNM analysis with pycbc inference [29]. We inject QNMs with physical parameters consistent with GW250114 in the ℓ=m=2\ell=m=2 multipole and recover the posterior distributions of (M,χ)(M,\,\chi) (see Supplemental Material for details). Then, we perform an independent parameter estimation using GreyRing, in which we inject a signal constructed from the same simulation and recover it using only the frequency-domain ℓ=m=2\ell=m=2 multipole. As expected for consistency, the posteriors on (M,χ)(M,\,\chi) recovered in this case are compatible with those from BH spectroscopy. Furthermore, the uncertainties of the two methods are similar.

Application to GW250114. We apply the GreyRing consistency test to real data, focusing on GW250114. For this event (the loudest detected so far) the real and imaginary parts of the ℓ=m=2\ell=m=2 fundamental QNM frequency were measured at the level of 6%6\% and 10%10\%, the first overtone frequency was measured with an accuracy of approximately 30%30\%, and there is some evidence for other modes in the ringdown [2, 1].

Our main results are shown in Fig. 3, where we apply GreyRing to the Fourier transform of the GW250114 time series. The resulting posteriors (blue contours) are compared with the standard LIGO-Virgo-KAGRA ringdown analysis using either the fundamental ℓ=m=2\ell=m=2 mode alone (red) or a model including also the first overtone (green) [2, 1, 81]. The ringdown analysis starts at tstart=13​Mt_{\rm start}=13M or tstart=8​Mt_{\rm start}=8M after the peak of the strain in the two cases, respectively, since including the first overtone better captures the early post-merger phase [73, 67]. The GreyRing posteriors are compatible with (and slightly narrower than) those from the standard ringdown analysis [2, 1], yielding (1+z)​M=66.9−6.4+6.1​M⊙(1+z)M=66.9_{-6.4}^{+6.1}\,M_{\odot} and χ=0.69−0.16+0.10\chi=0.69_{-0.16}^{+0.10} (90% credible intervals) for the final detector-frame mass and spin, respectively. The full corner plots, an independent validation of the standard ringdown analysis, and the effect of different choices for tstartt_{\rm start} and for the frequency range are discussed in the Supplemental Material. The GreyRing posteriors on MM and χ\chi are remarkably independent of the chosen frequency range, whereas in the standard BH spectroscopy analysis MM and χ\chi are very sensitive to the choice of tstartt_{\rm start}.

Discussion. Our greybody-factor model opens up a new class of tests of gravity in the ringdown regime. Although we have focused here on the dominant ℓ=m=2\ell=m=2 mode, analogous tests can be performed with subdominant harmonics, in parallel with standard QNM-based studies.

Compared to ordinary BH spectroscopy, GreyRing offers several key advantages:

  • (i)

    The greybody factor encodes the full linear response of the remnant in the frequency domain, and it does not require a QNM decomposition. This effectively resums all overtones and captures features of the response that are not included in a QNM expansion [10, 79, 54, 55, 118, 12, 111], such as late-time tails [111].

  • (ii)

    Unlike QNM expansions, the greybody factor is a complex, frequency-dependent function. The consistency tests proposed here probe the entire functional form of Rℓ​m​(M​ω,χ)R_{\ell m}(M\omega,\chi) rather than a discrete set of frequencies and damping times, thereby exploiting more of the signal’s information to reconstruct a “no-hair” property of the remnant.

  • (iii)

    Compared to standard QNM measurements, the greybody factor captures a broad frequency range, including relatively high frequencies. Therefore, it is expected to be more sensitive to short-length-scale modifications in the strong-field regime, such as higher-order curvature corrections to GR [11] or horizon-scale physics [110, 109].

The GreyRing model can be extended to perform additional consistency tests of gravity that are complementary to (and independent of) traditional inspiral-merger-ringdown tests. For example, a less conservative implementation of the agnostic test proposed in this work would rely on numerical relativity simulations to map the progenitor masses and spins to the model parameters (Aℓ​mA_{\ell m}, pℓ​mp_{\ell m}, cℓ​mc_{\ell m}) along the lines of Ref. [113], thus enabling consistency checks with the information extracted from the inspiral signal.

The increasing sensitivity of current detectors, together with the prospects of next-generation ground- and space-based observatories such as the Einstein Telescope [90, 32, 6], Cosmic Explorer [108, 59] and LISA [50], strongly motivates the development of accurate and robust frameworks to parametrize and constrain deviations from the Kerr metric in the ringdown regime. Systematic effects will be dominant at large signal-to-noise, particularly relevant in the context of ringdown, where modeling uncertainties in the starting time and in the number of modes can significantly bias the inference [39, 125]. In this context, approaches that minimize such systematics are especially valuable [69, 9].

GreyRing achieves mismatches of order 𝒪​(10−6){\cal O}(10^{-6}) for the dominant multipole of the radiation, typically outperforming state-of-the-art time-domain models [52]. Using the conservative criterion that two waveforms are indistinguishable for parameter-estimation purposes when ℳ≲(2​SNR2)−1\mathcal{M}\lesssim(2\,{\rm SNR}^{2})^{-1} [61, 83], where SNR is the signal-to-noise ratio, this level of accuracy is already sufficient for ringdown signals with SNR∼100{\rm SNR}\sim 100–10001000, as expected for next-generation detectors [28] and (in optimistic scenarios) for LISA [50, 24, 27].

Several extensions of our work are possible and desirbale. The model should be validated and generalized to precessing and eccentric binaries, possibly by developing fitting formulas for the model parameters that extend the analysis of Ref. [113]. Another important direction is the inclusion of spheroidal–spherical mode mixing [21] and nonlinearities [49, 91, 42], which is relevant for rapidly spinning remnants and in the high-frequency regime. It will be interesting to attach the model to inspiral–merger waveforms in the frequency domain, such as those of the IMRPhenom family [77, 102] or reduced-order frequency-domain implementations of effective-one-body models [106, 30, 64], and to quantify the resulting overall faithfulness. Finally, it is important to extend GreyRing to beyond-GR theories, complementing ordinary BH spectroscopy models beyond GR. Some of this work is currently underway and will be presented elsewhere.

Appendix B Greybody-factor waveform model and fits to numerical simulations

Here we provide further details on the GreyRing model and on the fitting procedure to numerical relativity simulations.

GreyRing model

Recently, Ref. [111] demonstrated that the reflection amplitude Rℓ​m​(M​ω,χ)R_{\ell m}(M\omega,\chi) is explicitly encoded in the component of the Green’s function governing the linear ringdown response of a BH in the frequency domain (previous work in this direction [10, 98] used a decomposition of the Green’s function valid only at asymptotic infinity: see [111] for a detailed discussion). This result provides analytical support to earlier phenomenological models of the spectral amplitude of the signal [98, 99, 112, 110, 113]. In particular, Ref. [113] showed that a model for the spectral amplitude of the form

|h~ℓ​m​(ω)|=M​Aℓ​m(M​ω)pℓ​m​|Rℓ​m​(M​ω,χ)||\tilde{h}_{\ell m}(\omega)|=\frac{MA_{\ell m}}{(M\omega)^{p_{\ell m}}}\,|R_{\ell m}(M\omega,\chi)| (14)

provides an accurate fit to numerical waveforms, but it did not include a model for the phase of the signal.

Motivated by this parametrization, and by the fact that the full signal must be proportional to the complex reflection amplitude Rℓ​m​(M​ω,χ)R_{\ell m}(M\omega,\chi) [111], we consider the following theory-informed model for the full frequency-domain signal:

h~ℓ​m​(ω)=M​Aℓ​m(M​ω)pℓ​m​Rℓ​m​(M​ω,χ)​ei​cℓ​m(M​ω)dℓ​m​ei​(ϕc+ω​tc).\tilde{h}_{\ell m}(\omega)=\frac{MA_{\ell m}}{(M\omega)^{p_{\ell m}}}\,R_{\ell m}(M\omega,\chi)\,e^{i\frac{c_{\ell m}}{(M\omega)^{d_{\ell m}}}}\,e^{i(\phi_{c}+\omega t_{c})}\,. (15)

The phase term cℓ​m/(M​ω)dℓ​mc_{\ell m}/(M\omega)^{d_{\ell m}} plays a role analogous to the amplitude correction Aℓ​m/(M​ω)pℓ​mA_{\ell m}/(M\omega)^{p_{\ell m}}.

We have explored different modeling choices for the phase. We first considered a minimal model in which the phase is entirely determined by arg⁡(Rℓ​m)\arg(R_{\ell m}) (i.e., we set cℓ​m=0c_{\ell m}=0 in Eq. (15)), supplemented by the extrinsic parameters ϕc\phi_{c} and tct_{c}. Remarkably, this simple prescription already provides a good description of the numerical phase, with typical mismatches of order 𝒪​(10−3)\mathcal{O}(10^{-3}) for the ℓ=m=2\ell=m=2 mode. This indicates that the phase of the reflection amplitude captures the dominant physical behavior of the signal. We then extended the model by including subleading corrections of the form ∼cℓ​m/(M​ω)dℓ​m\sim c_{\ell m}/(M\omega)^{d_{\ell m}} and, finally, the simplified expression ∼cℓ​m/(M​ω)\sim c_{\ell m}/(M\omega). Both extensions lead to a significant improvement in the mismatch, reducing it by up to three orders of magnitude.

These models are compared in Fig. 4 against 311311 comparable-mass, quasicircular, nonprecessing binary BH simulations from the Simulating eXtreme Spacetimes (SXS) collaboration catalog [31, 114]. The baseline model already achieves relatively small mismatches, confirming that arg⁡(Rℓ​m)\arg(R_{\ell m}) captures most of the phase structure. The inclusion of the cℓ​m/(M​ω)dℓ​mc_{\ell m}/(M\omega)^{d_{\ell m}} correction leads to a significant improvement, reducing the mismatch distribution by about three orders of magnitude. Allowing the exponent dℓ​md_{\ell m} to vary as a free parameter does not yield significant improvement in the mismatch: the best-fit values are consistently clustered around dℓ​m≃1d_{\ell m}\simeq 1, and fixing dℓ​m=1d_{\ell m}=1 yields nearly indistinguishable mismatches. For this reason, in the main text and below we fix dℓ​m=1d_{\ell m}=1 and we adopt the simpler cℓ​m/(M​ω)c_{\ell m}/(M\omega) parametrization, which retains the accuracy of the more general parametrization while reducing the number of free parameters. The cℓ​m/(M​ω)c_{\ell m}/(M\omega) correction can be understood as the leading-order high-frequency contribution in a Laurent expansion of the phase.

Fits to numerical simulations

Here we describe the fitting procedure used to compare and validate GreyRing against numerical relativity simulations. The latter encompass binaries with mass ratio ∈[1,4]\in[1,4]. We neglect the oldest (and presumably less accurate) simulations of the SXS catalog, focusing on simulation ID≥1000\geq 1000.

The frequency-domain waveform is modeled as

h~ℓ​m​(ω)=M​Aℓ​m(M​ω)pℓ​m​Rℓ​m​(M​ω,χ)​ei​cℓ​mM​ω​ei​(ϕc+ω​tc),\tilde{h}_{\ell m}(\omega)={MA_{\ell m}\over(M\omega)^{p_{\ell m}}}\,R_{\ell m}(M\omega,\chi)\,e^{i{c_{\ell m}\over M\omega}}e^{i(\phi_{c}+\omega t_{c})}\,, (16)

and the multipolar components h~ℓ​m\tilde{h}_{\ell m} are computed using the Fourier-transform convention used in the main text and in Eq. (17) below.

For given values of MM and χ\chi, the amplitude parameters Aℓ​mA_{\ell m} and pℓ​mp_{\ell m} are determined following the procedure introduced in Ref. [113], here extended to include the additional phase parameter cℓ​mc_{\ell m}. We perform separate fits for the amplitude and phase, using the absolute value and argument of the waveform defined in Eq. (16). As discussed in the main text, the phase contains two arbitrary constants, ϕc\phi_{c} and tct_{c}. To remove their effect, we detrend both the numerical phase and the model by subtracting a best-fit linear function in frequency, ensuring that the fit is not dominated by these unphysical degrees of freedom. Residual linear contributions may persist after detrending; we therefore perform the final fit using the full expression in Eq. (16). The fit is performed over a frequency interval [ωi,ωf][\omega_{i},\omega_{f}] defined as follows [113]:

  • •

    We set ωi=0.7​ωx\omega_{i}=0.7\,\omega_{x}, where ωx\omega_{x} is the knee frequency at which the Fourier-domain amplitude transitions from a power-law behavior to an exponential decay, approximately marking the onset of ringdown.

  • •

    We define ωf\omega_{f} such that |h~​(ωf)|=|h~​(ωx)|/20|\tilde{h}(\omega_{f})|=|\tilde{h}(\omega_{x})|/20, in order to suppress contamination from numerical noise at high frequencies, as discussed in Ref. [113].

The lower cutoff ωi\omega_{i} is chosen such that the fitted parameters vary by less than 0.1%0.1\% across the fitting range. While this condition is, strictly speaking, simulation-dependent, we find empirically that choosing ωi=0.7​ωx\omega_{i}=0.7\,\omega_{x} consistently satisfies this requirement across the dataset. The upper cutoff ωf\omega_{f} is set such that the high-frequency numerical-noise plateau of the Fourier-transformed SXS waveform is not larger than ∼10−4\sim 10^{-4} of the signal amplitude, preventing contamination of the fit and avoiding spurious constraints on the parameters. We have checked that the fit and mismatch are robust against variations of the frequency range.

We first apply the model to the dominant (ℓ,m)=(2,2)(\ell,m)=(2,2) mode, finding excellent agreement with the numerical data, as shown in the main text and in the top panel of Fig. 5. The mismatches are typically in the range ∼10−7\sim 10^{-7}–10−510^{-5}, with a distribution peaked around ∼10−6\sim 10^{-6}, demonstrating that the greybody-based description captures both the amplitude and phase of the ringdown signal with remarkable accuracy. We then extend the analysis to subdominant multipoles, focusing on (ℓ,m)=(3,3)(\ell,m)=(3,3) and (4,4)(4,4). Despite their lower intrinsic amplitudes and increased sensitivity to numerical artifacts, the model continues to provide an accurate description, as shown in the middle panel of Fig. 5. For the (3,3)(3,3) mode we obtain typical mismatches of order ∼10−5\sim 10^{-5}, while for the (4,4)(4,4) mode (bottom panel of Fig. 5) the mismatches increase to 𝒪​(10−3){\cal O}(10^{-3}). This is expected because of the stronger impact of numerical noise, the lower amplitude of higher multipoles, and possible spherical-spheroidal mode mixing at higher frequencies [21], as well as potential nonlinear effects [49, 91, 23]. Since the (3,3)(3,3) mode vanishes in the equal-mass limit [36, 18], we restrict the analysis to mass ratios q>1.001q>1.001 for this multipole. For the dominant (2, 2)(2,\,2) mode, equal-mass systems (q≃1q\simeq 1) yield the smallest mismatches, with a sharp peak around ℳ∼10−6\mathcal{M}\sim 10^{-6}, while higher mass ratios are clustered around ∼10−5\sim 10^{-5}. Note that the reflectivity is naturally defined in a spheroidal-harmonic basis, whereas gravitational-wave signals and numerical-relativity waveforms are typically decomposed in spherical harmonics. Transforming between these two bases induces mode mixing, with coefficients that depend on a​ωa\omega [16, 21]. In this analysis we neglect such mixing, as it is expected to be subdominant in the frequency range of interest (a​ω≲0.5a\omega\lesssim 0.5), but its impact may be nonnegligible for other modes. We defer a more careful treatment of mode mixing to future work.

The intrinsic parameters of the injection are calibrated using the NR simulation SXS:BBH:3617, rescaled to a redshifted remnant mass (1+z)​M=68.4​M⊙(1+z)M=68.4\,M_{\odot}, consistent with GW250114-like events. The corresponding dimensionless spin is χ≃ 0.686\chi\simeq\,0.686. The phenomenological parameters (A,p,c)(A,\,p,\,c) entering the amplitude and phase of the model (where we omit multipolar indices for ease of notation) are obtained by fitting the frequency-domain waveform of the simulation, following the procedure described in [113]. The resulting values are reported in Table 1.

We fix the extrinsic parameters (luminosity distance, sky location, inclination, polarization, coalescence time, and phase) to values consistent with those reported in Ref. [3] for the ringdown analysis of GW250114. Indeed, for small-redshift sources, the luminosity distance dLd_{L} is degenerate with the overall post-merger amplitude, and can therefore be neglected without loss of generality. Also, as we are only performing analyses with ℓ=|m|=2\ell=|m|=2 modes, the pattern functions will be the same, so we further fix the inclination angle. Finally, the sky location, coalescence, and time phases are fixed, as their impact on the results is mild. This choice allows us to assess the performance of the model under realistic observational conditions. The adopted values are listed in Table 2.

The signal is injected into a network of ground-based detectors, namely LIGO Hanford and LIGO Livingston, assuming zero-noise data and adopting the Advanced LIGO design sensitivity [80]. The resulting signal-to-noise ratio (SNR) is 24.1224.12 in Hanford and 27.3827.38 in Livingston, giving a total SNR=36.49{\rm SNR}=36.49. The analysis is restricted to the frequency band f∈[140,500]​Hzf\in[140,500]\,{\rm Hz}. The estimation of the parameters (M,χ,A,p,c)(M,\chi,A,p,c) is performed using Bilby [13] with the dynesty [115] sampler.

All injected parameters are accurately recovered within the 90%90\% credible intervals. The posterior distributions, shown in Fig. 6, are sharply peaked around the injected values, indicating that parameter degeneracies are well controlled within the relevant frequency range.

We have verified that the results are robust against variations of both the prior ranges and the frequency band. In particular, the recovery remains stable under changes of the lower and upper cutoffs, fminf_{\rm min} and fmaxf_{\rm max}, within a reasonable range around their fiducial values. We have also tested the relative-binning likelihood and found results to be consistent with those obtained using the standard likelihood. All results presented here are obtained with the standard likelihood.

Finally, we note that there is a computational advantage in using GreyRing over traditional QNM analyses. Because Eq. (16) is explicitly a frequency-domain model, the likelihood can be evaluated with the typical 𝒪​(N)\mathcal{O}(N) complexity of frequency-domain analyses, in contrast with the higher computational cost of direct time-domain or gated-and-inpainted analyses (see Sec. 6 of Ref. [23] for a detailed discussion).

Injection-recovery with QNMs: comparison of greybody factors and QNMs

Besides the analysis performed above, we perform an injection-recovery analysis with GW250114-like mock data, but described with a QNM model. Specifically, we inject a QNM-like signal with the mass and spin values reported in Table 1. The injected QNM signal contains the fundamental (ℓ,m,n)=(2, 2, 0)(\ell,\,m,\,n)=(2,\,2,\,0) mode and the first overtone (2, 2, 1)(2,\,2,\,1), with amplitudes and phase values evaluated by using the fits of Ref. [47], choosing as progenitor parameters those of SXS:BBH:3617: see Table 3. As in the GreyRing analysis, the extrinsic parameters are fixed to the values reported in Table 2, and we use the same network and sensitivity curves. We perform the analysis with the pycbc inference [29] pipeline, using a gated-and-inpainted Gaussian likelihood noise model [122, 126, 38] to remove the influence of pre-peak times not associated with the ringdown. The strain data within a time interval t∈[tc+toffset−0.5​s,tc+toffset]t\in[t_{c}+t_{\rm offset}-0.5\,{\rm s},\,t_{c}+t_{\rm offset}] are replaced/inpainted such that the filtered inverse power spectral density is zero at all times corresponding to the chosen interval [126]. In our case, we fix toffset=10​M≈3.4​mst_{\rm offset}=10M\approx 3.4\,{\rm ms}. We report the estimated posterior parameters in Fig. 7.

To further assess the performance of the model, we directly compare the recovery of the remnant properties (M,χ)(M,\,\chi) obtained with standard QNM-based spectroscopy and with GreyRing. This comparison isolates the determination of the fundamental physical parameters, independently of the specific modeling of the amplitude and phase. In Fig. 8 we show the corresponding corner plots for the two analyses. In both cases, the posterior distributions are centered around the injected values, indicating that both approaches provide an unbiased recovery of the remnant mass and spin. The posterior obtained with GreyRing is slightly tighter than the one derived from the QNM model. Overall, the two approaches yield consistent results and similar M−χM-\chi correlations, with GreyRing offering a modest improvement in parameter estimation within the considered frequency range.

Appendix D GreyRing tests with real data

We analyze publicly available strain data from the LIGO detectors using the Bilby framework [13]. The data are accessed through the GWpy interface [86] and consist of time-domain strain sampled at 4096​Hz4096\,\mathrm{Hz}.

The waveform model discussed in Sec. B is defined in the frequency domain and provides the two polarizations (h+,h×)(h_{+},h_{\times}), which are projected onto each detector using the standard antenna response functions. The extrinsic parameters (sky location, inclination, polarization, and distance) are fixed to the values reported in Table 2, while the coalescence time and phase are allowed to vary and analytically marginalized over.

The intrinsic parameters of the model, (M,χ,A,p,c)(M,\,\chi,\,A,\,p,\,c), are sampled in the inference. Parameter estimation is performed using the dynesty nested sampler [115] with nlive=1000n_{\rm live}=1000 live points. We have verified that the results are robust against variations of the sampler settings.

The analysis is restricted to a frequency band [fmin,fmax][f_{\min},\,f_{\max}] with fmax=512​Hzf_{\max}=512\,\mathrm{Hz}, where the data are already noise-dominated. The lower cutoff fminf_{\min} is varied within the range 4040–200​Hz200\,\mathrm{Hz} in order to probe the stability of the inference and to identify the regime of validity of the model.

In Fig. 9 we show the inferred values of the phenomenological parameters (A,p,c)(A,\,p,\,c) as functions of the lower cutoff frequency fminf_{\min}, while keeping fmax=512​Hzf_{\max}=512\,\mathrm{Hz} fixed. The posterior values of AA and pp in the stable frequency region are consistent with the expectations from a previous hyperfit analysis [113], where these parameters were modeled as functions of the properties of the progenitor binary. In particular, using the source parameters reported by the LIGO-Virgo-KAGRA Collaboration, the predicted values of AA and pp are in agreement with those inferred here.

At low values of fminf_{\min}, the recovered parameters are not stable and exhibit a clear bias with respect to the values recovered for intermediate values of fminf_{\min}. This is expected, as in this frequency region the signal contains significant inspiral contributions, which are not captured by our model. Despite the small statistical uncertainties in this regime, driven by the larger SNR, the inferred parameters are systematically biased.

At high values of fminf_{\min}, the inference also deteriorates. In this case, the available signal is progressively reduced and the data become increasingly dominated by detector noise, leading to larger uncertainties and loss of constraining power.

An intermediate frequency range, fmin∼110f_{\min}\sim 110–140​Hz140\,\mathrm{Hz}, provides a stable recovery of all parameters, with credible posterior intervals consistent with the reference values at the 90%90\% level in the region. This identifies the regime of validity of the model for the considered dataset.

While the phenomenological parameters (A,p,c)(A,\,p,\,c) exhibit a significant dependence on the choice of the lower cutoff frequency, a different behavior is observed for the physical parameters of the remnant. In Fig. 10 we show the 90%90\% posterior contours for the remnant mass MM and spin χ\chi obtained for representative values of the lower cutoff frequency fminf_{\min}. In contrast to the phenomenological parameters, the inferred values of (M,χ)(M,\,\chi) remain remarkably stable across the explored frequency range. Although small shifts appear at very low and very high values of fminf_{\min}, the posteriors largely overlap and are consistent within their uncertainties. This behavior is in contrast with standard QNM data analysis, where the inferred posteriors for (M,χ)(M,\,\chi) can be very sensitive to the choice of the starting time tstartt_{\mathrm{start}} [2, 1], as we also checked with an independent pycbc inference.

This robustness reflects the fact that MM and χ\chi set the overall frequency scale and structure of the ringdown signal, which are already well constrained within a relatively narrow frequency interval. Consequently, their estimation is only weakly sensitive to the precise choice of the frequency band and to residual modeling inaccuracies at low frequencies.

Although the results shown in the plots refer to the inference in which all extrinsic parameters are fixed to the values reported in Table 2, we have also performed additional runs in which the inclination angle ι\iota and the polarization angle ψ\psi are sampled rather than fixed, as a consistency check. We find that this has a negligible impact on the posterior distributions of the remnant mass and spin, confirming that our ability to constrain the remnant parameters is relatively insensitive to the treatment of these extrinsic parameters in the present setup.

This suggests that the remnant parameters are mainly controlled by the global structure of the signal and are therefore more robustly inferred than the auxiliary parameters (A,p,c)(A,\,p,\,c) in the present formulation of the model. Although the physical interpretation of (A,p,c)(A,\,p,\,c) is not yet fully understood and may evolve with further progress in the Green’s function description of the ringdown, the stability of the inferred (M,χ)(M,\,\chi) already indicates that incomplete knowledge of these quantities does not prevent robust access to the remnant properties. This may represent an advantage over standard QNM data analysis, in which initial conditions can affect the inference more directly.

To further characterize the structure of the inference, in Fig. 11 we show the full posterior distribution for all parameters, obtained for a representative choice of fmin=110​Hzf_{\min}=110\,\mathrm{Hz} within the validity regime identified above. The corner plot highlights correlations among the phenomenological parameters, and in particular a degeneracy between AA and pp, reflecting the freedom to redistribute the overall amplitude and spectral slope within the model. By contrast, AA and pp show no significant correlation with cc. The posteriors for (M,χ)(M,\,\chi) are well localized, while the phenomenological parameters AA and pp display broader and more correlated distributions. This confirms that the remnant properties are primarily determined by the global structure of the signal, while the phenomenological parameters encode subleading features and are more sensitive to the choice of frequency range and to modeling assumptions.

As an additional benchmark, we performed an independent parameter estimation using standard QNM-based spectroscopy within the pycbc inference framework, obtaining posterior distributions for (M,χ)(M,\,\chi) consistent with those reported by the LIGO-Virgo-KAGRA Collaboration. The results obtained with GreyRing are in agreement with these independent QNM-based analyses.
