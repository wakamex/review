Late-Transition Interacting Thawer Dark Energy: Physics and Validation

Slava G. Turyshev    Diogo H. F. de Souza Jet Propulsion Laboratory, California Institute of Technology,
4800 Oak Grove Drive, Pasadena, CA 91109-0899, USA
Abstract

We formulate the late-transition interacting thawer (LTIT) as a late-activating, variable-coupling realization of coupled quintessence in which a canonical scalar field couples conformally only to cold dark matter. The construction is designed to separate two issues that are often mixed in late-time dark-energy analyses: a genuine low-redshift deformation of the expansion history and a shift of the pre-recombination calibration sector. We derive the exact background equations, the exact CDM scaling identity ρc​(a)=ρc​0​a−3​C​[ϕ​(a)]/C​(ϕ0)\rho_{\rm c}(a)=\rho_{c0}a^{-3}C[\phi(a)]/C(\phi_{0}), and the linear scalar perturbation equations in synchronous gauge in a form suitable for Einstein–Boltzmann solvers. For representative benchmarks we find Ωϕ​(z∗)∼10−9\Omega_{\phi}(z_{*})\sim 10^{-9} and |Δ​rd/rd|<4×10−3|\Delta r_{d}/r_{d}|<4\times 10^{-3}, while the low-redshift background shift remains sub-percent and the approximate growth response ranges from sub-percent to several percent, depending on the asymptotic coupling amplitude. LTIT is therefore not a flexible w​(z)w(z) ansatz but a constrained interacting-dark-energy framework in which any apparent background success must survive perturbation-level closure tests.

I Introduction

The central observational problem raised by post-DESI dark-energy analyses is no longer whether late-time cosmic acceleration exists, but whether any small apparent departure from w=−1w=-1 is best interpreted as: (i) a genuine low-redshift dynamical effect, (ii) an early-time calibration shift through rdr_{\rm d}, the sound horizon at the baryon drag epoch, or (iii) a residual systematic mode in the joint likelihood built from supernovae, BAO, and CMB data. In practice, the DESI-era preference for evolving dark energy is mild and highly sensitive to data combination, supernova calibration/selection modeling, and the assumed parametrization of the expansion history [1, 2]. This naturally motivates physically explicit models that can be judged not only by background distances but by perturbation-level closure tests.

The late-transition interacting thawer (LTIT) is a deliberately constrained late-time branch of coupled quintessence in which a canonical scalar field is conformally coupled only to cold dark matter and the coupling becomes appreciable only after the field reaches a threshold in field space. The physical problem is narrow but nontrivial: can a genuine low-redshift deformation of the expansion history be generated without materially shifting the pre-recombination calibration sector, and can that deformation arise without introducing phantom microphysics?

What makes the problem restrictive is that the same interaction that alters the homogeneous background also acts directly on the coupled CDM perturbations. LTIT is therefore more informative than a background parametrization such as CPL: once the coupling turns on, it changes not only H​(z)H(z) but also the CDM continuity and Euler equations, so any apparent success in distance observables must survive a perturbation-level closure test. The aim of the present paper is accordingly modest but sharp. We do not claim that current data require such an interaction; rather, we formulate LTIT as an explicit late-transition model, derive its background and synchronous-gauge perturbation equations, and show through representative benchmarks that the intended hierarchy—tiny early-time effects, sub-percent background deformations, and a potentially larger late-time growth response—is technically realizable.

The paper is organized as follows. Section II defines the LTIT model and its field-theory structure. Section III derives the background dynamics and the effective phantom condition. Section IV presents the synchronous-gauge perturbation equations and initial conditions. Section V summarizes the physical interpretation and design criteria. Section VI presents benchmark validation calculations. Section VII summarizes the solver-level requirements implied by the LTIT system. Section VIII concludes.

II LTIT as a variable-coupling coupled-quintessence model

II.1 Action and Einstein-frame interpretation

We work in the Einstein frame with metric gμ​νg_{\mu\nu} and reduced Planck mass MPlM_{\rm Pl}. Baryons and radiation are minimally coupled. CDM is conformally coupled to a canonical scalar field ϕ\phi:

For constant β\beta, Eqs. (1)–(2) reduce to standard coupled quintessence [3, 4]. LTIT is the field-dependent generalization in which the coupling is strongly suppressed until the field reaches a threshold.

LTIT is not a separately established historical model class; rather, it is the specific late-transition, field-dependent coupling ansatz introduced in this work as a DESI-era, early-time-protected subclass of coupled quintessence.

II.2 Late-transition ansatz and closed-form conformal factor

The defining LTIT ansatz is a smooth step in field space,

Since Eq. (2) can be integrated explicitly, the conformal factor is

Eq. (5) makes explicit that LTIT is not a separate dark-sector species, but a late-activation branch of scalar–tensor/coupled-quintessence dynamics.

II.3 Benchmark potential and parameter regime

For the benchmark study we adopt the shallow exponential potential

V​(ϕ)=V0​exp⁡(−λ​ϕMPl),V(\phi)=V_{0}\exp\!\left(-\lambda\frac{\phi}{M_{\rm Pl}}\right), (6)

with λ≪1\lambda\ll 1. The exponential is used here for one reason only: it isolates the effect of the late coupling from unrelated structure in the scalar potential and keeps the background dynamics analytically transparent. LTIT itself is not defined by Eq. (6); any sufficiently shallow thawing potential that leaves the field effectively frozen until late times would preserve the same qualitative mechanism [5]. The present paper is therefore a controlled benchmark of the late transition, not a claim of uniqueness for the exponential choice.

II.4 Why the transition must be late

A non-negligible dark-sector coupling during the drag-epoch or recombination era generically perturbs the pre-recombination expansion rate, shifts the sound horizon rdr_{\rm d}, modifies the CMB acoustic scale, and changes the matter-radiation era growth history. LTIT is built to avoid that. The phenomenological purpose of the tanh activation is thus not ad hoc freedom for its own sake; it enforces a clean temporal separation between the well-calibrated early universe and any allowed late-time dynamics. In this sense LTIT should be regarded as a deliberately constrained, DESI-era-friendly subclass of variable-coupling coupled quintessence, rather than as a generic interacting-DE model.

III.3 Exact CDM identity

Using Eq. (2), the CDM continuity equation can be integrated exactly:

Eq. (18) is an exact identity and should be used as a stringent implementation-level validation test. In practice, a numerical solution that violates Eq. (18) at more than the 10−610^{-6} level is not under adequate control.

IV.4 Initial conditions

Because LTIT enforces β→0\beta\to 0 at early times, adiabatic initial conditions reduce to the standard uncoupled thawer limit. Deep in radiation domination one may initialize the scalar sector as

ϕ​(τi)=ϕi,ϕ′​(τi)≃0,δ​ϕ​(τi)≃0,δ​ϕ′​(τi)≃0,\phi(\tau_{i})=\phi_{i},\qquad\phi^{\prime}(\tau_{i})\simeq 0,\qquad\delta\phi(\tau_{i})\simeq 0,\qquad\delta\phi^{\prime}(\tau_{i})\simeq 0, (40)

while the fluid perturbations satisfy the usual synchronous-gauge adiabatic relations,

δc=δb=34​δγ=34​δν=−12​h+𝒪​(k2​τ2),θc,θb=𝒪​(β,k2​τ3).\delta_{c}=\delta_{b}=\frac{3}{4}\delta_{\gamma}=\frac{3}{4}\delta_{\nu}=-\frac{1}{2}h+\mathcal{O}(k^{2}\tau^{2}),\qquad\theta_{c},\theta_{b}=\mathcal{O}(\beta,k^{2}\tau^{3}). (41)

In practice one chooses ziz_{i} so that β​(zi)\beta(z_{i}) is numerically negligible and reuses the standard adiabatic initial-condition machinery of the Boltzmann solver for the fluid sector. In the benchmark integrations we fix the field origin by convention and take ϕi=0\phi_{i}=0. For the exponential benchmark potential, a constant shift of ϕ\phi can be absorbed into V0V_{0} and ϕt\phi_{t}, so only the displacement relative to the transition location is physically relevant.

These initial conditions are valid only while the interaction is negligible. Once the coupling activates, CDM is no longer geodesic and the synchronous-gauge CDM-comoving condition θc=0\theta_{c}=0 ceases to be admissible. Any implementation that freezes θc\theta_{c} after activation is solving a different system.

V Physical interpretation and closure logic

LTIT should be viewed as a restricted late-time deformation of coupled quintessence rather than as a free interacting-dark-energy parametrization. A constant or early-acting coupling would feed directly into the drag epoch and recombination dynamics, shifting rdr_{\rm d}, altering the CMB acoustic scale, and modifying the matter-era growth history. The field-dependent activation in Eq. (3) is therefore not inserted for phenomenological flexibility; it is the minimal mechanism that separates a well-tested early universe from the late sector the model is intended to probe.

This structure also clarifies why LTIT is more informative than a background parametrization such as CPL. A background fit can reproduce a smooth low-redshift trend in H​(z)H(z), but it does not specify how energy-momentum is transferred, whether the underlying scalar sector is microphysically acceptable, or what happens to the CDM perturbations. In LTIT those questions are explicit. The scalar remains canonical, so wϕ≥−1w_{\phi}\geq-1, while an apparent weff<−1w_{\rm eff}<-1 phase arises only through the transfer term in Eq. (16). The same interaction that alters the background also modifies the CDM continuity and Euler equations, Eqs. (30)–(31), so the model cannot improve distance data without paying a perturbation-level price.

The relevant standard of success is therefore not whether LTIT can imitate a CPL-like background trend, but whether it can do so while preserving early-time calibration and surviving the stronger consistency test from growth and lensing. That closure logic is the central physics argument of the paper.

VI Benchmark validation program

The purpose of the benchmark calculations is to validate the LTIT design logic before a full Boltzmann run. The benchmarks are obtained from exact integration of the background equations together with the controlled quasi-static growth approximation

D′′+(2+H′H)​D′−32​Ωm​(a)​μ​(a)​D≃0,μ​(a)≈1+2​β2​(ϕ)​Ωc​(a)Ωm​(a).D^{\prime\prime}+\left(2+\frac{H^{\prime}}{H}\right)D^{\prime}-\frac{3}{2}\Omega_{\rm m}(a)\,\mu(a)\,D\simeq 0,\qquad\mu(a)\approx 1+2\beta^{2}(\phi)\frac{\Omega_{\rm c}(a)}{\Omega_{\rm m}(a)}. (42)

This approximation is not used as a substitute for Einstein–Boltzmann evolution, but it is sufficient to show the expected magnitude and sign of the growth response.

The benchmark calculations are intended to establish internal consistency, scale setting, and the sign of the expected response. They are not likelihood-level predictions. Background quantities are exact, whereas the growth curves use the quasi-static approximation Eq. (42); a full statistical assessment of LTIT requires a dedicated Einstein–Boltzmann implementation and joint fits to background and perturbation-sensitive data.

VII Solver-level requirements

The LTIT equations are written in a form suitable for implementation in standard Einstein–Boltzmann solvers such as CLASS and CAMB [7, 8, 9]. A correct implementation must evolve both the scalar sector and the coupled CDM perturbations consistently, without restoring the CDM-comoving synchronous condition once the interaction activates.

Before any numerical scan is trusted, the solver must reproduce the decoupling limit β0→0\beta_{0}\to 0, the exact CDM identity Eq. (18), the early-time protection diagnostics Ωϕ​(z∗)\Omega_{\phi}(z_{*}) and Δ​rd/rd\Delta r_{d}/r_{d}, and cross-code agreement for matched parameter choices and gauge conventions. These conditions are more important than any particular software pathway, since they determine whether the LTIT system has been implemented correctly.

VIII Conclusions

LTIT is a late-transition realization of coupled quintessence in which a field-dependent CDM coupling remains negligible through the pre-recombination era and activates only at low redshift. The benchmark study presented here shows that such a construction can deform the late expansion history without materially shifting the calibration sector and without invoking phantom microphysics. For the representative cases studied here, Ωϕ​(z∗)∼10−9\Omega_{\phi}(z_{*})\sim 10^{-9} and |Δ​rd/rd|<4×10−3|\Delta r_{d}/r_{d}|<4\times 10^{-3}, so the model behaves as a genuinely late-time deformation, while the low-redshift background shift remains sub-percent and the approximate growth response reaches the percent level once the interaction turns on.

This asymmetry is the real scientific content of LTIT. A background-only parametrization can reproduce a smooth low-redshift trend in H​(z)H(z), but it does not specify the transfer channel, the microphysical viability of the scalar sector, or the perturbation response of the coupled matter component. LTIT does. Because the interaction acts directly on CDM, the model is informative only if its apparent background success remains consistent with the coupled growth response. The relevant standard is therefore not background flexibility but closure under the simultaneous background and perturbation dynamics derived in Secs. III and IV.
