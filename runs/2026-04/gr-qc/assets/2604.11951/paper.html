Distinguish Bardeen-like black holes by Gravitational lensing

Limei Yuan State Key Laboratory of Surface Physics, Center for Astronomy and Astrophysics, Department of Physics, Center for Field Theory and Particle Physics, and Institute for Nanoelectronic devices and Quantum computing, Fudan University, Shanghai 200433, China [ Chen-Hung Hsiao State Key Laboratory of Surface Physics, Center for Astronomy and Astrophysics, Department of Physics, Center for Field Theory and Particle Physics, and Institute for Nanoelectronic devices and Quantum computing, Fudan University, Shanghai 200433, China [ Yidun Wan State Key Laboratory of Surface Physics, Center for Astronomy and Astrophysics, Department of Physics, Center for Field Theory and Particle Physics, and Institute for Nanoelectronic devices and Quantum computing, Fudan University, Shanghai 200433, China Hefei National Laboratory, Hefei 230088, China [
Abstract

We study Bardeen-like regular black holes without Cauchy horizons via gravitational lensing. In the weak field, the deflection angle receives a positive ℓ\ell-dependent correction, producing a slightly larger Einstein ring. For the galaxy ESO 325‑G004, the predicted ring radius is consistent with current observations. In the strong field, for Sgr A* and M87*, the asymptotic position θ∞\theta_{\infty} remains identical to the Schwarzschild value; however, SDL coefficients are ℓ\ell-dependent, the angular separation ss increases and the relative flux ratio rmagr_{\mathrm{mag}} decreases as ℓ\ell increases. Time delays between relativistic images for Sgr A* and M87* also increase mildly with ℓ\ell. Our calculated values for these observables remain consistent with current observations. Future strong-field measurements of △​T2,1\triangle T_{2,1}, ss, and rmagr_{\mathrm{mag}} may offer a viable test for regular black holes free of Cauchy horizons and may distinguish Bardeen-like from Schwarzschild black holes.

I Introduction

Gravitational lensing provides a powerful tool for compact objects and the nature of gravity in both weak and strong fields. Regular black holes resolve the central singularity but may pose an issue with the potential emergence of Cauchy horizons Poisson:1990eh; Maeda:2005yd, such as Hayward and Bardeen black holes Hayward:2005gi; 1968qtr..conf...87B. The Bardeen-like regular black hole resolves the singularity without causing the Cauchy horizon. An important question arises: can the distinct geometry of this black hole be distinguished from a Schwarzschild black hole through observable lensing effects with modern or future telescopes? In this work, we systematically study the gravitational lensing signatures of Bardeen-like regular black holes in both weak and strong fields to address the question. We obtain the following key results:

  1. 1.

    In the weak field regime, the deflection angle increases with ℓ\ell, and the theoretical Einstein ring for the Bardeen-like black hole is slightly larger than one in the Schwarzschild case. Using data in Galaxy ESO 325-G004, the calculated Einstein ring is consistent with the observed one within the error bars.

  2. 2.

    In the Strong Deflection Limit (SDL), deflection angle is computed and SDL observables (θ∞,s,rmag)(\theta_{\infty},s,r_{\mathrm{mag}}) are computed using data in Sgr A* and M87*. Obervables (s,rmag)(s,r_{\mathrm{mag}}) are ℓ\ell-dependent, however, the black-hole shadow radius θ∞\theta_{\infty} is ℓ\ell-independent.

  3. 3.

    Time delay Δ​T2,1\Delta T_{2,1} between the first two images increases with ℓ\ell for Sgr A* and M87* cases. The calculated black hole shadows remain consistent with the measured values for M87* and Sgr A*.

Motivation and Background The phenomenon in which a massive object deflects a light ray is called gravitational lensing (GL). Wambsganss:1998gg; ORBi-f3f7ce61-7628-4d1a-b937-387c26218fd7; Prat:2025ucy; Bartelmann:2016dvf; Bozza_2002; Bozza:2001xd; Bozza:2002zj; Bozza:2003cp; Bozza:2008ev; Wang:2024iwt; Liu:2026yyj. Figure 1 depicts the lensing geometry. The spacetime we consider is asymptotically flat, where both observer OO and source SS reside in flat regions, separated by a lens LL. The line O​LOL connecting the observer and the lens defines the optical axis. A light ray emitted from a source SS at an angular position β\beta is deflected by the lens LL and reaches the observer OO at an apparent angular position θ\theta. The amount of the deflection is quantified by the deflection angle α^\hat{\alpha}. We model the lens as a static, spherically symmetric spacetime described by the line element:

d​s2=−A​(r)​d​t2+B​(r)​d​r2+C​(r)​(d​θ2+sin2⁡θ​d​ϕ2).ds^{2}=-A(r)dt^{2}+B(r)dr^{2}+C(r)\left(d\theta^{2}+\sin^{2}\theta d\phi^{2}\right). (1)

Spherical symmetry allows us to restrict the photon trajectories to the equatorial (paper) plane without loss of generality. There are two conserved quantities along the null geodesics: Energy ℰ\mathcal{E} and angular momentum 𝒥\mathcal{J}. The impact parameter bb is defined as

b≡𝒥ℰ=C​(r0)A​(r0),b\equiv\frac{\mathcal{J}}{\mathcal{E}}=\sqrt{\frac{C(r_{0})}{A(r_{0})}}\,, (2)

where r0r_{0} is the distance of closest approach, i.e., r˙|r0=0\dot{r}|_{r_{0}}=0. From the null geodesic equation, the deflection angle α\alpha is expressed as a function of r0r_{0} Virbhadra:1998dy; Weinberg:1972kfs:

α​(r0)=2​∫r0∞B​(r)C​(r)​[C​(r)​A​(r0)C​(r0)​A​(r)−1]−1/2​𝑑r−π,\alpha(r_{0})=2\int^{\infty}_{r_{0}}\sqrt{\frac{B(r)}{C(r)}}\left[\frac{C(r)A(r_{0})}{C(r_{0})A(r)}-1\right]^{-1/2}dr-\pi\;, (3)

where 2 is the consequence of the incoming and outgoing paths being symmetric.

In the strong deflection regime, the geometric relations between the source, lens, and observer are given by the exact lens equation (often referred to as the Ohanian or Virbhadra-Ellis lens equation) Bozza:2008ev:

DS​tan⁡β=DL​sin⁡θ−DL​S​sin⁡(α−θ)cos⁡(α−θ),D_{S}\tan\beta=\frac{D_{L}\sin\theta-D_{LS}\sin(\alpha-\theta)}{\cos(\alpha-\theta)}, (4)

where the observer-lens, lens-source, and observe-source angular-diameter distances are denoted by DLD_{L}, DL​SD_{LS}, and DS=DL+DL​SD_{S}=D_{L}+D_{LS}, respectively.

Since the surface brightness is preserved in gravitational lensing according to Liouville’s theorem, the magnification μ\mu is defined as the ratio of the solid angles of the image and the source:

μ≡d​Ωθd​Ωβ=(sin⁡βsin⁡θ​d​βd​θ)−1.\mu\equiv\frac{d\Omega_{\theta}}{d\Omega_{\beta}}=\left(\frac{\sin\beta}{\sin\theta}\frac{d\beta}{d\theta}\right)^{-1}. (5)

As we will see, the lensing observables are determined by deflection α\alpha.

Recent work Zhang:2025ccx shows that broad classes of static, spherically symmetric geometries can arise as exact vacuum solutions of generally covariant gravity theories. A regular black hole can potentially be found related to vacuum solutions arising from certain generally covariant gravity theories. Gravitational lensing of a regular black hole has been studied Eiroa:2010wm; Zhao:2017cwk; Liu:2026yyj; 10.1088/1674-1056/ae29fa. A recent work Calza:2025mrt constructs Bardeen-like regular black holes that resolve the Cauchy horizon issue in the original Bardeen black hole Poisson:1990eh. A Bardeen-like black hole Calza:2025mrt has the metric

d​s2=−(1−2​M​(ρ)ρ)​d​t2+(1−2​M​(ρ)ρ)−1​d​ρ2+r2​(ρ)​d​Ω2,ds^{2}=-\left(1-\frac{2M(\rho)}{\rho}\right)dt^{2}+\left(1-\frac{2M(\rho)}{\rho}\right)^{-1}d\rho^{2}+r^{2}(\rho)\,d\Omega^{2}\,, (6)

where the mass function M​(ρ)M(\rho) and the areal radius r​(ρ)r(\rho) are defined as

M​(ρ)=m​ρ3(ρ2+ℓ2)3/2,r​(ρ)=(ρ2+ℓ2)3/2ρ2,M(\rho)=\frac{m\rho^{3}}{(\rho^{2}+\ell^{2})^{3/2}},\qquad r(\rho)=\frac{(\rho^{2}+\ell^{2})^{3/2}}{\rho^{2}}\,, (7)

and satisfies the condition that ρ≥ρ0\rho\geq\rho_{0}, with d​r​(ρ)d​ρ|ρ0=0\frac{\mathrm{d}r(\rho)}{\mathrm{d}\rho}|_{\rho_{0}}=0. Here, mm represents the ADM mass, and ℓ\ell is the regular-core (length) scale and has an upper bound ℓ<4​m3​3\ell<\frac{4m}{3\sqrt{3}}. Setting ℓ=0\ell=0 recovers the standard Schwarzschild black hole. More details of Bardeen-like black holes can be found in Appendix A.

II Weak-Field Lensing

To investigate the lens’s gravitational signature at astrophysical distances, we turn to the weak-field regime. Here, the impact parameter bb is significantly larger than the event horizon, allowing us to treat the deflection of light as a perturbative effect.

In the weak field, the closest approach r0r_{0} is very large, which means we can expand the deflection angle in 1r0\frac{1}{r_{0}}. Meanwhile, we change the integration variable rr to r0z\frac{r_{0}}{z}, the deflection angle (3) becomes:

α​(1r0)=2​∫01B​(r0z)C​(r0z)​[C​(r0z)​A​(r0)C​(r0)​A​(r0z)−1]−1/2​r0z2​𝑑z−π.\alpha\left(\frac{1}{r_{0}}\right)=2\int^{1}_{0}\sqrt{\frac{B\left(\frac{r_{0}}{z}\right)}{C\left(\frac{r_{0}}{z}\right)}}\left[\frac{C\left(\frac{r_{0}}{z}\right)A(r_{0})}{C(r_{0})A\left(\frac{r_{0}}{z}\right)}-1\right]^{-1/2}\frac{r_{0}}{z^{2}}\,dz-\pi\;. (8)

After integration, we obtain:

α​(1r0)=4​mr0−4​m2r02+15​m2​π4​r02+3​l2​π4​r02.\alpha\left(\frac{1}{r_{0}}\right)=\frac{4m}{r_{0}}-\frac{4m^{2}}{r_{0}^{2}}+\frac{15m^{2}\pi}{4r_{0}^{2}}+\frac{3l^{2}\pi}{4r_{0}^{2}}\;. (9)

The relation between 1r0\frac{1}{r_{0}} and 2​mb\frac{2m}{b} can be obtained from the Taylor expansion in 1r0\frac{1}{r_{0}} of  (2):

2​mb=2​mr0−2​m2r02−m3+3​m​l2r03−m4−6​m2​l2r04+−5​m5+18​m3​l2+15​m​l44​r05+𝒪​(1r05).\frac{2m}{b}=\frac{2m}{r_{0}}-\frac{2m^{2}}{r_{0}^{2}}-\frac{m^{3}+3ml^{2}}{r_{0}^{3}}-\frac{m^{4}-6m^{2}l^{2}}{r_{0}^{4}}+\frac{-5m^{5}+18m^{3}l^{2}+15ml^{4}}{4r_{0}^{5}}+\mathcal{O}\left(\frac{1}{r_{0}^{5}}\right). (10)

We solve this transcendental equation and obtain:

1r0=1b+mb2+𝒪​(1b2).\frac{1}{r_{0}}=\frac{1}{b}+\frac{m}{b^{2}}+\mathcal{O}\left(\frac{1}{b^{2}}\right). (11)

Plugging (11) into (9), we obtain the deflection angle to first order correction of ℓ\ell:

α​(b)≃4​mb+15​π4​m2b2+3​π4​ℓ2b2+𝒪​(m3,m2​ℓ2),\alpha(b)\simeq\frac{4m}{b}+\frac{15\pi}{4}\frac{m^{2}}{b^{2}}+\frac{3\pi}{4}\frac{\ell^{2}}{b^{2}}+\mathcal{O}\!\left(m^{3},m^{2}\ell^{2}\right)\;, (12)

where the first term is the standard Einstein deflection angle for a point mass. The most notable feature of this model, however, is the sign of the ℓ\ell-correction term of the deflection angle. The correction involving ℓ2/b2\ell^{2}/b^{2} enters with a positive sign. This positive deviation distinguishes the Bardeen-like black holes from the standard Bardeen black holes. In the standard Bardeen case, the regular core typically produces a negative correction that reduces the deflection angle. In contrast, the Bardeen-like black hole enhances the deflection in the weak-field regime. The regular-core scale ℓ\ell effectively deepens the gravitational potential slightly at large distances, mimicking a small increase in the effective mass.

This behavior is shown in Fig. 2. Figure 2(a) displays the ratio of the total deflection angle, while Fig. 2(b) isolates the deviation from the Schwarzschild black hole. The deviation is consistently positive and grows monotonically with ℓ\ell.

The image is called the Einstein ring when the source, lens, and observer are perfectly aligned (β=0\beta=0). In this configuration, the lens equation simplifies to θE=DL​SDS​α​(DL​θE)\theta_{E}=\frac{D_{LS}}{D_{S}}\alpha(D_{L}\theta_{E}). Inserting our derived deflection angle, we obtain an implicit equation for the Einstein ring:

θE=DL​SDS​[4​mDL​θE+15​π4​m2(DL​θE)2+3​π4​ℓ2(DL​θE)2​…].\theta_{E}=\frac{D_{LS}}{D_{S}}\left[\frac{4m}{D_{L}\theta_{E}}+\frac{15\pi}{4}\frac{m^{2}}{(D_{L}\theta_{E})^{2}}+\frac{3\pi}{4}\frac{\ell^{2}}{(D_{L}\theta_{E})^{2}}\dots\right]. (13)

The first term corresponds to the point mass’s radius, and the correction terms yield a larger ring radius. In the Appendix B, we show that the calculated Einstein ring using observational data in ESO 325-G004 is consistent with the observed value of the Einstein ring in ESO 325-G004  Smith:2005pq; Smith:2013ena. The theoretical results of deflection angles and Einstein rings are consistently slightly larger than those in the Schwarzschild case. This implies that while the current model is consistent with available data, future observations with higher angular resolution may constrain ℓ\ell.

III Strong Field Limit and Lensing Observables

III.1 Lensing Observables in Strong Field Limit

To probe gravitational lensing in the vicinity of black holes, we must consider the strong field regime. In this region, the weak-field approximation breaks down as light rays are deflected near the photon sphere of a black hole. We assume that photons traversing this region follow null geodesics exterior to the photon sphere of the Bardeen-like black hole, allowing us to employ the SDL method. This method, developed by Bozza Bozza:2001xd; Bozza:2002zj, provides an accurate analytic framework for describing the relativistic images formed by photons that wind multiple times around the black hole before reaching the observer.

For convenience, we rewrite the Bardeen-like black hole metric (6) as:

d​s2=−(1−2​mr​(ρ))​d​t2+(1−2​mr​(ρ))−1​d​ρ2+r​(ρ)2​d​Ω2,ds^{2}=-\left(1-\frac{2m}{r(\rho)}\right)dt^{2}+\left(1-\frac{2m}{r(\rho)}\right)^{-1}d\rho^{2}+r(\rho)^{2}d\Omega^{2}, (14)

where the areal radius is defined as r​(ρ)=(l2+ρ2)3/2ρ2r(\rho)=\frac{(l^{2}+\rho^{2})^{3/2}}{\rho^{2}}. Meanwhile, we set

A​(ρ)≡1−2​mr​(ρ),B​(ρ)≡(1−2​mr​(ρ))−1,C​(ρ)≡r​(ρ).A(\rho)\equiv 1-\frac{2m}{r(\rho)},\;\;\;B(\rho)\equiv\left(1-\frac{2m}{r(\rho)}\right)^{-1},\;\;C(\rho)\equiv r(\rho). (15)

Near a black hole, gravity is strong enough to force photons into unstable circular orbits. The collection of such circular orbits is called the black hole’s photon sphere, whose radius is determined by the root of the equation:

A′​(ρp​s)A​(ρp​s)=C′​(ρp​s)C​(ρp​s).\frac{A^{\prime}(\rho_{ps})}{A(\rho_{ps})}=\frac{C^{\prime}(\rho_{ps})}{C(\rho_{ps})}. (16)

Solving this for the Bardeen-like black hole yields the critical condition:

r​(ρp​s)=3​m,r(\rho_{ps})=3m, (17)

where ρp​s\rho_{ps} is the largest real root of the equation (ρp​s2+l2)3/2=3​m​ρp​s2.(\rho_{ps}^{2}+l^{2})^{3/2}=3m\rho_{ps}^{2}. Consequently, we can define a critical impact parameter up​su_{ps}. Rays with impact parameters u<up​su<u_{ps} are captured by the black hole, contributing to the black hole’s dark “shadow”, while rays with u>up​su>u_{ps} escape. The boundary between the shadow and the photon sphere is given by:

up​s=C​(ρp​s)A​(ρp​s)=3​3​m.u_{ps}=\sqrt{\frac{C(\rho_{ps})}{A(\rho_{ps})}}=3\sqrt{3}m. (18)

Remarkably, this result implies that the critical impact parameter depends only on the value of r​(ρ)r(\rho) at the photon sphere, which remains effectively 3​m3m regardless of the regular-core scale ℓ\ell.

In the strong deflection limit (u→up​su\to u_{ps}), the deflection angle α​(u)\alpha(u) does not remain small; instead, it diverges logarithmically as the photon spends more time winding around the black hole Bozza:2002zj; Bozza:2001xd; Tsukamoto:2016jzh:

α​(u)=−a¯​log⁡(uup​s−1)+b¯+𝒪​(u−up​s),\alpha(u)=-\bar{a}\log\left(\frac{u}{u_{ps}}-1\right)+\bar{b}+\mathcal{O}(u-u_{ps}), (19)

where a¯\bar{a} and b¯\bar{b} are called SDL coefficients. The coefficient a¯\bar{a} has a closed form:

a¯=2​A​(ρp​s)​B​(ρp​s)C′′​(ρp​s)​A​(ρp​s)−A′′​(ρp​s)​C​(ρp​s)=(ρp​s3​ρp​s2+l2−6​m),\bar{a}=\sqrt{\frac{2A(\rho_{ps})B(\rho_{ps})}{C^{\prime\prime}(\rho_{ps})A(\rho_{ps})-A^{\prime\prime}(\rho_{ps})C(\rho_{ps})}}=\left(\frac{\rho_{ps}}{3\sqrt{\rho_{ps}^{2}+l^{2}}-6m}\right), (20)

but we were unable to derive an analytical expression for b¯\bar{b}; it can only be evaluated numerically. In the limit ℓ→0\ell\to 0, we recover the Schwarzschild values a¯=1\bar{a}=1 and b¯≈−0.40023\bar{b}\approx-0.40023.

As depicted in Fig. 4 and detailed in Table 1, a¯\bar{a} monotonically increases with ℓ\ell, whereas b¯\bar{b} decreases as ℓ\ell increases. The regular-core scale ℓ\ell of the Bardeen-like black hole modifies the lensing signature by altering the SDL coefficients.

The divergence of the deflection angle near up​su_{ps} (shown in Fig. 3) implies that photons can loop around the black hole multiple times (nn) before reaching the observer. Each full loop (2​π2\pi) creates a new “relativistic image”. Although the total deflection angle α\alpha is large (α>2​π\alpha>2\pi), the observed angular separation of these images from the optical axis is small as both source and observer far away from the lens. This physical setup justifies the use of the small-angle approximation (β,θ≪1\beta,\theta\ll 1) in the lens equation Bozza:2008ev; Virbhadra:1999nm:

β=θ−DL​SDS​Δ​αn,\beta=\theta-\frac{D_{LS}}{D_{S}}\Delta\alpha_{n}, (21)

where Δ​αn=α​(θ)−2​n​π\Delta\alpha_{n}=\alpha(\theta)-2n\pi. Solving Eq. (21) using the expansion in Eq. (19), we obtain the position of the nn-th relativistic image by Bozza_2002:

θn≃θn0+up​s​en​(β−θn0)​DSa¯​DL​S​DL,\theta_{n}\simeq\theta_{n}^{0}+\frac{u_{ps}e_{n}(\beta-\theta_{n}^{0})D_{S}}{\bar{a}D_{LS}D_{L}}, (22)

where en=exp⁡(b¯−2​n​πa¯)e_{n}=\exp\left(\frac{\bar{b}-2n\pi}{\bar{a}}\right), and θn0=up​s​(1+en)/DL\theta_{n}^{0}=u_{ps}(1+e_{n})/D_{L} represents the theoretical image position for a perfect 2​n​π2n\pi loop.

The magnification of these images can be obtained by applying the small-angle approximation to Eq. (5), the magnification of the nn-th image is found to be Bozza:2002zj:

μn=(βθ​d​βd​θ)−1|θn0=up​s2​en​(1+en)​DSa¯​β​DL​S​DL2.\mu_{n}=\left.\left(\frac{\beta}{\theta}\frac{d\beta}{d\theta}\right)^{-1}\right|_{\theta^{0}_{n}}=\frac{u^{2}_{ps}e_{n}(1+e_{n})D_{S}}{\bar{a}\beta D_{LS}D^{2}_{L}}. (23)

Since ene_{n} decays exponentially with nn, the brightness of the relativistic images drops rapidly. This implies that only the first relativistic image (n=1n=1) is likely to be detectable, appearing as a faint, thin ring closely hugging the black hole’s shadow.

Finally, we can find three strong lensing SDL observables Bozza:2001xd:

  • •

    θ∞=up​s/DL\theta_{\infty}=u_{ps}/D_{L}: The asymptotic position of relativistic images.

  • •

    s=θ1−θ∞s=\theta_{1}-\theta_{\infty}: The angular separation between the first relativistic image and the others. This effectively measures the “thickness” between the first relativistic image and the others.

  • •

    rmagr_{\mathrm{mag}}: The relative flux ratio between the first image and the aggregate flux of all subsequent images (∑n=2∞μn\sum_{n=2}^{\infty}\mu_{n}). This quantifies the dominance of the outermost ring.

These are explicitly calculated asBozza_2002:

s≈θ∞​exp⁡(b¯−2​πa¯),rmag≈5​πa¯​ln⁡10.s\approx\theta_{\infty}\exp{\left(\frac{\bar{b}-2\pi}{\bar{a}}\right)},\qquad r_{\mathrm{mag}}\approx\frac{5\pi}{\bar{a}\ln{10}}. (24)

Beyond purely geometric parameters, the time delay between two relativistic images offers a powerful diagnostic tool for probing the strong-field regime. This time delay originates from the additional loops photons execute around the photon sphere before escaping toward the observer. In the context of SDL and assuming a near-perfect alignment (β≈0\beta\approx 0), the delay between the nn-th and mm-th order images can be expressed as Bozza:2003cp; Lu:2016gsf:

Δ​Tn,m≃2​π​(n−m)​up​s+2​Bp​s​up​sAp​s​c^​exp⁡(b¯2​a¯)​[exp⁡(−m​πa¯)−exp⁡(−n​πa¯)].\Delta T_{n,m}\simeq 2\pi(n-m)u_{ps}+2\sqrt{\frac{B_{ps}u_{ps}}{A_{ps}\hat{c}}}\exp\left(\frac{\bar{b}}{2\bar{a}}\right)\left[\exp\left(-\frac{m\pi}{\bar{a}}\right)-\exp\left(-\frac{n\pi}{\bar{a}}\right)\right]. (25)

Here, the primary contribution to the delay is governed by the impact parameter at the photon sphere, up​su_{ps}, while the finer corrections are determined by the SDL coefficients a¯\bar{a} and b¯\bar{b}, as well as the metric-dependent factor c^\hat{c} Bozza_2002 expressed as

c^=β^p​s​Ap​sCp​s3​(Cp​s′)22​(1−Ap​s)2.\hat{c}=\hat{\beta}_{ps}\sqrt{\frac{A_{ps}}{C_{ps}^{3}}}\frac{(C^{\prime}_{ps})^{2}}{2(1-A_{ps})^{2}}. (26)

In the following subsection, we apply this formalism to the supermassive black hole candidates M87* and Sgr A*.

III.2 Lensing observables of the supermassive black holes Sgr A* and M87*

Assuming the supermassive black holes Sgr A* and M87* being Bardeen-like, we estimate and compare their lensing observables with those of the Schwarzschild black hole. The estimated mass of M87* is (6.5±0.7)×109​M⊙(6.5\pm 0.7)\times 10^{9}M_{\odot} at a distance of 16.816.8 Mpc EventHorizonTelescope:2019pgp; EventHorizonTelescope:2019ggy. For Sgr A*, the estimated mass is 4.28±0.1±0.21×106​M⊙4.28^{\pm 0.21}_{\pm 0.1}\times 10^{6}M_{\odot} at a distance of 8.32±0.14±0.078.32^{\pm 0.07}_{\pm 0.14} kpc EventHorizonTelescope:2022apq; EventHorizonTelescope:2022wkp.

We calculated the angular positions of the first and second relativistic images for Sgr A* and M87* using Eq. (22). Our numerical results shows that relative flux ratio rmagr_{\mathrm{mag}} is a function of ℓ\ell that deviates from the Schwarzschild case.

According to our numerical results in Table 3 and Table 3, the angular separation ss increases with ℓ\ell and ranges from about 33 to 65 nanoarcseconds (nas) for Sgr A* and from 25 to 49 nanoarcseconds (nas) for M87*. As shown in Fig. 5(a), as ℓ\ell increases, the separation between the first relativistic image and the others grows. This behavior follows the same pattern as observed in standard Bardeen black holes Eiroa:2010wm. In contrast, the relative flux ratio rmagr_{\mathrm{mag}} is smaller than that in the Schwarzschild limit and decreases as ℓ\ell increases, taking the same values for both Sgr A* and M87* as seen in Fig. 5(b), ranging from 6.82 to 5.91. This behavior also follows the same pattern as observed in standard Bardeen black holes Eiroa:2010wm. The θ∞\theta_{\infty} remains constant at 19.83​μ​as19.83\,\mu\text{as} for Sgr A* and 26.55​μ​as26.55\,\mu\text{as} for M87*, showing no deviation from the Schwarzschild prediction, but differs from the standard Bardeen prediction, where θ∞\theta_{\infty} typically decreases as ℓ\ell increases Eiroa:2010wm. While we primarily focus on the relative flux ratio rmagr_{\mathrm{mag}} to characterize the lensing signatures, the magnifications (μn\mu_{n}) and angular positions (θn\theta_{n}) of the individual images depend much on the source position β\beta. Tables 5 and 5 show the magnifications and image positions of the first two relativistic images for Sgr A* and M87*, respectively. Figure 7 depicts the total time delay Δ​T2,1\Delta T_{2,1} between the first and second relativistic images using the observational data for M87* and Sgr A*. For both supermassive black holes, the total time delay increases with ℓ\ell. For M87*, this increment is so small that it cannot be visually discerned on the primary scale. This increasing behavior and the deviation from the standard Schwarzschild prediction can be better observed in Fig. 7(a) and Fig. 7(b), respectively, where these deviations are from the second term of Eq. (25).

The angular diameter of the shadow of M87* is θsh=42±3​μ​as\theta_{\mathrm{sh}}=42\pm 3\,\mu\text{as} EventHorizonTelescope:2019dse; EventHorizonTelescope:2019pgp; EventHorizonTelescope:2019ggy, based on a mass M=(6.50±0.7)×109​M⊙M=(6.50\pm 0.7)\times 10^{9}M_{\odot} and distance DL=16.8D_{L}=16.8 Mpc. IV Conclusion and Discussion

In this work, we first investigated gravitational lensing in the weak-field regime. We found that the deflection angle increases with ℓ\ell, exhibiting a positive deviation. This contrasts the standard Bardeen black hole, which produces a negative deviation. To assess the observational viability of the Bardeen-like black hole, we compared our theoretical predictions with data from the Einstein ring (θE\theta_{E}) of the elliptical galaxy ESO 325-G004. In this calculation, the total lens mass was assumed to comprise a central Bardeen-like black hole, as well as the dark and visible matter of the galaxy. The predicted θE\theta_{E} for the Bardeen-like black hole falls within the 1​σ1\sigma observational uncertainty of 2.85±0.402.85\pm 0.40 arcsec.

Moving to the strong-field regime, we adopted the SDL approach. The SDL coefficient a¯\bar{a} was derived analytically, while b¯\bar{b} was computed numerically. This allowed us to calculate the strong-field deflection angle and key lensing observables, including the limiting angular position of the relativistic image sequence θ∞\theta_{\infty}, the angular separation ss between relativistic images, and the relative flux ratio rmagr_{\mathrm{mag}}. We showed that the strong-field deflection angle is larger than that in the Schwarzschild case and increases monotonically with ℓ\ell for a fixed impact parameter.

To explore the preliminary detectability of these strong-field features, we modeled the supermassive black hole candidates Sgr A∗ and M87∗ as Bardeen-like black holes and estimated their expected SDL observables. Notably, the Bardeen-like black hole possesses the exact same asymptotic angular position θ∞\theta_{\infty} as the Schwarzschild black hole. Based solely on θ∞\theta_{\infty}, however, future astronomical facilities achieving an angular resolution of roughly 100​nas100\,\mathrm{nas} (nanoarcseconds) or better could distinguish the Bardeen-like black hole from the standard Bardeen black hole.

Furthermore, if the observational resolution can reach ∼10​nas\sim 10\,\mathrm{nas}, the first relativistic image could in principle be resolved from the tightly packed inner photon ring. This would enable the direct measurement of the observables ss and rmagr_{\mathrm{mag}}. Measuring these parameters is crucial for helping constrain the Bardeen-like model and potentially differentiating it from the classical Schwarzschild solution. Because regular black holes can mathematically relate to vacuum solutions of generally covariant gravity theories Zhang:2025ccx, such high-resolution observations offer an exciting avenue to test fundamental alternative theories of gravity in the strong-field regime. Achieving such an extremely high resolution, however, is far beyond the capabilities of current technologies. With future high-precision observations, especially from the future ngEHT, these constraints could be further refined, potentially differentiating Bardeen-like black holes from Schwarzschild solutions. In fact, recent work Boos:2025nzc shows that the microlensing of regular black holes yields a higher magnification, which may present a more viable target for near-future astronomical observations.

In addition to the standard SDL observables θ∞\theta_{\infty}, ss, and rmagr_{\mathrm{mag}}, we also computed the signed magnifications and angular positions of the first two relativistic images, (μp,1,μp,2,μs,1,μs,2)(\mu_{p,1},\mu_{p,2},\mu_{s,1},\mu_{s,2}) and (θp,1,θp,2,θs,1,θs,2)(\theta_{p,1},\theta_{p,2},\theta_{s,1},\theta_{s,2}), together with the differential time delay Δ​T2,1\Delta T_{2,1} between the first two relativistic images. In this sense, the observables naturally separate into a set, {θ∞,s,rmag,θp,i,θs,i}\{\theta_{\infty},s,r_{\mathrm{mag}},\theta_{p,i},\theta_{s,i}\}, mainly limited by angular resolution and dynamic range, and a observable, Δ​T2,1\Delta T_{2,1}, which may admit a different interpretation if the source is a compact time-variable radio source. In the geometric-optics regime, the same strong-field trajectories that generate relativistic images also apply to radio emission. Therefore, if the source is a compact radio emitter, Δ​T2,1\Delta T_{2,1} could in principle be interpreted as the lag between two delayed signal copies, with relative amplitudes related by the corresponding image magnifications.

From this perspective, the quantities computed here may provide a useful phenomenological basis for future radio-timing studies in strong-gravity environments, including possible SKA-related applications. In the present Bardeen-like case, however, the deviation of Δ​T2,1\Delta T_{2,1} from the Schwarzschild value is so small that its role should be regarded primarily as conceptual rather than as a practically accessible discriminator at the current stage. Nevertheless, astronomical observations of gravitational lensing in both the weak and strong-field regimes remain a potential future opportunity to search for and detect regular black holes.

Recent work Calza:2025mrt provides a method to construct non-singular black holes with spherical horizon topology and no Cauchy horizon. The metric can be written as
