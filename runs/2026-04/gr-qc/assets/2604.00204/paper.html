No-Go Theorem for Singularity Resolution

Zhen-Xiao Zhang1 zx.zhang@mail.nankai.edu.cn    Chen Lan2 stlanchen@126.com    Yan-Gang Miao1 Corresponding author:miaoyg@nankai.edu.cn 1School of Physics, Nankai University, 94 Weijin Road, Tianjin 300071, China 2Department of Physics, Yantai University, 30 Qingquan Road, Yantai 264005, China
Abstract

We prove a No-Go theorem for singularity resolution in gravitational collapse: within any analytic gravitational theory, including general relativity and all theories with polynomial actions, quantum corrections introduced solely as effective matter sources are insufficient to halt singularities. This rules out singularity resolution via effective energy density in a broad class of quantum gravity approaches, including asymptotic safety and noncommutative geometry, which inevitably yield finite-time singularities or geodesic incompleteness. Resolution strictly requires either non-analytic modifications to the gravitational action, or a vanishing effective energy density at high densities (as realized in loop quantum gravity’s Planck stars). The theorem is proved via an intrinsic f​(ℚ)f(\mathbb{Q}) gravity framework, extended universally to general relativity, f​(ℝ)f(\mathbb{R}), and f​(𝕋)f(\mathbb{T}) through the geometrical trinity—with regularity criteria and junction conditions grounded in non-metricity, free of standard GR tools.

I Introduction

The fate of matter under gravitational collapse is one of the deepest open questions in theoretical physics. General Relativity (GR) predicts that collapse inevitably produces spacetime singularities [12], regions where predictability breaks down and classical gravity fails. The leading resolution strategy, shared by virtually all quantum gravity (QG) approaches, is to introduce quantum corrections as an effective energy density ϵeff\epsilon_{\text{eff}}, an equivalent matter source that suppresses collapse at high densities [1]. This underlies asymptotic safety [6], loop quantum gravity (LQG) [11, 22], and noncommutative geometry [10, 16, 9], as well as a broad class of regular black hole models [15]. Despite their diversity, these approaches share a common structure: they modify the matter sector while leaving the analytic structure of the gravitational action intact. By matching boundary conditions on the collapsing matter’s surface, these models enable matching to a static exterior geometry (often invoking generalizations of Birkhoff’s theorem [5, 18, 21]). However, existing efforts rely on phenomenological, model-specific analyses, requiring case-by-case verification of regularity. Whether the shared strategy of modifying the matter sector within an analytic gravitational theory can ever suffice for genuine singularity resolution has remained an open question.

We address this within a unified framework provided by the “geometrical trinity” of gravity. Replacing GR’s curved manifold with one where curvature vanishes but torsion is present yields the teleparallel equivalent of GR (TEGR); a manifold with vanishing curvature and torsion but non-metricity [3, 13] (Qα​μ​ν=∇αgμ​νQ_{\alpha\mu\nu}=\nabla_{\alpha}g_{\mu\nu}, where ∇\nabla denotes the general affine connection) yields the symmetric teleparallel equivalent of GR (STEGR). GR, TEGR, and STEGR are equivalent classical gravity descriptions whose extensions, f​(ℝ)f(\mathbb{R}), f​(𝕋)f(\mathbb{T}), and f​(ℚ)f(\mathbb{Q}) gravity [14, 24], offer complementary mathematical perspectives on collapse. We exploit f​(ℚ)f(\mathbb{Q}) gravity as a particularly clean arena: its flat-spacetime formulation eliminates curvature entirely, enabling a fully intrinsic collapse framework free of GR-derived tools. The No-Go theorem we prove, however, extends universally to GR, f​(ℝ)f(\mathbb{R}), and f​(𝕋)f(\mathbb{T}) via the geometrical trinity.

However, as extensions of GR, f​(𝕋)f(\mathbb{T}) and f​(ℚ)f(\mathbb{Q}) lack many GR-established methods, especially in nascent f​(ℚ)f(\mathbb{Q}) gravity. Studies often borrow GR concepts (e.g., Riemann and extrinsic curvatures via the Levi-Civita connection) as auxiliaries, despite f​(ℚ)f(\mathbb{Q})’s distinct non-metric-compatible connection. This introduces rigor issues, as discussing curvature in f​(ℚ)f(\mathbb{Q})’s flat spacetime is inappropriate.

The implications of our result are broad and model-independent. For asymptotic safety, our theorem implies that renormalization-group-improved black hole spacetimes [6, 5] cannot be rendered truly regular without modifying the polynomial structure of the gravitational action. For noncommutative geometry, the same conclusion applies. For regular black hole model-building, our result provides a no-go criterion that bypasses case-by-case analysis: any proposed regular black hole sourced by an effective QG density within an analytic theory must, upon closer inspection, harbor either a scalar singularity or geodesic incompleteness. The theorem thus transforms singularity resolution from a model-dependent question into a matter of algebraic structure.

In this Letter, leveraging f​(ℚ)f(\mathbb{Q})’s advantages for collapse analysis, we establish a purely intrinsic f​(ℚ)f(\mathbb{Q})-based framework, including regularity criteria and junction conditions grounded in non-metricity. These recover GR results when f​(ℚ)→ℚf(\mathbb{Q})\to\mathbb{Q}. Using this, we discuss QG contributions via ϵeff\epsilon_{\text{eff}} to dust collapse, encompassing most QG theories. We prove a No-Go theorem: in analytic theories with polynomial actions (including GR), ϵeff\epsilon_{\text{eff}} modifications alone fail to resolve singularities, leading to finite-time singularities or geodesic incompleteness in asymptotic collapse. Resolution requires non-analytic, non-polynomial action changes or solutions with vanishing effective energy density (as in LQG). We illustrate with a quadratic f​(ℚ)f(\mathbb{Q}) model under asymptotic safety. Via the geometrical trinity, our results extend to GR, f​(ℝ)f(\mathbb{R}), and f​(𝕋)f(\mathbb{T}). This suggests many QG approaches, like asymptotic safety and noncommutative geometry, cannot eliminate singularities without fundamental ultraviolet restructuring.

II.1 Intrinsic regularity criteria

Assessing black hole regularity in f​(ℚ)f(\mathbb{Q}) gravity poses a non-trivial challenge, as the theory’s non-Riemannian structure—with vanishing curvature and torsion—renders direct application of GR criteria ineffective. In GR, regularity requires finite curvature invariants (e.g., Kretschmann scalar K=Rμ​ν​ρ​σ​Rμ​ν​ρ​σK=R_{\mu\nu\rho\sigma}R^{\mu\nu\rho\sigma}) and geodesic completeness. However, in f​(ℚ)f(\mathbb{Q}), the Riemann tensor is identically zero, making such invariants trivially vanishing and unsuitable for detecting pathologies like divergences in tidal forces or metric deformations. The physical motivation is transparent: these invariants measure tidal distortion rates and metric deformation, playing the role of curvature invariants in GR. As we prove in the SM, geometric finiteness of ℚ\mathbb{Q} and ∇Q\nabla Q invariants implies finiteness of the Kretschmann scalar, recovering the standard GR criterion in the appropriate limit.

To address this, we propose an intrinsic framework grounded in f​(ℚ)f(\mathbb{Q})’s fundamental variable: the non-metricity tensor Qα​μ​νQ_{\alpha\mu\nu} and its covariant derivatives. A black hole solution is intrinsically regular if both conditions hold:

The regular black hole of Ref. [5] is therefore regular only from the perspective of external observers; the interior singularity is masked, not resolved.

V Conclusion

In this Letter, we establish a rigorous, intrinsic framework in f​(ℚ)f(\mathbb{Q}) gravity for analyzing gravitational collapse, avoiding GR-derived tools by defining regularity criteria and junction conditions based on non-metricity and momentum conservation. This self-consistent approach recovers GR limits and enables generalized studies of singularity resolution.

Applying it, we derive a No-Go theorem: in analytic gravitational theories with polynomial actions (including GR), quantum corrections via effective energy density ϵeff\epsilon_{\text{eff}} alone fail to resolve singularities. The dynamical response vanishes at zero expansion (𝒥​(0)=0\mathcal{J}(0)=0), obstructing bounces and leading to finite-time singularities or asymptotic collapse with divergent invariants and geodesic incompleteness—persisting even if masked for external observers.

Nonsingular resolution requires a non-singular bounce [4], achievable only through (i) non-analytic, non-polynomial action modifications at ultraviolet scales, or (ii) ϵeff\epsilon_{\text{eff}} vanishing at high densities (as in LQG’s Planck stars). These constraints guide future regular black hole constructions, with implications for evaporating black holes and potential observables like modified Hawking radiation.

Our results call to mind a known limitation in constructing regular black holes via Non-Linear Electrodynamics (NLED). While actions of regular metrics can be mathematically obtained by reverse-engineered NLED Lagrangians (the Ayon-Beato and Garcia approach [2]), physically realistic models like the Euler-Heisenberg theory generally fail to resolve singularities [20]. Similarly, we find here that QG theories providing an effective matter source do not automatically guarantee regularity; in fact, under analytic gravity, they inevitably lead to singularities. This reinforces the conclusion that resolving the singularity problem requires more than just a modified source. It requires a fundamental change in the gravitational sector itself [7].

In terms of future prospects, while our current analysis establishes the fundamental dynamical constraints under spherical symmetry, realistic astrophysical collapse inevitably involves angular momentum. Extending this intrinsic framework to axially symmetric spacetimes is crucial for confronting theoretical predictions with upcoming observational data, such as black hole shadows [23] and gravitational wave ringdowns [8]. However, this extension presents significant theoretical challenges. In particular, formulating the intrinsic junction conditions for rotating collapse in f​(ℚ)f(\mathbb{Q}) gravity is considerably more intricate than in the static case. The matching of the generalized momentum flux across a rotating boundary involves complex non-diagonal components of the non-metricity tensor, requiring a rigorous generalization of the matching procedure developed here. Addressing these complexities constitutes a primary direction for future research.

Via the geometrical trinity, our results extend universally to f​(ℝ)f(\mathbb{R}), f​(𝕋)f(\mathbb{T}), and GR, mandating a paradigm shift in QG phenomenology toward fundamental non-analytic frameworks or vanishing ϵeff\epsilon_{\text{eff}} models. Investigating non-polynomial actions offers promising directions. More broadly, our No-Go theorem reframes the singularity problem as a question about the analytic structure of gravity rather than the choice of matter source. The widespread practice of constructing regular black holes by sourcing GR with a carefully chosen ϵeff\epsilon_{\rm eff}, whether from QG, nonlinear electrodynamics, or phenomenological models, cannot produce genuine regularity within analytic theories. The singular core is at best displaced to infinite coordinate time, remaining present as geodesic incompleteness. True resolution demands a departure from perturbative, analytic gravity: precisely the kind of non-perturbative ultraviolet completion that approaches such as LQG aim to provide. Our results thus supply a sharp theoretical criterion for distinguishing genuine from apparent singularity resolution, with direct relevance to black hole phenomenology in the era of gravitational-wave and Event Horizon Telescope observations.
