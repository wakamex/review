Improved Constraints on Non-Kerr Deviations from Binary Black Hole Inspirals Using GWTC-4 Data

Debtroy Das ddebtroy22@m.fudan.edu.cn Center for Astronomy and Astrophysics, Department of Physics, Fudan University, Shanghai 200438, China    Swarnim Shashank swarnim.shashank@astro.uni-tuebingen.de Institut für Astronomie und Astrophysik, Eberhard-Karls Universität Tübingen, D-72076 Tübingen, Germany College of Fellows, Eberhard-Karls Universität Tübingen, D-72070 Tübingen, Germany    Cosimo Bambi bambi@fudan.edu.cn Center for Astronomy and Astrophysics, Department of Physics, Fudan University, Shanghai 200438, China School of Natural Sciences and Humanities, New Uzbekistan University, Tashkent 100007, Uzbekistan
Abstract

Gravitational wave (GW) observations of binary black hole (BBH) mergers provide a unique opportunity to probe the nature of spacetime in the strong-field and dynamical regime. We present updated constraints on deviations from the Kerr metric using BBH inspirals from the fourth Gravitational-Wave Transient Catalog (GWTC-4). Building on our previous GWTC-3 analysis, we employ a theory-agnostic framework in which non-Kerr effects of the Johannsen metric are incorporated as parametrized corrections to the GW phase within the post-Newtonian framework. We perform Bayesian parameter estimation on a selected subset of GWTC-4 events to constrain the deformation parameters α13\alpha_{13} and ϵ3\epsilon_{3}, yielding significantly tighter bounds compared to earlier results. When varied individually, the deformation parameters are found to be consistent with zero, providing no evidence for departures from the Kerr geometry. Our results reinforce the validity of General Relativity, particularly the Kerr hypothesis, and highlight the progress enabled by GWTC-4.

I Introduction

The direct detection of gravitational waves (GWs) by the LIGO–Virgo–KAGRA (LVK) collaboration Abbott et al. (2016a, b, a) has opened a unique window into the dynamics of compact and energetic systems, particularly in regions and environments inaccessible to electromagnetic probes. Binary black hole (BBH) mergers, in particular, provide a pristine laboratory to test the predictions of General Relativity (GR) in highly dynamical and nonlinear regimes Abbott et al. (2019, 2021, 2025); Abac et al. (2026a, b, c); Krishnendu and Ohme (2021); Abbott et al. (2016b); Li et al. (2012); Maggio et al. (2023); Ghosh et al. (2018); Carson and Yagi (2020a); Agathos et al. (2014); Krishnendu et al. (2017); Arun et al. (2006); Das et al. (2025). Within GR, astrophysical black holes are expected to be described by the Kerr metric, fully characterized by their mass and spin. Any statistically significant deviation from this description would indicate physics beyond GR or a breakdown of the Kerr hypothesis.

GR tests can be broadly categorized into two wide classes: theory-specific and theory-agnostic approaches. In the former, one compares GR directly with alternative theories, while in the latter, one looks for generic deviations from GR predictions without committing to a specific theory or model Bambi (2017). Parametrized frameworks Abac et al. (2026b); Maggio et al. (2023); Carson and Yagi (2020a); Konoplya et al. (2016); Ghasemi-Nodehi and Bambi (2016), such as the parametrized post-Einsteinian (ppE) formalism Shashank and Bambi (2022); Cárdenas-Avendaño et al. (2020); Yunes et al. (2016); Yunes and Pretorius (2009); Nissanke and Blanchet (2005), provide a powerful tool to search for such deviations in a model-independent way.

In our previous work Das et al. (2024), we adopted a theory-agnostic approach to test the Kerr hypothesis by introducing deviations in spacetime, considering the Johannsen metric Johannsen (2013). By incorporating leading-order corrections to the GW phase up to 4 post-Newtonian (PN) order, we constrained the deformation parameters using BBH events from the third Gravitational-Wave Transient Catalog (GWTC-3) Abbott et al. (2023). Our results showed that, while individual deformation parameters can be constrained when varied independently, strong degeneracies prevent simultaneous constraints.

Since then, the LVK collaboration has released the fourth Gravitational-Wave Transient Catalog (GWTC-4) Abac et al. (2025a), which includes 128 compact binary merger events with improved detector sensitivity and relatively higher signal-to-noise ratios (SNRs). This provides an opportunity to revisit and refine previous constraints on deviations from the Kerr metric.

In this work, we perform a follow-up analysis using BBH inspiral data from GWTC-4. The mathematical framework and waveform modeling remain identical to our earlier study. We summarize the main idea in Section II. The key difference lies in the expanded dataset, which enables tighter constraints on the deformation parameters and improved statistical robustness. Our goal is to assess how the increased data volume and quality impact the bounds on non-Kerr deviations and to evaluate the consistency of GW observations with the Kerr hypothesis. The data analysis and results are presented in Section III, followed by concluding remarks in Scetion IV

II Mathematical Framework

II.1 Parametrized deviations from the Kerr metric and Waveform modifications

We model potential deviations from the Kerr spacetime using the Johannsen metric Johannsen (2013). This metric is a phenomenological extension that introduces deformation parameters while preserving key properties such as stationarity, axisymmetry, asymptotic flatness, and particle motion still possesses a Carter-like constant. In the limit where all deformation parameters vanish, the metric reduces to the Kerr solution.

Following our previous work Das et al. (2024), we focus on the deformation parameters α13\alpha_{13} and ϵ3\epsilon_{3}. The presence of these non-Kerr deviations modifies the dynamics of BBH inspirals. Consequently, the phase evolution of the emitted GWs is also modified. We compute these corrections within the parametrized ppE framework, where deviations enter as additional terms in the GW phase Shashank and Bambi (2022); Cárdenas-Avendaño et al. (2020); Nissanke and Blanchet (2005). The total GW phase can be written schematically as a sum of the GR contribution and a non-Kerr correction term

ΨGW​(f)=ΨGWGR​(f)+ΨGWNK​(f).\displaystyle\Psi_{\mathrm{GW}}(f)=\Psi_{\mathrm{GW}}^{\mathrm{GR}}(f)+\Psi_{\mathrm{GW}}^{\rm NK}(f)\,. (1)

The non-Kerr contribution, labeled by NK\rm NK superscript, is derived by modifying the orbital dynamics through corrections to the binding energy and orbital frequency, obtained from the deformed metric. Assuming that dissipative effects remain unchanged from GR at leading order, the corrections enter primarily through the conservative sector. As in our earlier study Das et al. (2024), we retain terms up to 4PN order in the phase expansion following Ref. Carson and Yagi (2020b). This ensures consistency with the waveform approximant used and allows us to capture the dominant effects of the deformation parameters on the inspiral signal. The additional terms in GW phase due to non-vanishing α13\alpha_{13} and ϵ3\epsilon_{3} are

−10​α13​κ​u​log⁡(u)3​η8/5.\displaystyle-\frac{10\alpha_{13}\kappa u\log(u)}{3\eta^{8/5}}\,.

+5​ϵ3​κ​u​log⁡(u)3​η8/5.\displaystyle+\frac{5\epsilon_{3}\kappa u\log(u)}{3\eta^{8/5}}\,.

Here, η=μ/m\eta=\mu/m is the symmetric mass ratio (μ\mu is the reduced mass and mm is the total mass), u=η3/5​π​m​fu=\eta^{3/5}\pi mf, and ff is the Fourier frequency. We incorporate these phase corrections into a frequency-domain waveform model based on the IMRPhenom family Husa et al. (2016); Khan et al. (2016); Hannam et al. (2014); Bohé et al. (2016). As a baseline model, we adopt the IMRPhenomXPHM Pratten et al. (2021) waveform model, ensuring compatibility with standard GW data analysis pipelines.

II.2 Bayesian inference framework

We estimate the source parameters and deformation parameters using Bayesian inference Box and Tiao (2011); Veitch and Vecchio (2010); Romero-Shaw et al. (2020). Given GW data dd and model parameters θ\theta, the posterior distribution is given by

p​(𝜽∣d)∝ℒ​(d∣𝜽)​πPE​(𝜽).\displaystyle p(\bm{\theta}\mid d)\propto\mathcal{L}(d\mid\bm{\theta})\pi_{\rm PE}(\bm{\theta}). (4)

The term πPE​(𝜽)\pi_{\rm PE}(\bm{\theta}) is the prior distributions and ℒ​(d∣𝜽)\mathcal{L}(d\mid\bm{\theta}) is the likelihood. The parameters θ\theta include both the standard BBH parameters (masses, spins, distance, etc.) and the non-Kerr deformation parameters. ℒ​(d∣𝜽)\mathcal{L}(d\mid\bm{\theta}) determines the probability of GW data dd given a waveform model characterized by 𝜽\bm{\theta} Veitch and Vecchio (2010). The likelihood function takes the form of

ℒ​(d∣𝜽)∝exp⁡(−∑k2​|dk−hk​(𝜽)|2T​Sk).\displaystyle\mathcal{L}(d\mid\bm{\theta})\propto\exp\left(-\sum_{k}\frac{2\left|d_{k}-h_{k}(\bm{\theta})\right|^{2}}{TS_{k}}\right). (5)

where h​(𝜽)h(\bm{\theta}) denotes the gravitational waveform for the BBH signal with parameters 𝜽\bm{\theta}. TT is the duration of the data segment being analyzed and SkS_{k} is the noise power spectral density (PSD) characterizing the sensitivity of the detector. kk signifies the frequency dependence of the data, the waveform, and the PSD.

III Data Analysis and Results

For this study, we consider BBH events from the GWTC-4 catalog. To ensure consistency with our previous analysis, we select only those events in which the inspiral part of the signal is well captured by the detectors. Hence, we apply selection criteria similar to those used in our earlier work Das et al. (2024). These include cuts on total mass in the detector frame (M<80M<80 M⊙M_{\odot}), network SNR (>10>10), and spin-precession indicators (χp\chi_{\mathrm{p}} estimates) Abac et al. (2025a). Only events satisfying these criteria are included in the analysis. The final set consists of 11 BBHs from new events in GWTC-4.

We use real data strains made publicly available through the GW Open Science Center Abac et al. (2025b) from BBH merger events observed by the LIGO–Hanford (H1) and LIGO–Livingston (L1) detectors Aasi et al. (2015) (except for GW240109_050431 and GW231231_154016 which are observed by only LIGO–Hanford). The data strain used are obtained from channels: H1:GDS-CALIB_STRAIN_CLEAN_AR and L1:GDS-CALIB_STRAIN_CLEAN_AR. The attribute CLEAN in H1 and L1 indicates use of noise subtraction Vajente et al. (2020); Davis et al. (2021). AR stands for Analysis Ready and indicates that these channels contain only data for times that are ready for analysis. Bayesian parameter estimation was performed using Bilby Ashton et al. (2019) with the Dynesty nested-sampling algorithm Speagle (2020). We adopt prior distributions consistent with those used in the LVK analyses for BBH parameters Ashton et al. (2019); Romero-Shaw et al. (2020), while using uniform priors for the deformation parameters within the interval [−5,5][-5,5], which lies in a physically motivated range.

For each selected event, we perform two independent analyses varying one deformation parameter while fixing the other to zero. We repeat the procedure for the second parameter. In all cases where a single deformation parameter is allowed to vary, the posterior distributions remain consistent with the GR prediction (i.e., zero deformation) within the statistical uncertainties. This reinforces the conclusion that current GW observations do not provide evidence for deviations from the Kerr metric. The results from the GWTC-4 analysis show a clear improvement over the constraints obtained from GWTC-3 presented in Ref. Das et al. (2024). Tighter posterior distributions for the deformation parameters are obtained from analysis of events with higher SNRs.

Allowing both parameters to vary simultaneously results in neither being meaningfully constrained, as we observed in our previous study, so we do not display them in the paper. We again observe strong degeneracies between them. This prevents meaningful independent constraints on both parameters at the same time. The correlation structure remains largely unchanged.

Overall, the GWTC-4 data significantly improve the precision of the constraints and do not qualitatively alter the conclusions regarding the Kerr hypothesis. The summary of the constraints is displayed in Fig. 1. Tab. 1 shows the numerical values of the median of these posterior distributions along with the 99% intervals. We note that that there are four events (GW230628_231200, GW230904_051013, GW231108_125142, and GW240109_050431) that can provide only weak constraints on ϵ3\epsilon_{3} (their 99% intervals exceed our range of ϵ3\epsilon_{3}) and therefore these results are omitted in Fig. 1 and Tab. 1.

IV Concluding Remarks

In this work, we have extended our previous analysis of non-Kerr deviations in BBH inspirals to include data from the GWTC-4 catalog. Using the same theoretical framework and similar waveform modeling, we have assessed the impact of the expanded dataset on constraints of deformation parameters in a parametrized metric approach. Our results show that including GWTC-4 events yields tighter constraints on individual deformation parameters. The results remain consistent with the predictions of GR, with no statistically significant evidence of deviations from the Kerr metric.

Fig. 2 compares new and old GW constraints on α13\alpha_{13} with electromagnetic constraints Bambi (2017) and updates Fig. 5.13 in Ref. Bambi (2024). Every error bar shows the 3-σ\sigma constraint inferred from the source/event indicated by the label. We use different colors for the constraints inferred with different techniques. The error bars in green show the most precise and accurate measurements of α13\alpha_{13} from stellar-mass black holes with X-ray reflection spectroscopy Tripathi et al. (2021a); Zhang et al. (2019). The error bar in magenta is the measurements of α13\alpha_{13} from the stellar-mass black hole in LMC X-1 with the continuum-fitting method Tripathi et al. (2020). The error bars in blue are the measurements of α13\alpha_{13} from the stellar-mass black holes in GX 339, GRS 1915, and GRS 1716 obtained by combining X-ray reflection spectroscopy and the continuum-fitting method Tripathi et al. (2021b, 2022); Zhang et al. (2022). The error bars in red are the constraints from GWs: GW170608 is the event providing the most stringent constraint on α13\alpha_{13} in GWTC-3 Shashank and Bambi (2022) and GW230627 is the event providing the most stringent constraint on α13\alpha_{13} in GWTC-4 (and derived in the present work). The error bar in cyan shows the most precise and accurate measurement of α13\alpha_{13} from supermassive black holes with X-ray reflection spectroscopy Tripathi et al. (2019). The error bars in gray, which exceed the range of α13\alpha_{13} in our plot, are the constraints on α13\alpha_{13} of M87∗ and SgrA∗ from the Event Horizon Telescope Psaltis et al. (2020); Akiyama et al. (2022). While X-ray tests were able to provide the most stringent constraints on α13\alpha_{13} before GWTC-4, GW tests have now reached the same constraining power and we can expect they will provide more stringent constraints on α13\alpha_{13} than X-ray data in the next observing runs.

Our analysis strengthens the evidence that astrophysical black holes observed through GWs are consistent with the Kerr solution. Future GW observations, including the second and third parts of the fourth observing run, O4b and O4c Di Cesare (2025); Bersanetti and Was (2026), upcoming observing runs Abbott et al. (2016) and next-generation detectors Danzmann (2003); Li et al. (2025); Ziren et al. (2020); Punturo et al. (2010); Reitze et al. (2019), are expected to further improve the sensitivity to deviations from GR. Combining multiple events under the assumption of universal deformation parameters, or exploring alternative parametrizations, may provide a pathway to stronger constraints. The challenges and opportunities for future tests of gravity in the strong-field regime remain deeply intertwined, as each improvement in observational precision sharpens both our ability to confirm GR and our sensitivity to even the most subtle imprints of new physics.
