On the equivalence of the integrability obstructions for transitive Lie algebroids

Paolo Antonini, Alessio Giannotta paolo.antonini@unisalento.it, alessio.giannotta@unisalento.it
Abstract.

The integrability problem for transitive Lie algebroids can be looked at from different perspectives, revealing an interplay between cohomological methods and homotopical constructions. Mackenzie [14] introduced a cohomological obstruction defined via sheaf-theoretic methods. On the other hand, Crainic and Fernandes [6] used a path space approach and characterized integrability in terms of the monodromy. Recently, Meinrenken [18] formulated the monodromy in terms of a clutching construction.

We show that all of these agree. In particular, we identify the monodromy map with the Mackenzie obstruction class through the natural pairing between cohomology and homotopy.

1. Introduction

Let A⇒MA\Rightarrow M be a transitive algebroid over a (simply connected) manifold MM. The integrability problem for AA can be approached from two complementary perspectives: a cohomological one, given by the obstruction class eA∈Hˇ2​(M;Z​G~)e^{A}\in\check{H}^{2}(M;Z\widetilde{G}) introduced by Mackenzie [14], and an homotopical one, based on the monodromy map ∂A:π2​(M)→Z​G~\partial^{A}:\pi_{2}(M)\to Z\widetilde{G} constructed by Crainic and Fernandes [6].

In both cases, G~\widetilde{G} is the simply connected Lie group integrating the adjoint Lie algebra at a fixed reference point m∈Mm\in M and Z​G~Z\widetilde{G} is its center.

In this note we identify these two obstructions via the natural pairing between cohomology and homotopy. In doing so, we relate each of them to the description of the monodromy map given by Meinrenken [18] via a clutching construction.

We briefly recall the three approaches referring to the already existing literature for the details.

Mackenzie [14] constructed a class eA∈Hˇ2​(M;Z​G~)e^{A}\in\check{H}^{2}(M;Z\widetilde{G}) in the Čech cohomology of the base with coefficients in Z​G~Z\widetilde{G}. This can be viewed as a non abelian generalization of the Chern class of a U​(1)U(1)-bundle and completely controls integrability: the algebroid AA is integrable if and only if eAe^{A} lies in the image of a discrete subgroup of Z​G~Z\widetilde{G}. On the other hand Crainic and Fernandes [6] solved the integrability problem for any Lie algebroid. In the transitive, integrability is encoded by the image of a monodromy map, a group morphism ∂A:π2​(M)→Z​G~\partial^{A}:\pi_{2}(M)\to Z\widetilde{G}. The algebroid is integrable if and only if the image of ∂A\partial^{A} is discrete.

Mackenzie’s methods are of local to global type. First he proves that transitive algebroids are locally trivial, then constructs a class preventing the globalization of the local integrations. Crainic and Fernandes methods are purely global, they use a path space approach inspired by the Poisson sigma model [4, 20] and Sullivan’s rational homotopy theory. The monodromy map is related to the failure of the smoothness of the Weinstein groupoid of AA, a topological groupoid, which in a sense, already integrates AA. Finally Meinrenken has recently given a description of the monodromy map generalizing the clutching construction for principal bundles with connection [18].

Before stating the result identifying all these obstructions we look at the example of the transitive algebroid appearing in the geometric prequantization problem [7, 5, 1].

Let (M,ω)(M,\omega) be a symplectic manifold. Consider the Lie algebroid AωA_{\omega} given by the vector bundle Aω=T​M×ℝ=T​M⊕(M×ℝ)A_{\omega}=TM\times\mathbb{R}=TM\oplus(M\times\mathbb{R}), the projection onto T​MTM as anchor map, and Lie bracket on sections:

[X⊕ξ,Y⊕η]=[X,Y]⊕X​(η)−Y​(ξ)+ω​(X,Y).[X\oplus\xi,Y\oplus\eta]=[X,Y]\oplus X(\eta)-Y(\xi)+\omega(X,Y).

This example shows how the integrability problem, for algebroids coming from symplectic manifolds, is essentially equivalent to the prequantization problem in the approach of Weil [15, Theorem 8.1.3].

We can now state our result.

Theorem 1.1.

Let A⇒MA\Rightarrow M be a transitive Lie algebroid. Fix a point m∈Mm\in M and let 𝔤=Lm\mathfrak{g}=L_{m} be the adjoint Lie algebra at mm with simply connected associated Lie group G~\widetilde{G}. The Crainic–Fernandes monodromy map ∂A:π2​(M,m)→Z​G~\partial^{\,A}:\pi_{2}(M,m)\to Z\widetilde{G} and the Meinrenken map at mm agree. The Mackenzie class eA∈Hˇ2​(M;Z​G~)e^{A}\in\check{H}^{2}(M;Z\widetilde{G}) and the monodromy map are related via the natural isomorphism 𝒞:Hˇ2​(M;Z​G~)→H2​(M;Z​G~)\mathscr{C}:\check{H}^{2}(M;Z\widetilde{G})\to H^{2}(M;Z\widetilde{G}) and the natural pairing between singular cohomology and homotopy by the identity:

⟨𝒞​(eA),⋅⟩=−∂A.\langle\mathscr{C}(e^{A}),\,\cdot\,\rangle=-\partial^{A}.

The minus sign is only a matter of conventions, we have decided to keep the original choices in [14, 6] to help the direct comparison.

The proof of Theorem 1.1 proceeds by reducing the problem to the case of the sphere; both the monodromy map and the Mackenzie class are natural (with respect to pullbacks of Lie algebroids). This allows us to deduce the general case by pulling back along maps u:S2→Mu\colon S^{2}\to M. On the other hand, on the sphere, we identify the pairing with the Mackenzie class with the Meinrenken’s version of the monodromy map by an explicit computation, then we identify the Meinrenken and Crainic–Fernandes monodromy maps. In both cases the main instrument is the direct description of the group of gauge transformations of a trivial algebroid in terms of the monodromy of the associated Maurer–Cartan forms [18].

Finally we note that it would be of interest to study the role of integrability obstructions in the globalization problem of the local Lie groupoid integrating a Lie algebroid [3, 8, 11].

2. Background on groupoids and algebroids

In this section we recall basic informations about Lie groupoids and (transitive) algebroids. Trying to be quite concise here, we refer to [14, 17, 18] for the details.

Lie groupoids

Let MM be a manifold. A Lie groupoid 𝒢\mathcal{G} over MM, denoted by 𝒢⇉M\mathcal{G}\rightrightarrows M, is a (possibly non-Hausdorff) manifold of arrows endowed with two surjective submersions s,r:𝒢→Ms,r\colon\mathcal{G}\to M called the source and range maps, an embedding 1:M→𝒢1\colon M\to\mathcal{G} called unit map and a multiplication defined on the submanifold of composable arrows 𝒢(2)={(g2,g1)∈𝒢2∣r​(g1)=s​(g2)}\mathcal{G}^{(2)}=\{(g_{2},g_{1})\in\mathcal{G}^{2}\mid\,r(g_{1})=s(g_{2})\}. These structure maps satisfy:

  • •

    associativity: (g3​g2)​g1=g3​(g2​g1)(g_{3}g_{2})g_{1}=g_{3}(g_{2}g_{1});

  • •

    units: 1r​(g)​g=g​1s​(g)=g1_{r(g)}g=g1_{s(g)}=g;

  • •

    inverse: for all g∈𝒢g\in\mathcal{G} there exists an arrow g−1∈𝒢g^{-1}\in\mathcal{G} such that g​g−1=1r​(g)gg^{-1}=1_{r(g)} and g−1​g=1s​(g)g^{-1}g=1_{s(g)}.

Fix a point m∈Mm\in M. We denote by 𝒢m=s−1​(m)\mathcal{G}_{m}=s^{-1}(m) the ss-fibre of mm, and by 𝒢mm=s−1​(m)∩r−1​(m)\mathcal{G}_{m}^{m}=s^{-1}(m)\cap r^{-1}(m) the isotropy Lie group of 𝒢\mathcal{G} at mm. We say that 𝒢\mathcal{G} is ss-(simply) connected if, for every m∈Mm\in M, the ss-fiber 𝒢m\mathcal{G}_{m} is (simply) connected.

Typical examples of Lie groupoids include Lie groups viewed as Lie groupoids over a point, the pair groupoid M×MM\times M over the manifold MM, the action groupoid associated with a Lie group action on MM and the holonomy groupoid of a foliation on MM.

By identifying the base manifold with the units submanifold, a morphism between the groupoids 𝒢⇉M\mathcal{G}\rightrightarrows M and ℋ⇉N\mathcal{H}\rightrightarrows N is a smooth map F:𝒢→ℋF\colon\mathcal{G}\to\mathcal{H} such that for all (g2,g1)∈𝒢(2)(g_{2},g_{1})\in\mathcal{G}^{(2)} we have (F​(g2),F​(g1))∈ℋ(2)(F(g_{2}),F(g_{1}))\in\mathcal{H}^{(2)} and F​(g2​g1)=F​(g2)​F​(g1)F(g_{2}g_{1})=F(g_{2})F(g_{1}) .

As in the case of Lie groups, Lie groupoids also admit an associated infinitesimal object. Let 𝒢\mathcal{G} be a Lie groupoid over MM. The Lie algebroid of 𝒢\mathcal{G} is the vector bundle A​(𝒢):=ker⁡(d​s)|1MA(\mathcal{G}):=\left.\operatorname{ker}(ds)\right|_{1_{M}} over MM, given by the restriction of ker⁡(d​s)\operatorname{ker}(ds) on the units together with a bundle map ♯=d​r:A​(𝒢)→T​M\sharp=dr\colon A(\mathcal{G})\to TM, called the anchor, and the Lie algebra bracket [⋅,⋅][\cdot,\cdot] on Γ​(A​(𝒢))\Gamma(A(\mathcal{G})) induced from the Lie bracket on Γ​(T​𝒢)\Gamma(T\mathcal{G}).

Algebroids

Generalizing the algebroid of a Lie groupoid, an algebroid AA over MM, denoted with A⇒MA\Rightarrow M is given by a vector bundle A→MA\to M with a Lie algebra structure on the sections of AA and a vector bundle map ♯:A→T​M\sharp:A\to TM, the anchor, such that

Products, pullbacks, morphisms

The category of Lie algebroids admits products and morphisms. The example 2.1 corresponds to the product of a Lie algebra 𝔤⇒∙\mathfrak{g}\Rightarrow\bullet with T​MTM which serves as a local model for any transitive algebroid. We will refer to it as a trivial Lie algebroid.

Morphisms and pullbacks of algebroids require some care to be defined. Morphisms of algebroids over the same base are defined in a straightforward way as vector bundle maps preserving the anchor and inducing Lie algebra morphisms on sections. On the other hand, morphisms of algebroids over different bases will be described after introducing pullbacks.

The integrability problem

As we saw at the beginning of this section, every Lie groupoid has an associated Lie algebroid. This defines the Lie functor 𝒢↦A​(𝒢)\mathcal{G}\mapsto A(\mathcal{G}) from the category of Lie groupoids to the category of Lie algebroids [14, Proposition 3.5.10]. A Lie algebroid AA over MM is integrable if there exists a Lie groupoid 𝒢\mathcal{G} such that A=A​(𝒢)A=A(\mathcal{G}) up to isomorphism. When integrable, a Lie algebroid is always integrated to an ss-simply connected Lie groupoid [19, Proposition 3.3], which is unique up to isomorphism, as follows by the Lie II theorem for Lie algebroids.

Theorem 2.2.

[15, Theorem A.1] Let 𝒢\mathcal{G} and ℋ\mathcal{H} be Lie groupoids over MM and NN, respectively. Suppose that 𝒢\mathcal{G} is ss-simply connected and let Ψ:A​(𝒢)→A​(ℋ)\Psi\colon A(\mathcal{G})\to A(\mathcal{H}) be a Lie algebroid morphism. Then there exists a unique Lie groupoid morphism F:𝒢→ℋF\colon\mathcal{G}\to\mathcal{H} such that A​(F)=ΨA(F)=\Psi.

In the transitive case the integrability problem can be phrased in the world of principal bundles.

Recall that a Lie groupoid is transitive when it has one orbit i.e. for any point m∈Mm\in M the map r:𝒢m:=s−1​(m)→Mr:\mathcal{G}_{m}:=s^{-1}(m)\to M is surjective. Then 𝒢m\mathcal{G}_{m} is a (locally trivial) G=𝒢mmG=\mathcal{G}_{m}^{m}-principal bundle. The associated Atiyah sequence (see [14, 18]) defines a transitive algebroid on MM. On the other hand from a principal bundle, one has an associated transitive groupoid, its Gauge groupoid.

Start with an integrable transitive Lie algebroid A⇒MA\Rightarrow M; any integrating groupoid is transitive and any ss-fiber defines a principal bundle whose Atiyah sequence recovers AA.

However this correspondence involves the choice of basepoints and, as explained by Mackenzie [14, Chapter 1] it is prefeable not to identify the category of principal bundles with that of transitive Lie groupoids.

Gauge transformations

Let T​M×𝔤TM\times\mathfrak{g} be a trivial Lie algebroid. The group of gauge transformations Gauge⁡(T​M×𝔤)\operatorname{Gauge}(TM\times\mathfrak{g}) consists of all Lie algebroid automorphisms of T​M×𝔤TM\times\mathfrak{g} covering the identity on MM.

For the trivial Lie algebroid T​M×𝔤TM\times\mathfrak{g}, a connection Θ\Theta is given by

Θ​(X)=X⊕θ​(X),\Theta(X)=X\oplus\theta(X),

where θ∈Ω1​(M,𝔤)\theta\in\Omega^{1}(M,\mathfrak{g}) and a trivialization ψ:M×𝔤⟶L\psi\colon M\times\mathfrak{g}\longrightarrow L has the form

3.1. The cohomological obstruction

Let A⇒MA\Rightarrow M be a transitive algebroid and p:M~→Mp:\widetilde{M}\to M be the universal covering of MM. Mackenzie proved that AA is integrable if and only if the pullback algebroid p!​A⇒M~p^{!}A\Rightarrow\widetilde{M} is [14, Theorem 8.3.4]. Therefore the cohomological obstruction to the integrability is first defined for an algebroid on a simply connected base, then in the general case, one just defines it as the one of the lift to the universal cover [14, Definition 8.3.5].

The Mackenzie obstruction arises from reversing the differentiation process starting from a trivializing family of sections of a transitive groupoid 𝒢\mathcal{G} and recovering a system of transition datas for A=A​(𝒢)A=A(\mathcal{G}). So begin with a transitive algebroid A⇒MA\Rightarrow M over a simply connected base and fix a point m∈Mm\in M; let 𝔤=Lm\mathfrak{g}=L_{m} be the isotropy Lie algebra at mm. If AA comes from a Lie groupoid 𝒢\mathcal{G} then it necessarily admits an algebroid atlas with transition datas with ai​ja_{ij} and ξi​j\xi_{ij} satisfying the conditions (1)(1) and (2)(2) in the Proposition 3.4.

Recall that the adjoint group of 𝔤\mathfrak{g}, denoted with Ad⁡(𝔤)\operatorname{Ad}(\mathfrak{g}) is the unique connected Lie subgroup of Aut⁡(𝔤)\operatorname{Aut}(\mathfrak{g}) whose Lie algebra is the image of the adjoint representation 𝔤→𝔤​𝔩​(𝔤)\mathfrak{g}\to\mathfrak{gl}(\mathfrak{g}). For every connected Lie group GG integrating 𝔤\mathfrak{g} we have Ad⁡(𝔤)=Ad⁡(G)\operatorname{Ad}(\mathfrak{g})=\operatorname{Ad}(G), the image of the adjoint representation of GG. This group does not depends on the Lie group integrating 𝔤\mathfrak{g}; we can take the simply connected one G~\widetilde{G}.

Therefore, if the ss-fibers of 𝒢\mathcal{G} are connected, the isotropy group G=𝒢mmG=\mathcal{G}_{m}^{m} is connected too, and Ad⁡(G)=Ad⁡(𝔤)\operatorname{Ad}(G)=\operatorname{Ad}(\mathfrak{g}). The first compatibility condition in Proposition 3.4 implies that the cocycle takes values in Ad⁡(G)\operatorname{Ad}(G).

4. The Crainic–Fernandes monodromy map

In this section, we briefly recall the construction of the monodromy map introduced by Crainic and Fernandes [6]. Their solution of the integrability problem for Lie algebroids is based on the extension of the path-space approach for Lie algebras to the algebroid setting. The fundamental objects leading to the integrability obstructions are A−p​a​t​h​sA-paths and A−h​o​m​o​t​o​p​i​e​sA-homotopies.

Let 𝒢\mathcal{G} be a Lie groupoid. A 𝒢−p​a​t​h\mathcal{G}-path is a path g:I→𝒢g\colon I\to\mathcal{G} contained in a single ss-fiber of 𝒢\mathcal{G} and starting at the identity of that fiber. If AA is the Lie algebroid of 𝒢\mathcal{G}, the ss-simply connected Lie groupoid integrating AA, i.e. the monodromy groupoid of 𝒢\mathcal{G} is recovered as

𝒢~=P(𝒢)/∼,\widetilde{\mathcal{G}}=P(\mathcal{G})/\sim, (4.1)

where P​(𝒢)P(\mathcal{G}) is the space of 𝒢\mathcal{G}-paths endowed with the C2C^{2}-topology and ∼\sim is the equivalence relation given by C1C^{1}-homotopy with fixed endpoints. Groupoid multiplication is induced by the concatenation of paths. One of the most important points in the construction is the fact that 𝒢~\widetilde{\mathcal{G}} can be described entirely in terms of infinitesimal datas via AA-paths and AA-homotopies.

A-homotopies

Let A→MA\rightarrow M be a Lie algebroid. A C1C^{1}-path a:I→Aa\colon I\to A is an A−p​a​t​hA-path if

Appendix A Čech to singular cohomology

In this section we compare Čech and singular cohomology with coefficients in an abelian group GG. The topic is classical and can be found in many textbooks. Since the isomorphism is crucial in our treatment we include a somewhat detailed discussion using a bicomplex as in Weil’s proof of the de Rham Theorem [23] (cf. [12, 10, 2]).

We shall deal with smooth manifolds even if the results in this section hold in more generality (paracompact locally contractible spaces) perhaps with minor modifications in the proofs.
