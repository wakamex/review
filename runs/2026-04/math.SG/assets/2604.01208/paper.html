Topological algebra of symplectic geometry of symmetric powers

   Vivek Shende and Peng Zhou
Abstract

To a noncompact orientable surface with no closed boundary, we associate the sum of Fukaya categories of (Liouville sectors associated to) its symmetric powers. We construct sectorial covers with the combinatorics of the bar resolution to show this association extends to an open 2d topological field theory – without naming a Lagrangian, let alone a holomorphic disk. In particular, we recover results of Rouquier and Manion on extending Heegaard-Floer theory down to an interval.

1 Introduction

The ‘Heegaard-Floer’ homology of Oszvath and Szabo, now a fundamental theoretical and computational tool in low dimensional topology, is by definition constructed from the Floer homology of certain Lagrangians in symmetric products of surfaces [41, 40]. The theory was motivated by four dimensional gauge theory – and indeed, provides four dimensional invariants [42] – but is also luxuriously computable [47, 35]. Such computations have allowed at least the simplest ‘hat’ variant to be extended down to categories associated to two dimensional punctured surfaces [30], which in turn have been shown to have some gluing properties along one dimensional intervals [17, 16, 34].

Here we give a purely geometric argument that the categories associated to surfaces form a 2d open topological field theory. This result relies on no past or present holomorphic disk computations, is fully coherent, and independent of coefficients. The following category organizes our discussion:

Definition 1.1.

Let 𝒪\mathscr{O} be the topological category of oriented, possibly disconnected, finite type, noncompact surfaces-with-boundary, all of whose boundary components are noncompact (i.e. ℝ\mathbb{R} rather than S1S^{1}). Morphisms are proper inclusions for which the preimage of a nonempty boundary component is a union of nonempty boundary components. We give this category the symmetric monoidal structure of disjoint union.

Up to contractible choices, we could equivalently define the objects of 𝒪\mathscr{O} to be compact surfaces, of which each connected component has boundary, and each boundary component contains a possibly zero number of “space-like” intervals, the remaining boundary being “time-like”. The morphisms are inclusions carrying time-like boundary to time-like boundary, and each space-like boundary either to the interior, or onto a space-like boundary.

Observe that I=T∗​[−1,1]I=T^{*}[-1,1] is an algebra object in 𝒪\mathscr{O}: all structure maps are given by ‘putting intervals next to each other’, or in other words, taking cotangent bundle of the structure maps of the 1-dimensional little disks operad. Given any object Σ∈𝒪\Sigma\in\mathscr{O}, a choice of boundary of Σ\Sigma gives it structures of left and right II-module (depending on which end of T∗​[−1,1]T^{*}[-1,1] we consider gluing to it). Similarly, ∐nT∗​[−1,1]\coprod_{n}T^{*}[-1,1] carries an algebra structure, for which surfaces with nn chosen and ordered boundary components serve as modules.

We will be interested in symmetric monoidal functors ℱ:(𝒪,⊔)→(𝒞,⋆)\mathcal{F}:(\mathscr{O},\sqcup)\to(\mathcal{C},\star); such functors necessarily respect the algebra and module structures above. Recall that given an algebra object AA of (𝒞,⋆)(\mathcal{C},\star), and left and right AA-modules L,R∈𝒞L,R\in\mathcal{C}, we write L⊗ARL\otimes_{A}R for the co-equalizer of the two actions of AA on L⋆RL\star R; we say 𝒞\mathcal{C} admits tensor products if such co-equalizers always exist.

Definition 1.2.

By a gluing configuration, we mean a cover Σ=ΣL∪ΣR\Sigma=\Sigma_{L}\cup\Sigma_{R} where the inclusions ΣL,ΣR→Σ\Sigma_{L},\Sigma_{R}\to\Sigma are maps in 𝒪\mathscr{O}, and the overlap is a union of strips, i.e. A:=ΣL∩ΣR≃∐T∗​[−1,1]A:=\Sigma_{L}\cap\Sigma_{R}\simeq\coprod T^{*}[-1,1]. If (𝒞,⋆)(\mathcal{C},\star) admits tensor products, we say ℱ:(𝒪,⊔)→(𝒞,⋆)\mathcal{F}:(\mathscr{O},\sqcup)\to(\mathcal{C},\star) satisfies gluing if, given any gluing configuration,

ℱ​(ΣL)⊗ℱ​(A)ℱ​(ΣR)→ℱ​(Σ)\mathcal{F}(\Sigma_{L})\otimes_{\mathcal{F}(A)}\mathcal{F}(\Sigma_{R})\to\mathcal{F}(\Sigma) (1)

is an isomorphism.

Such a functor, satisfying gluing, gives an open 2d TFT valued in the ‘Morita category’ internal to (𝒞,⋆)(\mathcal{C},\star), which sends the interval to ℱ​(I)\mathcal{F}(I).111There are however additional structures. For instance, the inclusion ∅→Σ\emptyset\to\Sigma induces pointing of all structures, as Johnson-Freyd requires for a ‘Heisenberg-picture’ TFT [24]. (We recall that the Morita category has objects given by algebras in 𝒞\mathcal{C}, morphisms given by bimodules, and composition given by tensor of bimodules over algebras.) We note also that while the above formulation does not directly express cutting along a non-separating strip, we may achieve the same effect by cutting along two parallel such strips.

Example 1.3.

Homology gives a functor (𝒪,⊔)→(ℤ−mod,⊕)(\mathscr{O},\sqcup)\to(\mathbb{Z}-\mathrm{mod},\oplus), which satisfies gluing by the Mayer-Vietoris theorem. Because the maps in 𝒪\mathscr{O} are proper, the same is true for Borel-Moore homology.

The Borel-Moore version has a known categorification. Let us write Sect2​n\operatorname{Sect}_{2n} for the category of Liouville sectors. The objects of this category were introduced in [20, 22]: they are noncompact exact symplectic manifolds-with-boundary, satisfying certain conditions (we recall these below in Section 4.2).

We turn to questions of calculation. Various models for Fuk⁡(Sym⁡(Σ))\operatorname{Fuk}(\operatorname{Sym}(\Sigma)) and related categories have been obtained in previous works [30, 8, 17, 16, 34, 18, 15]. We conclude with a remark about future directions. The various symplectic approaches to Khovanov homology and its relatives [48, 36, 37, 1, 2, 6, 5, 4, 3, 29] involve spaces which admit natural maps to symmetric powers of surfaces. In an ongoing joint project with Aganagic and LePage, we will apply a generalization of the methods developed here to construct similar sectorial decompositions for such spaces, and construct Rouquier’s monoidal structure ([46]) for general 𝔤+\mathfrak{g}^{+}, by the analogue of the ⋆T\star_{T} above, and establish their expected properties. In another direction, we expect similar ‘exponentiated sectorial descent’ theorems can be proven in the ‘higher dimensional Heegaard-Floer’ theory [11], as would be compatible with the calculation [23] and of presumptive representation theoretic relevance visavis [51].

2 Clustering rules

One fundamental technical tool of the present article is a system of coordinates on symmetric products which allow us to separate dependence on the normal and tangential directions to the big diagonal. Were the diagonal smooth (it is not), it would suffice to fix a metric and choose a tubular neighborhood. Let us elaborate on the basic challenge. Given a point on the big diagonal, 𝐳=∑ni​zi\mathbf{z}=\sum n_{i}z_{i} with ni>1n_{i}>1, we might consider a class of nearby configurations 𝐳=∑𝐳i\mathbf{z}=\sum\mathbf{z}_{i}, with |𝐳i|=ni|\mathbf{z}_{i}|=n_{i} and 𝐳i\mathbf{z}_{i} centered at ziz_{i}. It is natural to view variation of the 𝐳i\mathbf{z}_{i} as tangential to the diagonal, and internal variation constituents of 𝐳i\mathbf{z}_{i} (constrainted to remain centered at ziz_{i}) as normal to the diagonal. Of course, each 𝐳\mathbf{z} admits many expressions of the form 𝐳=∑𝐳i\mathbf{z}=\sum\mathbf{z}_{i}. In this section we will describe a certain class of such decompositions (clusterings), in order to define a class of functions on symmetric powers, which depend only on the center of masses of clusterings.

We proceed with the construction. Let MM be a smooth Riemannian manifold with distance function dist:M×M→ℝ\operatorname{dist}:M\times M\to\mathbb{R}. 4.2 Recollections on Liouville structures

Given an exact symplectic manifold (X,ω=d​λ)(X,\omega=d\lambda), the Liouville vector field ZZ is characterized by ω​(Z,⋅)=λ\omega(Z,\cdot)=\lambda. Then XX is said to be Liouville if there is a compact submanifold-with-boundary X¯⊂X\overline{X}\subset X such that ZZ is outwardly transverse to ∂X¯\partial\overline{X} and the flow of ZZ identifies X∖X¯X\setminus\overline{X} with a half-cylinder on ∂X¯\partial\overline{X}. In this case λ|∂X¯\lambda|_{\partial\overline{X}} is a contact form, and the corresponding contact manifold is canonically independent of the choice of X¯\overline{X}; we correspondingly denote it ∂∞X\partial_{\infty}X and term it the ideal contact boundary of XX. The submanifold X¯\overline{X} is termed a Liouville domain with completion XX.

We now recall from [20, 22] the definitions and some properties of Liouville sectors.

Definition 4.12.

[20] Let (X,λ)(X,\lambda) be a Liouville manifold. A closed codimension one smooth submanifold H⊂XH\subset X is a sectorial hypersurface if there is a function I:H→ℝI:H\to\mathbb{R} such that (1) Z​I=IZI=I in the complement of a compact set and (2) d​IdI is nonvanishing on the characteristic foliation of HH.

Note that if H={ρ=0}H=\{\rho=0\} with d​ρ|Hd\rho|_{H} non-vanishing, then (2) above is equivalent to asking the Poisson bracket {ρ,I}\{\rho,I\} to be nowhere zero.

Definition 4.13.

[22, Def. 12.2] A union of cleanly intersecting sectorial hypersurfaces HiH_{i} with characteristic foliations ξi\xi_{i} is sectorial if (the functions IiI_{i} admit extensions to neighborhoods of the HiH_{i} so that) the ξi\xi_{i} are symplectically orthogonal over the intersections of the HiH_{i}, the IiI_{i} are in involution ({Ii,Ij}=0\{I_{i},I_{j}\}=0), and d​Ii|ξj=0dI_{i}|_{\xi_{j}}=0 when i≠ji\neq j.

A model and motivating example is that if M=⋃UiM=\bigcup U_{i} is a cover of a smooth manifold by open sets with smooth transverse boundaries, with boundary-defining-functions ρi\rho_{i}, then the T∗​UiT^{*}U_{i} give a sectorial cover of T∗​MT^{*}M, with witness functions given e.g. by choosing a metric and taking IiI_{i} to be the function on the cotangent bundle given by evaluation of a covector at ∇ρi\nabla\rho_{i}.

((𝐓/𝐤1)⊗𝐤1)⊕((𝐓/𝐤2)←(𝐤2⊗𝐤1)⊕(𝐤1⊗𝐤2)→𝐤((\mathbf{T}/\mathbf{k}_{1})\otimes\mathbf{k}_{1})\oplus((\mathbf{T}/\mathbf{k}_{2})\leftarrow(\mathbf{k}_{2}\otimes\mathbf{k}_{1})\oplus(\mathbf{k}_{1}\otimes\mathbf{k}_{2})\rightarrow\mathbf{k}

The left arrow is an isomorphism, so the colimit is 𝐤\mathbf{k}, as desired. We may cut the corner.

What remains is the diagram (1|n−1)←(0​|1|​n−1)→(0|n)(1|n-1)\leftarrow(0|1|n-1)\rightarrow(0|n), or, in other words

𝐓⊗Mn−1←(𝐤⊕𝐤)⊗Mn−1→Mn.\mathbf{T}\otimes M^{n-1}\leftarrow(\mathbf{k}\oplus\mathbf{k})\otimes M^{n-1}\rightarrow M^{n}.

This completes the proof. ∎
