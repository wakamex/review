An Algorithm to Legendrian Realize a Curve on a Ribbon Surface

Eric Stenhede University of Vienna, Faculty of Mathematics, Oskar-Morgenstern-Platz 1, 1090 Wien, Austria eric.stenhede@univie.ac.at
(Date: April 9, 2026)
Abstract.

We give an explicit algorithm to Legendrian realize a homologically nontrivial simple closed curve on a ribbon surface of a Legendrian graph in the standard contact structure (ℝ3,ξst)(\mathbb{R}^{3},\xi_{\mathrm{st}}). As an application, we obtain an algorithm that converts an abstract open book whose monodromy is written as a product of Dehn twists along homologically nontrivial curves into a contact surgery diagram for the supported contact manifold. Along the way, we also record a uniqueness statement which is implicit in earlier work but, to our knowledge, was never written in the form needed here: any two Legendrian realizations of the same curve on a ribbon surface are Legendrian isotopic, and likewise for Legendrian knots lying on pages of open books and representing the same isotopy class on the page.

Legendrian realization, contact surgery, open books, Legendrian knots

1. Introduction

A recurring theme in low-dimensional topology is the search for efficient and flexible ways to present geometric objects. In contact topology, two complementary languages have proved particularly effective for studying contact 33–manifolds:

  • •

    contact surgery diagrams, in which one presents a contact manifold as the result of contact (±1)(\pm 1)–surgery on a Legendrian link in (S3,ξst)(S^{3},\xi_{\mathrm{st}}), and

  • •

    open book decompositions, which, by Giroux’s correspondence, encode contact structures in terms of surfaces and mapping classes [4, 12].

The relationship between these two descriptions has been studied extensively. In one direction, Stipsicz described an algorithm for constructing a compatible open book from a Legendrian surgery diagram [22], and related constructions also appear in [1, 20, 2]. In the opposite direction, Avdek showed how to build a contact surgery diagram from an abstract open book with connected binding whose monodromy is written as a product of Dehn twists along the Lickorish generators [2].

The main difficulty in extending Avdek’s algorithm to more general abstract open books where the binding might not be connected or the monodromy might not be given in terms of Dehn twists along the Lickorish generators is making the Legendrian realization principle computable. If the monodromy is given as a product of Dehn twists along arbitrary homologically nontrivial curves, then one needs an explicit way to realize those curves as Legendrian knots on pages of an open book. The Legendrian realization principle guarantees existence after a suitable perturbation of the surface, but its proof is non-constructive and global in nature [14, 13, 6].

The goal of this article is to make this realization problem explicit in the setting of ribbon surfaces of Legendrian graphs in (ℝ3,ξst)(\mathbb{R}^{3},\xi_{\mathrm{st}}). More precisely, the core result of the paper, stated in Section 5.1, is an algorithm that takes as input a Legendrian graph G⊂(ℝ3,ξst)G\subset(\mathbb{R}^{3},\xi_{\mathrm{st}}) together with a homologically nontrivial simple closed curve 𝔞\mathfrak{a} on a ribbon surface of GG, and produces the front projection of a Legendrian realization of 𝔞\mathfrak{a} (Theorem 5.2). As an application, Section 6 yields an explicit procedure that converts an abstract open book whose monodromy is written as a product of Dehn twists along homologically nontrivial curves into a contact surgery diagram for the supported contact manifold (Corollary 6.1).

Given the front projection of a ribbon surface in (ℝ3,ξst)(\mathbb{R}^{3},\xi_{\mathrm{st}}), it is important to notice that there is no naive local way to Legendrian realize a curve on it. Many of the crossings occurring in the front projection of the boundary link of a ribbon violate the Legendrian condition, so in general one cannot simply introduce cusps where needed and hope to obtain a Legendrian knot. Figure 1.1 shows a concrete instance of this phenomenon.

Very briefly, the algorithm works as follows. Starting from a Legendrian graph G⊂(ℝ3,ξst)G\subset(\mathbb{R}^{3},\xi_{\mathrm{st}}), we use the handle decomposition of a ribbon surface Σ\Sigma induced by the vertices and edges of GG to put the curve 𝔞\mathfrak{a} in a standard position. We then decompose 𝔞\mathfrak{a} into finitely many segments, each lying in a single handle. For every segment in a 11–handle we assign a combinatorial quantity, the relative gain, which governs the slope of the corresponding Legendrian segment in the front projection. By modifying 𝔞\mathfrak{a} within its isotopy class on the ribbon, we arrange that these relative gains balance globally, so that the resulting Legendrian segments glue together with matching zz–coordinates and close up to a Legendrian knot.

Along the way, we also record a uniqueness statement that is implicit in earlier work but, to our knowledge, has not been written down in the form required here: any two Legendrian realizations of the same curve on a ribbon surface are Legendrian isotopic, and similarly for Legendrian knots lying on pages of open books and representing the same isotopy class on the page; see Theorems 4.2 and 4.6. These uniqueness results are of independent interest beyond the algorithm itself, and they will also be used in forthcoming work of the author with collaborators on a contact version of Kirby’s theorem [15].

2. Preliminaries

2.1. Mapping class groups and Dehn twists

Let Σ\Sigma be a smooth, compact, oriented surface with non-empty boundary. We denote by Diff+​(Σ,∂Σ)\mathrm{Diff}^{+}(\Sigma,\partial\Sigma) the group of orientation-preserving diffeomorphisms of Σ\Sigma that restrict to the identity on a neighborhood of the boundary, and by Diff0+​(Σ,∂Σ)\mathrm{Diff}^{+}_{0}(\Sigma,\partial\Sigma) the connected component containing the identity. The mapping class group MCG​(Σ,∂Σ)\mathrm{MCG}(\Sigma,\partial\Sigma) is the quotient

MCG​(Σ,∂Σ):=Diff+​(Σ,∂Σ)/Diff0+​(Σ,∂Σ).\mathrm{MCG}(\Sigma,\partial\Sigma):=\mathrm{Diff}^{+}(\Sigma,\partial\Sigma)/\mathrm{Diff}^{+}_{0}(\Sigma,\partial\Sigma).

This group is generated by Dehn twists along simple closed curves. We denote by τ𝔞±\tau_{\mathfrak{a}}^{\pm} the mapping class represented by a positive or negative Dehn twist along a simple closed curve 𝔞\mathfrak{a} in Σ\Sigma.

2.3. Convex surfaces and Legendrian realization

Given an oriented surface SS in a contact manifold (M,ξ)(M,\xi), we denote by SξS_{\xi} its characteristic foliation. We say that SS is convex if there exists a contact vector field XX transverse to SS. Equivalently, SS admits a vertically invariant neighborhood: there is a neighborhood S×(−ε,ε)⊂MS\times(-\varepsilon,\varepsilon)\subset M of S≅S×{0}S\cong S\times\{0\} in which the contact structure is invariant under translation in the second factor. In suitable coordinates the contact structure is then given as the kernel of a 11–form

β+u​d​s,\beta+u\,ds,

where β∈Ω1​(S)\beta\in\Omega^{1}(S), u∈C∞​(S)u\in C^{\infty}(S), and s∈(−ε,ε)s\in(-\varepsilon,\varepsilon) is the coordinate in the second factor. The dividing set ΓS\Gamma_{S} is the set of points where the transverse contact vector field lies in ξ\xi; in the vertically invariant model this is precisely the zero set of uu.

A singular foliation ℱ\mathcal{F} on SS is divided by a multicurve Γ\Gamma if Γ\Gamma is transverse to ℱ\mathcal{F} and there exist an area form ω\omega on SS and a vector field vv defining ℱ\mathcal{F} such that ℒv​ω≠0\mathcal{L}_{v}\omega\neq 0 on S∖ΓS\setminus\Gamma, with vv pointing out of the positive region and into the negative region along Γ\Gamma. If SS is convex, then its characteristic foliation is divided by the dividing set.

The flexibility theorem of Giroux says that, once the dividing set is fixed, the characteristic foliation may be changed arbitrarily inside the class of foliations divided by it.

Let ℱ\mathcal{F} be a singular foliation on SS divided by ΓS\Gamma_{S}, and let 𝒩​(S)\mathcal{N}(S) be any neighborhood of SS of the form S×(−ε,ε)S\times(-\varepsilon,\varepsilon) in MM. Then there exists an isotopy

ψt:S⟶𝒩​(S),t∈[0,1],\psi_{t}\colon S\longrightarrow\mathcal{N}(S),\qquad t\in[0,1],

of embeddings such that:

  • •

    ψ0\psi_{0} is the inclusion S↪MS\hookrightarrow M;

  • •

    for all t∈[0,1]t\in[0,1], the surface ψt​(S)\psi_{t}(S) is convex with respect to XX and has dividing set ψt​(ΓS)\psi_{t}(\Gamma_{S});

  • •

    the characteristic foliation (ψ1​(S))ξ(\psi_{1}(S))_{\xi} coincides with ψ1​(ℱ)\psi_{1}(\mathcal{F}).

Moreover, if ℱ\mathcal{F} coincides with SξS_{\xi} on a neighborhood of ΓS\Gamma_{S} in SS, then the isotopy may be chosen fixed on a small enough neighborhood of ΓS\Gamma_{S}.

A graph C⊂SC\subset S is called nonisolating if it is properly embedded, disjoint from ∂S\partial S, and transverse to the dividing set when they intersect. 2.4. Contact surgery and open books

Let LL be a Legendrian knot in a contact manifold (M,ξ)(M,\xi). A standard neighborhood 𝒩​(L)\mathcal{N}(L) of LL is a solid torus whose boundary is a convex surface. The slope of the dividing set on ∂𝒩​(L)\partial\mathcal{N}(L) determines the contact framing of LL. Removing the interior of 𝒩​(L)\mathcal{N}(L) and gluing back a solid torus by (±1/n)(\pm 1/n)–surgery with respect to the contact framing produces, in a canonical way, a new contact manifold; this is called contact (±1/n)(\pm 1/n)–surgery on LL. In this article we only consider contact (±1)(\pm 1)–surgery. If LL is equipped with such a surgery coefficient, we write L±L^{\pm}.

An open book on a closed oriented 33–manifold MM is a pair (B,π)(B,\pi) where B⊂MB\subset M is a link, called the binding, and π:M∖B→S1\pi\colon M\setminus B\to S^{1} is a fibration which coincides with the angular coordinate on a neighborhood B×D2B\times D^{2} of the binding. The closures of the fibers of π\pi are the pages. An abstract open book is a pair (Σ,ϕ)(\Sigma,\phi) where Σ\Sigma is a compact oriented surface with non-empty boundary and ϕ∈Diff+​(Σ,∂Σ)\phi\in\mathrm{Diff}^{+}(\Sigma,\partial\Sigma) is the identity near the boundary. Equivalent abstract open books correspond to diffeomorphic open books.

We say that a contact structure ξ\xi on MM is compatible with (or supported by) an open book (B,π)(B,\pi) if there exists a contact form α\alpha for ξ\xi such that the binding is positively transverse and d​αd\alpha restricts to a positive area form on the interior of every page. The fundamental correspondence due to Giroux can be stated as follows.

Theorem 2.4 (Giroux’s correspondence [12, 3, 16, 17]).

Let MM be a closed, oriented 33–manifold. There is a one-to-one correspondence between positive cooriented contact structures on MM up to isotopy and open books on MM up to isotopy and positive stabilization.

The version in terms of abstract open books identifies contact 33–manifolds up to contactomorphism with abstract open books up to equivalence and positive stabilization.

The next theorem will only be used in the final application. Once ribbon surfaces have been introduced in Section 3, it says that suitable ribbons arise as pages of supporting open books.

Theorem 2.5 (Giroux [12]).

Let GG be the 11–skeleton of a contact cell decomposition of a contact 33–manifold (M,ξ)(M,\xi). Then any ribbon surface RGR_{G} of GG is a page of an open book compatible with ξ\xi.

Finally, we recall the effect of contact surgery on knots sitting in pages of an open book.

Proposition 2.6 ([8, 7]).

Let

𝑳=⋃i=1nLiδi,δi∈{+,−},\boldsymbol{L}=\bigcup_{i=1}^{n}L_{i}^{\delta_{i}},\qquad\delta_{i}\in\{+,-\},

be a contact surgery link whose components are embedded in different pages of an open book (B,π)(B,\pi) supporting a contact structure ξ\xi in a manifold MM. Let (Σ,h)(\Sigma,h) be the corresponding abstract open book, where Σ\Sigma is a page chosen just before the page containing L1L_{1}. Then the contact manifold obtained by contact surgery on 𝐋\boldsymbol{L} is contactomorphic to the contact manifold supported by the abstract open book (Σ,h∘τ𝐋)(\Sigma,h\circ\tau_{\scriptscriptstyle{\boldsymbol{L}}}), where

τ𝑳:=τLn¯−δn​⋯​τL1¯−δ1∈MCG​(Σ,∂Σ)\tau_{\scriptscriptstyle{\boldsymbol{L}}}:=\tau_{\scriptscriptstyle{\overline{L_{n}}}}^{-\delta_{n}}\cdots\tau_{\scriptscriptstyle{\overline{L_{1}}}}^{-\delta_{1}}\in\mathrm{MCG}(\Sigma,\partial\Sigma)

and Li¯\overline{L_{i}} denotes the isotopy class on the page represented by LiL_{i}.

Every Legendrian graph GG admits a ribbon surface: one can take a sufficiently small “thickening” of GG in the direction of the contact structure. Any such ribbon is automatically convex, since it is transverse to a Reeb vector field. However, we can say more about this.

Thus, from now on a ribbon surface will always be regarded as a convex surface with dividing set equal to its boundary.

3.0.2. Legendrian realization for ribbon surfaces

The Legendrian realization principle (Theorem 2.2) gives a sufficient condition (being nonisolating) for a graph on a convex surface to be Legendrian realizable. While this condition is well known to be necessary, we provide a proof in this specific context for the sake of completeness. To this end, note that since a ribbon surface Σ\Sigma is a convex surface whose dividing set coincides with its boundary, a curve 𝔞\mathfrak{a} in Σ\Sigma is nonisolating if and only if it is homologically nontrivial.555In this article, we prefer to phrase results using the term “homologically nontrivial” rather than “nonisolating,” as it is independent of the choice of contact vector field that makes the ribbon convex.

Lemma 3.3.

Proof.

Assume by contradiction that LL is homologically trivial. Then LL bounds a subsurface Σ~⊂Σ\widetilde{\Sigma}\subset\Sigma. ∫Lβ=0.\int_{L}\beta=0.

0=∫Lβ=∫Σ~𝑑β.0=\int_{L}\beta=\int_{\widetilde{\Sigma}}d\beta.

4.0.1. Uniqueness of the Legendrian realization for ribbon surfaces

Definition 4.1.

Theorem 4.2.

Let Σ\Sigma be a compact, oriented surface with ∂Σ≠∅\partial\Sigma\neq\emptyset. Before proving Theorem 4.2 we need a couple more definitions and a Lemma.

We say that an isotopy ϕt\phi_{t} of (Σ×ℝ,ξ)(\Sigma\times\mathbb{R},\xi) is a Moser isotopy if it is obtained by applying the Moser trick to a family αt\alpha_{t} of contact forms defining ξt:=T​ϕt−1​(ξ)\xi_{t}:=T\phi_{t}^{-1}(\xi). More precisely, we are assuming that the Moser trick applied to αt\alpha_{t} gives an isotopy ϕ~t\widetilde{\phi}_{t} such that ξt=T​ϕ~t​(ξ0)\xi_{t}=T\widetilde{\phi}_{t}(\xi_{0}), and we are imposing that ϕ~t=ϕt−1\widetilde{\phi}_{t}=\phi_{t}^{-1}.

It is important to note that not all isotopies are Moser isotopies. For example, a contact isotopy ϕt\phi_{t} of ξ\xi will never be of this form, as in this case the path ξt\xi_{t} would be constant and so ϕ~t=Id\widetilde{\phi}_{t}=\mathrm{Id}.

We say that a contact form α\alpha is compatible with Σ×ℝ\Sigma\times\mathbb{R} if

  1. (1)

    α\alpha is vertically invariant;

  2. (2)

    RαR_{\alpha} is positively transverse to Σ\Sigma;

  3. (3)

    ∂Σ\partial\Sigma is a positive transverse link for ker⁡α\ker\alpha.

If we are in the hypothesis of Theorem 4.2, then the contact form α\alpha (whose Reeb vector field is ∂z\partial_{z}) is clearly compatible with Σ×I\Sigma\times I.

The following is an obvious adaptation of Etnyre’s proof of Giroux’s intuition that two contact structures compatible with the same open book are isotopic [12, 7].   • •

  • •

  • •

  • •

    the isotopy ϕt\phi_{t} is vertically invariant, i.e.

    ϕt​(x,z)=(ϕt​(x,0)Σ,ϕt​(x)ℝ+z);\phi_{t}(x,z)=(\phi_{t}(x,0)_{\Sigma},\phi_{t}(x)_{\mathbb{R}}+z);
  • •

These properties are all consequences of the fact that each αt\alpha_{t} is vertically invariant.

Given all of this, it is easy to check that there is a surjection between:

  • •

    the space of paths αt\alpha_{t} of contact forms compatible with Σ×ℝ\Sigma\times\mathbb{R}

onto:

  • •

    the space of vertically invariant Moser isotopies ϕt\phi_{t} such that ϕt​(Σ)\phi_{t}(\Sigma) is transverse to ∂z\partial_{z}.

It is then clear why having a control over the first space (given by Lemma 4.4) has consequences over the second space.

4.0.2. Uniqueness of the Legendrian realization for open book decompositions

Notice that the open book setting is very similar to our setting.

Definition 4.5.

Let (M,ξ)(M,\xi) be a contact manifold and let (B,π)(B,\pi) be an open book compatible with ξ\xi.

In this setting, we call an isotopy ϕt\phi_{t} of MM admissible if

  • •

    ϕt\phi_{t} is the identity on a neighborhood of BB;

  • •

    there exists a contact form α\alpha for ξ\xi such that

    • –

      α\alpha is compatible with (B,π)(B,\pi) in the sense that the Reeb vector field RαR_{\alpha} is always positively transverse to the pages and positively tangent to BB;

    • –

      for each z∈S1z\in S^{1}, the surface ϕt​(π−1​(z))\phi_{t}(\pi^{-1}(z)) is always transverse to RαR_{\alpha}.

Let 𝔞\mathfrak{a} be the isotopy class of a simple closed curve on a page Σ\Sigma of (B,π)(B,\pi). We say that a Legendrian knot L⊂M∖BL\subset M\setminus B is a Legendrian realization of 𝔞\mathfrak{a} if there is an admissible isotopy ϕt\phi_{t} such that L⊂ϕ1​(Σ)L\subset\phi_{1}(\Sigma) and the isotopy class of ϕ1−1​(L)\phi_{1}^{-1}(L) on Σ\Sigma coincides with 𝔞\mathfrak{a}.

Let ϕt\phi_{t} be an admissible isotopy of (M,(B,π),ξ)(M,(B,\pi),\xi) as in Definition 4.5, and let α\alpha be the corresponding contact form. We say that ϕt\phi_{t} is a Moser isotopy if it is obtained by applying the Moser trick to the path of contact forms

αt:=T​ϕt−1​(α).\alpha_{t}:=T\phi_{t}^{-1}(\alpha).

In this language, in [18, Theorem 2.7] it is proved that, given a homologically nontrivial isotopy class 𝔞\mathfrak{a} of a simple closed curve on a page Σ\Sigma, we have:

  • •

    there exists a Legendrian realization of 𝔞\mathfrak{a} given by a Moser isotopy;

  • •

    any two Legendrian realizations of 𝔞\mathfrak{a} given by a Moser isotopy are Legendrian isotopic.

The proof uses the fact that the isotopies are Moser because it uses that the space of contact forms compatible with a given open book is convex [12, 7].

However, a simple adaptation of the argument in part (b) of the proof of Theorem 4.2, together with the proof of [18, Theorem 2.7], shows the following:

Theorem 4.6 (Uniqueness of Legendrian knots on open books).

Let (B,π)(B,\pi) be an open book supporting a contact manifold (M,ξ)(M,\xi). Let Σ\Sigma be a page and let 𝔞\mathfrak{a} be the isotopy class of a homologically nontrivial simple closed curve on Σ\Sigma. Then:

  • •

    There exists a Legendrian knot LL embedded on a page of an open book (B′,π′)(B^{\prime},\pi^{\prime}) supporting ξ\xi such that

    • –

      (B,π)(B,\pi) and (B′,π′)(B^{\prime},\pi^{\prime}) are isotopic through open books compatible with the same contact form α\alpha for ξ\xi;

    • –

      if we denote by ϕt\phi_{t} the isotopy between (B,π)(B,\pi) and (B′,π′)(B^{\prime},\pi^{\prime}), then ϕ1−1​(L)⊂Σ\phi_{1}^{-1}(L)\subset\Sigma defines the same isotopy class 𝔞\mathfrak{a}.

  • •

    If L′L^{\prime} is another Legendrian knot that is embedded on a page of an open book (B′′,π′′)(B^{\prime\prime},\pi^{\prime\prime}) such that

    • –

      (B,π)(B,\pi) and (B′′,π′′)(B^{\prime\prime},\pi^{\prime\prime}) are isotopic through open books compatible with the same contact form α′\alpha^{\prime} for ξ\xi;

    • –

      if we denote by ϕt′\phi_{t}^{\prime} the isotopy between (B,π)(B,\pi) and (B′′,π′′)(B^{\prime\prime},\pi^{\prime\prime}), then ϕ1′⁣−1​(L′)⊂Σ\phi_{1}^{\prime-1}(L^{\prime})\subset\Sigma defines the same isotopy class 𝔞\mathfrak{a},

    then L′L^{\prime} is Legendrian isotopic to LL.

We need this stronger version (in fact, Theorem 4.2) of the result in [18] for later applications to contact Kirby calculus. Indeed, one may encounter two Legendrian knots that lie in admissible isotopies of the same ribbon surface without literally arising as Legendrian realizations of a fixed curve on the ribbon via a Moser isotopy. In that situation, Theorem 4.2 still implies that the knots are Legendrian isotopic, whereas the result of [18] alone would not suffice.

5. An algorithm to Legendrian realize a curve on a ribbon surface

5.1. Statement of the result

Before stating the main theorem, we formalize the notion of an “abstract ribbon”, which allows us to separate the combinatorics on a surface from its concrete embedding in (ℝ3,ξst)(\mathbb{R}^{3},\xi_{\mathrm{st}}).

Let Σ\Sigma be a ribbon surface of a Legendrian graph GG. We write [Σ][\Sigma] for the isotopy class of Σ\Sigma through embeddings that fix GG pointwise. It is straightforward to check that if Σ\Sigma and Σ′\Sigma^{\prime} are ribbon surfaces of the same Legendrian graph GG, then [Σ]=[Σ′][\Sigma]=[\Sigma^{\prime}]. This leads to:

Definition 5.1.

The abstract ribbon 𝒮\mathcal{S} of a Legendrian graph GG is the class [Σ][\Sigma], where Σ\Sigma is any ribbon surface of GG.

The abstract ribbon inherits a well-defined handle decomposition from any representative Σ\Sigma, since the handle decomposition only depends on GG.

We can now state the main result.

Theorem 5.2.

There exists an algorithm with the following input and output:

Input:

  • •

    a generic Legendrian graph G⊂(ℝ3,ξst)G\subset(\mathbb{R}^{3},\xi_{\mathrm{st}}), given by its front projection;

  • •

    a homologically nontrivial simple closed curve 𝔞\mathfrak{a} on the abstract ribbon 𝒮\mathcal{S} of GG.

Output: the front projection of a generic Legendrian knot LL which is a Legendrian realization of 𝔞\mathfrak{a} in the following sense. There exists a generic ribbon surface Σ\Sigma of GG and an isotopy ψt:Σ→ℝ3\psi_{t}\colon\Sigma\to\mathbb{R}^{3}, t∈[0,1]t\in[0,1], such that:

  1. (1)

    ψ0\psi_{0} is the inclusion Σ⊂ℝ3\Sigma\subset\mathbb{R}^{3}, and ψt\psi_{t} is fixed on a neighborhood of ∂Σ\partial\Sigma;

  2. (2)

    each surface ψt​(Σ)\psi_{t}(\Sigma) is transverse to ∂z\partial_{z} and hence convex;

  3. (3)

    for every ε>0\varepsilon>0, the algorithm can be carried out so that

    Im​ψt⊂Σ×[−ε,ε]for all ​t∈[0,1],\mathrm{Im}\psi_{t}\subset\Sigma\times[-\varepsilon,\varepsilon]\qquad\text{for all }t\in[0,1],

    where the second factor is parametrized by the zz-coordinate.

  4. (4)

    LL is a Legendrian knot properly embedded in ψ1​(Σ)\psi_{1}(\Sigma);

  5. (5)

    the curve ψ1​(𝔞)\psi_{1}(\mathfrak{a}) is smoothly isotopic to LL in the surface ψ1​(Σ)\psi_{1}(\Sigma).

We first describe the algorithm step by step, and then justify that it produces the Legendrian realization claimed in Theorem 5.2.

Remark 5.3.

By Theorem 4.2, LL is the unique, up to Legendrian isotopy, Legendrian realization of the isotopy class of 𝔞\mathfrak{a}.

Remark 5.4.

Intuitively, Δ​(𝔩)\Delta(\mathfrak{l}) measures how much the Legendrian realization of a segment 𝔩\mathfrak{l} will “gain” or “lose” height in the front projection relative to the core 𝒆\boldsymbol{e}. The following discussion provides an informal explanation. Consider a 11-handle HH of a ribbon surface Σ\Sigma of a Legendrian graph GG (see, for example, Figure 5.7).

Denote by 𝒆\boldsymbol{e} the Legendrian core of the handle HH (the central blue segment in Figure 5.7). This is one of the inputs to our algorithm, as we know the front projection of GG. Assume there are three segments of 𝔞\mathfrak{a} in this handle HH. In Step 1, we first straighten these segments to look like the three segments (green, blue, and red) in Figure 5.7 (left). The central blue segment is the Legendrian core, the red segment is “a bit closer to us” (in the sense that it has a higher xx-coordinate), and the green segment is “a bit further away from us.”

The Legendrian realization of the blue segment is a copy of itself, possibly translated in the ∂z\partial_{z} direction. Intuitively, the Legendrian realizations of the green and red segments will look similar to the blue segment but slightly rotated due to the contact condition (the (y,z)(y,z)-slope of the contact planes decreases as xx increases), and possibly translated in the ∂z\partial_{z} direction (see Figure 5.7, right). The relative gain Δ\Delta measures how much these Legendrian realizations lose or gain height relative to the blue segment when traversed in their oriented direction. For example, if all the segments are oriented from left to right, the red segment loses some height, while the green segment gains some height.

The amount by which we need to translate these Legendrian realizations in the ∂z\partial_{z} direction depends on the entire curve 𝔞\mathfrak{a} and will be encoded by the quantity PP defined later in Step 4.

5.4. Justification of the algorithm

The algorithm clearly produces a knot LL (and not a link): we connect the Legendrian realization of a segment 𝔩i\mathfrak{l}_{i} to the Legendrian realization of the consecutive segment 𝔩i+1\mathfrak{l}_{i+1} of 𝔞\mathfrak{a}, and 𝔞\mathfrak{a} is a connected closed curve. Moreover, LL is a generic Legendrian knot, as all the front projections we have produced are generic and never violate the Legendrian condition.

We now make precise what remains to be shown and encapsulate it in a single statement.

Proposition 5.8 (Validity of the algorithm).

Let G⊂(ℝ3,ξst)G\subset(\mathbb{R}^{3},\xi_{\mathrm{st}}) be a generic Legendrian graph, let 𝒮\mathcal{S} be the abstract ribbon surface of GG, and let 𝔞\mathfrak{a} be a homologically nontrivial simple closed curve on 𝒮\mathcal{S}. Let LL be the Legendrian knot produced by the algorithm of Theorem 5.2. Then the following hold:

  • •

    There exists a generic ribbon surface Σ\Sigma of GG that can be perturbed relative to the boundary, through surfaces transverse to ∂z\partial_{z}, so as to contain LL.

  • •

    The isotopy class of LL in the perturbation of Σ\Sigma agrees with the isotopy class of 𝔞\mathfrak{a}.

  • •

    The perturbation of Σ\Sigma can be chosen to be arbitrarily C0C^{0}–small if the algorithm is applied carefully.

The rest of this section is devoted to the proof of Proposition 5.8. We split the argument into several lemmas corresponding to the subsequent subsections.

6. Applications: from open books to contact surgery diagrams

The next result can be viewed as a refinement of Avdek’s algorithm in [2].

Corollary 6.1.

Given an abstract open book decomposition (Σ,ϕ)(\Sigma,\phi) whose monodromy ϕ\phi is given explicitly as a composition of Dehn twists along homologically nontrivial simple closed curves, there exists an algorithm that produces a contact surgery diagram for the contact manifold supported by the open book (Σ,ϕ)(\Sigma,\phi).

This algorithm produces a contact surgery link 𝐋=𝐋𝟏∪𝐋𝟐\boldsymbol{L}=\boldsymbol{L_{1}}\cup\boldsymbol{L_{2}} for which the components of 𝐋𝟏\boldsymbol{L_{1}} are in bijection with the Dehn twists in the factorization of ϕ\phi, and all the components of 𝐋𝟐\boldsymbol{L_{2}} are +1+1–framed Legendrian unknots with Thurston–Bennequin number t​b=−1tb=-1.

Before delving into the proof, we first describe a suitable family of open books for (S3,ξst)(S^{3},\xi_{\mathrm{st}}) whose pages have arbitrary genus and number of boundary components. We start from two basic open books:

  • •

    one with page a genus 11 surface with connected boundary;

  • •

    one with page an annulus.

Each of these pages can be realized as the ribbon of a specific Legendrian graph in (ℝ3,ξst)(\mathbb{R}^{3},\xi_{\mathrm{st}}), shown in Figure 6.1.

Let AA denote the graph on the left and BB the graph on the right. By assembling several copies of AA and BB as in Figure 6.2, we obtain a Legendrian graph whose ribbon has genus equal to the number of AA–pieces and number of boundary components equal to one plus the number of BB–pieces.

The ribbons of these graphs are pages of open books supporting (S3,ξst)(S^{3},\xi_{\mathrm{st}}) (because of Theorem 2.5). Thus we have explicit models of open books for (S3,ξst)(S^{3},\xi_{\mathrm{st}}) with any prescribed topology of the page.
