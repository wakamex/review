The hat and plus version of the Heegaard Floer contact invariant are not equivalent

Alberto Cavallo and Irena Matkovič HUN-REN Alfréd Rényi Insitute of Mathematics, Budapest 1053, Hungary acavallo@impan.pl Uppsala Universitet, Uppsala 751 06, Sweden irma6504@student.uu.se
Abstract.

We advance Matkovič ideas, originally applied to complete the classification of tight structures on small Seifert fibred LL-spaces, to show the existence of contact structures on Brieskorn spheres which are tight and zero-twisting. This uncovers a phenomenon that has never appeared in literature before: namely, that a contact structure ξ\xi on a 3-manifold can be such that c^​(ξ)\widehat{c}(\xi) is non-vanishing, but c+​(ξ)c^{+}(\xi) is zero.

1. Introduction

In [1] we classified all the fillable structures on a Seifert fibred space with negative-definite standard graph Γ\Gamma; moreover, we showed that they all appear as the boundary of the same compact 4-manifold, which is obtained by blowing down Γ\Gamma completely. Later on, in [2] we classified all the negative-twisting structures when the standard graph is indefinite. Part of these results can be summarised in the following theorem; we refer to [1, 2] for the definition of negative- and zero-twisting tight structures, and the notation.

Theorem 1.1 (Cavallo-Matkovič).

Suppose that M=M​(e0;r1,…,rn)M=M(e_{0};r_{1},...,r_{n}) is a Seifert fibred space, and that ξ\xi is a contact structure on MM. If MM has negative-definite standard graph then the following are equivalent:

  • •

    the structure ξ\xi is Stein fillable;

  • •

    the structure ξ\xi is symplectically fillable;

  • •

    the structure ξ\xi has non-vanishing contact invariant c+​(ξ)c^{+}(\xi);

  • •

    the structure ξ\xi is negative-twisting.

If MM has indefinite standard graph, is not an LL-space, and n=3n=3 then the following are equivalent:

  • •

    the structure ξ\xi is symplectically fillable;

  • •

    the structure ξ\xi has non-vanishing contact invariant c+​(ξ)∈H​Fred​(−M)c^{+}(\xi)\in HF_{\emph{red}}(-M);

  • •

    the structure ξ\xi is negative-twisting.

When the graph is negative-definite the only tight structures possibly left out of our classification would necessarily have vanishing invariant c+c^{+}, and be zero-twisting. Generalising the techniques used by Matkovič in [8], which together with [5, 4, 13] led to the classification of contact structures on small Seifert fibred LL-spaces with indefinite standard graph, that is with e0⩾−1e_{0}\geqslant-1 according to our notation, we show that Theorem 1.1 cannot be strengthened.

Theorem 1.2.

All the 33-manifolds in the infinite family of Seifert fibred spaces Sk3​(T8,13)S^{3}_{k}(T_{8,13}) for k⩽35k\leqslant 35 carry a zero-twisting tight contact structure ξk\xi_{k} such that c^​(ξk)≠0\widehat{c}(\xi_{k})\neq 0, see Figure 1. Furthermore, they provide the first examples of:

  1. (1)

    contact 33-manifolds with non-vanishing c^\widehat{c}, but c+c^{+} equal to zero;

  2. (2)

    zero-twisting structures such that their d3d_{3}-invariant is different from the correction term;

  3. (3)

    tight structures on a small Seifert fibred space that is not an LL-space which are not (weakly) symplectically fillable.

The manifolds in Theorem 1.2 are also one of the first examples of Seifert fibred spaces which admit both negative- and zero-twisting structures compatible with the same orientation. However, the existence of these has already been known to Lisca and Stipsicz, as can be understood from the comment below [6, Theorem 1.2]. In fact, they mention a zero-twisting tight structure on M​(−1;512,13,13)M(-1;\frac{5}{12},\frac{1}{3},\frac{1}{3}) whose d3d_{3}-invariant is equal to the correction term; hence, with non-zero c+c^{+}. According to a result of Matkovič [9, Theorem 1.1] this structure is also not fillable.

Note that when k⩽−1k\leqslant-1 the manifolds above have negative-definite standard graph, while only when k⩾83k\geqslant 83 they are LL-spaces. These structures appear even on Brieskorn spheres as we have that Σ​(8,13,105)≃S−13​(T8,13)\Sigma(8,13,105)\simeq S^{3}_{-1}(T_{8,13}).

Corollary 1.3.

Suppose that MM is a Brieskorn sphere among Σ​(8,13,105)\Sigma(8,13,105) and −Σ​(8,13,103)-\Sigma(8,13,103). Then there exist zero-twisting structures on MM that are not fillable, and are therefore not included in our classification in Theorem 1.1.

2. Zero-twisting structures on small Seifert fibred spaces

We start our discussion here by recalling the seminal result of Lisca and Stispicz about zero-twisting structures. They showed in [6] that every such structure on the space M​(−1;r1,r2,r3)M(-1;r_{1},r_{2},r_{3}) with ri∈ℚ∩(0,1)r_{i}\in\mathbb{Q}\>\cap(0,1) has a contact surgery presentation which consists of an appropriate Legendrian link whose components are a T5,−5T_{5,-5} and some unknots determined as follows.

Let ri=[m1i,…,mkii]r_{i}=[m^{i}_{1},...,m^{i}_{k_{i}}] for i=1,2,3i=1,2,3 be the negative continued fraction of rir_{i}, so that each mjim^{i}_{j} is the framing of a vertex of the standard graph; then there are three chains of unknots, attached to the last three components of T5,−5T_{5,-5}, and the ii-th chain contains ki−1k_{i}-1 unknots. The tb\operatorname{tb}-number of each component of T5,−5T_{5,-5} is given by the vector (−1,−1,m11,m12,m13)(-1,-1,m^{1}_{1},m^{2}_{1},m^{3}_{1}), while on the three legs it is given by (m2i+1,…,mkii+1)(m^{i}_{2}+1,...,m^{i}_{k_{i}}+1) for i=1,2,3i=1,2,3. The structure is then obtained by performing contact (+1)(+1)-surgery on the first two components of T5,−5T_{5,-5}, and contact (−1)(-1)-surgery on all the other Legendrian knots. An example of this construction can be seen in Figure 1.

In a similar way in [4] it is also shown that the zero-twisting structures on M​(0,r1,r2,r3)M(0,r_{1},r_{2},r_{3}) have a contact surgery presentation as the one described above, but without one of the two (+1)(+1)-surgeries. By modifying these constructions we can describe all the zero-twisting structures on M​(e0,r1,r2,r3)M(e_{0},r_{1},r_{2},r_{3}) with e0⩾−1e_{0}\geqslant-1; we do not give details about this generalisation as it is not relevant in this paper, but refer to [4] for details.

There is not yet a complete classification of zero-twisting structures on these small Seifert fibred spaces in general, but either when the structures are fillable, or the manifold is an LL-space, this has been done by Matkovič in [8, 9]. We describe the tools involved in such a classification (when e0=−1e_{0}=-1) before stating the result. Let us consider the vector in Char​(G,𝔰ξ)\text{Char}(G,\mathfrak{s}_{\xi}) whose first coordinate (the one corresponding to the centre) is 11, and the others are given by the rotation numbers in the chains of unknots and by one less than the rotation numbers of the last three components of T5,−5T_{5,-5} in the surgery presentation.

Motivated by the work of Lisca and Stipsicz [7], we describe an algorithm in [8] that has the above vector as an input while it has as an output a vector C∈Char​(G∗,𝔰ξ)C\in\text{Char}(G^{*},\mathfrak{s}_{\xi}); we briefly sketch it here. By [7, Lemma 3.2] the star-shaped plumbed 44-manifolds PGP_{G} embeds into some R=ℂ​P2​#​N​ℂ​P¯2R=\mathbb{C}P^{2}\#N\overline{\mathbb{C}P}^{2} in the way that its complement is diffeomorphic to PG∗P_{G^{*}}. More precisely, the configurations of the two intersection graphs GG and G∗G^{*} are obtained by blowing-up the initial lines l1,…,lk⊂ℂ​P2:l1∩⋯∩lk={p}l_{1},\dots,l_{k}\subset\mathbb{C}P^{2}:l_{1}\cap\cdots\cap l_{k}=\{p\}, and l⊂ℂ​P2:p∉ll\subset\mathbb{C}P^{2}:p\notin l, as shown in Figure 3.

Now, we can extend the vector we read from surgery presentation on PGP_{G} to a cohomology class 𝔠\mathfrak{c} on the whole RR, which we describe through its Poincaré dual by PD​(𝔠):=α​h+∑i=1Nαi​ei\text{PD}(\mathfrak{c}):=\alpha h+\sum_{i=1}^{N}\alpha_{i}e_{i} where hh and eie_{i} denote the standard generators of H2​(R;ℤ)H_{2}(R;\mathbb{Z}) and α,αi∈{±1}\alpha,\alpha_{i}\in\{\pm 1\}. The characteristic vector CC is identified with the restriction 𝔠|PG∗\mathfrak{c}\lvert_{P_{G^{*}}}; note that there is a natural correspondence between the Spinc\operatorname{Spin}^{c}-structures 𝔠|M\mathfrak{c}\lvert_{M} and 𝔠|−M\mathfrak{c}\lvert_{-M}.

From [10] one has that [C]∈Im⁡ψ∗⊂H​F^​(−M,𝔰ξ)[C]\in\operatorname{Im}\psi^{*}\subset\widehat{HF}(-M,\mathfrak{s}_{\xi}) where [C][C] is the full path of CC and ψ∗:H​F−→H​F^\psi^{*}:HF^{-}\rightarrow\widehat{HF} is the map induced by the projection. For the terminology about the full path algorithm we refer to [10, 1, 2]. In [8, Corollary 1.4 and Theorem 2.2] it is shown that CC is well-defined, up to steps in the full-path, and that for LL-spaces it is identified with the contact invariant c^\widehat{c} of the structure. Such a vector has been informally called magic CC, and we are going to refer to it in this way in the paper.

Theorem 2.1 (Matkovič).

Let M=M​(e0;r1,r2,r3)M=M(e_{0};r_{1},r_{2},r_{3}) with e0∈{−1,0}e_{0}\in\{-1,0\} be a Seifert fibred LL-space. Then a contact structure ξ\xi on MM is tight if and only if the full path [C][C] ends correctly; in other words, when c^​(ξ)≠0\widehat{c}(\xi)\neq 0. Furthermore, two tight structures ξ1\xi_{1} and ξ2\xi_{2} on MM are contact isotopic if and only if their magic CC’s are in the same full path; in other words, when c^​(ξ1)=c^​(ξ2)\widehat{c}(\xi_{1})=\widehat{c}(\xi_{2}).

In [8] only e0=−1e_{0}=-1 is addressed explicitly, because the classification of contact structures with e0=0e_{0}=0 was known before [4]. However, magic CC can be defined analogously for e0=0e_{0}=0 and has the same properties; therefore, we include it in the statement of Theorem 2.1.

Our goal now is to show that a similar result holds for any M=M​(−1;r1,r2,r3)M=M(-1;r_{1},r_{2},r_{3}). Since MM can either have indefinite or negative-definite standard graph, according to our work in [2] in the latter case we have [C]∈Ker⁡ρ∗⊂H​F^​(−M,𝔰ξ)[C]\in\operatorname{Ker}\rho^{*}\subset\widehat{HF}(-M,\mathfrak{s}_{\xi}) where ρ∗:H​F^→H​F+\rho^{*}:\widehat{HF}\rightarrow HF^{+} is induced by the inclusion.

Let M​(−1;r1,r2,r3)M(-1;r_{1},r_{2},r_{3}) be a Seifert fibred space and ξ\xi be a contact structure presented as in [6].
