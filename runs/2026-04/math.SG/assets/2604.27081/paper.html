Symplectic structure on the character varieties of Sasakian threefolds

Indranil Biswas Department of Mathematics, Shiv Nadar University, NH91, Tehsil Dadri, Greater Noida, Uttar Pradesh 201314, India indranil.biswas@snu.edu.in, indranil29@gmail.com and Ambar N. Sengupta Department of Mathematics, University of Connecticut, Storrs, CT 06269, USA ambarnsg@gmail.com
Abstract.

Take a compact Sasakian threefold MM and consider the associated irreducible SL​(r,ℂ)\text{SL}(r,{\mathbb{C}})–character variety ℛ:=Hom​(π1​(M,x0),SL​(r,ℂ))i​r/SL​(r,ℂ){\mathcal{R}}\,:=\,\text{Hom}(\pi_{1}(M,\,x_{0}),\,\text{SL}(r,{\mathbb{C}}))^{ir}/\text{SL}(r,{\mathbb{C}}) of MM, where Hom​(π1​(M,x0),SL​(r,ℂ))i​r\text{Hom}(\pi_{1}(M,\,x_{0}),\,\text{SL}(r,{\mathbb{C}}))^{ir} is the space of irreducible homomorphisms. We first construct a natural algebraic 22–form on ℛ\mathcal{R}. Then it is shown that this 22–form is closed. Finally we show that the restriction of this 22–form to Hom​(π1​(M,x0),SU​(r))i​r\text{Hom}(\pi_{1}(M,\,x_{0}),\,\text{SU}(r))^{ir} is symplectic.

Key words and phrases:
Sasakian manifold, character variety, symplectic form, moment map

1. Introduction

Sasakian geometry is generally qualified as an odd-dimensional “analog” of Kähler geometry. In fact, just as the contact manifolds serve as substitutes for symplectic manifolds in odd dimensions, the Sasakian manifolds are the odd dimensional counterparts of Kähler manifolds. Although they are around for quite some time, in recent years they were extensively studied. This resurgence is substantially influenced by the recently found relevance of Sasakian manifolds in string theory. C. Boyer and K. Galicki published a series of papers investigating various differential geometric aspects of Sasakian manifolds (see [BG] and references therein). Sasakian manifolds appeared in string theory through the work of J. Maldacena [Ma]; see the papers of J. Sparks, [Sp1], [Sp2], [Sp3], [MS], [GMSY], [MSY], and references therein for more details.

We consider compact Sasakian manifolds of dimension three. Take a three dimensional compact Sasakian manifold MM, and consider the irreducible homomorphisms from π1​(M,x0)\pi_{1}(M,\,x_{0}), where x0∈Mx_{0}\,\in\,M is a base point, to SL​(r,ℂ)\text{SL}(r,{\mathbb{C}}). Each connected component of the irreducible character variety ℛ:=Hom​(π1​(M,x0),SL​(r,ℂ))i​r/SL​(r,ℂ){\mathcal{R}}\,:=\,\text{Hom}(\pi_{1}(M,\,x_{0}),\,\text{SL}(r,{\mathbb{C}}))^{ir}/\text{SL}(r,{\mathbb{C}}) is a smooth affine variety defined over the complex numbers.

We construct a holomorphic 22–form on ℛ{\mathcal{R}} (see Theorem 4.2), which is denoted by ℬ\mathcal{B}. We prove that the 22–form ℬ\mathcal{B} on ℛ\mathcal{R} is closed (see Theorem 5.2). Finally we show that the restriction of ℬ\mathcal{B} to

Hom(π1(M,x0),SU(r)i​r⊂ℛ\text{Hom}(\pi_{1}(M,\,x_{0}),\,\text{SU}(r)^{ir}\ \subset\ \mathcal{R}

is a symplectic form (see Theorem 6.2).

This work has been much inspired by [Go] and [AB]. Our construction of the form ℬ\mathcal{B} is a three-dimensional generalization of the construction of Goldman on the character variety of a compact oriented surface (see [Go]). The proof of Theorem 6.2 uses the ideas of [AB].

The symplectic structure also arises from a different setting, in the context of Yang-Mills gauge theory.

2. Sasakian manifolds

Let MM be a (2​n+1)(2n+1)-dimensional real C∞C^{\infty} orientable manifold. Let T​Mℂ=T​M⊗ℝℂTM_{\mathbb{C}}\,=\,TM\otimes_{\mathbb{R}}{\mathbb{C}} be its complexified tangent bundle. The Lie bracket operation on the locally defined vector fields on MM extends to a Lie bracket operation on the locally defined C∞C^{\infty} sections of T​MℂTM_{\mathbb{C}}. A complex subbundle of T​MℂTM_{\mathbb{C}} whose sheaf of sections are closed under the Lie bracket operation is called integrable.

A CR-structure on MM is an nn-dimensional complex sub-bundle T1,0​MT^{1,0}M of T​MℂTM_{\mathbb{C}} such that T1,0​M∩T1,0​M¯={0}T^{1,0}M\cap\overline{T^{1,0}M}\,=\,\{0\} and T1,0​MT^{1,0}M is integrable. Given such a subbundle T1,0​MT^{1,0}M, there is a unique sub-bundle SS of rank 2​n2n of the real tangent bundle T​MTM together with a vector bundle homomorphism I:S⟶SI\,:\,S\,\longrightarrow\,S satisfying the following two conditions:

  1. (1)

    I2=−IdSI^{2}\,=\,-{\rm Id}_{S}, and

  2. (2)

    T1,0​MT^{1,0}M is the −1\sqrt{-1}–eigenbundle of II acting on S⊗ℝℂS\otimes_{\mathbb{R}}{\mathbb{C}}.

The subbundle T1,0​M¯\overline{T^{1,0}M} of T​MℂTM_{\mathbb{C}} will be denoted by T0,1​MT^{0,1}M.

A (2​n+1)(2n+1)-dimensional manifold MM equipped with a triple (T1,0​M,S,I)(T^{1,0}M,\,S,\,I) as above is called a CR-manifold. A contact CR-manifold is a CR-manifold MM with a contact 11-form η\eta on MM such that kernel​(η)=S{\rm kernel}(\eta)\,=\,S. The Reeb vector field is the unique vector field on ξ\xi on MM such that iξ​(d​η)= 0i_{\xi}(d\eta)\,=\,0 and iξ​η= 1i_{\xi}\eta\,=\,1. On a contact CR-manifold MM, the above homomorphism II extends to entire T​MTM by setting I​(ξ)= 0I(\xi)\,=\,0.

Definition 2.1.

A contact CR-manifold (M,(T1,0​M,S,I),(η,ξ))(M,\,(T^{1,0}M,\,S,\,I),\,(\eta,\,\xi)) is a strongly pseudo-convex CR-manifold if the Hermitian form LηL_{\eta} on SxS_{x} defined by

Lη​(X,Y)=d​η​(X,I​Y),X,Y∈Sx,L_{\eta}(X,\,Y)\,=\,d\eta(X,\,IY),\ \ \,X,\,Y\,\in\,S_{x},

is positive definite for every point x∈Mx\,\in\,M.

Given any strongly pseudo-convex CR-manifold (M,(T1,0​M,S,I),(η,ξ))(M,\,(T^{1,0}M,\,S,\,I),\,(\eta,\,\xi)), there is a canonical Riemannian metric gηg_{\eta} on MM which is defined to be

gη​(X,Y):=Lη​(X,Y)+η​(X)​η​(Y),X,Y∈Tx​M,x∈M.g_{\eta}(X,Y)\ :=\ L_{\eta}(X,Y)+\eta(X)\eta(Y),\ \ \,X,\,Y\,\in\,T_{x}M,\,\ x\,\in\,M.
Definition 2.2.

A Sasakian manifold is a strongly pseudo-convex CR-manifold

(M,(T1,0,S,I),(η,ξ))(M,\,(T^{1,0},\,S,\,I),\,(\eta,\,\xi))

satisfying the condition that

[ξ,C∞​(M;T1,0​M)]⊂C∞​(M;T1,0​M).[\xi,\,C^{\infty}(M;\,T^{1,0}M)]\,\subset\,C^{\infty}(M;\,T^{1,0}M).

In this case, the canonical metric gηg_{\eta} is called the Sasakian metric.

For the Sasakian metric gηg_{\eta}, the Reeb vector field ξ\xi is Killing and also |ξ|= 1|\xi|\,=\,1. For a Sasakian manifold (M,(T1,0,S,I),(η,ξ))(M,\,(T^{1,0},\,S,\,I),\,(\eta,\,\xi)), the Reeb vector filed ξ\xi induces a 11-dimensional foliation; it is called the Reeb foliation. The Reeb foliation will be denoted by

ℱξ⊂T​M.{\mathcal{F}}_{\xi}\ \subset\ TM. (2.1)

The sub-bundle 𝒢ξ=T1,0​M⊕T​ℱξ⊂T​Mℂ{\mathcal{G}}_{\xi}\,=\,T^{1,0}M\oplus T{\mathcal{F}}_{\xi}\subset TM_{\mathbb{C}} produces a transversely holomorphic structure for the foliation ℱξ{\mathcal{F}}_{\xi}. The form d​ηd\eta is a transversely Kähler structure. See [BG] and references therein for Sasakian manifolds.

A compact Sasakian manifold

(M,(T1,0,S,I),(η,ξ))(M,\,(T^{1,0},\,S,\,I),\,(\eta,\,\xi))

is called quasi-regular if every leaf of the foliation ℱξ{\mathcal{F}}_{\xi} is closed. For any given compact Sasakian manifold (M,(T1,0,S,I),(η,ξ))(M,\,(T^{1,0},\,S,\,I),\,(\eta,\,\xi)), there is another contact form η′\eta^{\prime} with the Reeb vector field ξ′\xi^{\prime} so that (M,(T1,0,S,I),(η′,ξ′))(M,\,(T^{1,0},\,S,\,I),\,(\eta^{\prime},\,\xi^{\prime})) is quasi-regular [BG, Section 8.2.3]; see also [Ru], [OV]. More precisely, we can take a Killing vector field χ\chi commuting with ξ\xi such that ξ′=ξ+χ\xi^{\prime}\,=\,\xi+\chi and η′=η1+η​(χ)\eta^{\prime}\,=\,\frac{\eta}{1+\eta(\chi)}.

We give some simple examples of Sasakian manifolds; many more can be found in [BG].

Take a complex projective manifold XX. Chose a Kähler form ω\omega on XX such that the cohomology class of ω\omega — which is an element of H2​(X,ℝ)H^{2}(X,\,{\mathbb{R}}) — lies in the image of H2​(X,ℤ)H^{2}(X,\,{\mathbb{Z}}). Then there is a holomorphic line bundle LL on XX equipped with a Hermitian structure hh such that the curvature of the corresponding Chern connection ∇L,h\nabla^{L,h} is ω\omega. Consider the unit circle bundle

M:={v∈L|‖v‖h= 1}⊂L.M\ :=\ \{v\,\in\,L\ \big|\ \,||v||_{h}\,=\,1\}\ \subset\ L. (2.2)

The connection ∇L,h\nabla^{L,h} on LL preserves MM. Therefore, the tangent bundle T​MTM decomposes into a direct sum of horizontal and vertical tangent bundles. The horizontal tangent subbundle of T​MTM will be denoted by SS. The multiplication action of the unit circle S1=U​(1)S^{1}\,=\,{\rm U}(1) on MM produces a vertical vector field; it will be denoted by ξ\xi. Using the decomposition T​M=S⊕ℂ⋅ξTM\,=\,S\oplus{\mathbb{C}}\cdot\xi, the Kähler form ω\omega and the standard metric on S1S^{1} together produce a Riemannian metric on MM; this Riemannian metric will be denoted by gηg_{\eta}. More precisely, ω\omega produces a positive symmetric form on SS (as SS is identified with the pullback of the tangent bundle of XX) and standard metric on S1S^{1} produces a positive symmetric form on T​ℱξ:=ℂ⋅ξT{\mathcal{F}}_{\xi}\,:=\,{\mathbb{C}}\cdot\xi (using the multiplication action of S1=U​(1)S^{1}\,=\,{\rm U}(1) on MM); the metric gηg_{\eta} is the direct sum of these two. The connection form defining ∇L,h\nabla^{L,h} produces a contact form η\eta on MM; so the kernel of η\eta is the horizontal tangent bundle. The almost complex structure on XX produces a homomorphism I:S⟶SI\,:\,S\,\longrightarrow\,S such that I2=−IdSI^{2}\,=\,-{\rm Id}_{S}. These data (M,g,S,ξ,η,I)(M,\,g,\,S,\,\xi,\,\eta,\,I) define a Sasakian manifold. Every leaf of the foliation ℱξ\mathcal{F}_{\xi} is closed; in fact, MM is a principal S1S^{1}–bundle over the leaf-space XX. These are called regular Sasakian manifolds.

Suppose a finite group Γ\Gamma acts on XX via holomorphic isometries (for the Kähler structure ω\omega) such that the action of lift to an action of Γ\Gamma on LL via holomorphic isometries (for hh). So the action of Γ\Gamma on LL preserves MM defined in (2.2). Assume that the action of Γ\Gamma on MM is free. Note that the action of Γ\Gamma on XX need not be free. Then M/ΓM/\Gamma is a Sasakian manifold. In fact, it is a quasi-regular Sasakian manifold. If the action of Γ\Gamma on XX is not free, then M/ΓM/\Gamma is not a regular Sasakian manifold.

Here we are interested in Sasakian manifolds of dimension three. In other words, it will be assumed that n= 1n\,=\,1.
