Which Phases Are Thermodynamically Realizable? A Local Entropy Criterion

C. Evans Hedges
(Date: April 11, 2026)
Abstract.

In the variational approach to statistical mechanics, equilibrium states are the rigorous analogues of thermodynamic phases; the question of which invariant measures can arise as equilibrium states is therefore the question of which phases are thermodynamically realizable. We prove that for continuous actions of locally compact amenable groups on compact metrizable spaces with finite topological entropy, an ergodic measure μ\mu is an equilibrium state for some continuous potential if and only if the entropy map hh is upper semicontinuous at μ\mu; equivalently, the unrealizable phases are exactly those hidden behind the convex envelope of the free energy.

More generally, the same criterion applies whenever (X,T)(X,T) has bounded entropy and embeds as an invariant subsystem of a compact metrizable system. As a canonical case, one-point compactification yields a C0C_{0}-potential realization theorem for locally compact σ\sigma-compact systems, with applications to countable-state Markov shifts. We also show that the equilibrium-face realization stated by Jenkinson (2006) omits a necessary continuity hypothesis, exhibiting a counterexample on the full shift, and give the sharp corrected statement: a weak-∗* closed set ℰ\mathcal{E} of ergodic measures determines an equilibrium face if and only if h|ℰh|_{\mathcal{E}} is continuous and hh is upper semicontinuous at each point of ℰ\mathcal{E}.

Key words and phrases:
Equilibrium states, thermodynamic formalism, Choquet simplex, entropy, ergodic optimization, variational principle, Maxwell construction, Markov shifts

1. Introduction

In the variational formulation of thermodynamic formalism, equilibrium states play the role of thermodynamic phases: they are the invariant measures that maximize the pressure h​(μ)+∫ϕ​𝑑μh(\mu)+\int\phi\,d\mu (equivalently minimize the free energy) for a given potential (interaction) ϕ\phi. A basic question is which phases are thermodynamically realizable, i.e., which invariant measures arise as equilibrium states for some continuous potential. This paper gives a complete answer: an ergodic measure μ\mu is attainable as an equilibrium state if and only if the entropy map hh is upper semicontinuous at μ\mu. The obstruction to realizability is thus a local regularity condition on the entropy landscape near the phase in question. This result extends to weak-∗* closed sets of ergodic measures ℰ\mathcal{E} with the additional hypothesis that hh is continuous when restricted to ℰ\mathcal{E}. More generally, the criterion applies whenever (X,T)(X,T) embeds as an invariant subsystem of a compact metrizable system with bounded entropy; one-point compactification then yields a C0C_{0}-potential realization theorem for locally compact σ\sigma-compact systems, with applications to countable-state Markov shifts.

The equilibrium-state realization problem sits between classical thermodynamic formalism and ergodic optimization. In lattice statistical mechanics, the Gibbs/equilibrium correspondence goes back to Dobrushin [2] and Lanford–Ruelle [12]; in uniformly hyperbolic dynamics, the corresponding framework was developed by Sinai [17], Bowen [1], Ruelle [16], and Walters [18]. Israel [9] recast the theory in a convex-analytic framework developed specifically for lattice gas models, and the Israel–Phelps [8] study of the subdifferential of the pressure was motivated by the geometry of phase diagrams. In the Choquet-theoretic setting, Phelps [15] proved that when the entropy function on the simplex of invariant measures is affine and upper semicontinuous, every ergodic measure is the unique equilibrium state of some continuous potential. On the ergodic-optimization side, Jenkinson [11] proved that every ergodic measure is uniquely maximizing and stated that, under global upper semicontinuity, weak-∗* closed ergodic sets generate equilibrium faces (see also [10]). The main compact result of the present paper is a local sharpening of the Israel–Phelps framework, replacing global upper semicontinuity of the entropy map by upper semicontinuity at the chosen ergodic measure.

Specifically, let GG be a locally compact amenable group acting on a compact metric space XX via homeomorphisms {Tg}g∈G\{T_{g}\}_{g\in G}. Let MT​(X)M_{T}(X) be the collection of TT-invariant probability measures on XX and let C​(X)C(X) be the collection of all real-valued potentials on XX. We say that μ∈MT​(X)\mu\in M_{T}(X) is an equilibrium state for ϕ∈C​(X)\phi\in C(X) if h​(μ)+∫ϕ​𝑑μ=P​(ϕ)h(\mu)+\int\phi d\mu=P(\phi) where hh is the Kolmogorov-Sinai entropy map and PP is the pressure function, which via the variational principle can be shown to satisfy P​(ϕ)=sup{h​(ν)+∫ϕ​𝑑ν:ν∈MT​(X)}P(\phi)=\sup\{h(\nu)+\int\phi d\nu:\nu\in M_{T}(X)\}. Throughout, upper semicontinuity of hh refers to the weak-∗* topology on MT​(X)M_{T}(X). We can now state our primary theorem:

Theorem 1.

If μ∈MTe​r​g​(X)\mu\in M_{T}^{erg}(X) and hh is upper semicontinuous at μ\mu, then there exists ϕ∈C​(X)\phi\in C(X) such that μ\mu is the unique equilibrium state for ϕ\phi.

Since the converse is immediate, we obtain:

Corollary 2.

For an ergodic μ∈MTe​r​g​(X)\mu\in M_{T}^{erg}(X), the following are equivalent:

  1. (1)

    hh is upper semicontinuous at μ\mu.

  2. (2)

    μ\mu is an equilibrium state for some ϕ∈C​(X)\phi\in C(X).

  3. (3)

    μ\mu is the unique equilibrium state for some ϕ∈C​(X)\phi\in C(X).

We note here that by combining Corollary 2 with [5], we see that μ\mu is a freezing state for some potential if and only if hh is upper semicontinuous at μ\mu.

Corollary 3.2 provides an equivalent formulation via the pointwise dual formula: for ergodic μ\mu, hh is upper semicontinuous at μ\mu if and only if

h​(μ)=minϕ∈C​(X)⁡(P​(ϕ)−∫ϕ​𝑑μ).h(\mu)=\min_{\phi\in C(X)}\left(P(\phi)-\int\phi\,d\mu\right).

This dual formula admits a convex-analytic interpretation in the spirit of Israel [9] and Israel–Phelps [8]. Setting Φ​(μ)=−h​(μ)\Phi(\mu)=-h(\mu), the pressure P=Φ∗P=\Phi^{*} is the Legendre-Fenchel conjugate, and the biconjugate Φ∗∗​(μ)=supϕ(ϕ^​(μ)−P​(ϕ))=−infϕ(P​(ϕ)−ϕ^​(μ))\Phi^{**}(\mu)=\sup_{\phi}(\hat{\phi}(\mu)-P(\phi))=-\inf_{\phi}(P(\phi)-\hat{\phi}(\mu)) recovers the continuous-affine majorant envelope of hh at μ\mu. The dual formula is then a pointwise Fenchel-Moreau theorem: Φ∗∗​(μ)=Φ​(μ)\Phi^{**}(\mu)=\Phi(\mu) if and only if Φ\Phi is lower semicontinuous at μ\mu.

In the language of classical thermodynamics, the passage from Φ\Phi to Φ∗∗\Phi^{**} is the Maxwell construction (see [9]): the biconjugate replaces a non-convex free energy with its convex envelope, and phases where Φ∗∗​(μ)<Φ​(μ)\Phi^{**}(\mu)<\Phi(\mu) are thermodynamically inaccessible. Our main result thus characterizes the realizable pure phases as exactly those not hidden behind the Maxwell construction: an ergodic measure is an equilibrium state for some interaction if and only if it lies on the convex envelope of the free energy landscape.

The singleton result extends to the equilibrium-face realization problem. In [11], Jenkinson stated (Theorems 5 and 6) that under global upper semicontinuity of entropy, every weak-∗* closed set of ergodic measures determines an equilibrium face. We show that this formulation omits a necessary continuity hypothesis (Example 3.4), and give the corrected statement:

Theorem 3.

Let ℰ⊂MTe​r​g​(X)\mathcal{E}\subset M_{T}^{erg}(X) be nonempty and weak-∗* closed. If h|ℰh|_{\mathcal{E}} is weak-∗* continuous and hh is upper semicontinuous at every μ∈ℰ\mu\in\mathcal{E}, then there exists ϕ∈C​(X)\phi\in C(X) such that the set of equilibrium states for ϕ\phi is exactly co¯​(ℰ)\overline{\operatorname{co}}(\mathcal{E}).

The continuity hypothesis on h|ℰh|_{\mathcal{E}} is sharp: if F=Eq⁡(ϕ)F=\operatorname{Eq}(\phi) for any ϕ∈C​(X)\phi\in C(X), then h|Fh|_{F} is automatically continuous affine (Proposition 3.3). In physical terms, the face realization problem asks which families of pure thermodynamic phases can coexist at equilibrium for a single interaction; our corrected theorem shows that coexistence requires both a local entropy regularity condition at each phase and continuity of entropy across the coexisting phases.

These results extend beyond the compact setting. If (X,T)(X,T) embeds as an invariant subsystem of a compact metrizable system (X¯,T¯)(\bar{X},\bar{T}) and the entropy map on MT​(X)M_{T}(X) is bounded, the realization theorem carries over. Natural examples include countable-state Markov shifts, which embed into compact shift spaces via one-point compactification of the alphabet; geodesic flows on non-compact finite-volume hyperbolic manifolds, which admit compactifications to which the flow extends; and amenable group actions on non-compact homogeneous spaces that admit equivariant compactifications.

Theorem 4.

Let (X¯,T¯)(\bar{X},\bar{T}) be a compact metrizable system, let X⊂X¯X\subset\bar{X} be T¯\bar{T}-invariant, and suppose h:MT​(X)→[0,∞)h:M_{T}(X)\to[0,\infty) is bounded. If μ∈MTe​r​g​(X)\mu\in M_{T}^{erg}(X) and hh is upper semicontinuous at μ\mu, then there exists ϕ∈C​(X¯)\phi\in C(\bar{X}) such that μ\mu is the unique equilibrium state for ϕ|X\phi|_{X}.

When XX is locally compact and σ\sigma-compact, one-point compactification provides a canonical ambient compact system and upgrades the potential to C0​(X)C_{0}(X):

Theorem 5.

Let GG be a locally compact amenable group acting continuously by homeomorphisms on a metrizable, locally compact, σ\sigma-compact space XX, with supμ∈MT​(X)h​(μ)<∞\sup_{\mu\in M_{T}(X)}h(\mu)<\infty. Let μ∈MTe​r​g​(X)\mu\in M_{T}^{erg}(X). Then hh is upper semicontinuous at μ\mu if and only if there exists f∈C0​(X)f\in C_{0}(X) such that μ\mu is the unique equilibrium state for ff.

By [6], we have an immediate application to countable-state Markov shifts:

Corollary 6.

Let XX be a locally compact two-sided countable-state Markov shift with supμh​(μ)<∞\sup_{\mu}h(\mu)<\infty. For every ergodic μ∈Mσ​(X)\mu\in M_{\sigma}(X), there exists f∈C0​(X)f\in C_{0}(X) such that μ\mu is the unique equilibrium state for ff.

The structure of the rest of this paper is as follows: we begin with Section 2 on the relevant preliminaries and definitions. In Section 3 we prove our main results, starting in the abstract Choquet setting and moving to the dynamical setting, as well as identifying a few immediate consequences of these results. In Section 4 we extend the results to non-compact systems: first via a general invariant-subsystem embedding, then via one-point compactification to locally compact σ\sigma-compact systems with C0C_{0} potentials, and finally with an application to countable-state Markov shifts.

2. Preliminaries

2.1. Choquet simplices and facial topology

We refer to Phelps [15] for background on Choquet simplices. Let KK be a compact metrizable Choquet simplex with extreme boundary E=ex⁡(K)E=\operatorname{ex}(K). Write A​(K)A(K) for the space of continuous affine real-valued functions on KK. For each x∈Kx\in K, let ξx\xi_{x} denote the unique representing Borel probability measure on EE, characterized by a​(x)=∫Ea​(η)​𝑑ξx​(η)a(x)=\int_{E}a(\eta)\,d\xi_{x}(\eta) for all a∈A​(K)a\in A(K).

A bounded Borel function f:K→ℝf:K\to\mathbb{R} is strongly affine if f​(x)=∫Ef​(η)​𝑑ξx​(η)f(x)=\int_{E}f(\eta)\,d\xi_{x}(\eta) for all x∈Kx\in K; bounded Borel strong affineness is the only regularity assumption on the entropy map used throughout this paper. A nonempty convex subset F⊂KF\subset K is a face if α​x+(1−α)​y∈F\alpha x+(1-\alpha)y\in F with 0<α<10<\alpha<1 implies x,y∈Fx,y\in F. The facial topology on EE, denoted ds​Ed_{s}E, is the weakest topology making a|Ea|_{E} continuous for every a∈A​(K)a\in A(K). This topology is the natural one in which to construct continuous functions on EE since they admit continuous affine extensions on all of KK by Lau’s extension theorem:

Theorem 2.1 (Lau [13]; see also [15, Theorem 11.7]).

Every continuous function g:(E,ds​E)→ℝg:(E,d_{s}E)\to\mathbb{R} extends to a function a∈A​(K)a\in A(K).

2.2. Invariant measures, entropy, and pressure

Let XX be a compact metrizable space and GG a locally compact amenable group acting continuously on XX by homeomorphisms, denoted T={Tg}g∈GT=\{T_{g}\}_{g\in G}. Let MT​(X)M_{T}(X) denote the set of TT-invariant Borel probability measures on XX, equipped with the weak-∗* topology, and MTe​r​g​(X)M_{T}^{erg}(X) for the ergodic invariant measures. In the compact amenable-group setting, MT​(X)M_{T}(X) is nonempty, and is a compact metrizable Choquet simplex under the weak-∗* topology with ex⁡(MT​(X))=MTe​r​g​(X)\operatorname{ex}(M_{T}(X))=M_{T}^{erg}(X) (see e.g. [15]). Further, for each μ∈MT​(X)\mu\in M_{T}(X), the representing measure ξμ\xi_{\mu} on MTe​r​g​(X)M_{T}^{erg}(X) is the ergodic decomposition of μ\mu.

In the dynamical setting we also have the two following important simplifications relative to the general Choquet simplex case. First, every continuous affine function on MT​(X)M_{T}(X) has the form μ↦∫f​𝑑μ\mu\mapsto\int f\,d\mu for some f∈C​(X)f\in C(X) [15, 19]. As a consequence, the facial topology on MTe​r​g​(X)M_{T}^{erg}(X) coincides with the weak-∗* subspace topology and we provide a brief proof here for context.

Proposition 2.2.

The facial topology on MTe​r​g​(X)M_{T}^{erg}(X) coincides with the weak-∗* subspace topology inherited from MT​(X)M_{T}(X).

Proof.

Set K=MT​(X)K=M_{T}(X) and E=MTe​r​g​(X)=ex⁡(K)E=M_{T}^{erg}(X)=\operatorname{ex}(K). By definition, ds​Ed_{s}E is the weakest topology on EE for which a|Ea|_{E} is continuous for every a∈A​(K)a\in A(K). By the identification quoted above, these are exactly the maps μ↦∫f​𝑑μ\mu\mapsto\int f\,d\mu with f∈C​(X)f\in C(X) (possibly represented by more than one ff, which does not change which maps are required to be continuous).

The weak-∗* topology on KK is the weakest topology for which μ↦∫f​𝑑μ\mu\mapsto\int f\,d\mu is continuous on KK for every f∈C​(X)f\in C(X), hence the subspace topology on EE is the weakest topology for which those same maps are continuous on EE. Thus ds​Ed_{s}E agrees with the weak-∗* subspace topology. ∎

In particular, Proposition 2.2 ensures that functions constructed on MTe​r​g​(X)M_{T}^{erg}(X) that are continuous in the facial topology are automatically weak-∗* continuous, and vice versa.

For the dynamical results below, we take h:MT​(X)→[0,∞)h:M_{T}(X)\to[0,\infty) to be the Kolmogorov–Sinai entropy map, defined via the Ornstein–Weiss formalism for amenable-group actions (see [3]). However, the proofs in Section 3 use only three properties of hh:

  1. (1)

    hh is strongly affine: h​(μ)=∫MTe​r​g​(X)h​(η)​𝑑ξμ​(η)h(\mu)=\int_{M_{T}^{erg}(X)}h(\eta)\,d\xi_{\mu}(\eta) for all μ∈MT​(X)\mu\in M_{T}(X),

  2. (2)

    htop​(X,T)=supμ∈MT​(X)h​(μ)<∞h_{\mathrm{top}}(X,T)=\sup_{\mu\in M_{T}(X)}h(\mu)<\infty, and

  3. (3)

    the variational principle [14, 3]: P​(ϕ)=supμ∈MT​(X)(h​(μ)+∫ϕ​𝑑μ)P(\phi)=\sup_{\mu\in M_{T}(X)}\left(h(\mu)+\int\phi\,d\mu\right) for every ϕ∈C​(X)\phi\in C(X).

These three properties hold for continuous actions of locally compact amenable groups on compact metrizable spaces with finite topological entropy, but may hold for other dynamical systems outside of the amenable group setting.

For ϕ∈C​(X)\phi\in C(X), define the set of equilibrium states Eq⁡(ϕ)={μ∈MT​(X):P​(ϕ)=h​(μ)+∫ϕ​𝑑μ}\operatorname{Eq}(\phi)=\{\mu\in M_{T}(X):P(\phi)=h(\mu)+\int\phi\,d\mu\}. In order to construct such measures in the sequel it will be useful to use the following fact from [11]:

Theorem 2.3 (Jenkinson [11]).

For every ergodic μ∈MTe​r​g​(X)\mu\in M_{T}^{erg}(X), there exists ϕ∈C​(X)\phi\in C(X) such that μ\mu is the unique measure in MT​(X)M_{T}(X) satisfying ∫ϕ​𝑑μ=supν∈MT​(X)∫ϕ​𝑑ν\int\phi\,d\mu=\sup_{\nu\in M_{T}(X)}\int\phi\,d\nu. More generally, if ℰ⊂MTe​r​g​(X)\mathcal{E}\subset M_{T}^{erg}(X) is nonempty and weak-∗* closed, then there exists ϕ∈C​(X)\phi\in C(X) such that co¯​(ℰ)\overline{\operatorname{co}}(\mathcal{E}) is exactly the set of measures maximizing ν↦∫ϕ​𝑑ν\nu\mapsto\int\phi\,d\nu.

3. Primary Results

In this section we prove our main results, starting in the abstract Choquet setting with Theorem 3.1, which will serve as the foundation for the dynamical implications.

3.1. Abstract Choquet Theorem

Theorem 3.1.

Let KK be a compact metrizable Choquet simplex, let E=ex⁡(K)E=\operatorname{ex}(K), and let A​(K)A(K) denote the continuous affine real-valued functions on KK. Let h:K→ℝh:K\rightarrow\mathbb{R} be bounded and strongly affine and fix μ∈E\mu\in E. If

  1. (1)

    hh is upper semicontinuous at μ\mu and

  2. (2)

    there exists p∈A​(K)p\in A(K) with p≥0p\geq 0 and p−1​(0)={μ}p^{-1}(0)=\{\mu\},

In the dynamical setting this hypothesis is automatic, and the full argument appears in Theorem 3.

3.2. Dynamical Implications

We now turn to the setting of dynamical systems. Although the theorem below is stated for locally compact amenable groups with finite topological entropy, the proof relies only on the three abstract properties of hh listed in Section 2.2. It therefore applies to any group action for which MT​(X)M_{T}(X) is a Choquet simplex and the entropy map satisfies strong affineness, boundedness, and the variational principle.

Theorem 1.

Let GG be a locally compact amenable group acting on XX by TT with finite topological entropy. Let μ∈MTe​r​g​(X)\mu\in M_{T}^{erg}(X). If hh is upper semicontinuous at μ\mu, then there exists ϕ∈C​(X)\phi\in C(X) such that μ\mu is the unique equilibrium state for ϕ\phi.

Proof.

Set K=MT​(X)K=M_{T}(X) and E=MTe​r​g​(X)E=M_{T}^{erg}(X). It follows that KK is a compact metrizable Choquet simplex with E=ex⁡(K)E=\operatorname{ex}(K), and hh is strongly affine. By Jenkinson [11], there exists u∈C​(X)u\in C(X) such that μ\mu is the unique maximizing measure for uu. Define for all ν∈K\nu\in K

p​(ν)=∫u​𝑑μ−∫u​𝑑ν.p(\nu)=\int u\,d\mu-\int u\,d\nu.

Then p∈A​(K)p\in A(K), p≥0p\geq 0, and p−1​(0)={μ}p^{-1}(0)=\{\mu\}.

By application of Theorem 3.1, we obtain a∈A​(K)a\in A(K) such that

a≥hon ​K,a​(μ)=h​(μ).a\geq h\quad\text{on }K,\qquad a(\mu)=h(\mu).

As noted in Section 2, there exists f∈C​(X)f\in C(X) such that a​(ν)=∫f​𝑑νa(\nu)=\int f\,d\nu for all ν∈K\nu\in K. Define ϕ=u−f\phi=u-f. Then for every ν∈K\nu\in K,

h​(ν)+∫ϕ​𝑑ν=h​(ν)−a​(ν)+∫u​𝑑ν≤∫u​𝑑ν≤∫u​𝑑μ=h​(μ)−a​(μ)+∫u​𝑑μ=h​(μ)+∫ϕ​𝑑μ.h(\nu)+\int\phi\,d\nu=h(\nu)-a(\nu)+\int u\,d\nu\leq\int u\,d\nu\leq\int u\,d\mu=h(\mu)-a(\mu)+\int u\,d\mu=h(\mu)+\int\phi\,d\mu.

Since μ\mu is the unique maximizing measure for uu, equality in ∫u​𝑑ν≤∫u​𝑑μ\int u\,d\nu\leq\int u\,d\mu forces ν=μ\nu=\mu. Hence μ\mu is the unique equilibrium state for ϕ\phi. ∎

As a corollary we arrive at the following complete equivalence:

Corollary 2.

For an ergodic μ∈MTe​r​g​(X)\mu\in M_{T}^{erg}(X), the following are equivalent:

  1. (1)

    hh is upper semicontinuous at μ\mu.

  2. (2)

    μ\mu is an equilibrium state for some ϕ∈C​(X)\phi\in C(X).

  3. (3)

    μ\mu is the unique equilibrium state for some ϕ∈C​(X)\phi\in C(X).

Additionally, the following pointwise dual statement follows immediately from the definition of pressure combined with Corollary 2:

Corollary 3.2.

For an ergodic μ\mu,

h​(μ)=minϕ∈C​(X)⁡(P​(ϕ)−∫ϕ​𝑑μ)h(\mu)=\min_{\phi\in C(X)}\left(P(\phi)-\int\phi\,d\mu\right)

if and only if hh is upper semicontinuous at μ\mu.

Equivalently: for ergodic μ\mu, hh is upper semicontinuous at μ\mu if and only if there exists a∈A​(K)a\in A(K) with a≥ha\geq h and a​(μ)=h​(μ)a(\mu)=h(\mu).

3.3. Ergodic Collection Realizability

Next we can generalize Theorem 1 to closed families of ergodic measures. However we begin by observing that continuity of entropy on the prescribed ergodic boundary is a necessary condition for face realization.

Proposition 3.3.

If F⊂MT​(X)F\subset M_{T}(X) is the set of equilibrium states for some ϕ∈C​(X)\phi\in C(X), then h|Fh|_{F} is continuous affine. In particular, if F=co¯​(ℰ)F=\overline{\operatorname{co}}(\mathcal{E}) for a weak-∗* closed ergodic set ℰ⊂MTe​r​g​(X)\mathcal{E}\subset M_{T}^{erg}(X), then h|ℰh|_{\mathcal{E}} must be weak-∗* continuous.

Proof.

If ν∈F=Eq⁡(ϕ)\nu\in F=\operatorname{Eq}(\phi), then h​(ν)+∫ϕ​𝑑ν=P​(ϕ)h(\nu)+\int\phi\,d\nu=P(\phi), so h​(ν)=P​(ϕ)−∫ϕ​𝑑νh(\nu)=P(\phi)-\int\phi\,d\nu. The right-hand side is continuous and affine in ν\nu. ∎

In [11], Jenkinson states (Theorems 5 and 6) that if the entropy map is globally upper semicontinuous and ℰ⊂MTe​r​g​(X)\mathcal{E}\subset M_{T}^{erg}(X) is nonempty and weak-∗* closed, then there exists ϕ∈C​(X)\phi\in C(X) with Eq⁡(ϕ)=co¯​(ℰ)\operatorname{Eq}(\phi)=\overline{\operatorname{co}}(\mathcal{E}). This formulation omits a necessary continuity hypothesis, as the following example shows.

Example 3.4.

Let (X,T)(X,T) be the full ℤ\mathbb{Z}-shift on two symbols. The entropy map is globally upper semicontinuous. Let (δn)n≥1(\delta_{n})_{n\geq 1} be a sequence of distinct periodic orbit measures converging weak-∗* to the Bernoulli(1/2,1/2)(1/2,1/2) measure μ\mu. Set ℰ={δn:n≥1}∪{μ}\mathcal{E}=\{\delta_{n}:n\geq 1\}\cup\{\mu\}. Then ℰ\mathcal{E} is weak-∗* closed, but h​(δn)=0h(\delta_{n})=0 for all nn while h​(μ)=log⁡2h(\mu)=\log 2, so h|ℰh|_{\mathcal{E}} is not continuous. By Proposition 3.3, co¯​(ℰ)\overline{\operatorname{co}}(\mathcal{E}) cannot be the equilibrium set of any continuous potential.

This continuity issue has been observed independently. Iommi and Velozo [7], studying face realization for countable Markov shifts, explicitly note that an additional continuity assumption appears to be required in Jenkinson’s Theorems 5 and 6. Wolf [20], in a related setting, observes that a continuity claim traced to Jenkinson has an incomplete argument in higher dimensions and identifies the underlying convex-geometric obstruction.

The correct theorem is the following, which replaces global upper semicontinuity with the sharp local conditions: continuity of h|ℰh|_{\mathcal{E}} and pointwise upper semicontinuity at each μ∈ℰ\mu\in\mathcal{E}.

Theorem 3.

Let GG be a locally compact amenable group acting on XX by TT with htop​(X,T)<∞h_{\mathrm{top}}(X,T)<\infty. Let ℰ⊂MTe​r​g​(X)\mathcal{E}\subset M_{T}^{erg}(X) be nonempty and weak-∗* closed, and set F=co¯​(ℰ)F=\overline{\operatorname{co}}(\mathcal{E}). Assume:

  1. (1)

    h|ℰh|_{\mathcal{E}} is weak-∗* continuous;

  2. (2)

    hh is upper semicontinuous at every μ∈ℰ\mu\in\mathcal{E}.

Then there exists ϕ∈C​(X)\phi\in C(X) such that the set of equilibrium states for ϕ\phi is exactly FF.

Set K=MT​(X)K=M_{T}(X) and E=MTe​r​g​(X)E=M_{T}^{erg}(X). By Proposition 2.2, the facial topology on EE coincides with the weak-∗* subspace topology.

Since ℰ\mathcal{E} is a compact subset of KK and h|ℰh|_{\mathcal{E}} is continuous, Tietze’s theorem provides a continuous extension b:K→ℝb:K\to\mathbb{R} with b|ℰ=h|ℰb|_{\mathcal{E}}=h|_{\mathcal{E}}. 4. Extension to Non-Compact Systems

4.1. General Non-Compact Setting

We now extend our results beyond the compact setting. The key observation is that the proof of Theorem 1 can be applied to any compact metrizable system containing (X,T)(X,T) as an invariant subsystem, provided the subsystem entropy is bounded, and then restricting the discovered potential to XX. Throughout this section, we identify MT​(X)M_{T}(X) with the subset {ν∈MT¯​(X¯):ν​(X)=1}\{\nu\in M_{\bar{T}}(\bar{X}):\nu(X)=1\} via the embedding that extends measures by zero on X¯∖X\bar{X}\setminus X, and equip MT​(X)M_{T}(X) with the induced weak-∗* subspace topology. Upper semicontinuity of hh at μ\mu is understood with respect to this topology.

Theorem 4.

Let (X¯,T¯)(\bar{X},\bar{T}) be a compact metrizable system, let X⊂X¯X\subset\bar{X} be T¯\bar{T}-invariant, and suppose the entropy map of the subsystem h:MT​(X)→[0,∞)h:M_{T}(X)\to[0,\infty) is bounded. 4.2. One-point compactification and C0C_{0} potentials

When XX is locally compact and σ\sigma-compact, the one-point compactification provides a canonical ambient compact system and upgrades the potential to C0​(X)C_{0}(X). Let GG be a locally compact amenable group acting continuously on a metrizable, locally compact, σ\sigma-compact space XX by homeomorphisms T={Tg}g∈GT=\{T_{g}\}_{g\in G}. Let X′=X∪{∞}X^{\prime}=X\cup\{\infty\} denote the one-point compactification. Since XX is metrizable, locally compact, and σ\sigma-compact, X′X^{\prime} is a compact metric space.

Lemma 4.1.

The action extends uniquely to a continuous action T′={Tg′}g∈GT^{\prime}=\{T^{\prime}_{g}\}_{g\in G} on X′X^{\prime} with Tg′​(∞)=∞T^{\prime}_{g}(\infty)=\infty for all g∈Gg\in G. Each Tg′T^{\prime}_{g} is a homeomorphism.

Proof.

For each g∈Gg\in G, define Tg′​(∞)=∞T^{\prime}_{g}(\infty)=\infty and Tg′|X=TgT^{\prime}_{g}|_{X}=T_{g}. Fix g∈Gg\in G. Let xn→∞x_{n}\to\infty in X′X^{\prime} and let C⊂XC\subset X be compact. Since TgT_{g} is a homeomorphism, Tg−1​CT_{g}^{-1}C is compact, so xn∉Tg−1​Cx_{n}\notin T_{g}^{-1}C for all large nn. Hence Tg​(xn)∉CT_{g}(x_{n})\notin C eventually, giving Tg′​(xn)→∞T^{\prime}_{g}(x_{n})\to\infty. The same argument applied to Tg−1T_{g^{-1}} shows (Tg′)−1=Tg−1′(T^{\prime}_{g})^{-1}=T^{\prime}_{g^{-1}} is continuous at ∞\infty, so Tg′T^{\prime}_{g} is a homeomorphism. It remains to verify joint continuity of (g,x)↦Tg′​(x)(g,x)\mapsto T^{\prime}_{g}(x) at points (g0,∞)(g_{0},\infty), since the action is jointly continuous on G×XG\times X by hypothesis. Suppose gn→g0g_{n}\to g_{0} and xn→∞x_{n}\to\infty. Theorem 5.

Let GG be a locally compact amenable group acting continuously by homeomorphisms on a metrizable, locally compact, σ\sigma-compact space XX, with supμ∈MT​(X)h​(μ)<∞\sup_{\mu\in M_{T}(X)}h(\mu)<\infty. 4.3. Application to Countable State Markov Shifts

We apply Theorem 5 to two-sided countable-state Markov shifts, where the ℤ\mathbb{Z}-action by the shift map σ\sigma acts by homeomorphisms. Recall that a two-sided countable-state Markov shift X⊂ℕℤX\subset\mathbb{N}^{\mathbb{Z}} is locally compact if and only if its transition matrix is both row-finite and column-finite (each symbol has finitely many successors and finitely many predecessors). Under this condition, each cylinder [a]0={x∈X:x0=a}[a]_{0}=\{x\in X:x_{0}=a\} is compact: the set of allowable symbols at each coordinate kk, given x0=ax_{0}=a, is finite (by induction on |k||k| using the row/column-finiteness), so [a]0[a]_{0} is a closed subset of a product of finite discrete sets, hence compact by Tychonoff. Since X=⋃a∈ℕ[a]0X=\bigcup_{a\in\mathbb{N}}[a]_{0} is a countable union of compact sets, XX is σ\sigma-compact.

The thermodynamic formalism for countable-state Markov shifts was developed by Fiebig, Fiebig, and Yuri [4]. Iommi, Todd, and Velozo [6] proved that for any countable-state Markov shift with finite entropy, hh is upper semicontinuous at every ergodic measure. Combining with Theorem 5 we arrive at:

Corollary 6.

Let XX be a locally compact two-sided countable-state Markov shift with supμh​(μ)<∞\sup_{\mu}h(\mu)<\infty. For every ergodic μ∈Mσ​(X)\mu\in M_{\sigma}(X), there exists f∈C0​(X)f\in C_{0}(X) such that μ\mu is the unique equilibrium state for ff.

We note here that in independent work, Iommi and Velozo [7] prove that for a transitive countable Markov shift, every ergodic measure with finite one-cylinder entropy Hμ​(ℬ)H_{\mu}(\mathcal{B}) is the unique equilibrium measure of a uniformly continuous function; they also show that measures which are not equilibrium measures for any bounded-above continuous function are dense. Their argument adapts the global upper semicontinuity framework of Phelps [15, Chapter 12]. This does not subsume Corollary 6, which requires only local compactness and produces a C0C_{0} potential, but the two results address closely related questions in the countable-state setting.

Corollary 6 requires local compactness (equivalently, that the transition matrix is both row-finite and column-finite). Theorem 4 removes this restriction: any countable-state Markov shift X⊂ℕℤX\subset\mathbb{N}^{\mathbb{Z}} embeds as a shift-invariant subset of (ℕ∪{∞})ℤ(\mathbb{N}\cup\{\infty\})^{\mathbb{Z}}, which is compact metrizable under the product topology. Therefore, if supμh​(μ)<∞\sup_{\mu}h(\mu)<\infty and hh is upper semicontinuous at an ergodic μ∈Mσ​(X)\mu\in M_{\sigma}(X), then μ\mu is the unique equilibrium state for the restriction of some ϕ∈C​((ℕ∪{∞})ℤ)\phi\in C((\mathbb{N}\cup\{\infty\})^{\mathbb{Z}}) to XX. By [6], the upper semicontinuity hypothesis is automatic when htop​(X)<∞h_{\mathrm{top}}(X)<\infty, so the conclusion holds for every ergodic measure on any countable-state Markov shift with finite entropy, without any local compactness assumption.
