Algebraic Toric Quasifolds

Abstract.

Symplectic and complex toric quasifolds are a generalization of toric manifolds and orbifolds to the nonrational case. In this paper, we reframe these notions from the viewpoint of algebraic geometry.

The authors were partially supported by the PRIN Project “Real and Complex Manifolds: Topology, Geometry and Holomorphic Dynamics” (MUR, Italy) and by GNSAGA (INdAM, Italy).

Keywords. Toric variety, toric quasifold, quasitorus, nonrational fan, Laurent monomial.

Mathematics Subject Classification: Primary: 14M25. Secondary: 58B20.

Introduction

Toric quasifolds were first introduced by the second author in [21, 22] for the purpose of generalizing symplectic toric manifolds to the case of simple polytopes that are not necessarily rational. Toric quasifolds are highly singular spaces that are locally modeled by open sets of ℝ2​n\mathbb{R}^{2n} modulo the action of countable groups; as such, they are a natural generalization of both toric manifolds and toric orbifolds. The work in the symplectic category was shortly thereafter extended to the complex category by both authors in [4]. The aim of this paper is to reframe everything in the setting of algebraic geometry, following, as our main references and inspiration, the books by Fulton [16] and Cox–Little–Schenck [14].

The foundation of the symplectic works [21, 22] consisted of establishing the right framework for the classical Delzant construction of symplectic toric manifolds [15] to make sense in the nonrational case. Let us explain this in the language of fans. Let Σ\Sigma be any simplicial fan in ℝn\mathbb{R}^{n}. If the fan is rational, the rays intersect a lattice LL. In general, this will no longer be the case. However, one can always choose a set of ray generators {v1,…,vd}\{v_{1},\ldots,v_{d}\} and consider their ℤ\mathbb{Z}–span, QQ. This object is not necessarily a lattice; in the worst case scenario, it will be a dense subset of ℝn\mathbb{R}^{n}. It is an example of quasilattice (see Definition 1.1). A more general way of proceeding is the following. Fix any quasilattice QQ in ℝn\mathbb{R}^{n} first, and then consider a simplicial fan Σ\Sigma whose ray generators all have nonempty intersection with QQ. Finally, choose a set of ray generators {v1,…,vd}\{v_{1},\ldots,v_{d}\} belonging to QQ. These choices define what we now call a fundamental triple

(Σ,Q,{v1,…,vd}).(\Sigma,Q,\{v_{1},\ldots,v_{d}\}).

Fundamental triples are the initial data required for the construction of all toric quasifolds: symplectic, complex, or algebraic.

Once the right framework is established, it is quite surprising to see that, in all categories, many constructions have the same flavor as their rational counterparts. In our work [4], for example, it had already emerged that complex toric quasifolds admit a canonical atlas with ”affine” charts given by quotients of ℂn\mathbb{C}^{n} modulo the linear action of countable subgroups Γ\Gamma of (ℂ∗)n(\mathbb{C}^{*})^{n}. This realization is necessarily the starting point for any algebraic formulation of the theory.

In this article, we build everything with the explicit aim of remaining as close as possible to the standard treatments of toric varieties [16, 14]. In fact, we begin by carefully reviewing the classical constructions from our viewpoint, and we modify them only when needed. The key idea is that of replacing the lattice with the quasilattice QQ, the primitive ray generators with the chosen ray generators v1,…,vd∈Qv_{1},\ldots,v_{d}\in Q, and making the necessary changes along the way. This process yields, in a very natural way, the notion of affine toric quasifolds, which turn out to be quotients of ℂr×(ℂ∗)n−r\mathbb{C}^{r}\times(\mathbb{C}^{*})^{n-r} modulo the action of the countable groups Γ\Gamma described above. As a special case, we get the quasitori (ℂ∗)n/Γ(\mathbb{C}^{*})^{n}/\Gamma, which turn out to be isomorphic to the quotient ℂn/Q\mathbb{C}^{n}/Q.

The same idea applies to morphisms. We slightly modify the notion of cone morphism, only by requiring that the underlying linear map sends quasilattice to quasilattice. The induced affine toric morphism is then defined explicitly in terms of generalized Laurent monomials (see also [8]). These monomials have real exponents, but in our quotients this is permissible (see Theorem 3.1). Verifying functoriality and equivariance with respect to quasitorus actions is a matter of direct computations (see Theorem 3.5, and Proposition 3.11).

Affine morphisms allow us to suitably glue affine toric quasifolds, yielding the notions of algebraic toric quasifolds (see Theorem 4.1) and their equivariant morphisms (see Theorem 5.1). An interesting family of morphisms is provided by fan subdivisions and, in particular, blow–ups. We focus on the case of generalized Hirzebruch surfaces, ℍa\mathbb{H}_{a}, which form a one–parameter family of toric quasifolds containing the standard Hirzebruch surfaces ℍn\mathbb{H}_{n} (see also [9]). We show that they can be obtained by blowing–up generalized weighted projective space, in exactly the same way as in the classical case.

In fact, by the way that everything is constructed and generalized, it is straightforward to realize that, when the fan is rational, we obtain all of the standard theory back.

We remark that the nonsimplicial case was originally addressed by the first author in both the symplectic [2] and the complex [3] setting and involves stratifications by symplectic and complex toric quasifolds, respectively. The algebraic point of view is work in progress and will appear in subsequent work.

Since the appearance of [21, 22, 4, 2, 3], nonrational toric geometry has been studied by a number of authors from different points of view. See, for example, [10, 11, 24, 18, 17, 20, 19, 12]. Most of these treatments rely, in some way or another, on a choice of initial data that contain a choice of ray generators and the quasilattice given by their ℤ\mathbb{Z}–span. A discussion about how some of these different approaches are related can be found in [7].

The article is structured as follows. In Section 1 we discuss prerequisites in convex geometry, in the rational and nonrational setting. In Section 2 we introduce affine toric quasifolds and in Section 3 their morphisms. In Section 4 we introduce algebraic toric quasifolds and in Section 5 their morphisms and blow–ups.

1. Convex geometry

1.1. Cones and fans

We recall a few facts about convex polyhedral cones; we refer to [14] and the references therein for a detailed treatment.

A convex polyhedral cone in ℝn\mathbb{R}^{n} is a set

ρ={∑i=1rti​Yi∣ti∈ℝ,ti≥0}.\rho=\left\{\sum_{i=1}^{r}t_{i}Y_{i}\mid t_{i}\in\mathbb{R},t_{i}\geq 0\right\}.

The vectors Yi∈ℝnY_{i}\in\mathbb{R}^{n} are the generators of the cone ρ\rho. The cone {0}\{0\} is generated by the empty set. The dimension of ρ\rho is the dimension of the linear subspace of ℝn\mathbb{R}^{n} spanned by ρ\rho. The dual of the convex polyhedral cone ρ\rho is given by

ρ∨={α∈(ℝn)∗∣α​(X)≥0∀X∈ρ}\rho^{\vee}=\{\alpha\in(\mathbb{R}^{n})^{*}\mid\alpha(X)\geq 0\quad\forall X\in\rho\}

and is itself a convex polyhedral cone. Moreover, (ρ∨)∨=ρ(\rho^{\vee})^{\vee}=\rho. Each non zero α∈(ℝn)∗\alpha\in(\mathbb{R}^{n})^{*} defines a hyperplane Hα={X∈ℝn|α​(X)=0}H_{\alpha}=\{X\in\mathbb{R}^{n}\;|\;\alpha(X)=0\} and a half-space Hα+={X∈ℝn|α​(X)≥0}H^{+}_{\alpha}=\{X\in\mathbb{R}^{n}\;|\;\alpha(X)\geq 0\}. A hyperplane is said to be a supporting hyperplane for a cone ρ\rho if ρ⊂Hα+\rho\subset H_{\alpha}^{+}. The intersection of ρ\rho with any supporting hyperplane, including the whole space, is called a face of ρ\rho. Faces that are strictly contained in ρ\rho are called proper. Notice that a cone may contain a linear subspace, which is therefore contained in every face of the cone. For any face η\eta, we can consider its relative interior, relint​(η)\text{relint}(\eta), which is the interior of η\eta in the linear subspace spanned by η\eta itself. A cone ρ\rho is said to be strongly convex if it does not contain any line; this is equivalent to requiring that {0}\{0\} is a face of ρ\rho. In a strongly convex polyhedral cone the 11–dimensional faces are all half–lines, the rays of the cone. Given a strongly convex polyhedral cone ρ\rho with rr rays, and ray generators v1,…,vrv_{1},\ldots,v_{r}, we have that ρ\rho is generated by the viv_{i}’s, namely

ρ={∑i=1rti​vi∣ti∈ℝ,ti≥0}.\rho=\left\{\sum_{i=1}^{r}t_{i}v_{i}\mid t_{i}\in\mathbb{R},t_{i}\geq 0\right\}.

If, in addition, the vectors v1,…,vrv_{1},\ldots,v_{r} are linearly independent, the cone ρ\rho has dimension rr and we will say that it is simplicial. In this case, for any subset Iη={i1,…,ih}I_{\eta}=\{i_{1},\ldots,i_{h}\} of {1,…,r}\{1,\ldots,r\}, the corresponding vectors vi1,…,vihv_{i_{1}},\ldots,v_{i_{h}} generate an hh–dimensional face η\eta of the cone ρ\rho, which is itself a simplicial cone, having dimension hh. A maximal simplicial cone is a simplicial cone of dimension nn. Consider an rr–dimensional simplicial cone ρ\rho as above and a basis {v1,…,vr,vr+1,…,vn}\{v_{1},\ldots,v_{r},v_{r+1},\ldots,v_{n}\} that completes the linearly independent set {v1,…,vr}\{v_{1},\ldots,v_{r}\}. If {α1,…,αn}\{\alpha_{1},\ldots,\alpha_{n}\} is its dual basis, then the dual cone ρ∨\rho^{\vee} can be described, relatively to this basis, as the cone generated by αi\alpha_{i}, with 1≤i≤r1\leq i\leq r and ±αi\pm\alpha_{i} with r+1≤i≤nr+1\leq i\leq n. In particular, notice that when ρ\rho is maximal, ρ∨\rho^{\vee} is generated by {α1,…,αn}\{\alpha_{1},\ldots,\alpha_{n}\}. Thus we have that, for any rr–dimensional simplicial cone, its dual is an nn–dimensional cone that is strongly convex only when ρ\rho is maximal.

Let LL be the lattice in ℝn\mathbb{R}^{n} that is generated by vectors v1,…,vn∈ℝnv_{1},\ldots,v_{n}\in\mathbb{R}^{n}:

L={∑i=1nni​vi∣ni∈ℤ}.L=\left\{\sum_{i=1}^{n}n_{i}v_{i}\mid n_{i}\in\mathbb{Z}\right\}.

The dual lattice Hom​(L,ℤ)\text{Hom}(L,\mathbb{Z}) is isomorphic to

L∨={α∈(ℝn)∗|α​(X)∈ℤ∀X∈L}.L^{\vee}=\{\alpha\in(\mathbb{R}^{n})^{*}\;|\;\alpha(X)\in\mathbb{Z}\quad\forall\quad X\in L\}.

Notice that the dual basis α1,…,αn\alpha_{1},\ldots,\alpha_{n} to v1,…,vnv_{1},\ldots,v_{n} is a basis of L∨L^{\vee}.

A fan Σ\Sigma in ℝn\mathbb{R}^{n} is a finite collection of strongly convex polyhedral cones in ℝn\mathbb{R}^{n}, such that each face of a cone in Σ\Sigma belongs to Σ\Sigma and the intersection of any two cones of Σ\Sigma is a face of each. The one–dimensional cones are called rays of the fan.

A fan is simplicial if its cones are all simplicial.

The support |Σ||\Sigma| of a fan Σ\Sigma is the union in ℝn\mathbb{R}^{n} of its cones. From now on, we will consider fans that have convex support of full dimension; this implies that |Σ||\Sigma| is the union of the maximal cones of Σ\Sigma. A notable example is the case of a complete fan, namely a fan whose support is the whole space ℝn\mathbb{R}^{n} like, for instance, the normal fan of a convex polytope.

1.2. Rational and smooth cones and fans

Given a lattice LL, a strongly convex polyhedral cone ρ\rho is rational with respect to LL, or in LL, if each of its rays has a nonempty intersection with LL. Thus, an rr–dimensional rational cone ρ\rho determines a set of rr canonical generators. In fact, the intersection of each ray with the given lattice LL is nonempty and a discrete subset of the ray; therefore, the shortest vector in this intersection is well defined and is called the primitive generator of the ray. A fan is rational with respect to LL, or in LL, if every cone of the fan is rational in LL.

A simplicial cone ρ\rho that is rational in LL is said to be smooth if its canonical set of generators can be extended to a basis of LL. A simplicial fan that is rational in LL is said to be smooth if each of its cones is smooth.

1.3. Nonrational cones and fans

In this subsection, we build the framework for toric geometry in the nonrational setting. We begin by recalling from [21, 22] the following

Definition 1.1 (Quasilattice).

A quasilattice QQ in ℝn\mathbb{R}^{n} is the ℤ\mathbb{Z}–span of a set of generators of ℝn\mathbb{R}^{n}.

A quasilattice in ℝn\mathbb{R}^{n} is therefore a free ℤ\mathbb{Z}–module of rank greater or equal to nn. The key feature that distinguishes a true lattice from a quasilattice of rank strictly greater than nn is of topological nature: a quasilattice in ℝn\mathbb{R}^{n} has rank equal to nn if and only if it is a discrete subset of ℝn\mathbb{R}^{n}. A basic, though important, example of quasilattice is Q=ℤ+a​ℤQ=\mathbb{Z}+a\mathbb{Z}, with aa an irrational number; it is dense in ℝ\mathbb{R}.

Definition 1.2 (Quasirational cone).

A strongly convex polyhedral cone δ\delta in ℝn\mathbb{R}^{n} is quasirational with respect to a quasilattice QQ, or in QQ, if each of its rays has nonempty intersection with QQ.

Remark 1.3.

Notice that, unlike the rational case, here we no longer have a canonical choice of generators, since there might be a ray whose intersection with QQ is not a discrete subset of the ray. Hence, the notion of primitive ray generator does not make sense. There are other significant changes with respect to the rational case. To begin with, Gordan’s Lemma does not hold. Consider, for example, the one–dimensional cone σ=ℝ≥0\sigma=\mathbb{R}_{\geq 0} in ℝ\mathbb{R}. Its intersection with the quasilattice ℤ+2​ℤ\mathbb{Z}+\sqrt{2}\,\mathbb{Z} is a semigroup that is dense in σ\sigma, whilst if it were finitely generated as a semigroup, it would be discrete. Another remarkable difference is that

Q∨={α∈(ℝn)∗∣α​(X)∈ℤ,∀X∈Q}Q^{\vee}=\{\alpha\in(\mathbb{R}^{n})^{*}\mid\alpha(X)\in\mathbb{Z},\;\forall\;X\in Q\}

in general is a lattice in (ℝn)∗(\mathbb{R}^{n})^{*} whose rank is lower than nn. In particular, when QQ is dense in ℝn\mathbb{R}^{n}, Q∨=0Q^{\vee}=0 . On the other hand, the rank of the free ℤ\mathbb{Z}–module Hom​(Q,ℤ)\text{Hom}(Q,\mathbb{Z}) is equal to rank​(Q)≥n\text{rank}(Q)\geq n, therefore in general Hom​(Q,ℤ)\text{Hom}(Q,\mathbb{Z}) can no longer be identified with Q∨Q^{\vee}.

Definition 1.4 (Quasirational fan).

A fan Σ\Sigma in ℝn\mathbb{R}^{n} is quasirational with respect to a quasilattice QQ, or in QQ, if every cone of Σ\Sigma is quasirational in QQ.

Remark 1.5.

It is very important to notice that a cone, or a fan, is always quasirational with respect to some quasilattice QQ. It suffices to take the quasilattice that is generated by a choice of ray generators. So, unlike rationality, quasirationality, perse, is not restrictive at all.

Remark 1.6.

Let δ\delta be an rr–dimensional simplicial cone in ℝn\mathbb{R}^{n} that is quasirational in QQ. Then, since QQ is generated by a set of spanning vectors of ℝn\mathbb{R}^{n}, by a standard linear algebra argument, we can always choose v1δ,…,vnδv_{1}^{\delta},\ldots,v_{n}^{\delta} in QQ which form a basis of ℝn\mathbb{R}^{n} and Iδ={i1,…,ir}⊆{1,…,n}I_{\delta}=\{i_{1},\ldots,i_{r}\}\subseteq\{1,\ldots,n\} such that vi1δ,…,virδv_{i_{1}}^{\delta},\ldots,v_{i_{r}}^{\delta} are ray generators of δ\delta. Notice that this implies that we can always construct a simplicial maximal cone σ\sigma that is quasirational in QQ such that δ\delta is a face of σ\sigma.

We adapt to this context the notion of fundamental triple.

Definition 1.7 (Cone fundamental triple).

Let Q⊂ℝnQ\subset\mathbb{R}^{n} be a quasilattice. For any simplicial cone δ\delta in ℝn\mathbb{R}^{n} that is quasirational in QQ, a fundamental triple is given by

(δ,Q,{v1δ,…,vnδ})(\delta,Q,\{v_{1}^{\delta},\ldots,v_{n}^{\delta}\})

where v1δ,…,vnδv_{1}^{\delta},\ldots,v_{n}^{\delta} are vectors chosen as in Remark 1.6 above.

Notice that there is a lattice which is naturally associated with such a triple. It is the lattice LδL_{\delta} generated by v1δ,…,vnδv_{1}^{\delta},\ldots,v_{n}^{\delta}. With respect to this lattice, δ\delta is a smooth cone in the classical sense.

Remark 1.8 (Induced triple on a cone face).

Consider a cone δ\delta with fundamental triple (δ,Q,{v1δ,…,vnδ}).(\delta,Q,\{v^{\delta}_{1},\ldots,v^{\delta}_{n}\}). Whenever we have a face ρ⊂δ\rho\subset\delta, we will consider the naturally induced triple (ρ,Q,{v1δ,…,vnδ})(\rho,Q,\{v^{\delta}_{1},\ldots,v^{\delta}_{n}\}) for ρ\rho. Notice that Iρ⊆IδI_{\rho}\subseteq I_{\delta} and Lρ=LδL_{\rho}=L_{\delta}.

Definition 1.9 (Fan fundamental triple).

Let Σ\Sigma be a simplicial fan in ℝn\mathbb{R}^{n} that is quasirational in QQ. A fundamental triple for Σ\Sigma is given by

(Σ,Q,{v1,…,vd}),(\Sigma,Q,\{v_{1},\ldots,v_{d}\}),

where the vectors v1,…,vdv_{1},\ldots,v_{d} belong to QQ and generate the dd rays of Σ\Sigma.

Notice that for the maximal cones of the fan, the corresponding triples are uniquely determined by the fan triple. The lower dimensional cones may be faces of different maximal cones and, as such, will be endowed with different induced triples. A fan triple yields a collection of cone triples. If there is a unique lattice associated with each of them, then the fan is smooth with respect to this lattice in the classical sense.

Classically, given a lattice LL in ℝn\mathbb{R}^{n}, and a rational simplicial fan Σ\Sigma, there is an underlying fundamental triple which is given by

(Σ,L,{v1,…,vd}),(\Sigma,L,\{v_{1},\ldots,v_{d}\}),

where the vectors v1,…,vdv_{1},\ldots,v_{d} are the primitive ray generators for Σ\Sigma.

1.4. Cone and fan morphisms

The notions of cone and fan morphism have to be adapted to the nonrational setting in the following natural way. Consider a cone δ1⊂ℝn\delta_{1}\subset\mathbb{R}^{n} that is quasirational in a quasilattice Q1⊂ℝnQ_{1}\subset\mathbb{R}^{n}, and a cone δ2⊂ℝm\delta_{2}\subset\mathbb{R}^{m} that is quasirational in a quasilattice Q2⊂ℝmQ_{2}\subset\mathbb{R}^{m}.

Definition 1.10 (Cone morphism).

A cone morphism δ1→δ2\delta_{1}\rightarrow\delta_{2} is the datum of a linear mapping f:ℝn→ℝmf\colon\mathbb{R}^{n}\rightarrow\mathbb{R}^{m} such that f​(Q1)⊂Q2f(Q_{1})\subset Q_{2} and f​(δ1)⊂δ2f(\delta_{1})\subset\delta_{2}.

The identity on ℝn\mathbb{R}^{n} defines a morphism of any cone δ⊂ℝn\delta\subset\mathbb{R}^{n} into itself which we call the identity morphism. Two cone morphisms can be naturally composed by taking the composition of the corresponding linear mappings. Moreover, we have

Definition 1.11 (Cone isomorphism).

A cone isomorphism δ1→δ2\delta_{1}\rightarrow\delta_{2} is the datum of a linear isomorphism ff of ℝn\mathbb{R}^{n} such that f​(Q1)=Q2f(Q_{1})=Q_{2} and f​(δ1)=δ2f(\delta_{1})=\delta_{2}. The two cones are then said to be isomorphic.

Let Σ1⊂ℝn\Sigma_{1}\subset\mathbb{R}^{n} and Σ2⊂ℝm\Sigma_{2}\subset\mathbb{R}^{m} be fans that are quasirational, respectively, in quasilattices Q1⊂ℝnQ_{1}\subset\mathbb{R}^{n} and Q2⊂ℝmQ_{2}\subset\mathbb{R}^{m}.

Definition 1.12 (Fan morphism).

A fan morphism Σ1→Σ2\Sigma_{1}\rightarrow\Sigma_{2} is the datum of a linear mapping f:ℝn→ℝmf\colon\mathbb{R}^{n}\rightarrow\mathbb{R}^{m} with f​(Q1)⊂Q2f(Q_{1})\subset Q_{2}, and such that, for each cone δ1\delta_{1} in Σ1\Sigma_{1}, there is a cone δ2\delta_{2} in Σ2\Sigma_{2}, with the property that f​(δ1)⊂δ2f(\delta_{1})\subset\delta_{2}.

As in the case of cones, the identity of ℝn\mathbb{R}^{n} induces a morphism of any Σ⊂ℝn\Sigma\subset\mathbb{R}^{n}, into itself, called the identity morphism, and two fan morphisms can be composed by taking the composition of the corresponding linear maps. We also have

Definition 1.13 (Fan isomorphism).

A fan isomorphism Σ1→Σ2\Sigma_{1}\rightarrow\Sigma_{2} is the datum of an isomorphism ff of ℝn\mathbb{R}^{n} with f​(Q1)=Q2f(Q_{1})=Q_{2}, and such that, for each cone δ1\delta_{1} in Σ1\Sigma_{1}, there is a cone δ2\delta_{2} in Σ2\Sigma_{2}, with the property that f​(δ1)=δ2f(\delta_{1})=\delta_{2}. The two fans are then said to be isomorphic.

2. Affine toric quasifolds

In this section, we introduce affine toric quasifolds. We do so in stages, by first reviewing, from our perspective, what happens in the rational case.

2.1. Smooth affine toric varieties

We recall the classical construction of affine toric varieties corresponding to smooth cones.

To begin with, let σ\sigma be a maximal simplicial cone in ℝn\mathbb{R}^{n} and let v1σ,…,vnσv_{1}^{\sigma},\ldots,v_{n}^{\sigma} be ray generators for σ\sigma. Denote by LσL_{\sigma} the lattice in ℝn\mathbb{R}^{n} that is generated by these vectors. Then σ\sigma is smooth with respect to LσL_{\sigma}. Notice that our point of view here is slightly different from the classical one, where the lattice is given from the beginning, and the cone is required to be smooth with respect to that lattice. As we shall see, our approach is better suited to being generalized to the nonrational case.

The intersection

Sσ=σ∨∩Lσ∨S_{\sigma}=\sigma^{\vee}\cap L_{\sigma}^{\vee}

The corresponding semigroup algebra ℂ​[Sσ]\mathbb{C}[S_{\sigma}] is the set of finite formal sums ∑α∈Sσcα​χα\sum_{\alpha\in S_{\sigma}}c_{\alpha}\chi^{\alpha} with cα∈ℂc_{\alpha}\in\mathbb{C} and multiplication rule given by χα⋅χα′=χα+α′\chi^{\alpha}\cdot\chi^{\alpha^{\prime}}=\chi^{\alpha+\alpha^{\prime}}, α,α′∈Sσ\alpha,\alpha^{\prime}\in S_{\sigma}. Note that any α∈Sσ\alpha\in S_{\sigma} is of the form h1​α1σ+⋯+hn​αnσh_{1}\alpha_{1}^{\sigma}+\cdots+h_{n}\alpha_{n}^{\sigma}, with h1,…,hnh_{1},\ldots,h_{n} nonnegative integers. Therefore, if we denote ξi=χαi\xi_{i}=\chi^{\alpha_{i}}, i=1,…,ni=1,\ldots,n, we obtain ℂ​[Sσ]=ℂ​[ξ1,…,ξn]\mathbb{C}[S_{\sigma}]=\mathbb{C}[\xi_{1},\ldots,\xi_{n}], the ring of polynomials in ξ1,…,ξn\xi_{1},\ldots,\xi_{n} with complex coefficients. The associated affine toric variety is the set of all maximal ideals of ℂ​[Sσ]\mathbb{C}[S_{\sigma}], that we denote for brevity Spec​ℂ​[Sσ]\text{Spec}\,\mathbb{C}[S_{\sigma}] instead of Specm​ℂ​[Sσ]\text{Specm}\,\mathbb{C}[S_{\sigma}]. By identifying the maximal ideal ⟨ξ1−z1,…,ξn−zn⟩\langle\xi_{1}-z_{1},\ldots,\xi_{n}-z_{n}\rangle with the point (z1,…,zn)∈ℂn(z_{1},\ldots,z_{n})\in\mathbb{C}^{n}, we obtain that the affine toric variety is ℂn\mathbb{C}^{n} with coordinate ring ℂ​[Sσ]=ℂ​[ξ1,…,ξn]\mathbb{C}[S_{\sigma}]=\mathbb{C}[\xi_{1},\ldots,\xi_{n}].

Take now any rr–dimensional simplicial cone δ\delta in ℝn\mathbb{R}^{n}. Choose a basis v1δ,…,vnδv_{1}^{\delta},\ldots,v_{n}^{\delta} of ℝn\mathbb{R}^{n} such that v1δ,…,vrδv_{1}^{\delta},\ldots,v_{r}^{\delta} are ray generators of δ\delta and consider the lattice LδL_{\delta} that is generated by v1δ,…,vnδv_{1}^{\delta},\ldots,v_{n}^{\delta}. 2.4. Quasitori as affine toric quasifolds

The affine toric quasifold corresponding to the cone {0}⊂δ\{0\}\subset\delta is a very special one: X{0}δ=(ℂ∗)n/ΓδX_{\{0\}}^{\delta}=(\mathbb{C}^{*})^{n}/\Gamma_{\delta}. As we shall see, this is what is called a quasitorus.

3. Morphisms of affine toric quasifolds

We are now ready to introduce, in this setting, the notion of affine toric morphism.

4. Algebraic Toric quasifolds

We adapt from [14, Ch. 3.0] the general construction of abstract toric varieties obtained by gluing together affine varieties.

4.1. Definition

We consider a triple (Σ,Q,{v1,…,vd})(\Sigma,Q,\{v_{1},\ldots,v_{d}\}), with Σ⊂ℝn\Sigma\subset\mathbb{R}^{n} a simplicial fan with convex support of full dimension nn and we define the corresponding abstract toric quasifold by suitably gluing the following collection of affine toric quasifolds:

{Xγτ|γ⊂τ,τ​maximal cone in​Σ}.\left\{X^{\tau}_{\gamma}\;|\;\gamma\subset\tau,\quad\tau\;\text{maximal cone in}\;\Sigma\right\}.

Note that when we use the inclusion symbol between two cones, here and in the following, it is understood that the first is a face of the second and the corresponding affine toric quasifold is the one given in Remark 2.5. We now describe the gluing maps in detail and prove the compatibility conditions.

Let δ\delta and γ\gamma be two cones of the fan Σ\Sigma of dimension rr and ss respectively and let σ\sigma and τ\tau be any two maximal cones such that δ⊂σ\delta\subset\sigma and γ⊂τ\gamma\subset\tau. Suppose for simplicity that the cone σ\sigma is generated by the first nn vectors, {v1,…,vn}\{v_{1},\ldots,v_{n}\}, and that the cone τ\tau is generated by {vi1,…,vin}\{v_{i_{1}},\ldots,v_{i_{n}}\}, with {i1,…,in}⊂{1,…,d}\{i_{1},\ldots,i_{n}\}\subset\{1,\ldots,d\}. According to (1), the corresponding affine quasifold isomorphism hγ​δ:Xδ∩γσ→Xδ∩γτh_{\gamma\delta}\colon X^{\sigma}_{\delta\cap\gamma}\rightarrow X^{\tau}_{\delta\cap\gamma} is given by

Note that δ∩γ\delta\cap\gamma is also a face of σ∩τ\sigma\cap\tau. By Proposition 3.8, hγ​δh_{\gamma\delta} is the restriction of hτ​σh_{\tau\sigma} to Xδ∩γσX^{\sigma}_{\delta\cap\gamma}. We call the quotient space XΣ=X~Σ/∼X_{\Sigma}=\widetilde{X}_{\Sigma}/\sim the algebraic toric quasifold associated with the triple (Σ,Q,{v1,…,vd})(\Sigma,Q,\{v_{1},\ldots,v_{d}\}). We will say that nn is the dimension of XΣX_{\Sigma}.

Remark 4.3 (The quotient construction of toric quasifolds).

As in the rational case [1, 13], algebraic toric quasifolds can also be constructed as complex quotients [4]. The link between the two constructions is again the atlas of the complex quotient, whose charts are naturally identified with the algebraic affine quasifolds corresponding to the maximal cones.

5. Morphisms and blow–ups of algebraic toric quasifolds

We are now ready to define toric morphisms for algebraic toric quasifolds. All fans are again simplicial with convex support of full dimension.

Definition 5.2 (Toric quasifold morphism).

A morphism between two toric quasifolds XΣ1X_{\Sigma_{1}} and XΣ2X_{\Sigma_{2}} is any mapping that is induced by a fan morphism.

Theorem 5.3.

The composition of two toric quasifold morphisms is the morphism induced by the composition of the corresponding fan morphisms. Moreover, the identity map on an algebraic toric quasifold XΣX_{\Sigma} is induced by the identity map on the corresponding fan.

Proof: This is an immediate consequence of Theorem 3.5. ■\blacksquare See [20] for a view of fan and toric morphisms, and their functoriality, in a different nonrational toric framework.

Definition 5.4 (Toric quasifold isomorphism).

An isomorphism between two toric quasifolds XΣ1X_{\Sigma_{1}} and XΣ2X_{\Sigma_{2}} is any mapping that is induced by a fan isomorphism. Whenever this happens, we will say that the two toric quasifolds are isomorphic.

5.2. Blow–ups

As in the rational case, fan subdivisions naturally induce morphisms of the corresponding toric quasifolds. Of course now we have more freedom and we can even subdivide a rational fan in an arbitrary direction, using the remark above (see also [6, p. 15]).

An interesting special case of morphisms that are induced by subdivision is provided by blow–ups. We illustrate the construction with a crucial example.

It is well known classically that, for nn a positive integer, ℍn\mathbb{H}_{n} is the blow–up of ℂ​ℙ(1,1,n)2\mathbb{C}\mathbb{P}^{2}_{(1,1,n)} at its singular point. It will be of no surprise to the reader that the same holds, in this nonrational framework, when nn is replaced by any positive real number aa (see [9] for a discussion of this in the symplectic and complex context).
