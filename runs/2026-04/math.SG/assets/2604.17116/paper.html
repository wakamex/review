Jordan curves inscribe a positive measure of rectangles

Joshua Evan Greene Department of Mathematics, Boston College, USA joshua.greene@bc.edu https://sites.google.com/bc.edu/joshua-e-greene and Andrew Lobb Mathematical Sciences, Durham University, UK andrew.lobb@durham.ac.uk http://www.maths.dur.ac.uk/users/andrew.lobb/
Abstract.

Suppose that γ⊂ℂ\gamma\subset{\mathbb{C}} is a Jordan curve of diameter 2​R2R which encloses a region of area AA. We prove that there exists a subset I⊂(0,π)I\subset(0,\pi) of measure at least A/R2A/R^{2} such that if θ∈I\theta\in I, then there exist four points on γ\gamma at the vertices of a rectangle whose diagonals meet at angle θ\theta.

1. Introduction

A polygon P⊂ℂP\subset{\mathbb{C}} inscribes in a Jordan curve γ⊂ℂ\gamma\subset{\mathbb{C}} if there exists an orientation-preserving similar copy of PP whose vertex set is contained in γ\gamma. The case of a quadrilateral PP is distinguished, because a generic differentiable curve γ\gamma admits a finite set of inscriptions of PP. In this case, it is interesting to determine whether this set is nonempty. The original problem in this domain is the Square Peg Problem due to Toeplitz, which asks whether every Jordan curve γ⊂ℂ\gamma\subset{\mathbb{C}} inscribes a square [14]. It is still unsolved.

For Jordan curves possessing additional regularity, much is now known. Schnirelmann used a bordism argument to affirmatively solve the Square Peg Problem for differentiable curves [12]. We used symplectic techniques to prove the optimal result that every quadrilateral that inscribes in a circle also inscribes in every differentiable Jordan curve [4, 5]. On the other hand, the only quadrilaterals which inscribe in the circle and in all triangles are isosceles trapezoids [11]. The Trapezoidal Peg Problem for continuous curves asks whether every isosceles trapezoid inscribes in every Jordan curve. Intermediate between it and Toeplitz’s original question, the Rectangular Peg Problem for continuous curves asks whether every rectangle inscribes in every Jordan curve. Asano and Ike used microlocal sheaf techniques to affirmatively solve the Rectangular Peg Problem for rectifiable curves [1].

For Jordan curves without any regularity, much less is known. Vaughan gave a proof from the book that every Jordan curve inscribes a rectangle [10]. Schwartz improved this result by showing that all but at most four points on every Jordan curve are vertices of inscribed rectangles in the curve [13]. The result is sharp, by considering the case of a noncircular ellipse. The techniques used to prove these results are not symplectic, and they do not detect the similarity classes of the inscribed rectangles.

The purpose of this note is to obtain progress on the Rectangular Peg Problem for continuous curves using symplectic techniques. To state our main result, we introduce some notation. For an angle 0<θ<π0<\theta<\pi, a θ\theta-rectangle is a rectangle in the plane whose diagonals make angle θ\theta with one another. For a Jordan curve γ⊂ℂ\gamma\subset{\mathbb{C}}, let A​(γ)A(\gamma) denote the area enclosed by γ\gamma, let R​(γ)R(\gamma) denote the radius (half of the diameter) of γ\gamma, and let B​(γ)=A​(γ)/R​(γ)2B(\gamma)=A(\gamma)/R(\gamma)^{2}. The notation honors Bieberbach: the 2-dimensional case of his Isodiametric Inequality reads 0<B​(γ)≤π0<B(\gamma)\leq\pi, and B​(γ)=πB(\gamma)=\pi if and only if γ\gamma is a circle [2].

Theorem A.

If γ⊂ℂ\gamma\subset{\mathbb{C}} is a Jordan curve, then there exists a subset 𝒮​(γ)⊂(0,π){\mathcal{S}}(\gamma)\subset(0,\pi) of measure at least B​(γ)B(\gamma) such that for all θ∈𝒮​(γ)\theta\in{\mathcal{S}}(\gamma), γ\gamma inscribes a θ\theta-rectangle.

The proof of A is based on our earlier construction of Jordan Floer homology [6, 7]. In particular, we establish and use some properties of the spectral invariants that are defined in that theory. Previously, we used properties of the spectral invariants to prove A under the assumption that γ\gamma is rectifiable, with the added conclusion that 𝒮​(γ){\mathcal{S}}(\gamma) can be chosen to be an interval [6, Theorem A]. Similarly, spectral invariants are used in the microlocal sheaf approach to solve the Rectangular Peg Problem for rectifiable curves [1, Corollary 1.2]. We give a short proof of the following result, intermediate in strength between these two results:

Theorem B.

If γ⊂ℂ\gamma\subset{\mathbb{C}} is a rectifiable Jordan curve, then γ\gamma inscribes a θ\theta-rectangle for all 0<θ<B​(γ)0<\theta<B(\gamma).

B implies a positive solution to the Rectifiable Peg Problem for every rectifiable Jordan curve γ\gamma with B​(γ)>π/2B(\gamma)>\pi/2, i.e. which encloses more than half of the area of a circle of equal radius.

The remainder of the paper is organized as follows. Section 2 contains the proofs of Theorems A and B. The proof of B relies on the Triangle Inequality for spectral invariants, which already appeared in the literature. The proof of A relies on a Derivative Property for spectral invariants, which is new. It relates the derivative of the spectral invariant to the diameter of an inscribed rectangle. We also state a few refinements of A which follow from its proof. Section 3 describes the result that for an open, dense set of smooth Jordan curves, the space of inscribed rectangles and binormals has the structure of a smooth, 1-dimensional, closed manifold. It is an analogue for smooth Jordan curves of a result of Schwartz for PL Jordan curves [13]. Its proof is given in the Appendix. Section 4 equates a derivative of the action with the value of the Hamiltonian in the setting of Lagrangian Floer homology. We expect that the main result of this section is well-known to experts, although we could not locate such a statement in the literature. Section 5 combines the results of Section 3 and Section 4 in order to establish the Derivative Property.

2. Proofs of Theorems A and B

We prove Theorems A and B in this Section. We begin with the proof of B, which reviews our framework and sets up the related proof of A. We then state a new property of the spectral invariants that we need to prove A, which gets proven over the course of the later sections.

Fix a smooth Jordan curve γ⊂ℂ\gamma\subset{\mathbb{C}} and an angle 0<θ<π0<\theta<\pi. Jordan Floer homology is the homology of an integer-graded 𝔽2{\mathbb{F}}_{2}-chain complex JFC​(γ,θ){\rm JFC}(\gamma,\theta), which is generically generated by inscriptions of a θ\theta-rectangle in γ\gamma. It carries a real-valued action filtration 𝒜:JFC​(γ,θ)→ℝ{\mathcal{A}}:{\rm JFC}(\gamma,\theta)\to{\mathbb{R}}. Its homology is a 2-dimensional graded 𝔽2{\mathbb{F}}_{2}-vector space JF​(γ,θ)≈(𝔽2)(2)⊕(𝔽2)(1){\rm JF}(\gamma,\theta)\approx({\mathbb{F}}_{2})_{(2)}\oplus({\mathbb{F}}_{2})_{(1)}, and the action filtration descends to a spectral invariant ℓ:JF​(γ,θ)→ℝ\ell:{\rm JF}(\gamma,\theta)\to{\mathbb{R}}. We abbreviate by ℓ​(γ,θ)\ell(\gamma,\theta) the spectral invariant attached to the homogeneous element in grading 2. The spectral invariant enjoys several useful properties for a fixed smooth curve γ\gamma [6, 8]:

    Monotonicity: it defines a monotone increasing bijection ℓ​(γ,⋅):[0,π]⟶[0,A​(γ)]\ell(\gamma,\cdot):[0,\pi]\longrightarrow[0,A(\gamma)].

  • •

    Lipschitz Continuity: it is Lipschitz continuous in θ\theta with Lipschitz constant R​(γ)2R(\gamma)^{2}.

  • •

    Spectrality: there exists an inscribed θ\theta-rectangle PP in γ\gamma for which ℓ​(γ,θ)=𝒜​(P)\ell(\gamma,\theta)={\mathcal{A}}(P).

  • •

    Triangle Inequality: for all angles θ1,θ2≥0\theta_{1},\theta_{2}\geq 0 such that θ1+θ2≤π\theta_{1}+\theta_{2}\leq\pi, we have

    ℓ​(γ,θ1+θ2)≤ℓ​(γ,θ1)+ℓ​(γ,θ2).\ell(\gamma,\theta_{1}+\theta_{2})\leq\ell(\gamma,\theta_{1})+\ell(\gamma,\theta_{2}).

Now drop the smoothness hypothesis, so that γ⊂ℂ\gamma\subset{\mathbb{C}} denotes an arbitrary Jordan curve. Approximate γ\gamma in C0C^{0} by a sequence of smooth Jordan curves γn\gamma_{n}, each enclosing area A​(γ)A(\gamma). By Spectrality, there exists a θ\theta-rectangle PnP_{n} inscribed in γn\gamma_{n} such that 𝒜​(Pn)=ℓ​(γn,θ){\mathcal{A}}(P_{n})=\ell(\gamma_{n},\theta). By passing to a convergent subsequence, we may assume that (a) L:=limn→∞ℓ​(γn,θ)L:=\lim_{n\to\infty}\ell(\gamma_{n},\theta) exists and (b) the PnP_{n} converge to a point set PP inscribed in γ\gamma. A priori, PP is either a single point or a θ\theta-rectangle.

Lemma 2.1 (Lemma 5.1, [6]).

If γ\gamma is rectifiable and 0<L<A​(γ)0<L<A(\gamma), then PP is an inscribed θ\theta-rectangle in γ\gamma. ∎

Proof of B.

For the proof of A, we record one more property of the spectral invariants:

    Derivative Property: for an open dense subset of smooth Jordan curves γ\gamma, there exists a set Θ​(γ)⊂(0,π)\Theta(\gamma)\subset(0,\pi) of full measure such that every θ∈Θ​(γ)\theta\in\Theta(\gamma) is a point of differentiability of ℓ​(γ,⋅)\ell(\gamma,\cdot), and there exists an inscribed θ\theta-rectangle PP in γ\gamma such that ℓ′​(γ,θ)=R​(P)2\ell^{\prime}(\gamma,\theta)=R(P)^{2}.

Proof of A.

Suppose that γ⊂ℂ\gamma\subset{\mathbb{C}} is an arbitrary Jordan curve, and approximate it in C0C^{0} by smooth Jordan curves γn\gamma_{n} as before. We may assume that each γn\gamma_{n} belongs to the open dense set of Jordan curves to which the Derivative Property applies.

Corollary 2.2.

3. Genericity

The purpose of this section is to make precise the result that for an open dense subset of smooth Jordan curves, the space of inscribed rectangles and binormals has the structure of a smooth, closed 1-dimensional manifold. Its proof is given in the Appendix. It is an analogue in the smooth setting of a result of Schwartz for PL curves [13, Theorem 1.5]. It is this type of Jordan curve to which the Derivative Property applies. We begin by framing its context.
