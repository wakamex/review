Contact flexibility and rigidity for toric Gorenstein prequantizations and Ehrhart theory of toric diagrams

Miguel Abreu , Leonardo Macarini and António Rocha-Neves Center for Mathematical Analysis, Geometry and Dynamical Systems, Instituto Superior Técnico, Universidade de Lisboa, Av. Rovisco Pais, 1049-001 Lisboa, Portugal miguel.abreu@tecnico.ulisboa.pt, antonio.r.neves@tecnico.ulisboa.pt IMPA, Estrada Dona Castorina, 110, Rio de Janeiro, 22460-320, Brazil leonardo@impa.br
Abstract.

Gorenstein toric contact manifolds are good toric contact manifolds with zero first Chern class that are completely determined by certain integral convex polytopes called toric diagrams. The Ehrhart polynomial of these toric diagrams determines and is determined by the contact Betti numbers of the corresponding contact manifolds, i.e. the dimension of their cylindrical contact homology in each degree. In this paper we look into the following natural question: to what extent do these contact invariants determine the Gorenstein toric contact manifold? Flexibility is the norm and we illustrate it with the family of Gorenstein toric contact manifolds that arise as the prequantization of monotone iterated ℙ1\mathbb{P}^{1}-bundles, i.e. monotone Bott manifolds. In each dimension, the Ehrhart polynomial of their toric diagrams is equal to the Ehrhart polynomial of the cross-polytope, corresponding to the monotone prequantization of ℙ1×⋯×ℙ1\mathbb{P}^{1}\times\cdots\times\mathbb{P}^{1}, and we describe the unimodular classification of these toric diagrams. On the rigidity side, we will show that the primitive prequantization of ℙ1×⋯×ℙ1\mathbb{P}^{1}\times\cdots\times\mathbb{P}^{1} is rigid, i.e. completely determined as a Gorenstein toric contact manifold by its contact Betti numbers. More precisely, in each dimension, we show that its toric diagram, which we name small cross-polytope, is the unique toric diagram with its particular Ehrhart polynomial. We will also prove a rigidity result for a family of Gorenstein toric contact manifolds that arise as the primitive prequantization of monotone ℙ1\mathbb{P}^{1}-bundles over ℙn−1\mathbb{P}^{n-1}.

Key words and phrases:
Gorenstein toric contact manifolds; contact invariants; toric diagrams; Ehrhart theory; Ehrhart determined polytopes.

1. Introduction

Gorenstein toric contact manifolds are good toric contact manifolds with zero first Chern class. They are in 11-11 correspondence with toric diagrams, i.e. integral simplicial polytopes with unimodular facets (see Section 4):

𝒟⊂ℝn⟷(N𝒟2​n+1,ξ𝒟).{\mathcal{D}}\subset\mathbb{R}^{n}\longleftrightarrow(N^{2n+1}_{\mathcal{D}},\xi_{\mathcal{D}})\,.

The Ehrhart polynomial of 𝒟{\mathcal{D}} counts its number of rational points and is defined by

L𝒟​(t):=#​(t​𝒟∩ℤn),∀t∈ℤ>0,L_{\mathcal{D}}(t):=\#(t\mathcal{D}\cap\mathbb{Z}^{n}),\forall t\in\mathbb{Z}_{>0}\,,

where t​𝒟={t​𝒙∈ℝn:𝒙∈𝒟}t\mathcal{D}=\{t\bm{x}\in\mathbb{R}^{n}:\bm{x}\in\mathcal{D}\}. The Ehrhart polynomial L𝒟​(t)L_{\mathcal{D}}(t) is indeed a degree nn polynomial in tt and, as proved in [4], its coefficients are contact invariants of (N𝒟2​n+1,ξ𝒟)(N^{2n+1}_{\mathcal{D}},\xi_{\mathcal{D}}). More precisely, if we write the Ehrhart polynomial in the form

L𝒟​(t)=∑k=0nhk∗​(𝒟)​(t+n−kn),L_{{\mathcal{D}}}(t)=\sum_{k=0}^{n}h^{\ast}_{k}({\mathcal{D}})\binom{t+n-k}{n}\,,

we have that the non-negative coefficients hk∗​(𝒟)∈ℤ0+h^{\ast}_{k}({\mathcal{D}})\in\mathbb{Z}^{+}_{0} are the contact invariants of (N𝒟,ξ𝒟)(N_{{\mathcal{D}}},\xi_{{\mathcal{D}}}) given by

hn−k∗​(𝒟)=c​b2​k​(N𝒟,ξ𝒟)−c​b2​(k−1)​(N𝒟,ξ𝒟),h^{\ast}_{n-k}({\mathcal{D}})=cb_{2k}(N_{{\mathcal{D}}},\xi_{{\mathcal{D}}})-cb_{2(k-1)}(N_{{\mathcal{D}}},\xi_{{\mathcal{D}}})\,,

where c​b∗​(N𝒟,ξ𝒟)cb_{\ast}(N_{{\mathcal{D}}},\xi_{{\mathcal{D}}}) are the contact Betti numbers, i.e.

c​b∗​(N𝒟,ξ𝒟)=rank⁡H​C∗​(N𝒟,ξ𝒟)cb_{\ast}(N_{{\mathcal{D}}},\xi_{{\mathcal{D}}})=\operatorname{rank}HC_{\ast}(N_{\mathcal{D}},\xi_{\mathcal{D}})

where H​C∗​(N𝒟,ξ𝒟)HC_{\ast}(N_{\mathcal{D}},\xi_{\mathcal{D}}) is the cylindrical contact homology with grading given by the SFT degree. Note that for toric contact manifolds we have that c​b∗​(N𝒟,ξ𝒟)=0cb_{\ast}(N_{\mathcal{D}},\xi_{\mathcal{D}})=0 whenever ∗<0\ast<0 or ∗\ast is odd, which means in particular that the Ehrhart polynomial determines and is determined by the contact Betti numbers.

Remark 1.1.

As explained in [4], despite some foundational technical difficulties in proving that cylindrical contact homology is a well defined contact invariant, these contact Betti numbers are indeed well defined contact invariants in many contexts. These include Gorenstein toric contact manifolds that have crepant (i.e. with zero first Chern class) toric symplectic fillings and all the particular examples considered in this paper have this property.

In this paper we address the following natural question:

To what extent do these contact invariants determine the Gorenstein toric contact manifold or, equivalently, to what extent does the Ehrhart polynomial determine a toric diagram 𝒟{\mathcal{D}} up to integral translation and G​L​(n,ℤ)GL(n,\mathbb{Z}) transformations (i.e. up to unimodular equivalence)?

Definition 1.2.

A Gorenstein toric contact manifold is said to be rigid if it is completely determined, as a toric contact manifold, by its contact Betti numbers. Equivalently, (N𝒟,ξ𝒟)(N_{\mathcal{D}},\xi_{\mathcal{D}}) is rigid if its toric diagram 𝒟{\mathcal{D}} is Ehrhart determined, i.e. whenever 𝒟′{\mathcal{D}}^{\prime} is a toric diagram with L𝒟′​(t)=L𝒟​(t)L_{{\mathcal{D}}^{\prime}}(t)=L_{{\mathcal{D}}}(t) we have that 𝒟′{\mathcal{D}}^{\prime} is unimodularly equivalent to 𝒟{\mathcal{D}}. A Gorenstein toric contact manifold is said to be flexible if it is not rigid. Two toric diagrams 𝒟{\mathcal{D}} and 𝒟′{\mathcal{D}}^{\prime} are said to be Ehrhart equivalent if they have the same Ehrhart polynomial, i.e. if L𝒟​(t)=L𝒟′​(t)L_{{\mathcal{D}}}(t)=L_{{\mathcal{D}}^{\prime}}(t).

The Ehrhart polynomial L𝒟​(t)L_{{\mathcal{D}}}(t) determines the volume of 𝒟⊂ℝn{\mathcal{D}}\subset\mathbb{R}^{n} through its tnt^{n} coefficient. When n=1n=1 the volume is the only invariant of a toric diagram 𝒟⊂ℝ{\mathcal{D}}\subset\mathbb{R}, up to unimodular equivalence, and so we have that all Gorenstein toric contact 33-manifolds are rigid. These are S3S^{3} with its standard contact structure (toric diagram =[0,1]=[0,1]), and the Lens spaces Lp+13​(1,p)L^{3}_{p+1}(1,p), p∈ℕp\in\mathbb{N}, with standard quotient contact structure coming from S3S^{3} (toric diagram =[0,p+1]=[0,p+1]).

Any odd-dimensional sphere S2​n+1S^{2n+1} with its standard contact structure is an example of a rigid Gorenstein toric contact manifold. In fact, its toric diagram is the unimodular simplex

𝒟=conv⁡{𝟎,𝒆1,…,𝒆n}⊂ℝn,where 𝒆1,…,𝒆n are the canonical basis vectors of ℝn,{\mathcal{D}}=\operatorname{conv}\{\bm{0},\bm{e}_{1},\ldots,\bm{e}_{n}\}\subset\mathbb{R}^{n}\,,\ \text{where $\bm{e}_{1},\ldots,\bm{e}_{n}$ are the canonical basis vectors of $\mathbb{R}^{n}$,}

which is well-known to be Ehrhart determined.

Despite these examples, flexibility is the norm and this can be seen already when n=2n=2. For example, given p∈ℕp\in\mathbb{N}, the toric contact manifolds

(S3×S2,ξp)​with toric diagram​𝒟p=conv⁡{(0,0),(1,0),(0,1),(p+1,p+1)}(S^{3}\times S^{2},\xi_{p})\ \text{with toric diagram}\ {\mathcal{D}}_{p}=\operatorname{conv}\{(0,0),(1,0),(0,1),(p+1,p+1)\}

and

S∗​(Lp+13​(1,q))​with toric diagram​𝒟p,q=conv⁡{(0,0),(1,0),(p+1,q),(p+1,q+1)},S^{\ast}(L^{3}_{p+1}(1,q))\ \text{with toric diagram}\ {\mathcal{D}}_{p,q}=\operatorname{conv}\{(0,0),(1,0),(p+1,q),(p+1,q+1)\}\,,

for all q∈ℕq\in\mathbb{N} coprime with p+1p+1, where S∗​(Lp+13​(1,q))≅Lp+13​(1,q)×S2S^{\ast}(L^{3}_{p+1}(1,q))\cong L^{3}_{p+1}(1,q)\times S^{2} is the unit cosphere bundle of Lp+13​(1,q)L^{3}_{p+1}(1,q), have the same contact Betti numbers

c​b0=p,c​b2=2​p+1​and​c​b2​k=2​p+2,∀k≥n.cb_{0}=p\,,\ cb_{2}=2p+1\ \text{and}\ cb_{2k}=2p+2\,,\ \forall\,k\geq n\,.

See [1, 3] for more information on these examples.

In this paper we will describe some families of flexible and rigid Gorenstein toric contact manifolds that arise as the smooth prequantization of monotone toric symplectic manifolds. These families are interesting both from the toric contact geometry and the Ehrhart theory points of view.

Recall that a symplectic manifold (M2​n,ω)(M^{2n},\omega) is said to be monotone when the first Chern class c1​(M)c_{1}(M) is a multiple of [ω]∈H2​(M;ℝ)[\omega]\in H^{2}(M;\mathbb{R}). As we recall in Section 4, if (M2​n,[ω]=c1​(M))(M^{2n},[\omega]=c_{1}(M)) is a monotone toric symplectic manifold, its moment polytope 𝒫⊂ℝn{\mathcal{P}}\subset\mathbb{R}^{n} can be translated to be a reflexive Delzant polytope and its prequantization (N2​n+1,ξ)(N^{2n+1},\xi) is a Gorenstein toric contact manifold with toric diagram 𝒟=−𝒫∗⊂ℝn{\mathcal{D}}=-{\mathcal{P}}^{\ast}\subset\mathbb{R}^{n}, where 𝒫∗{\mathcal{P}}^{\ast} is the polar dual of 𝒫{\mathcal{P}}:

Our second family arises from the fact that the first Chern class of the trivial Bott manifold ℙ1×⋯×ℙ1\mathbb{P}^{1}\times\cdots\times\mathbb{P}^{1} is divisible by 2 and we can consider an integral symplectic form ω\omega with [ω]=c1​(ℙ1×⋯×ℙ1)/2[\omega]=c_{1}(\mathbb{P}^{1}\times\cdots\times\mathbb{P}^{1})/2. Theorem 1.5.

In any dimension n∈ℕn\in\mathbb{N}, the prequantization (N2​n+1,ξ)(N^{2n+1},\xi) of ℙ1×⋯×ℙ1\mathbb{P}^{1}\times\cdots\times\mathbb{P}^{1} with symplectic form ω\omega such that [ω]=c1​(ℙ1×⋯×ℙ1)/2[\omega]=c_{1}(\mathbb{P}^{1}\times\cdots\times\mathbb{P}^{1})/2 is a rigid Gorenstein toric contact manifold.

Remark 1.8.

All the rigidity examples considered in Theorems 1.5 and 1.7 arise from primitive prequantizations of monotone toric symplectic manifolds with divisible (non-primitive) first Chern class. It would be interesting to find rigid examples without this feature but we do not know if they exist.

2. Toric symplectic manifolds

A toric symplectic manifold is a closed symplectic manifold (M,ω)(M,\omega) of dimension 2​n2n equipped with an effective hamiltomian nn-torus action, i.e.

τ:𝕋n≅ℝn/2​π​ℤn↪Ham⁡(M,ω).\tau:\mathbb{T}^{n}\cong\mathbb{R}^{n}/2\pi\mathbb{Z}^{n}\hookrightarrow\operatorname{Ham}(M,\omega)\,.

The nn hamiltonian functions (h1,…,hn)(h_{1},\ldots,h_{n}) generating these nn commuting circle actions give rise to the moment map

𝝁:M→ℝn,𝝁=(h1,…,hn).\bm{\mu}:M\to\mathbb{R}^{n}\,,\ \bm{\mu}=(h_{1},\ldots,h_{n})\,.

The Atiyah-Guillemin-Sternberg convexity theorem implies that the moment map image, 𝒫:=𝝁​(M){\mathcal{P}}:=\bm{\mu}(M), is the convex hull of the images of the fixed points of the action, i.e. 𝒫=conv⁡(𝝁​(M𝕋n)){\mathcal{P}}=\operatorname{conv}(\bm{\mu}(M^{\mathbb{T}^{n}})), and can be described as a finite intersection of half-spaces:

𝒫=∩j=1d{𝒙∈ℝn:𝒙⋅𝝂j+λj≥0},{\mathcal{P}}=\cap_{j=1}^{d}\left\{\bm{x}\in\mathbb{R}^{n}\,:\ \bm{x}\cdot\bm{\nu}_{j}+\lambda_{j}\geq 0\right\}\,,

where 𝝂j∈ℤn\bm{\nu}_{j}\in\mathbb{Z}^{n} is the primitive integral interior normal to the jj-th facet of PP and λj∈ℝ\lambda_{j}\in\mathbb{R} determines its position relative to the origin. 𝒫⊂ℝn{\mathcal{P}}\subset\mathbb{R}^{n} is called the moment polytope of the toric symplectic manifold (M,ω,τ)(M,\omega,\tau). It is a Delzant polytope and a complete invariant of (M,ω,τ)(M,\omega,\tau) [11]. Note that the hamiltonian functions (h1,…,hn)(h_{1},\ldots,h_{n}) are only determined up to constants and hence the moment polytope 𝒫{\mathcal{P}} is only determined up to translation in ℝn\mathbb{R}^{n}.

The real numbers λj\lambda_{j}, j=1,…,dj=1,\ldots,d, can be used to determine the cohomology class of the symplectic form ω\omega and we have that [ω]=c1​(M)[\omega]=c_{1}(M), i.e. (M,ω,τ)(M,\omega,\tau) is a monotone toric symplectic manifold, if and only if there is a translation of 𝒫{\mathcal{P}} such that all the λj\lambda_{j}’s are equal to 11, i.e.

𝒫=∩j=1d{𝒙∈ℝn:𝒙⋅𝝂j+1≥0}.{\mathcal{P}}=\cap_{j=1}^{d}\left\{\bm{x}\in\mathbb{R}^{n}\,:\ \bm{x}\cdot\bm{\nu}_{j}+1\geq 0\right\}\,.

4. Gorenstein toric contact manifolds

In this section we will briefly recall the 11-11 correspondence between Gorenstein toric contact manifolds, i.e. good toric contact manifolds (in the sense of [16]) with zero first Chern class, and toric diagrams (defined below). We follow closely the presentations in [2, 4], which contain further details.

Via symplectization, there is a 11-11 correspondence between co-oriented contact manifolds and symplectic cones, i.e. triples (W,ω,X)(W,\omega,X) where (W,ω)(W,\omega) is a connected symplectic manifold and XX is a vector field, the Liouville vector field, generating a proper ℝ\mathbb{R}-action ρt:W→W\rho_{t}:W\to W, t∈ℝt\in\mathbb{R}, such that ρt∗​(ω)=et​ω\rho_{t}^{\ast}(\omega)=e^{t}\omega. A closed symplectic cone is a symplectic cone (W,ω,X)(W,\omega,X) for which the corresponding contact manifold N=W/ℝN=W/\mathbb{R} is closed.

A toric contact manifold is a closed contact manifold of dimension 2​n+12n+1 equipped with an effective hamiltonian action of the standard torus of dimension n+1n+1: 𝕋n+1=ℝn+1/2​π​ℤn+1\mathbb{T}^{n+1}=\mathbb{R}^{n+1}/2\pi\mathbb{Z}^{n+1}. Also via symplectization, toric contact manifolds are in 11-11 correspondence with toric symplectic cones, i.e. closed symplectic cones (W,ω,X)(W,\omega,X) of dimension 2​(n+1)2(n+1) equipped with an effective XX-preserving hamiltonian 𝕋n+1\mathbb{T}^{n+1}-action, with moment map 𝝁:W→ℝn+1\bm{\mu}:W\to\mathbb{R}^{n+1} such that 𝝁​(ρt​(w))=et​𝝁​(w)\bm{\mu}(\rho_{t}(w))=e^{t}\bm{\mu}(w), for all w∈Ww\in W and t∈ℝt\in\mathbb{R}. Its moment cone is defined to be C:=𝝁​(W)∪{0}⊂ℝn+1C:=\bm{\mu}(W)\cup\{0\}\subset\mathbb{R}^{n+1}.

A toric contact manifold is good if its toric symplectic cone has a moment cone with the following properties.

Definition 4.1.

A cone 𝒞⊂ℝn+1{\mathcal{C}}\subset\mathbb{R}^{n+1} is good if it is strictly convex and there exists a minimal set of primitive vectors 𝛎1,…,𝛎d∈ℤn+1\bm{\nu}_{1},\ldots,\bm{\nu}_{d}\in\mathbb{Z}^{n+1}, with d≥n+1d\geq n+1, such that

  • (i)

    𝒞=⋂j=1d{𝒙∈ℝn+1∣𝒙⋅𝝂j≥0}{\mathcal{C}}=\bigcap_{j=1}^{d}\{\bm{x}\in\mathbb{R}^{n+1}\mid\bm{x}\cdot\bm{\nu}_{j}\geq 0\}.

  • (ii)

    Any codimension-kk face of 𝒞{\mathcal{C}}, 1≤k≤n1\leq k\leq n, is the intersection of exactly kk facets whose set of normals can be completed to an integral basis of ℤn+1\mathbb{Z}^{n+1}.

The primitive vectors 𝛎1,…,𝛎d∈ℤn+1\bm{\nu}_{1},\ldots,\bm{\nu}_{d}\in\mathbb{Z}^{n+1} are called the defining normals of the good cone 𝒞⊂ℝn+1{\mathcal{C}}\subset\mathbb{R}^{n+1}.

The analogue for good toric contact manifolds of Delzant’s classification theorem for closed toric symplectic manifolds is the following result (see [16]).

Theorem 4.2.

For each good cone 𝒞⊂ℝn+1{\mathcal{C}}\subset\mathbb{R}^{n+1} there exists a unique closed toric symplectic cone (W𝒞,ω𝒞,X𝒞,𝛍𝒞)(W_{{\mathcal{C}}},\omega_{{\mathcal{C}}},X_{{\mathcal{C}}},\bm{\mu}_{{\mathcal{C}}}) with moment cone 𝒞{\mathcal{C}}.

6.1. Polytopes, cones and polar duality

Here we give a quick introduction to polyhedra in ℝn\mathbb{R}^{n}. For a thorough presentation of this subject see for example [21].

Definition 6.1 (Polytope).

A polytope 𝒫⊂ℝn\mathcal{P}\subset\mathbb{R}^{n} is the convex hull of a finite set of points in ℝn\mathbb{R}^{n}, i.e.,

𝒫\displaystyle\mathcal{P} =conv⁡{𝒗1,𝒗2,…,𝒗m}\displaystyle=\operatorname{conv}\{\bm{v}_{1},\bm{v}_{2},\dots,\bm{v}_{m}\}

This is commonly referred as the vertex or 𝒱\mathcal{V}-representation of 𝒫\mathcal{P}. Depending on the context, it might be better to deal with the hyperplane or ℋ\mathcal{H}-representation of a polytope in which we define a polytope by the intersection of a finite set of halfspaces delimited by hyperplanes, i.e.,

𝒫\displaystyle\mathcal{P} ={𝒙∈ℝn:A​𝒙≤𝒃}\displaystyle=\{\bm{x}\in\mathbb{R}^{n}:A\bm{x}\leq\bm{b}\}

where A∈ℝd×nA\in\mathbb{R}^{d\times n} and b∈ℝdb\in\mathbb{R}^{d}. This intersection is always a polyhedron but, since it might be unbounded, it is not always a polytope. A polytope is a bounded polyhedron.

All polytopes can be expressed in both representations, although the problem of algorithmically converting between them is non-trivial.

The interior of a polytope 𝒫\mathcal{P} will be denoted by 𝒫∘\mathcal{P}^{\circ}.

We call a polytope integral if all its vertices lie on the lattice ℤn\mathbb{Z}^{n}. An hyperplane

𝒫⊂{𝒙∈ℝn:𝒂⋅𝒙≤b}​ or ​𝒫⊂{𝒙∈ℝn:𝒂⋅𝒙≥b}.\mathcal{P}\subset\{\bm{x}\in\mathbb{R}^{n}:\bm{a}\cdot\bm{x}\leq b\}\text{ or }\mathcal{P}\subset\{\bm{x}\in\mathbb{R}^{n}:\bm{a}\cdot\bm{x}\geq b\}.
Definition 6.2 (Face).

A set F⊂ℝnF\subset\mathbb{R}^{n} is face of a polytope 𝒫\mathcal{P} if

F=𝒫∩ℋ\displaystyle F=\mathcal{P}\cap\mathcal{H}

for some supporting hyperplane ℋ\mathcal{H}.

The dimension of a set S⊂ℝnS\subset\mathbb{R}^{n} equals the dimension of the affine span generated by the points in SS. If SS is a polytope and dimS=n\dim S=n we refer to SS as a nn-polytope.

A nn-polytope 𝒫{\mathcal{P}} has faces of every dimension between −1-1 and nn, in particular:

  • •

    𝒫\mathcal{P} has a single face of dimension nn, which is itself;

  • •

    𝒫\mathcal{P} has faces of dimension n−1n-1, called facets;

  • •

    𝒫\mathcal{P} has faces of dimension 11, called edges;

  • •

    𝒫\mathcal{P} has faces of dimension 0, called vertices;

  • •

    𝒫\mathcal{P} has a single face of dimension −1-1, the ∅\emptyset.

Throughout the text we will refer to the set of faces ordered by the (usual) inclusion as the face poset. We will say that two polytopes are combinatorially equivalent if their face posets are isomorphic.

A useful construction on a polytope is cone⁡(𝒫)\operatorname{cone}(\mathcal{P}) that we define now.

For a polytope 𝒫⊂ℝn\mathcal{P}\subset\mathbb{R}^{n}

We define the polar dual of a polytope 𝒫⊂ℝn\mathcal{P}\subset\mathbb{R}^{n}.

For a polytope 𝒫⊂ℝn\mathcal{P}\subset\mathbb{R}^{n} its polar dual

An integral polytope 𝒫\mathcal{P} is said to be reflexive if 𝒫∗\mathcal{P}^{*} is also integral.

A nn-polytope is simple if every vertex is contained in nn edges.

6.2. Ehrhart theory

In this subsection we define the basics of Ehrhart theory that will be used in the paper. For a more detailed exposition and proofs see for example [5].

Ehrhart theory studies functions that count the number of integer points in the (integer) dilates of a polytope 𝒫\mathcal{P}. Formally, we are interested in the function

If 𝒫\mathcal{P} is an integral nn-polytope then L𝒫​(t)L_{\mathcal{P}}(t) is polynomial in tt with degree exactly nn.

6.3. Delzant polytopes, toric diagrams and prequantizations

Let us introduce two classes of polytopes that are relevant in the context of toric symplectic and contact geometry.

Definition 6.13 (Delzant polytope).

A Delzant polytope is a simple nn-polytope such that for every vertex the nn primitive edge vectors form a basis of ℤn\mathbb{Z}^{n}.

Definition 6.14 (Toric Diagrams).

A toric diagram is a simplicial nn-polytope such that the nn vertices of each facet can be translated to form a basis of ℤn\mathbb{Z}^{n}.

We will now define the prequantization combinatorial construction that relates certain Delzant polytopes with toric diagrams.

9.2. Classification via Rooted 33-Cacti

To systematically classify the toric diagrams in 𝒞n\mathcal{C}_{n} up to unimodular equivalence, we encode the iterative pseudo-bipyramid construction into a purely combinatorial object, a rooted 33-cactus graph. There are several possible choices of combinatorial objects to carry out this classification. We chose rooted 33-cacti because they have already been related to monotone Bott manifolds in [10].

A 33-cactus (or triangle-cactus) is a connected graph in which every edge belongs to exactly one simple cycle, and every such cycle is a triangle (K3K_{3}). We can naturally construct a bijection between the lattice points in a polytope 𝒫∈𝒞n\mathcal{P}\in\mathcal{C}_{n} and the vertices of a rooted 33-cactus TT, where the root corresponds to the origin 𝟎\bm{0}, and every other node corresponds to a non-zero boundary lattice point of the polytope. Note that whenever a rooted 33-cactus has nn triangles then it has 3​n−(n−1)=2​n+13n-(n-1)=2n+1 vertices.

We define this mapping explicitly by building the graph incrementally, alongside the pseudo-bipyramid construction. For the base case n=1n=1, we start with the polytope 𝒫1=[−1,1]∈𝒞1\mathcal{P}_{1}=[-1,1]\in\mathcal{C}_{1}. It contains exactly three lattice points: the origin 0, and the boundary points −1-1 and 11. The corresponding graph G1G_{1} is initialized as a single triangle (K3K_{3}), where the root node represents 0 and the other two vertices represent −1-1 and 11. Suppose we have a 33-cactus GkG_{k} representing a polytope 𝒫k∈𝒞k\mathcal{P}_{k}\in\mathcal{C}_{k}. To construct the next iteration 𝒫k+1=P-bipyr​(𝒫k,𝟎,𝒚)\mathcal{P}_{k+1}=\text{P-bipyr}(\mathcal{P}_{k},\bm{0},\bm{y}), we first map 𝒙↦(𝒙,0)\bm{x}\mapsto(\bm{x},0), and then select an existing lattice point 𝒚∈ℤk+1∩𝒫k×{0}\bm{y}\in\mathbb{Z}^{k+1}\cap\mathcal{P}_{k}\times\{0\}, which corresponds to a node in GkG_{k} (where choosing 𝒚=0\bm{y}=0 corresponds to selecting the root node). This operation introduces exactly two new lattice points to the newly formed polytope: 𝒗k+1:=𝒆k+1\bm{v}_{k+1}:=\bm{e}_{k+1} and 𝒘k+1:=𝒚−𝒆k+1\bm{w}_{k+1}:=\bm{y}-\bm{e}_{k+1}. In our graph, we represent this geometric addition by attaching a new triangle directly to the node corresponding to 𝒚\bm{y}. The two newly added vertices of this triangle naturally represent 𝒗k+1\bm{v}_{k+1} and 𝒘k+1\bm{w}_{k+1} and we will later refer to 𝒚\bm{y} as the parent of 𝒗k+1\bm{v}_{k+1} and 𝒘k+1\bm{w}_{k+1}. This procedure can be visualized in Figure 11.

Notice the invariant of this construction: the two newly added vertices always perfectly sum to their parent, 𝒗k+𝒘k=𝒚\bm{v}_{k}+\bm{w}_{k}=\bm{y}. Before proving the main equivalence theorem, we must establish a lemma regarding this additive relationship.

Lemma 9.3.

For any toric diagram 𝒫∈𝒞n\mathcal{P}\in\mathcal{C}_{n}, three distinct points 𝐚,𝐛,𝐜∈𝒫∩ℤn\bm{a},\bm{b},\bm{c}\in\mathcal{P}\cap\mathbb{Z}^{n} satisfy 𝐚+𝐛=𝐜\bm{a}+\bm{b}=\bm{c} if and only if the corresponding vertices form a triangle in the associated 33-cactus GG, with 𝐜\bm{c} as the parent of the others.

(⟸)(\impliedby) This follows directly from the construction.

(⟹)(\implies) Suppose 𝒂+𝒃=𝒄\bm{a}+\bm{b}=\bm{c} for three distinct lattice points in 𝒫\mathcal{P}. Because they are distinct, neither 𝒂\bm{a} nor 𝒃\bm{b} can be the origin 𝟎\bm{0} (otherwise we would have 𝒂=𝒄\bm{a}=\bm{c} or 𝒃=𝒄\bm{b}=\bm{c}).

Let k≤nk\leq n be the largest coordinate index where at least one of these vertices has a non-zero entry. All coordinates in our construction belong to {−1,0,1}\{-1,0,1\}.

If ck≠0c_{k}\neq 0, assume without loss of generality ck=1c_{k}=1. By the pseudo-bipyramid construction, there is a unique point in 𝒫\mathcal{P} with a kk-th coordinate of 11 and all subsequent coordinates 0. This forces 𝒂=𝒄\bm{a}=\bm{c}, which contradicts the assumption that 𝒂\bm{a} and 𝒄\bm{c} are distinct points.

Thus, we must have ck=0c_{k}=0, forcing {ak,bk}={1,−1}\{a_{k},b_{k}\}=\{1,-1\}. This uniquely identifies 𝒂\bm{a} and 𝒃\bm{b} as the exact pair of vertices added at dimension kk. We define a leaf triangle in a rooted 33-cactus GG as a triangle containing exactly two vertices of degree 22 (the leaves of the graph), with the third vertex acting as their parent.

Lemma 9.4.

The unimodular equivalence class of a polytope 𝒫∈𝒞n\mathcal{P}\in\mathcal{C}_{n} depends only on its associated 33-cactus GG, and not on the specific sequence in which its triangles are constructed. Consequently, any leaf triangle of GG can be assumed to correspond to the final construction step in dimension nn.

Proof.

The rooted structure of GG imposes a natural partial order on its triangles, as any parent vertex must be generated before its children. A valid geometric construction of 𝒫\mathcal{P} corresponds to choosing a linear extension of this partial order, assigning each of the nn triangles to a distinct step k∈{1,…,n}k\in\{1,\dots,n\}.

By the pseudo-bipyramid construction, at each step k∈{2,…,n}k\in\{2,\dots,n\}, the operation embeds the (k−1)(k-1)-dimensional base into ℝk\mathbb{R}^{k} and introduces two new vertices. Specifically, the two vertices added at step kk take the values ±1\pm 1 exclusively in the kk-th coordinate. As a result, the geometric embedding of each triangle in GG is entirely determined by the axis 𝒆k\bm{e}_{k} assigned at its creation step.

Suppose we generate two polytopes, 𝒫1\mathcal{P}_{1} and 𝒫2\mathcal{P}_{2}, using two different valid construction sequences for the exact same graph GG. Because the set of triangles and their parent-child adjacencies are identical, the step sequence for 𝒫2\mathcal{P}_{2} is merely a permutation of the sequence for 𝒫1\mathcal{P}_{1}. Geometrically, this simply permutes the assignment of the orthogonal basis vectors {𝒆1,…,𝒆n}\{\bm{e}_{1},\dots,\bm{e}_{n}\} to the triangles of GG. The linear transformation that maps the vertices of 𝒫1\mathcal{P}_{1} to 𝒫2\mathcal{P}_{2} is exactly this permutation of the coordinate axis. Because permutation matrices have determinant ±1\pm 1, this transformation is unimodular, therefore, any two valid attachment sequences for GG generate polytopes that are unimodularly equivalent.

This allows us to freely reorder the construction so that any chosen leaf triangle is generated at the final step nn. ∎

Theorem 10.9.

Let 𝒮{\mathcal{S}} be a nn-dimensional toric diagram. If its h∗h^{*}-polynomial is h𝒮∗​(z)=1+z+z2+⋯+zn−1h_{{\mathcal{S}}}^{\ast}(z)=1+z+z^{2}+\dots+z^{n-1}, then 𝒮{\mathcal{S}} is unimodularly equivalent to 𝒟k\mathcal{D}_{k} for a unique k∈ℤk\in\mathbb{Z} satisfying 0≤k<n0\leq k<n and k≡n(mod2)k\equiv n\pmod{2}.

Proof.

From the given h∗h^{\ast}-polynomial, the normalized volume of 𝒮{\mathcal{S}} is h𝒮∗​(1)=nh_{{\mathcal{S}}}^{\ast}(1)=n. The total number of lattice points is L𝒮​(1)=h1∗+n+1=n+2L_{{\mathcal{S}}}(1)=h_{1}^{\ast}+n+1=n+2. Since the degree of h𝒮∗h_{{\mathcal{S}}}^{\ast} is n−1n-1 and it is palindromic, 𝒮{\mathcal{S}} is Gorenstein of index 22 thus contains no interior lattice points. Because 𝒮{\mathcal{S}} is a toric diagram, its facets are primitive, so all n+2n+2 lattice points are vertices.

Next, we determine the number of facets. The standard expansion of the Ehrhart polynomial dictates that the coefficient of tn−1t^{n-1} in L𝒮​(t)L_{{\mathcal{S}}}(t) is

12​∑F∈ℱ​(𝒮)Voln−1​(F).\frac{1}{2}\sum_{F\in\mathcal{F}({\mathcal{S}})}\text{Vol}_{n-1}(F).

Because every facet FF of a toric diagram is a unimodular simplex, the relative volume Voln−1​(F)=1(n−1)!\text{Vol}_{n-1}(F)=\frac{1}{(n-1)!}, making this coefficient exactly fn−12​(n−1)!\frac{f_{n-1}}{2(n-1)!}. Using our specific h∗h^{\ast}-polynomial, the Ehrhart polynomial is L𝒮​(t)=∑j=0n−1(t+n−jn)L_{{\mathcal{S}}}(t)=\sum_{j=0}^{n-1}\binom{t+n-j}{n}, where the coefficient of tn−1t^{n-1} is n(n−1)!\frac{n}{(n-1)!}. Equating these gives a facet count of fn−1=2​nf_{n-1}=2n.

Any nn-dimensional polytope with n+2n+2 vertices admits a unique (up to scaling) affine dependency ∑λi​𝒗i=0\sum\lambda_{i}\bm{v}_{i}=0 with ∑λi=0\sum\lambda_{i}=0. Because every facet of 𝒮{\mathcal{S}} is a simplex, no subset of n+1n+1 vertices can be affinely dependent. This strictly guarantees that λi≠0\lambda_{i}\neq 0 for all ii, meaning every vertex participates in the dependency. This dependency therefore partitions the vertices into exactly two disjoint sets I={𝒗i:λi>0}I=\{\bm{v}_{i}:\lambda_{i}>0\} and J={𝒗j:λj<0}J=\{\bm{v}_{j}:\lambda_{j}<0\}. A subset of vertices forms a facet if and only if it is obtained by removing exactly one vertex from II and one from JJ. Thus, the number of facets is given by the product |I|⋅|J||I|\cdot|J|. We are led to the system

|I|+|J|=n+2and|I|⋅|J|=2​n,|I|+|J|=n+2\quad\text{and}\quad|I|\cdot|J|=2n\,,

whose unique integer solution is {|I|,|J|}={n,2}\{|I|,|J|\}=\{n,2\}. A polytope whose vertices are partitioned in this way is, by definition, a bipyramid over a (n−1)(n-1)-simplex. Let ℰ={𝒗1,…,𝒗n}{\mathcal{E}}=\{\bm{v}_{1},\dots,\bm{v}_{n}\} be the base vertices and A={𝒚1,𝒚2}A=\{\bm{y}_{1},\bm{y}_{2}\} be the apices.

The bipyramid 𝒮{\mathcal{S}} is triangulated by nn lattice simplices Si=conv​((ℰ∖{𝒗i})∪𝒜)S_{i}=\text{conv}(({\mathcal{E}}\setminus\{\bm{v}_{i}\})\cup{\mathcal{A}}), therefore the sum of their normalized volumes must be nn. Since each SiS_{i} is a lattice simplex, its normalized volume must be an integer greater than or equal to 11. This forces the normalized volume of SiS_{i} to be 11 for all 1≤i≤n1\leq i\leq n. The equality of these volumes implies that the coefficients of the base vertices in the affine dependency are identical. The dependency relation is thus

∑i=1n𝒗i=μ1​𝒚1+μ2​𝒚2,\sum_{i=1}^{n}\bm{v}_{i}=\mu_{1}\bm{y}_{1}+\mu_{2}\bm{y}_{2}\,,

where μ1+μ2=n\mu_{1}+\mu_{2}=n. By setting k=μ2−μ1k=\mu_{2}-\mu_{1}, we find μ1=n−k2\mu_{1}=\frac{n-k}{2} and μ2=n+k2\mu_{2}=\frac{n+k}{2}. Up to swapping the apices 𝒚1\bm{y}_{1} and 𝒚2\bm{y}_{2}, we may assume 0≤k<n0\leq k<n.

Finally, because Vol​(Sn)=1\text{Vol}(S_{n})=1, the vectors {𝒗1−𝒚1,…,𝒗n−1−𝒚1,𝒚2−𝒚1}\{\bm{v}_{1}-\bm{y}_{1},\dots,\bm{v}_{n-1}-\bm{y}_{1},\bm{y}_{2}-\bm{y}_{1}\} form a ℤ\mathbb{Z}-basis for the lattice. ϕ​(𝒚1)=𝒂n,ϕ​(𝒚2)=𝒂n+1,ϕ​(𝒗i)=𝒂i​ for ​1≤i≤n−1.\phi(\bm{y}_{1})=\bm{a}_{n},\quad\phi(\bm{y}_{2})=\bm{a}_{n+1},\quad\phi(\bm{v}_{i})=\bm{a}_{i}\text{ for }1\leq i\leq n-1\,.
