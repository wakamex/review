Bott-integrable contact forms with large systolic ratio

Hansjörg Geiges Mathematisches Institut, Universität zu Köln, Weyertal 86–90, 50931 Köln, Germany geiges@math.uni-koeln.de , Jakob Hedicke Afdeling Wiskunde, Radboud Universiteit, Heyendaalseweg 135, 6525AJ Nijmegen, Netherlands jakob.hedicke@gmail.com and Murat Sağlam Mathematisches Institut, Universität zu Köln, Weyertal 86–90, 50931 Köln, Germany msaglam@math.uni-koeln.de
Abstract.

We show that there is no universal upper bound for the systolic ratio of Bott-integrable contact forms on closed 33-manifolds, thus providing further evidence for the relative flexibility of integrable contact forms. For the proof, we study piecewise linear approximations of Lutz forms and establish integrability of a ‘plug’ constructed by Abbondandolo, Bramham, Hryniewicz and Salomão for pushing up the systolic ratio.

Key words and phrases:
Reeb dynamics, Bott integrability, systolic ratio, contact structure

37J35, 37J55, 53C23, 53D35

1. Introduction

The systole of a closed Riemannian or Finsler manifold is the length of the shortest non-contractible closed geodesic. The systolic ratio is the scale-invariant dimensionless quotient between the nthn^{\mathrm{th}} power of the systole and the volume of the nn-dimensional manifold. The systolic ratio has a long history, especially in the Riemannian geometry of surfaces, and under a variety of assumptions upper bounds are known; see [4] for an overview, also with a view towards Finsler geometry.

The (co-)geodesic flow can be interpreted as the Reeb flow of the Liouville contact form on the unit cotangent bundle; see [8] for the Riemannian and [7] for the Finsler case. Based on this fact, in [5] it was first observed that contact geometry is a natural setting to study questions in systolic geometry; see also [3, 4].

Given a closed manifold MM of dimension 2​n+12n+1 admitting a contact form α\alpha, we denote by R=RαR=R_{\alpha} the Reeb vector field of α\alpha, and by vol⁡(M,α)\operatorname{vol}(M,\alpha) the volume of MM with respect to the volume form α∧(d​α)n\alpha\wedge(\mathrm{d}\alpha)^{n}. If there are any periodic Reeb orbits—which is always the case in dimension three [17] and conjecturally in all (odd) dimensions—the infimum over the periods of all closed Reeb orbits is finite, and actually a minimum by an argument as in [11, p. 109]. We denote this (positive) minimum by Tmin​(M,α)T_{\mathrm{min}}(M,\alpha). The scale-invariant systolic ratio is then defined as

ρ​(M,α):=Tmin​(M,α)n+1vol⁡(M,α).\rho(M,\alpha):=\frac{T_{\mathrm{min}}(M,\alpha)^{n+1}}{\operatorname{vol}(M,\alpha)}.

In general, there is no upper bound on this systolic ratio, that is, given a contact manifold (M,ξ)(M,\xi), one can find contact forms α\alpha, defining ξ=ker⁡α\xi=\ker\alpha, with arbitrarily large systolic ratio; see [3, 4, 15, 16]. However, for many classes of Reeb flows in dimension three—to which we now restrict our attention—the imposition of additional symmetries leads to such bounds. Examples are Reeb flows of contact forms that are C2C^{2}-close to a Zoll contact form [1, 2], or Reeb flows of S1S^{1}-invariant contact forms on Seifert bundles with non-vanishing Euler number [18, 19].

A symmetry assumption weaker than S1S^{1}-invariance, but still believed to be quite strong, is Bott integrability of the contact form as defined in [9]. The main result of the present paper says that there is no universal systolic inequality on the class of Bott-integrable contact forms on any given closed contact 33-manifold. This shows that integrable contact forms abound within the class of contact forms defining a given integrable contact structure, and it provides further evidence that the integrability requirement may be less restrictive than expected. This theorem complements the results about overtwisted Bott-integrable contact structures in [10], which can be read as statements about the abundance of integrable contact structures within the space of all contact structures.

Recall from [9, 10] that a contact form α\alpha on a compact 33-manifold MM is called Bott integrable if there is a Morse–Bott function f:M→ℝf\colon\thinspace M\rightarrow\mathbb{R} invariant under the Reeb flow of α\alpha. A contact structure ξ\xi on MM said to be Bott integrable if there is some Bott-integrable contact form α\alpha with ξ=ker⁡α\xi=\ker\alpha.

Theorem 1.

Let ξ\xi be a Bott-integrable contact structure on a closed 33-manifold MM. Then for every C∈ℝ+C\in\mathbb{R}^{+} there exists a Bott-integrable contact form α\alpha defining ξ=ker⁡α\xi=\ker\alpha with systolic ratio ρ​(M,α)>C\rho(M,\alpha)>C.

The strategy for the proof of this theorem is as follows. As shown in [9], Bott-integrable contact forms have a simple description as so-called Lutz forms away from the singular level sets of the Morse–Bott function. In Sections 2 and 3 we show that outside a set of small volume these Lutz forms can be replaced by linear Lutz forms, while keeping control over TminT_{\mathrm{min}}. Into these linear pieces we then insert plugs as constructed by Abbondandolo, Bramham, Hryniewicz and Salomão in [3, 4]. These plugs are solid tori with a contact form of small volume, standard near the boundary, and with Tmin≥1T_{\mathrm{min}}\geq 1. By filling all but a small volume part of the linear Lutz pieces with such plugs, the total volume can be made arbitrarily small, whereas TminT_{\mathrm{min}} stays bounded from below. In order to apply this construction in the Bott-integrable setting, we need to construct an invariant Morse–Bott function on the plug; this will be done in Section 4. The estimates on volume and TminT_{\mathrm{min}} that establish Theorem 1 are given in Section 5.

A long-standing open question of Katok [12] asks whether every low-dimensional volume-preserving dynamical system with zero entropy can be approximated by integrable systems. A version of that question on surfaces was studied in [6] and [13], where the authors show that there is a gap between autonomous Hamiltonian diffeomorphisms and integrable Hamiltonian diffeomorphisms in the C0C^{0}-topology and the Hofer metric, respectively. Theorem 1, taken together with the results of Abbondandolo, Benedetti and Edtmair [1, 2] on Zoll contact forms, implies that there exist integrable contact forms that are far from Zoll contact forms in the C2C^{2}-topology. It would be interesting to understand whether there are integrable contact forms far from S1S^{1}-invariant ones. The same question can be asked with the C2C^{2}-topology replaced by the Banach–Mazur pseudometric on contact forms introduced in [2], which may be regarded as an analogue of the Hofer metric on Hamiltonian diffeomorphisms.

2. The systolic geometry of Lutz forms

A Lutz form is a contact form α\alpha on W:=[−1,1]×T2W:=[-1,1]\times T^{2} that can be written as

α=h1​(r)​d​x1+h2​(r)​d​x2,\alpha=h_{1}(r)\,\mathrm{d}x_{1}+h_{2}(r)\,\mathrm{d}x_{2},

where rr is the coordinate on [−1,1][-1,1], and x1,x2x_{1},x_{2} are those on T2=(ℝ/ℤ)2T^{2}=(\mathbb{R}/\mathbb{Z})^{2}. With the orientation of WW defined by the volume form d​r∧d​x1∧d​x2\mathrm{d}r\wedge\mathrm{d}x_{1}\wedge\mathrm{d}x_{2}, the contact condition becomes

Δh:=|h1h1′h2h2′|<0,\Delta_{h}:=\begin{vmatrix}h_{1}&h_{1}^{\prime}\\ h_{2}&h_{2}^{\prime}\end{vmatrix}<0,

since d​α=h1′​d​r∧d​x1+h2′​d​r∧d​x2\mathrm{d}\alpha=h_{1}^{\prime}\,\mathrm{d}r\wedge\mathrm{d}x_{1}+h_{2}^{\prime}\,\mathrm{d}r\wedge\mathrm{d}x_{2} and α∧d​α=−Δh​d​r∧d​x1∧d​x2\alpha\wedge\mathrm{d}\alpha=-\Delta_{h}\,\mathrm{d}r\wedge\mathrm{d}x_{1}\wedge\mathrm{d}x_{2}.

By the Reeb–Liouville theorem [9, Theorem 2.2], every connected component of a regular level set of a Bott integral is a torus, and the Bott-integrable contact form is a Lutz form in a neighbourhood of this torus.

From these formulae it follows immediately that both RR (and hence TminT_{\mathrm{min}}) and vol⁡(W,α)\operatorname{vol}(W,\alpha) are invariant under reparametrisations [a,b]∋s↦r​(s)∈[−1,1][a,b]\ni s\mapsto r(s)\in[-1,1]. 3. Linearising the contact form

Let α\alpha be a Bott-integrable contact form on a closed 33-manifold MM, with Bott integral f:M→ℝf\colon\thinspace M\rightarrow\mathbb{R}. The aim of this section is to show that one can homotope α\alpha to a linear Lutz form

a​d​x1−(b​r+c)​d​x2,a\,\mathrm{d}x_{1}-(br+c)\,\mathrm{d}x_{2},

a,b∈ℝ+a,b\in\mathbb{R}^{+}, c∈ℝc\in\mathbb{R}, with Reeb vector field a−1​∂x1a^{-1}\partial_{x_{1}}, on a collection of product neighbourhoods [−1,1]×T2⊂M[-1,1]\times T^{2}\subset M whose complement is a set of arbitrarily small volume with respect to the new contact form.

Proposition 2.

Given (M,α,f)(M,\alpha,f), for every ε>0\varepsilon>0 one can find a Bott-integrable contact form β=βε\beta=\beta_{\varepsilon} with the following properties:

  1. (i)

    β\beta is homotopic to α\alpha via Bott-integrable contact forms, all sharing the Bott integral ff.

  2. (ii)

    Tmin​(M,β)>Tmin​(M,α)−εT_{\mathrm{min}}(M,\beta)>T_{\mathrm{min}}(M,\alpha)-\varepsilon and vol⁡(M,β)<vol⁡(M,α)+ε\operatorname{vol}(M,\beta)<\operatorname{vol}(M,\alpha)+\varepsilon.

  3. (iii)

    There is an open subset U⊂MU\subset M with vol⁡(U,β)<ε\operatorname{vol}(U,\beta)<\varepsilon such that M∖UM\setminus U is the disjoint union of finitely many subsets Wℓ⊂MW_{\ell}\subset M diffeomorphic to [−1,1]×T2[-1,1]\times T^{2}, with

    β|Wℓ=aℓ​d​x1−(bℓ​r+cℓ)​d​x2\beta|_{W_{\ell}}=a_{\ell}\,\mathrm{d}x_{1}-(b_{\ell}r+c_{\ell})\,\mathrm{d}x_{2}

    and f|Wℓ=f​(r)f|_{W_{\ell}}=f(r) with f′​(r)≠0f^{\prime}(r)\neq 0. Here aℓ∈ℝ+a_{\ell}\in\mathbb{R}^{+} is the minimal period of the Reeb flow on WℓW_{\ell}, and bℓ∈ℝ+b_{\ell}\in\mathbb{R}^{+} is determined by the identity vol⁡(Wℓ,β)=2​aℓ​bℓ\operatorname{vol}(W_{\ell},\beta)=2a_{\ell}b_{\ell}.

Proof.

The Morse–Bott function ff on the closed manifold MM has finitely many singular levels c1,…,ck∈ℝc_{1},\ldots,c_{k}\in\mathbb{R}. For δ>0\delta>0 sufficiently small, the union

U0:=⋃i=1k{p∈M:f​(p)∈(ci−δ,ci+δ)}U_{0}:=\bigcup_{i=1}^{k}\bigl\{p\in M\colon\thinspace f(p)\in(c_{i}-\delta,c_{i}+\delta)\bigr\}

of neighbourhoods of the singular level sets f−1​(ci)f^{-1}(c_{i}) will satisfy vol⁡(U0,α)<ε/4\operatorname{vol}(U_{0},\alpha)<\varepsilon/4.

By the Reeb–Liouville theorem [9, Theorem 2.2], the complement M∖U0M\setminus U_{0} is foliated by tori, each of which has a neighbourhood diffeomorphic to [−1,1]×T2[-1,1]\times T^{2}, where f=f​(r)f=f(r) with f′​(r)≠0f^{\prime}(r)\neq 0, and α=h1​(r)​d​x1+h2​(r)​d​x2\alpha=h_{1}(r)\,\mathrm{d}x_{1}+h_{2}(r)\,\mathrm{d}x_{2}. Thanks to M∖U0M\setminus U_{0} being compact, we can choose finitely many of these neighbourhoods whose interiors cover the complement of U0U_{0}. Moreover, it may be assumed that there are no triple intersections between these Lutz pieces or with U0U_{0}, and that the total volume of the pairwise intersections is smaller than ε/4\varepsilon/4.

Next we modify the contact form α\alpha on each of the finitely many product neighbourhoods [−1,1]×T2[-1,1]\times T^{2} as follows. In a small neighbourhood of r=±1r=\pm 1, containing the intersection with U0U_{0} or with another Lutz piece, we leave the plane curve r↦(h1​(r),h2​(r))r\mapsto\bigl(h_{1}(r),h_{2}(r)\bigr) unchanged. Outside these neighbourhoods, which we may assume to have total volume smaller than ε/2\varepsilon/2, we choose a C1C^{1}-close approximation by a piecewise linear curve r↦(g1​(r),g2​(r))r\mapsto\bigl(g_{1}(r),g_{2}(r)\bigr) with vertices on the curve (h1,h2)(h_{1},h_{2}) and rational (or infinite) slope g2′/g1′g_{2}^{\prime}/g_{1}^{\prime} of the linear pieces; the linear pieces need not be parametrised linearly. The convex linear interpolation between (h1,h2)(h_{1},h_{2}) and (g1,g2)(g_{1},g_{2}) defines a homotopy via contact forms sharing the same Bott integral.

Finally, we smoothen the curve (g1,g2)(g_{1},g_{2}) in small neighbourhoods of the vertices of total volume (with respect to the volume form β∧d​β\beta\wedge\mathrm{d}\beta defined by the contact form β\beta corresponding to the new curves (g1,g2)(g_{1},g_{2}) on all the Lutz pieces) smaller than ε/4\varepsilon/4. The last comment about convex linear interpolation still applies, so condition (i) in the proposition is satisfied by the resulting contact form β\beta.

With {Wℓ}\{W_{\ell}\} we denote the collection of linear pieces, and with UU their complement, made up of U0U_{0}, the smoothing regions, and the regions near the boundary of the original Lutz pieces where we have left the contact form α\alpha unchanged. By construction, we have vol⁡(U,β)<ε\operatorname{vol}(U,\beta)<\varepsilon.

For a sufficiently C1C^{1}-close approximation of the curves (g1,g2)(g_{1},g_{2}) describing the Lutz pieces, the volume conditions in (ii) and (iii) will be satisfied by the formula for vol⁡(W,α)\operatorname{vol}(W,\alpha) from Section 2.

The finitely many Lutz pieces making up the complement of UU are now of the form

[r0,r1]×T2,β=(b1​r+c1)​d​x1+(b2​r+c2)​d​x2,[r_{0},r_{1}]\times T^{2},\;\;\;\beta=(b_{1}r+c_{1})\,\mathrm{d}x_{1}+(b_{2}r+c_{2})\,\mathrm{d}x_{2},

with b2/b1∈ℚ∪{∞}b_{2}/b_{1}\in\mathbb{Q}\cup\{\infty\}. By a linear change of coordinates in T2T^{2}, using an element of SL​(2,ℤ)\mathrm{SL}(2,\mathbb{Z}), and a reparametrisation of the rr-coordinate, we can bring the Lutz pieces into the form described in (iii). The characterisations of aℓ,bℓa_{\ell},b_{\ell} in terms of minimal period and volume are clear from the formulae in Section 2.

It remains to show that Tmin​(M,β)T_{\mathrm{min}}(M,\beta) is bounded from below by Tmin​(M,α)−εT_{\mathrm{min}}(M,\alpha)-\varepsilon for a sufficiently C1C^{1}-close approximation β\beta. To this end, we need to relate the periodic orbits of RαR_{\alpha} and RβR_{\beta} on a Lutz piece [−1,1]×T2[-1,1]\times T^{2}, where α=h1​(r)​d​x1+h2​(r)​d​x2\alpha=h_{1}(r)\,\mathrm{d}x_{1}+h_{2}(r)\,\mathrm{d}x_{2} and β=g1​(r)​d​x1+g2​(r)​d​x2\beta=g_{1}(r)\,\mathrm{d}x_{1}+g_{2}(r)\,\mathrm{d}x_{2}. By formula (1) from Section 2 for the Reeb vector field on a Lutz piece, the flow of RβR_{\beta} is periodic on {r}×T2\{r\}\times T^{2} precisely when the slope g2′​(r)/g1′​(r)g_{2}^{\prime}(r)/g_{1}^{\prime}(r) is rational or infinite. In particular, this is the case on the linear pieces of (g1,g2)(g_{1},g_{2}).

By the choice of vertices of the piecewise linear approximation on the curve (h1,h2)(h_{1},h_{2}), and by the mean value theorem, the slope sℓ=g2′/g1′s_{\ell}=g_{2}^{\prime}/g_{1}^{\prime} of a linear piece [rℓ,rℓ+1][r_{\ell},r_{\ell+1}]—before the smoothing of (g1,g2)(g_{1},g_{2})—is realised by h2′​(rℓ∗)/h1′​(rℓ∗)h_{2}^{\prime}(r_{\ell}^{*})/h_{1}^{\prime}(r_{\ell}^{*}) at some point rℓ∗∈(rℓ,rℓ+1)r_{\ell}^{*}\in(r_{\ell},r_{\ell+1}); see Figure 1. Then

Rβ=Δh​(rℓ∗)Δg​(rℓ∗)⋅g1′​(rℓ∗)h1′​(rℓ∗)⋅RαR_{\beta}=\frac{\Delta_{h}(r_{\ell}^{*})}{\Delta_{g}(r_{\ell}^{*})}\cdot\frac{g_{1}^{\prime}(r_{\ell}^{*})}{h_{1}^{\prime}(r_{\ell}^{*})}\cdot R_{\alpha}

on {rℓ∗}×T2\{r_{\ell}^{*}\}\times T^{2} for h1′​(rℓ∗),g1′​(rℓ∗)≠0h_{1}^{\prime}(r_{\ell}^{*}),g_{1}^{\prime}(r_{\ell}^{*})\neq 0, else we replace the second factor by the quotient g2′​(rℓ∗)/h2′​(rℓ∗)g_{2}^{\prime}(r_{\ell}^{*})/h_{2}^{\prime}(r_{\ell}^{*}). The approximation being C1C^{1}-close implies that RβR_{\beta} and RαR_{\alpha} (and hence the corresponding minimal periods) differ by a factor close to 11.

In the smoothing regions of (g1,g2)(g_{1},g_{2}), the slope g2′/g1′g_{2}^{\prime}/g_{1}^{\prime} varies between two rational slopes sℓs_{\ell} and sℓ+1s_{\ell+1} of linear segments. This proves the estimate for TminT_{\mathrm{min}} in (ii) and hence the proposition. ∎

4. Integrability of the ABHS plug

The following proposition, due to Abbondandolo, Bramham, Hryniewicz and Salomão (ABHS), lists the essential properties of their plug; see [4, Proposition 3.1]. The details of the construction are contained in [3, Proposition 2.27] and [3, Proposition 3.1]. We write x,yx,y for the Cartesian coordinates and rr for the radial coordinate on the closed unit disc 𝔻\mathbb{D}, and ss for the angular coordinate on the circle ℝ/ℤ\mathbb{R}/\mathbb{Z}.

Proposition 3 (ABHS).

Let r0,δ>0r_{0},\delta>0 and a primitive λ\lambda of the standard area form d​x∧d​y\mathrm{d}x\wedge\mathrm{d}y on r0​𝔻r_{0}\mathbb{D} be given. Then there is a contact form β=βr0,λ,δ\beta=\beta_{r_{0},\lambda,\delta} on the solid torus r0​𝔻×ℝ/ℤr_{0}\mathbb{D}\times\mathbb{R}/\mathbb{Z} with the following properties:

  1. (i)

    β=λ+d​s\beta=\lambda+\mathrm{d}s near the boundary of the solid torus; in particular, the Reeb vector field equals ∂s\partial_{s} near the boundary.

  2. (ii)

    β\beta is isotopic relative to a neighbourhood of the boundary and via contact forms to λ+d​s\lambda+\mathrm{d}s.

  3. (iii)

    The minimal period of the Reeb vector field is at least 11.

  4. (iv)

    The volume vol⁡(r0​𝔻×ℝ/ℤ,β)\operatorname{vol}(r_{0}\mathbb{D}\times\mathbb{R}/\mathbb{Z},\beta) is smaller than δ\delta.

We want to prove the following addendum to this proposition.

Addendum 4.

The contact form β\beta in the preceding proposition admits a Bott integral ff whose level sets near the boundary of the solid torus are regular tori {r=const.}\{r=\mbox{\rm const.}\}, that is, f=f​(r)f=f(r) and f′​(r)>0f^{\prime}(r)>0 for rr close to r0r_{0}.

The key to Proposition 3 is the construction of an area-preserving diffeomorphism φ\varphi of r0​𝔻r_{0}\mathbb{D}, equal to the identity near the boundary, with control over the compactly supported primitive of the closed 11-form φ∗​λ−λ\varphi^{*}\lambda-\lambda, the periodic points and the Calabi invariant; see [4, Proposition 3.2]. Choose a positive primitive τ:r0​𝔻→ℝ+\tau\colon\thinspace r_{0}\mathbb{D}\rightarrow\mathbb{R}^{+} of φ∗​λ−λ\varphi^{*}\lambda-\lambda, constant near the boundary. Then the contact form λ+d​s\lambda+\mathrm{d}s on r0​𝔻×ℝr_{0}\mathbb{D}\times\mathbb{R} with Reeb vector field ∂s\partial_{s} is invariant under the diffeomorphism

Φ:r0​𝔻×ℝ⟶r0​𝔻×ℝ(z,s)⟼(φ​(z),s−τ​(z)),\begin{array}[]{rccc}\Phi\colon\thinspace&r_{0}\mathbb{D}\times\mathbb{R}&\longrightarrow&r_{0}\mathbb{D}\times\mathbb{R}\\ &(z,s)&\longmapsto&\bigl(\varphi(z),s-\tau(z)\bigr),\end{array}

and hence descends to a contact form on (r0​𝔻×ℝ)/⟨Φ⟩(r_{0}\mathbb{D}\times\mathbb{R})/\langle\Phi\rangle.

In order to prove Addendum 4, we simply need to exhibit a Morse function h:r0​𝔻→ℝh\colon\thinspace r_{0}\mathbb{D}\rightarrow\mathbb{R} (or the corresponding level sets) invariant under φ\varphi, and with regular levels {r=const.}\{r=\mbox{\rm const.}\} in a neighbourhood of the boundary of the disc. This Morse function then gives rise to a Bott integral, first on the mapping torus (r0​𝔻×ℝ)/⟨Φ⟩(r_{0}\mathbb{D}\times\mathbb{R})/\langle\Phi\rangle, and then on its diffeomorphic copy r0​𝔻×ℝ/ℤr_{0}\mathbb{D}\times\mathbb{R}/\mathbb{Z}, with the properties claimed in the addendum.

We now describe the relevant properties of φ\varphi; details of the construction are given in [3, pp. 734–736]. For ease of notation, we assume r0=1r_{0}=1; the general case follows by an appropriate rescaling as discussed after [4, Proposition 3.2]. The area-preserving diffeomorphism φ\varphi is a composition φ=φ+∘φ−\varphi=\varphi^{+}\circ\varphi^{-} of two Hamiltonian diffeomorphisms.

The construction of φ±\varphi^{\pm} depends on a parameter n∈ℕn\in\mathbb{N}; when chosen sufficiently large, this yields the required estimates in Proposition 3. The properties of the diffeomorphism φ+\varphi^{+} of 𝔻\mathbb{D} are:

  1. (i)

    Each circle {r=const.}\{r=\mbox{\rm const.}\} is rotated in positive direction through an angle at most 2​π/n2\pi/n.

  2. (ii)

    On a sufficiently large disc ρ​𝔻⊂𝔻\rho\mathbb{D}\subset\mathbb{D}, the rotation angle equals 2​π/n2\pi/n.

  3. (iii)

    Near the boundary of 𝔻\mathbb{D}, the map φ+\varphi^{+} is the identity.

Next one chooses a finite collection of pairwise disjoint closed discs DjD_{j}, j∈Jj\in J, in the sector

S:={z∈𝔻:|z|<ρ,arg⁡(z)∈(0,2​π/n)},S:=\bigl\{z\in\mathbb{D}\colon\thinspace|z|<\rho,\,\arg(z)\in(0,2\pi/n)\bigr\},

with the total area of the DjD_{j} close to π​ρ2/n\pi\rho^{2}/n. For each j∈Jj\in J one defines a Hamiltonian diffeomorphism φj\varphi_{j}, compactly supported in the interior of DjD_{j} and acting on DjD_{j} analogously to the action of φ+\varphi^{+} on 𝔻\mathbb{D}, but rotating in negative direction. Then extend this diffeomorphism of SS to a diffeomorphism φ−\varphi^{-} of 𝔻\mathbb{D} equivariant with respect to the ℤn\mathbb{Z}_{n}-action generated by the rigid rotation of 𝔻\mathbb{D} through an angle 2​π/n2\pi/n. We may think of the disc 𝔻/ℤn\mathbb{D}/\mathbb{Z}_{n} as being obtained by gluing the radial boundaries of the sector {arg⁡(z)∈(0,2​π/n)}⊂𝔻\{\arg(z)\in(0,2\pi/n)\}\subset\mathbb{D}, so we regard the DjD_{j} as discs in 𝔻/ℤn\mathbb{D}/\mathbb{Z}_{n}.

The relevant properties of the Morse function h:𝔻→ℝh\colon\thinspace\mathbb{D}\rightarrow\mathbb{R} are encoded in the Morse foliation of level sets of hh, which we now describe. Away from a small disc around the centre 0∈𝔻/ℤn0\in\mathbb{D}/\mathbb{Z}_{n} disjoint from the DjD_{j}, the branched covering 𝔻→𝔻/ℤn\mathbb{D}\rightarrow\mathbb{D}/\mathbb{Z}_{n} restricts to a covering A~→An\widetilde{A}\rightarrow A_{n} of annuli. Join the ‘beads’ DjD_{j} in AnA_{n} into a necklace as shown in Figure 2. This gives rise to a Morse foliation with a critical point of Morse index 0 at the centre of each DjD_{j}, and a critical point of index 11 on each connecting piece of the necklace. The corresponding Morse function on AnA_{n} extends to a Morse function on the disc 𝔻/ℤn\mathbb{D}/\mathbb{Z}_{n}, radially symmetric near the centre, and with an index 22 critical point at the centre. This function lifts to the desired Morse function hh on 𝔻\mathbb{D}.
