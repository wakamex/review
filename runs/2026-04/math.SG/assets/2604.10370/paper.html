Symplectic algebroids, groupoid Toeplitz operators and deformation quantization

Clement Cren, Jean-Marie Lescure, Omar Mohsen
Abstract.

We use Toeplitz operators to define a star-product on Poisson manifolds whose Poisson structure is induced by a symplectic Lie algebroid. The Toeplitz operators we consider are defined on groupoids whose algebroid can be endowed with a Heisenberg group structure on the fibers. This generalizes an approach due to Guillemin and Melrose in the symplectic case.

1. Introduction

Let (M,{⋅,⋅})(M,\{\cdot,\cdot\}) be a Poisson manifold. A deformation quantization of the Poisson structure is an algebra structure on the vector space formal power series

∗:𝒞∞(M)[[t]]⊗𝒞∞(M)[[t]]→𝒞∞(M)[[t]]\ast\colon\mathcal{C}^{\infty}(M)[[t]]\otimes\mathcal{C}^{\infty}(M)[[t]]\to\mathcal{C}^{\infty}(M)[[t]]

such that for all f,g∈𝒞∞​(M)f,g\in\mathcal{C}^{\infty}(M)

f∗g=f​g+O​(t),\displaystyle f\ast g=fg+O(t),
f∗g−g∗f=t​{f,g}+O​(t).\displaystyle f\ast g-g\ast f=t\{f,g\}+O(t).

Kontsevich [9] proved algebraically that any Poisson manifold admits a deformation quantization. In the special case of symplectic manifolds, several constructions of deformation quantization exist. We are interested here in the construction of Guillemin for prequantizable symplectic manifolds [8], later generalized by Melrose to arbitrary symplectic manifolds [14]. This construction uses the class of pseudodifferential operators on contact manifolds introduced in [1], also called Toeplitz operators. These operators are defined by compression of the pseudodifferential operators, using a certain projection, the so-called Szegö projection. The main idea of Guillemin and Melrose is that the symbol algebra of Toeplitz operators is isomorphic to C∞​(M)​[[t]]C^{\infty}(M)[[t]]. The star product then comes from composition of pseudo-differential operators.

In this paper, we adapt this method to Poisson structures induced by a symplectic algebroid introduced by Nest and Tsygan [18], recovering their construction of the star-product in an analytic way.

Our strategy is the following: using the symplectic form on the algebroid 𝒜s→M\mathcal{A}_{s}\to M, we can construct another algebroid 𝒜c=𝒜s⊕ℝ→M\mathcal{A}_{c}=\mathcal{A}_{s}\oplus\mathbb{R}\to M which is the contact analog of 𝒜s\mathcal{A}_{s}. The algebroid 𝒜c\mathcal{A}_{c} is filtered and the corresponding grading is a bundle of Heisenberg Lie algebras (this is where the symplectic structure is crucial). If the algebroid 𝒜c\mathcal{A}_{c} is integrable, and GcG_{c} is a groupoid integrating 𝒜c\mathcal{A}_{c}, we can construct pseudodifferential operators on GcG_{c} in the Heisenberg calculus. This calculus allows us to define a Szegö projector (at least formally, the operator itself does not need to be a projector). This operator yields an algebra of Toeplitz operators, for which the symbol algebra is C∞​(M)​[[t]]C^{\infty}(M)[[t]]. We then show that the composition of pseudodifferential operators gives a star product.

The algebroid 𝒜c\mathcal{A}_{c} does not need to be integrable in general. However, the previous construction can be done in any neighborhood of the unit and can thus be performed on a local groupoid, which always exists.

The paper is organized as follows. In Section 2 we introduce the geometric context, symplectic algebroids, and give examples of Poisson structures arising from them. We also define the contact analog of these algebroids by means of a canonical central extension, which corresponds to a prequantization of the symplectic Lie algebroid. In Section 3 we explain the structure of the (local) groupoids integrating these algebroids. They happen to have a right-invariant symplectic (or contact) structure on their range fibers. Groupoids integrating the symplectic algebroid can thus be seen as a family of symplectic manifolds desingularising the initial Poisson structure. Groupoids integrating the contact algebroid extension can then be seen as a family of contact manifolds prequantizing the previous family of symplectic ones. In Section 4 we review some features of the Heisenberg calculus on groupoids whose algebroid is filtered by a corank one subbundle. This calculus and its symbolic properties allow us in Section 5 to construct a (formal) Szegö projector and study the properties of the corresponding Toeplitz operators. In Section 6 we use these operators to construct a star product on Poisson manifolds whose Poisson structure is induced by a symplectic algebroid.

These examples showcase a symplectic structure on an open dense subset, which degenerates on its boundary in various ways, leading to different Poisson structures. We can also have foliated behaviors on the interior.

Example 2.6 (Regular symplectic foliations).

If the symplectic foliation is regular, the algebroid is given by the tangent space to the leaves. The Poisson structure then induces a symplectic structure on the algebroid in a tautological way. Toeplitz operators have been considered in that context (when the foliation is a fibration) in [4].

These examples can also be combined, for instance one could consider a foliated manifold, whose leaves are b-symplectic. The b-tangent bundles of the leaves would then induce a Lie algebroid on the manifold which is also symplectic…

Remark 2.7.

On a EE-symplectic manifold, the anchor map is injective over an open dense subset Mr​e​gM_{reg}. Over this subset Mr​e​gM_{reg} the image of π#\pi^{\#} then coincides with the one of ρ\rho and the symplectic leaves are exactly the ones of the foliation induced by the Lie algebroid (this foliation also happens to be regular over Mr​e​gM_{reg}).

On the whole manifold however, the two foliations may differ. In the bb-symplectic case for instance, both foliations will have an open dense leaf (which is the regular part as above), but they differ on the hypersurface. The algebroid foliation only has one other leaf which is the whole hypersurface. On the other hand, the symplectic leaves form a foliation of codimension 1 of the boundary.

The class of Poisson structures induced by a symplectic algebroid is also not well established in the literature. There are however examples of Poisson structures that are induced by symplectic algebroids but that are not EE-symplectic.

3. Integration of symplectic algebroids and their extensions

We keep the notations of the previous section. We denote by (𝒜s,ω)(\mathcal{A}_{s},\omega) a symplectic Lie algebroid and by 𝒜c\mathcal{A}_{c} the corresponding Lie algebroid extension by the 2-cocycle ω\omega.

Consider the one form θ∈Ω1​(𝒜c)\theta\in\Omega^{1}(\mathcal{A}_{c}) given by

∀(X,t)∈Ac,θ​(X,t)=−t.\forall(X,t)\in A_{c},\quad\theta(X,t)=-t.

This one form has kernel 𝒜s\mathcal{A}_{s} and using Equation 1, its differential is the pullback of ω\omega to 𝒜c\mathcal{A}_{c}. This form should be seen as a contact form on the algebroid 𝒜c\mathcal{A}_{c}, and the central extension as a prequantization of the symplectic structure (𝒜s,ω)(\mathcal{A}_{s},\omega) to the contact structure (𝒜c,θ)(\mathcal{A}_{c},\theta). We make sense of these intuition by integrating the algebroids 𝒜s\mathcal{A}_{s} and 𝒜c\mathcal{A}_{c}.

Assume for now that the algebroids 𝒜s\mathcal{A}_{s} and 𝒜c\mathcal{A}_{c} are integrable. Denote by Gs⇉MG_{s}\rightrightarrows M and Gc⇉MG_{c}\rightrightarrows M the ss-connected, ss-simply connected Lie groupoids integrating 𝒜s\mathcal{A}_{s} and 𝒜c\mathcal{A}_{c} respectively. The forms ω\omega and θ\theta induce right-invariant forms on the groupoids GsG_{s} and GcG_{c}, which we will denote by Ω\Omega and Θ\Theta respectively.

Proposition 3.1.

For each x∈Mx\in M, the range-fibers (Gs)x(G_{s})^{x} and (Gc)x(G_{c})^{x} are symplectic and contact manifolds for the restrictions of the form Ω\Omega and Θ\Theta respectively.

Proof.

Let γ∈Gs\gamma\in G_{s} with r​(γ)=xr(\gamma)=x. We have Tγ(Gs)x=ker(dr)γT_{\gamma}(G_{s})^{x}=\ker(\mathrm{d}r)_{\gamma}, hence Tγ​(Gs)x≅Rγ∗​(𝒜s)xT_{\gamma}(G_{s})_{x}\cong R_{\gamma}^{*}(\mathcal{A}_{s})_{x}. This linear isomorphism is a symplectomorphism for the restriction of Ωγ\Omega_{\gamma} and ωx\omega_{x} respectively. In particular the restriction of Ω\Omega to the range fibers is non-degenerate. Now the restriction of d​Ω\mathrm{d}\Omega to a range fiber is the right-invariant form corresponding to d​ω=0\mathrm{d}\omega=0 on the algebroid. Therefore (Gs)x(G_{s})^{x} is a symplectic manifold for the restriction of Ω\Omega. A similar reasoning holds for the contact case. ∎

The structure of the groupoid GsG_{s} was also identified in [19], with a more systematic approach on groupoids integrating symplectic algebroids. Such groupoids are then called rr-symplectic Lie groupoids (rr being the range map). In the same fashion, groupoid integrating the central extension of a symplectic algebroid could be called rr-contact Lie groupoids.

Remark 3.2.

Let H⊂ker⁡(d​r)⊂T​GcH\subset\ker(\mathrm{d}r)\subset TG_{c} be the right-invariant subbundle corresponding to 𝒜s⊂𝒜c\mathcal{A}_{s}\subset\mathcal{A}_{c}. Let RR be the right invariant vector field on GcG_{c} corresponding to the section

of the algebroid 𝒜c\mathcal{A}_{c}. The form Θ\Theta vanishes on HH and Θ​(R)=1\Theta(R)=1. Therefore HH restricts on each fiber to the contact distribution and RR to the Reeb vector field.

Proposition 3.3.

The groupoid Gs⇉MG_{s}\rightrightarrows M is a Poisson groupoid. In particular, the source map is a Poisson map and the target is anti-Poisson.

Proof.

Since ω\omega is non-degenerate, we can define π∈Γ​(Λ2​𝒜)\pi\in\Gamma(\Lambda^{2}\mathcal{A}) so that contraction by π\pi is the inverse of contraction by ω\omega. The identity d​ω=0\mathrm{d}\omega=0 then translates to [π,π]=0[\pi,\pi]=0. The pair (𝒜s,𝒜s∗)(\mathcal{A}_{s},\mathcal{A}_{s}^{*}) is then a Lie bialgebroid and the groupoid GsG_{s} is a Poisson groupoid [11, 10]. ∎

This proposition is the reason why we put the symplectic (and contact) structure on the range fibers. Indeed, for x∈Mx\in M, we get a Poisson map s:(Gs)x→Gs⋅xs\colon(G_{s})^{x}\to G_{s}\cdot x where Gs⋅xG_{s}\cdot x is the orbit of the point xx under the groupoid action. We identified the orbit to a Poisson manifold in Proposition 2.2 and the range fiber to a symplectic manifold in the previous proposition. The groupoid GsG_{s} can thus be seen as a family of symplectic realizations for the orbits it induces on the base.

In the next example, we relate the roles of the groupoids GsG_{s} and GcG_{c} to the objects used when prequantizing symplectic manifolds.

Example 3.4.

Let (M,ω)(M,\omega) be a (connected, simply connected) symplectic manifold. The algebroid T​MTM is then symplectic. In general one can define the so called periods groupoid Per​(ω)⊂M×ℝ\mathrm{Per}(\omega)\subset M\times\mathbb{R}. This philosophy of applying the quantization procedure to a groupoid originates in [23] for symplectic groupoids. Here the symplectic structure is only on the range fibers but the idea is similar.

In this paper, we are only interested in the neighborhood of the units in GcG_{c} so considering GcG_{c} or G~c\widetilde{G}_{c} doesn’t matter. Note that in general, algebroids need not being integrable [3]. We can however integrate them by local groupoids. These local Lie groupoids will inherit symplectic symplectic and contact structure as above. The local Lie groupoid integrating an algebroid is unique up to equivalence. Therefore with this local point of view, we do not see the difference between GcG_{c} and G~c\widetilde{G}_{c} anyway.

Now an algebroid is not always integrable. Even if GsG_{s} and GcG_{c} exist as topological groupoids, they may fail to have a compatible smooth structure. We can however always assume that there are local Lie groupoids.

By ‘whenever it makes sense’, we mean for instance that the associativity relation:

4. Heisenberg calculus on a groupoid

In this section we expose the features of the Heisenberg calculus on a groupoid. Given a Lie groupoid GG with algebroid 𝒜\mathcal{A}. One can construct a pseudodifferential calculus for which the kernels of operators will be distributions on GG, conormal to the units. The symbols are then functions on 𝒜∗\mathcal{A}^{*}. The differential operators on that calculus are constructed from the sections of the Lie algebroid, which act as order 1 operators. Consider now a groupoid GG whose algebroid 𝒜\mathcal{A} is filtered by a co-rank 1 subbundle 𝒜1⊂𝒜\mathcal{A}_{1}\subset\mathcal{A}. We can consider a new order on differential operators by considering sections of 𝒜\mathcal{A} that are not section of 𝒜1\mathcal{A}_{1} as having order 2 instead of 1. The construction of pseudodifferential operators is then modified to take into account this new order. The symbols are then operator valued functions on the unitary dual of a family of nilpotent group. If 𝒜\mathcal{A} is the central extension of a symplectic algebroid 𝒜1\mathcal{A}_{1} then these groups will be Heisenberg groups. Historically, this calculus has been invented to study the hypoelliptic properties of sub-laplacians in a sub-riemannian setting (e.g. contact manifolds). We are here interested in the symbolic properties. Since the principal symbols are families of operators on Hilbert spaces, they have non-trivial projectors. This allows to construct the Szegö projector: a projector SS such that both its kernel and image have infinite dimension. On a general groupoid it is not clear that such a projector would exist, but for our purpose we only need such an operator to be a projector modulo smoothing operators.

Let 𝒜→M\mathcal{A}\to M be a Lie algebroid and 𝒜1⊂𝒜\mathcal{A}_{1}\subset\mathcal{A} a co-rank 1 subbundle. We will assume that the vector bundle 𝒜\mathcal{A} decomposes as 𝒜=𝒜1⊕ℝ\mathcal{A}=\mathcal{A}_{1}\oplus\mathbb{R}. Define

ω:Γ​(𝒜1)∧Γ​(𝒜1)\displaystyle\omega\colon\Gamma(\mathcal{A}_{1})\wedge\Gamma(\mathcal{A}_{1}) →𝒞∞​(M)\displaystyle\to\mathcal{C}^{\infty}(M)

5. Toeplitz operators

Classically, Toeplitz operators arise as the compression of multiplication operators by a projection. Typically on the circle 𝕊1\mathbb{S}^{1}, we can consider the Hardy space H2⊂L2​(𝕊1)H^{2}\subset L^{2}(\mathbb{S}^{1}), the subspace of L2​(𝕊1)L^{2}(\mathbb{S}^{1}) of functions that extend holomorphically to the whole unit disk. Let SS be the orthogonal projector onto that subspace. Toeplitz operators are operators of the form S​Mf​SSM_{f}S where MfM_{f} is the multiplication operator by a continuous function f∈𝒞​(𝕊1)f\in\mathcal{C}(\mathbb{S}^{1}). The same construction applies if we replace 𝕊1\mathbb{S}^{1} by the boundary of a complex manifold, under a pseudoconvexity assumption. This pseudoconvexity actually implies that the boundary is a contact manifold. The construction of the Szegö projector and the corresponding Toeplitz operators has been extended to contact manifolds [1, 6, 5] (two constructions use a priori different operators to define the Szegö projection but are equivalent). It was also recently extended to contact fibrations in [4] and corresponds to families of Toeplitz operators parameterized by a base space.

Let (𝒜s,ω)(\mathcal{A}_{s},\omega) be a symplectic algebroid over a manifold MM, and 𝒜c\mathcal{A}_{c} its corresponding central extension. Let 𝔄\mathfrak{A} be the corresponding osculating groupoid. Since we took the central extension of a symplectic algebroid, it is a bundle of Heisenberg algebras.

Heisenberg groups have two types of irreducible unitary representations: the infinite dimensional ones, parameterized by ℝ∗\mathbb{R}^{*} and the characters. The characters of 𝔄\mathfrak{A} are parameterized by 𝒜s∗\mathcal{A}_{s}^{*}. Its infinite dimensional representations are parameterized by M×ℝ∗M\times\mathbb{R}^{*}. The representation corresponding to λ≠0\lambda\neq 0 is characterized by the fact that it sends ZZ, the generator of the center, to i​λ​Id\mathrm{i}\lambda\mathrm{Id}. We will use the infinite dimensional representations to define the symbol of the Szegö projection.
