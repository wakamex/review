A note on the cardinality of Lagrangian packings

Joé Brendel∗ ETH Zürich, Zurich, Switzerland joe.brendel@math.ethz.ch , Jean-Philippe Chassé† CRM, Montreal, Canada jean-philippe.chasse@umontreal.ca and Laurent Côté‡ Universität Bonn, Mathematical Institute, Bonn, Germany lcote@math.uni-bonn.de
Abstract.

Given a symplectic manifold, can one pack uncountably many Lagrangian submanifolds in a given Hamiltonian isotopy class of this symplectic manifold? We address C∞C^{\infty} and C0C^{0} versions of this question.

1. Introduction

1.1. Lagrangian packing problems

Given a symplectic manifold M=(M,ω)M=(M,\omega), let Ham⁡(M)⊂Diff0⁡(M)\operatorname{Ham}(M)\subset\operatorname{Diff}_{0}(M) be the group of compactly-supported Hamiltonian diffeomorphisms of MM. By definition, the elements of Ham⁡(M)\operatorname{Ham}(M) are those diffeomorphisms which occur as the time-11 flow of a compactly-supported Hamiltonian function H:[0,1]×M→ℝH:[0,1]\times M\to\mathbb{R}. Two (closed) submanifolds L,L′⊂ML,L^{\prime}\subset M are said to be Hamiltonian isotopic if L′=ϕ​(L)L^{\prime}=\phi(L) setwise, for some ϕ∈Ham⁡(M)\phi\in\operatorname{Ham}(M).

A Lagrangian packing problem in symplectic geometry usually takes the following form:

Fix a symplectic manifold MM and a closed Lagrangian submanifold L⊂ML\subset M. How many pairwise disjoint Hamiltonian isotopic copies of LL can you pack into MM?

  • •

    Sometimes this number is 11: this happens, for example, if the Floer homology is defined and nonzero, so that LL is non-displaceable, e.g. the equator in S2S^{2} or the zero section in any cotangent bundle.

  • •

    Sometimes this number is finite but strictly greater than 11: this holds trivially for small contractible Lagrangians for area reasons whenever dim⁡(M)=2\operatorname{dim}(M)=2. Remarkably, Polterovich–Shelukhin exhibited displaceable Lagrangian tori in a non-monotone S2×S2S^{2}\times S^{2} with finite packing number [p-s, Theorem C], using asymptotic Hofer geometry.

  • •

    Sometimes this number is infinite: for instance, it follows from work of Chekanov [ch] that there are infinite packings of Lagrangian tori in any Darboux ball of dimension at least six. In subsequent work, Chekanov–Schlenk [c-s, Theorem 1.5] give similar examples in dimension four. See also [Bre23, BreKim23] for constructions of such packings using symmetric probes.

These results beg the following question:

Question 1.1.

Do there exist uncountable Lagrangian packings?

As it turns out, the answer is no:

Proposition 1.2.

The cardinality of any Lagrangian packing is at most countable.

Proof.

Let ℋ=C∞​(S1×M)⊂C2​(S1×M)\mathcal{H}=C^{\infty}(S^{1}\times M)\subset C^{2}(S^{1}\times M) be endowed with the subspace topology. Let ψH1\psi^{1}_{H} denote the time-11 flow of H∈ℋH\in\mathcal{H} and let d​(−,−)d(-,-) be the restriction of the C2C^{2}-metric to ℋ\mathcal{H}.

Suppose there exists an uncountable packing {Lα}\{L_{\alpha}\}, and choose Hamiltonians Hα∈ℋH_{\alpha}\in\mathcal{H} so that Lα=ψHα1​(L)L_{\alpha}=\psi^{1}_{H_{\alpha}}(L). Note that, for each α\alpha, there exists ϵα>0\epsilon_{\alpha}>0 such that ψHα1​(L)∩ϕK1​(L)≠∅\psi^{1}_{H_{\alpha}}(L)\cap\phi^{1}_{K}(L)\neq\emptyset whenever d​(Hα,K)<ϵαd(H_{\alpha},K)<\epsilon_{\alpha}. Indeed, if two Hamiltonians are C2C^{2}-close, then their associated Hamiltonian diffeomorphism must be C1C^{1}-close, so that we can see one Lagrangian as a graph over the other (in a Weinstein neighbourhood). Hence the balls of size ϵα/2\epsilon_{\alpha}/2 around each HαH_{\alpha} must all be disjoint. But ℋ\mathcal{H} is second countable (being separable and metrizable), so does not admit an uncountable collection of disjoint balls. ∎

Using classical results of Laudenbach–Sikorav [laudenbach1994hamiltonian], one can in fact show that a (closed connected) half-dimensional submanifold L⊂ML\subset M admits an uncountable Hamiltonian packing if and only if LL is not Lagrangian and its normal bundle admits a nowhere vanishing section; see Proposition 3.2.111Note the following amusing consequence: if LL admits an uncountable Hamiltonian packing, then LL admits a packing with the cardinality of the reals, independently of the continuum hypothesis!

1.2. The C0C^{0} setting

Question 1.1 can also be formulated in the context of C0C^{0} symplectic geometry, where it intersects subtle questions related to flux and C0C^{0}-rigidity.

The study of C0C^{0} symplectic geometry was initiated by Gromov and Eliashberg’s discovery that the group of symplectomorphisms of a closed symplectic manifold is C0C^{0}-closed in the group of volume-preserving diffeomorphisms [gromov1986, eliashberg1987]. Since then, a number of symplectic invariants and properties — such as a submanifold being coisotropic, the closure of Ham⁡(M)\operatorname{Ham}(M) in Symp⁡(M)\operatorname{Symp}(M), and continuity properties of spectral invariants — have been shown to extend in the C0C^{0} topology (see e.g. [HumiliereLeclercqSeyfaddini2015, buhovsky2015, BuhovskyHumiliereSeyfaddini2021, m-o] and the references therein). We briefly recall the relevant definitions below.

Given a symplectic manifold M=(M,ω)M=(M,\omega), let

Ham¯​(M)⊂Homeoc⁡(M)\overline{\operatorname{Ham}}(M)\subset\operatorname{Homeo}_{c}(M)

the C0C^{0} closure of Ham⁡(M)\operatorname{Ham}(M) in the group of all compactly-supported homeomophisms of MM. Elements of Ham¯​(M)\overline{\operatorname{Ham}}(M) are called Hamiltonian homeomorphisms.

Definition 1.3.

Fix a smooth submanifold Σ⊂M\Sigma\subset M. A C0C^{0} Lagrangian packing of Σ\Sigma is a collection of pairwise disjoint smooth submanifolds {Σα}\{\Sigma_{\alpha}\} such that Σα=ϕα​(Σ)\Sigma_{\alpha}=\phi_{\alpha}(\Sigma) for some ϕα∈Ham¯​(M,ω)\phi_{\alpha}\in\overline{\operatorname{Ham}}(M,\omega).

We can now ask:

Question 1.4.

Do there exist uncountable C0C^{0} Lagrangian packings (i.e. does there exist a (closed) Lagrangian submanifold L⊂ML\subset M which admits an uncountable packing in the sense of Definition 1.3)?

A naive attempt to replicate the proof of Proposition 1.2 breaks down. We of course know that Ham¯​(M)\overline{\operatorname{Ham}}(M) is metrizable and second countable (since it sits inside the space of continuous self maps of MM). However, given ψ∈Ham¯​(M)\psi\in\overline{\operatorname{Ham}}(M), we do not know whether there exists a ball BB centered at ψ\psi with the property that ψ′​(L)∩ψ​(L)≠∅\psi^{\prime}(L)\cap\psi(L)\neq\emptyset whenever ψ′∈B\psi^{\prime}\in B. If LL is such that ω​(H2​(M,L))\omega(H_{2}(M,L)) is discrete, then this is proved in [a-c-l-s]; the general case is essentially [a-c-l-s, Conjecture E]. Note that this rationality condition is much stronger than what appears below.

Instead, we consider a different line of argument in the spirit of [a-c-l-s], which involves an analysis of the flux morphism. This yields another elementary222Meaning both that it is equally easy and that neither proof needs JJ-holomophic curves. proof of Proposition 1.2, and also gives the harder:333Meaning both that the argument is more difficult and that it relies on JJ-holomorphic curves (although these only enter indirectly, through results we quote).

Theorem 1.5.

Let M=(M,ω)M=(M,\omega) be a symplectic manifold. Let L⊂ML\subset M be a (closed connected) half-dimensional submanifold with the property that

(1.1) ⟨Γt​o​p,ι∗​(H1​(L))⟩∩ω​(π2​(M))⊂ℝ\langle\Gamma_{top},\iota_{*}(H_{1}(L))\rangle\cap\omega(\pi_{2}(M))\subset\mathbb{R}

is discrete. Then LL admits an uncountable C0C^{0} packing if and only if LL is not Lagrangian and its normal bundle admits a nowhere vanishing section.

Here Γt​o​p\Gamma_{top} is the topological flux group of MM, a notion which we review in Subsection 2.3. Essentially, ⟨Γt​o​p,ι∗​(H1​(L))⟩\langle\Gamma_{top},\iota_{*}(H_{1}(L))\rangle consists of evaluations of ω\omega on a suitable subgroup of H2​(M)H_{2}(M) associated to certain tori S1×S1→MS^{1}\times S^{1}\to M such that the restriction to {1}×S1\{1\}\times S^{1} is a loop in LL. In practice, (1.1) is reasonably checkable: it obviously holds whenever ω​(π2​(M))\omega(\pi_{2}(M)) is discrete, or when ι∗:H1​(L;ℝ)→H1​(M;ℝ)\iota_{*}:H_{1}(L;\mathbb{R})\to H_{1}(M;\mathbb{R}) is zero (so e.g. it is enough for MM to be simply connected). Nevertheless, we expect that the conclusion of Theorem 1.5 holds for all symplectic manifolds, without any additional topological condition.

2. Preparations

2.1. Conventions

All rings are understood to be commutative and unital. All manifolds are by definition boundaryless, Hausdorff and second-countable. Unless otherwise indicated, all manifolds and all maps between them are assumed to be smooth.

2.2. Some topological properties of the space of Lagrangians

The purpose of this paragraph is to show that the space of compact Lagrangian embeddings into some symplectic manifold (M,ω)(M,\omega) is second-countable and to deduce the following.

Lemma 2.1.

Let M=(M,ω)M=(M,\omega) be a connected symplectic manifold. Let 𝒳\mathcal{X} be a set of pairwise disjoint, compact Lagrangian submanifolds of MM. If 𝒳\mathcal{X} is uncountable, then there exists K∈𝒳K\in\mathcal{X} and a Weinstein neighborhood of KK containing uncountably many elements of 𝒳\mathcal{X}.

To start, let LL be a smooth, compact manifold, and let MM be a smooth manifold. Let C∞​(L,M)C^{\infty}(L,M) be the set of infinitely differentiable maps from LL to MM, endowed with the Whitney topology [g-g, II.§3]. Recall that a basis for this topology is given by the preimages of all open subsets U⊂Jk​(L,M),1≤k<∞U\subset J^{k}(L,M),1\leq k<\infty under the natural map sending a function to its kk-th jet. The space C∞​(L,M)C^{\infty}(L,M) is metrizable and separable, hence second countable [hirsch, Sec. 1.1].

Since the Jk​(L,M)J^{k}(L,M) are manifolds and hence second-countable, C∞​(L,M)C^{\infty}(L,M) is second-countable. Let Ce​m​b∞​(L,M)⊂C∞​(L,M)C^{\infty}_{emb}(L,M)\subset C^{\infty}(L,M) be the (open) subset of embeddings and let

Σ∞​(L,M):=Ce​m​b∞​(L,M)/Diff⁡(L)\Sigma^{\infty}(L,M):=C^{\infty}_{emb}(L,M)/\operatorname{Diff}(L)

be the space of embedded smooth submanifolds of MM which are diffeomorphic to LL. Here Diff⁡(L)\operatorname{Diff}(L) acts on the right by precomposition. It follows that Σ∞​(L,M)\Sigma^{\infty}(L,M) is second-countable.

Now let (M,ω)(M,\omega) be a symplectic manifold and denote by ΣL​a​g∞​(L,M)⊂Σe​m​b∞​(L,M)\Sigma^{\infty}_{Lag}(L,M)\subset\Sigma^{\infty}_{emb}(L,M) the subset of Lagrangian embeddings of LL into MM. It follows that ΣL​a​g∞​(L,M)\Sigma^{\infty}_{Lag}(L,M) is second-countable.

Definition 2.2.

Let (M,ω)(M,\omega) be a symplectic manifold and let ι:L→M\iota:L\to M be a Lagrangian embedding. A Weinstein neighborhood subordinate to the embedding ι\iota is the data (𝒰,ι~)(\mathcal{U},\tilde{\iota}) of an open subset 𝒰⊂T∗​L\mathcal{U}\subset T^{*}L containing the zero section, and a symplectic embedding ι~:𝒰→M\tilde{\iota}:\mathcal{U}\to M extending ι\iota.

Let ι:L→(M,ω)\iota:L\to(M,\omega) be a Lagrangian embedding. Fix a Weinstein neighborhood (𝒰,ι~)(\mathcal{U},\tilde{\iota}) and let

(2.1) 𝒪L​a​g​(𝒰,ι~)⊂ΣL​a​g∞​(L,M)\mathcal{O}_{Lag}(\mathcal{U},\tilde{\iota})\subset\Sigma^{\infty}_{Lag}(L,M)

be the subset of those elements K∈ΣL​a​g∞​(L,M)K\in\Sigma^{\infty}_{Lag}(L,M) such that there exists a 11-form αK∈Ω1​(L)\alpha_{K}\in\Omega^{1}(L) on LL such that graph⁡(αK)⊂𝒰\operatorname{graph}(\alpha_{K})\subset\mathcal{U} and ι~​(graph⁡αK)=K\tilde{\iota}(\operatorname{graph}\alpha_{K})=K. Since KK is Lagrangian, any such 11-form is necessarily closed, d​αK=0d\alpha_{K}=0. The sets 𝒪L​a​g​(𝒰,ι~)\mathcal{O}_{Lag}(\mathcal{U},\tilde{\iota}) are open. We are now in a position to prove the lemma.

Proof of Lemma 2.1.

Since there are countably many diffeomorphism types, we may assume all elements of 𝒳\mathcal{X} are diffeomorphic to some compact manifold LL. In other words, 𝒳\mathcal{X} is a subset of ΣL​a​g∞​(L,M)\Sigma^{\infty}_{Lag}(L,M). If we endow 𝒳\mathcal{X} with the subspace topology, it is second-countable, since Σl​a​g∞​(L,M)\Sigma^{\infty}_{lag}(L,M) is. For each K∈𝒳K\in\mathcal{X}, choose a Weinstein neighborhood (𝒰K,ι~K)(\mathcal{U}_{K},\tilde{\iota}_{K}), where ιK:K↪M\iota_{K}:K\hookrightarrow M is the tautological inclusion. The 𝒪​(𝒰K,ι~K)\mathcal{O}(\mathcal{U}_{K},\tilde{\iota}_{K}) are open in Σl​a​g∞​(L,M)\Sigma^{\infty}_{lag}(L,M); hence their restriction to 𝒳\mathcal{X} is also open. Hence they form an open cover of 𝒳\mathcal{X}. By second-countability, there exists a countable subcover. Hence there exists some K∈𝒳K\in\mathcal{X} such that uncountably many of the elements of 𝒳\mathcal{X} are contained in 𝒪​(𝒰K,ι~K)\mathcal{O}(\mathcal{U}_{K},\tilde{\iota}_{K}). ∎

2.3. Notions of flux

There are several notions of “flux” in symplectic geometry which all arise from variants of the same construction. We briefly review (some of) these here.

Let M=(M,ω)M=(M,\omega) be a symplectic manifold. Let {Lt}t∈[0,1]\{L_{t}\}_{t\in[0,1]} be a Lagrangian isotopy. The (Lagrangian) flux of the isotopy is the class Flux⁡(Ls)∈H1​(L;ℝ)\operatorname{Flux}(L_{s})\in H^{1}(L;\mathbb{R}) defined as follows: let ξ\xi be (a representative of) any cycle in H1​(L;ℝ)H_{1}(L;\mathbb{R}). Let Zξ:[0,1]×S1→MZ_{\xi}:[0,1]\times S^{1}\to M be the trace of ξ\xi under the isotopy. Then Flux⁡(Ls)∈H1​(L;ℝ)\operatorname{Flux}(L_{s})\in H^{1}(L;\mathbb{R}) is the unique class satisfying ⟨ω,Zξ⟩=⟨Flux⁡(Ls),ξ⟩\langle\omega,Z_{\xi}\rangle=\langle\operatorname{Flux}(L_{s}),\xi\rangle. It is a basic fact that the Lagrangian flux of an isotopy induced by an ambient Hamiltonian isotopy on MM vanishes.

One can similarly define the (symplectic) flux of a symplectic isotopy {ψs}\{\psi_{s}\} to be the unique class Fluxω⁡(ψs)\operatorname{Flux}_{\omega}(\psi_{s}) of H1​(M;ℝ)H^{1}(M;\mathbb{R}) such that ⟨ω,Zξ⟩=⟨Fluxω⁡(ψs),ξ⟩\langle\omega,Z_{\xi}\rangle=\langle\operatorname{Flux}_{\omega}(\psi_{s}),\xi\rangle for all loops ξ\xi of MM. Alternatively, it is the Lagrangian flux of the Lagrangian isotopy {graph⁡ψs}\{\operatorname{graph}\psi_{s}\} in (M×M,ω⊕−ω)(M\times M,\omega\oplus-\omega). The symplectic flux induces a homomorphism Fluxω:Symp~⁡(M)→H1​(M;ℝ)\operatorname{Flux}_{\omega}:\operatorname{\widetilde{Symp}}(M)\to H^{1}(M;\mathbb{R}). Its image is called the flux group of MM and denoted by Γω⊂H1​(M;ℝ)\Gamma_{\omega}\subset H^{1}(M;\mathbb{R}).

More generally, let fs:N→M,s∈[0,1]f_{s}:N\to M,s\in[0,1], be a continuous family of continuous maps and set F:[0,1]×N→M,(s,n)↦fs​(n)F:[0,1]\times N\to M,(s,n)\mapsto f_{s}(n). There is a natural map H1​(N;ℤ)→ℝH_{1}(N;\mathbb{Z})\to\mathbb{R} sending a cycle ξ\xi to ⟨ω,F​([0,1]×ξ)⟩\langle\omega,F([0,1]\times\xi)\rangle which induces a homomorphism Fluxt​o​p:π1​(C0​(N,M))→H1​(N;ℝ).\operatorname{Flux}_{top}:\pi_{1}(C^{0}(N,M))\to H^{1}(N;\mathbb{R}).

Definition 2.3.

The image of the map Fluxt​o​p:π1​(C0​(N,M))→H1​(N;ℝ)\operatorname{Flux}_{top}:\pi_{1}(C^{0}(N,M))\to H^{1}(N;\mathbb{R}) is called the topological flux group and is denoted by Γt​o​p⊂H1​(N;ℝ)\Gamma_{top}\subset H^{1}(N;\mathbb{R}).

3. Proofs

We first record an elementary linear algebra lemma which will be used later.

Lemma 3.1.

Let LL be a finite dimensional lattice and set V:=L⊗ℤℝV:=L\otimes_{\mathbb{Z}}\mathbb{R}. If S⊂V∗S\subset V^{*} is a subset with the property that for every ξ∈L⊂V\xi\in L\subset V, the set

(3.1) {⟨s,ξ⟩∣s∈S}⊂ℝ\{\langle s,\xi\rangle\mid s\in S\}\subset\mathbb{R}

is countable, then SS is countable. (Here ⟨−,−⟩:V∗×V→ℝ\langle-,-\rangle:V^{*}\times V\to\mathbb{R} denotes the natural pairing of VV with its dual.)

Proof.

Choose a basis for VV and let s=(s1,…,sn)∈Ss=(s_{1},\dots,s_{n})\in S. By testing against the dual basis, we find that the coordinates sis_{i} take values in a countable set.444So in particular, it is enough to know (3.1) on any subset of lattice elements which form a real basis for VV. ∎

3.1. The C∞C^{\infty} case

As a warm-up for the proof of Theorem 1.5, we give an alternative proof of Proposition 1.2 based on the Lagrangian flux as discussed in Subsection 2.3.

Second proof of Proposition 1.2.

Suppose for contradiction that 𝒫\mathcal{P} is an uncountable packing. By Lemma 2.1, we may assume without loss of generality that 𝒫\mathcal{P} is entirely contained in 𝒪L​a​g​(𝒰,ι~)\mathcal{O}_{Lag}(\mathcal{U},\tilde{\iota}) for some Weinstein neighborhood (𝒰,ι)(\mathcal{U},\iota) of LL. For each K∈𝒫K\in\mathcal{P}, there is a 11-form αK∈Ω1​(L)\alpha_{K}\in\Omega^{1}(L) so that K=graph⁡(αK)K=\operatorname{graph}(\alpha_{K}). It is enough to prove that the set of 11-forms αK\alpha_{K} representing elements of 𝒫\mathcal{P} is countable.

The first step is to construct a piecewise smooth path of Lagrangians (Ks)s∈[0,1](K_{s})_{s\in[0,1]} as follows:

  1. (1)

    first apply the linear isotopy [0,1/2]∋s↦φ~​(graph⁡(2​s​αK))[0,1/2]\ni s\mapsto\tilde{\varphi}(\operatorname{graph}(2s\alpha_{K}))

  2. (2)

    then let {Ks}s∈[1/2,1]\{K_{s}\}_{s\in[1/2,1]} be any Hamiltonian isotopy taking KK back to LL setwise (this exists since K∈𝒫K\in\mathcal{P})

Now fix a curve ξ:S1→L\xi:S^{1}\to L representing a cycle [ξ]∈H1​(L;ℤ)[\xi]\in H_{1}(L;\mathbb{Z}). Let Zξ:[0,1]×S1→MZ_{\xi}:[0,1]\times S^{1}\to M be the cylinder swept out by the family (Ks)(K_{s}). We write Zξ=Zξ(1)∪Zξ(2)Z_{\xi}=Z_{\xi}^{(1)}\cup Z_{\xi}^{(2)}, where Zξ(i)Z_{\xi}^{(i)} is the cylinder from step (i)(i) of the isotopy.

Finally, we compute

(3.2) ⟨Flux⁡(Ks),ξ⟩=∫Zξω=∫Zξ(1)ω+∫Zξ(2)ω=∫Zξ(1)ω=⟨αK,ξ⟩\langle\operatorname{Flux}(K_{s}),\xi\rangle=\int_{Z_{\xi}}\omega=\int_{Z_{\xi}^{(1)}}\omega+\int_{Z_{\xi}^{(2)}}\omega=\int_{Z_{\xi}^{(1)}}\omega=\langle\alpha_{K},\xi\rangle

By Lemma 3.1, it follows that only countably many classes [αK]∈H1​(L;ℝ)[\alpha_{K}]\in H^{1}(L;\mathbb{R}) can be represented by elements in 𝒫\mathcal{P}. Recall however that if [αK]=[αK′][\alpha_{K}]=[\alpha_{K^{\prime}}], then KK and K′K^{\prime} intersect. The conclusion follows. ∎

Recall that a submanifold N⊂MN\subset M is called instantaneously displaceable if there is a Hamiltonian vector field of MM that is nowhere tangent to NN.

Proposition 3.2.

Let NN be a (closed) submanifold of M2​nM^{2n} of dimension k≤nk\leq n. The following statements are equivalent.

  1. (a)

    NN admits an uncountable packing.

  2. (b)

    NN is instantaneously displaceable.

  3. (c)

    NN is not Lagrangian and its normal bundle admits a nowhere vanishing section.

We first note:

Lemma 3.3.

If a submanifold NN of MM admits an uncountable packing by smooth isotopies, then its normal bundle has a nowhere vanishing section.

Proof.

In the C∞C^{\infty} topology, every element of Σ∞​(N,M)\Sigma^{\infty}(N,M) admits a neighbourhood such that every element in it can be represented by the graph of a section of the normal bundle of NN in MM. Let {Nα}\{N_{\alpha}\} be an uncountable packing of NN. By second countability of Σ∞​(N,M)\Sigma^{\infty}(N,M), there is a countable subpacking {Nαi}i=1∞\{N_{\alpha_{i}}\}_{i=1}^{\infty} such that ⋃αNα\bigcup_{\alpha}N_{\alpha} is covered by neighbourhoods as above, centered at NαiN_{\alpha_{i}}. But then, there is some α∉{αi}\alpha\notin\{\alpha_{i}\} such that NαN_{\alpha} is in the neighbourhood associated to some NαjN_{\alpha_{j}}. Therefore, there is a section ν\nu of the normal bundle of NαjN_{\alpha_{j}} such that NαN_{\alpha} is the graph of ν\nu. Since Nα∩Nαj=∅N_{\alpha}\cap N_{\alpha_{j}}=\emptyset, ν\nu does not vanish at any point. ∎

Proof of Proposition 3.2.

We first show that (a) implies (c). Assume that (a) holds. By Lemma 3.3, NN has a nowhere vanishing normal vector field. Moreover, NN cannot be Lagrangian by Proposition 1.2.

We now deduce (b) from (c). Assume that (c) holds. If k=nk=n, then NN is instantaneously displaceable by the main result of Laudenbach–Sikorav [laudenbach1994hamiltonian]. If k<nk<n, this is folklore (see also [gurel2008] for a formal proof of a more general result).

Finally, (b) clearly implies (a): if {ϕt}\{\phi^{t}\} is the flow of the Hamiltonian vector field that is nowhere tangent to NN, then ϕt​(N)∩ϕs​(N)=∅\phi^{t}(N)\cap\phi^{s}(N)=\emptyset for all s,ts,t small enough. ∎

3.2. Proof of Theorem 1.5

We deduce Theorem 1.5 by combining Proposition 3.2 with the following:

Proposition 3.4.

Let M=(M,ω)M=(M,\omega) be a closed symplectic manifold. Let L⊂ML\subset M be a Lagrangian submanifold with the property that

(3.3) ⟨Γt​o​p,ι∗​(H1​(L))⟩∩ω​(π2​(M))⊂ℝ\langle\Gamma_{top},\iota_{*}(H_{1}(L))\rangle\cap\omega(\pi_{2}(M))\subset\mathbb{R}

is discrete. The packing cardinality of LL is at most countable.

We will need the following lemma, whose proof is an elementary exercise using the exponential map.

Lemma 3.5.

Let (M,g)(M,g) be a Riemannian manifold with injectivity radius r0>0r_{0}>0. Suppose that f0,f1:M→Mf_{0},f_{1}:M\to M are compactly supported smooth maps with the property that d​i​s​t​(f0​(x),f1​(x))<ϵ<r0dist(f_{0}(x),f_{1}(x))<\epsilon<r_{0} for all xx. Then there exists a smooth homotopy {ht:M→M}t∈[0,1],h0=f0,h1=f1\{h^{t}:M\to M\}_{t\in[0,1]},h^{0}=f_{0},h^{1}=f_{1}, with the property that s↦d​i​s​t​(hs​(x),h0​(x))s\mapsto dist(h^{s}(x),h^{0}(x)) is non-decreasing for all x∈Mx\in M. ∎

Remark 3.10.

Tracing through the above argument, the only place where we used the assumption that (3.3) is discrete was to ensure that ω​(Zk,ξ)\omega(Z_{k,\xi}) is eventually independent of kk. Instead, it would also be enough to assume that ϕ\phi can be “well approximated” by Hamiltonian diffeomorphisms, i.e. the approximating sequence {ϕk}\{\phi_{k}\} may be chosen so that the relative homotopy class of the path ϕk​#​fk\phi_{k}\#f_{k} in C0​(M,M)C^{0}(M,M) is constant.

This can always be done if, on a C0C^{0}-neighbourhood of the identity in Ham⁡(M)\operatorname{Ham}(M), every Hamiltonian diffeomorphism is the time-1 map of a Hamiltonian isotopy {ϕHt}\{\phi^{t}_{H}\} such that maxt⁡dC0​(i​d,ϕHt)≤C​dC0​(i​d,ϕH1)α\max_{t}d_{C^{0}}(id,\phi^{t}_{H})\leq Cd_{C^{0}}(id,\phi^{1}_{H})^{\alpha}, for some C,α>0C,\alpha>0 independent of the isotopy. In general, this is a very hard property to prove, but it is known to hold when MM is a closed surface or the Euclidean ball (see Remark 3.4 and Lemma 3.2 of [seyfaddini2013], respectively). However, in those examples, ω​(π2​(M))\omega(\pi_{2}(M)) is discrete so Proposition 3.4 already does the trick as currently stated.

4. Further questions

  1. (1)

    A Legendrian variant: let (V,ξ)(V,\xi) be a contact manifold. Can one find uncountably many closed Legendrian submanifolds which are pairwise Legendrian isotopic, no two of which are connected by a Reeb chord?

  2. (2)

    Let (M2​n,ω)(M^{2n},\omega) be symplectic and let K⊂M,d​i​m​(K)≥nK\subset M,dim(K)\geq n be a submanifold whose normal bundle has a nowhere vanishing section. Prove or disprove: KK admits an uncountable packing if and only if it KK is not coisotropic.

    The case d​i​m​(K)=ndim(K)=n is handled by Proposition 1.2 and Proposition 3.2. See Gürel [gurel2008] for a partial result when d​i​m​(K)>ndim(K)>n.

  3. (3)

    Other variants of Definition 1.3 are certainly possible, in fact arguably more natural. For example, one could define a C0C^{0}-packing as any collection of arbitrary subsets {Σα}\{\Sigma_{\alpha}\} such that Σα=ϕα​(Σ)\Sigma_{\alpha}=\phi_{\alpha}(\Sigma) for some ϕα∈Ham¯​(M,ω)\phi_{\alpha}\in\overline{\operatorname{Ham}}(M,\omega).
